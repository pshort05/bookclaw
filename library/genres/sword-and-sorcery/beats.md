Sword-and-sorcery structure is episodic, not three-act epic — each story or major sequence must be self-contained, opening in media res and closing with the protagonist alive and moving on rather than triumphant.

## Structural Model

S&S does not follow the save-the-world story engine. The unit is the **job**: a problem the protagonist is hired to solve, stumbles into, or is forced to confront. The beats below reflect this episodic job-arc model. A novel-length S&S work typically chains two to four of these arcs with light connective tissue.

## The Seven S&S Structural Beats

**Beat 1 — Opening in Media Res: The Protagonist in Motion.**
Introduce the protagonist already doing something physical, dangerous, or morally compromised. Do not open with backstory, a prophecy, or a peaceful life about to be disrupted. The reader should be oriented within two paragraphs: who is this person, what are they doing right now, and what is immediately at stake. Howard almost never opened a Conan story with exposition — he opened in a tavern brawl, a dungeon, a chase.

**Beat 2 — The Job or the Complication.**
The protagonist acquires a goal. This is rarely heroic: they are hired, they stumble on an opportunity, they are coerced, or they are owed a debt that must be collected. The job defines the story's scope. Establish what success looks like and what the payment is (coin, safety, revenge). The complication is that the job is not what it appears — the patron has withheld information, the target is something far worse, or the environment is about to become actively hostile.

**Beat 3 — First Contact with the Real Threat.**
The sorcerous or supernatural element reveals itself. This is not the climax — it is the moment the protagonist discovers the job has escalated past normal violence into something genuinely dangerous. The tone should shift here: from action-thriller into dread. The protagonist does not yet know how to defeat this threat, and their existing competence is not sufficient.

**Beat 4 — Reversal, Capture, or Catastrophic Loss.**
Something goes wrong in a way the protagonist cannot skill their way out of. They are captured, separated from their companion, lose their weapon, or make a choice that costs them something they cannot get back. This beat exists to establish genuine stakes — that the protagonist can lose, be humiliated, and suffer. It is not a "dark night of the soul" in the epic sense; there is no time for extended reflection. The reversal is tactical, not spiritual.

**Beat 5 — The Protagonist's Advantage Identified.**
The protagonist identifies — through observation, conversation, or desperate reasoning — the specific vulnerability of the threat they face. The sorcerer has a binding that can be turned. The ancient creature has a weakness in how it was imprisoned. The villain's hired guards are loyal only to payment. This beat rewards the protagonist's competence and establishes how the climax will be won. The solution should feel earned, not deus-ex-machina.

**Beat 6 — The Violent, Costly Resolution.**
The protagonist exploits the vulnerability. The climax is fast, physical, and dangerous. They win, but not cleanly — something is spent, broken, or lost. A companion is wounded. The coin is gone. The escape requires burning down something valuable. S&S victories are satisfying precisely because they are not triumphant: the problem is solved, the immediate threat is neutralized, and the protagonist is alive. Nothing else is guaranteed.

**Beat 7 — The Road Again.**
The protagonist leaves. No victory lap, no restoration, no return home. The city may be behind them, the sorcerer's tower may be rubble, and the patron may be dead or fled. The protagonist pockets what they have, tends their wounds, and moves toward the next horizon. This ending beat is essential to S&S's episodic identity: it signals to the reader that the genre's promise is the journey, not the destination.

## Obligatory Scenes

**The tavern or den scene.** Establishes the social texture of the world — corrupt, dangerous, and transactional. The protagonist is observed by someone, overhears something, or is approached. Information changes hands.

**The combat that establishes physical register.** An early fight that shows how the protagonist moves, what they are capable of, and what violence actually feels like in this world. This scene calibrates the reader for everything that follows.

**The sorcerer reveal.** The moment the audience understands what kind of supernatural threat the story is dealing with. The reveal should produce dread, not wonder. The sorcerer's motivation is alien or self-serving in ways that resist easy comprehension.

**The moral compromise.** A moment where the protagonist makes a choice that a heroic protagonist would not make. They leave someone behind. They destroy something that should have been protected. They take the coin from someone who probably needed it. The scene should be written without extended moralizing — the choice is made, the story moves on.

**The dark place.** Physical and psychological. The protagonist in a dungeon, a ruin, a cursed temple, or an enemy's stronghold, with reduced resources and genuine uncertainty about survival. The genre's dread peaks here.
