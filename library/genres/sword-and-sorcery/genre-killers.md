Each of these mistakes breaks the implicit contract with S&S readers — readers who chose this genre specifically to avoid what epic fantasy, heroic fantasy, or grimdark provides.

- [ ] **A chosen-one destiny arc.** This converts S&S into epic fantasy and destroys the morally grey contract. If the protagonist has a prophecy, a bloodline destiny, or a cosmic role, they are no longer a mercenary trying to survive — they are a hero with plot armor. S&S readers specifically reject this framing. A chosen-one protagonist fighting to save the world is not S&S, regardless of how gritty the prose is.

- [ ] **Magic that is systematic, learnable, and safe.** A magic system with clear rules, schools, and progression is a hallmark of epic fantasy and progression fantasy. In S&S, magic is a transgression. A magic academy, a skill-tree power system, or a protagonist who gets progressively more magically powerful without cost will read as genre misfire to S&S readers.

- [ ] **A protagonist whose motives are altruistic from the start.** If the protagonist is fighting because it is the right thing to do, not because they are paid, coerced, or personally invested, the moral register has drifted toward heroic fantasy. The protagonist can make altruistic choices under pressure, but their baseline orientation must be self-interested.

- [ ] **A multi-book plot that only pays off at series end.** S&S's episodic DNA means each story must be complete. An open-ended cliffhanger that requires the next volume to resolve the core conflict betrays the genre's self-contained structure. Readers expect to finish the book satisfied, not waiting for volume three.

- [ ] **Slow opening with backstory, worldbuilding, or "ordinary world" setup.** S&S readers know they signed up for fast, visceral action. An opening chapter that delivers family dynamics, peaceful village life, or extended world-history exposition signals that the author has not mastered the genre's pacing contract. Open in action or do not open at all.

- [ ] **A sorcerer or supernatural villain who is merely a political schemer.** If the antagonist's threat is primarily political — they want the throne, they are manipulating factions, they have agents in the court — the story has drifted toward political intrigue fantasy. The S&S sorcerer must be personally, supernaturally dangerous in a way that makes the reader's skin crawl.

- [ ] **Violence without physical cost to the protagonist.** If the protagonist fights continuously without accumulating damage, fatigue, or the psychological weight of killing, the combat register has become action-movie rather than S&S. The protagonist must bleed, tire, and carry the cost of their violence.

- [ ] **An ensemble cast with multiple POV characters across different factions.** Multiple POVs tracking armies, throne rooms, and political factions belongs to epic fantasy. S&S lives inside one skull. Adding extensive secondary POVs — especially of antagonists, rulers, or characters far from the protagonist — dilutes the genre's close, visceral intensity.

- [ ] **The protagonist finding a home, community, or cause they fully commit to.** S&S protagonists are rootless by nature. A story that ends with the protagonist settling down, joining an order, finding a family, or fully committing to a political cause has resolved the genre's central tension — and resolved it in the direction of domestic fantasy or epic fantasy. The protagonist should remain on the road.

- [ ] **Magic or technology that eliminates physical danger.** If the protagonist can fly, teleport, heal instantly, or call on forces that trivialize the physical danger of the world, the genre's dread evaporates. S&S tension is rooted in the protagonist's physical vulnerability. The moment they become supernaturally invulnerable, the reader stops worrying — and stops reading.
