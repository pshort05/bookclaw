These are the non-negotiable elements that define sword-and-sorcery — missing any one of them risks writing generic fantasy that will disappoint readers who picked up S&S deliberately.

- [ ] **A morally grey protagonist who fights for personal, not altruistic, motives.** Coin, survival, revenge, or personal loyalty — not justice, the greater good, or a prophecy. The protagonist may do heroic things incidentally, but their motivation must be self-interested. A protagonist who fights to save the world has crossed into epic fantasy territory.

- [ ] **Magic that is dark, costly, or threatening rather than academic or systematic.** Sorcery in S&S is not a skill tree. It involves forces that predate civilization, costs the wielder something real, and produces dread in onlookers. Even if the protagonist wields magic, it should feel dangerous rather than empowering in a clean sense.

- [ ] **A physically immediate and visceral violence register.** Combat must have weight: fatigue, injury, blood, tactical thinking under pressure. The protagonist wins through skill, cunning, and ferocity — not special powers or plot armor. Fights should feel like they could go wrong.

- [ ] **An episodic or self-contained story structure.** The story must be complete without trilogy context. Readers should be able to pick up this book cold and receive a full, satisfying narrative. The protagonist survives and moves on; the world is not saved or fundamentally changed.

- [ ] **A dangerous secondary world with a pre-industrial technology ceiling.** Swords, shields, horses, sailing ships. No gunpowder, no industrial machinery. Magic may supplement but does not replace the physical danger of the setting. Cities should feel corrupt and claustrophobic; wilderness should feel primordial and threatening.

- [ ] **A sorcerer or supernatural threat that is genuinely alien and dangerous.** The antagonist's power must be real, their nature unsettling, and their motivation not easily comprehensible in human terms. They are not a political villain in robes; they are something wrong.

- [ ] **Prose that is visceral, fast, and sensory.** The reader should feel the protagonist's physical reality: temperature, smell, exertion, pain. Sentences should accelerate during action. Exposition must earn its place — S&S has no patience for backstory that slows the engine.

- [ ] **The city as corrupt environment and the wilderness as primal threat.** The setting contrast between urban decadence and savage frontier is structural to the genre. The protagonist should navigate both and be shaped by neither.

- [ ] **A resolution that is costly, not triumphant.** The protagonist wins — or survives — at a price. Something is lost, spent, or left behind. The ending is satisfying without being clean. The road continues.

- [ ] **The protagonist's outsider status preserved throughout.** The protagonist does not become a king, join an order, or find a community they belong to. They remain on the margins of the civilized world by temperament and circumstance, not by tragic choice awaiting resolution. Rootlessness is their natural state, not a problem to be solved.
