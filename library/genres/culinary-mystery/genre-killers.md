# Culinary Mystery Genre Killers

What causes DNF (Did Not Finish) and one-star reviews.

## Failure Modes

- [ ] Food scenes that stop the plot dead: every cooking scene must reveal character, advance a clue, or do something. Descriptions that could be cut entirely signal food is decoration, not story.
- [ ] Recipes that don't work: readers will attempt them. An incorrect ratio or impossible technique generates one-star reviews. Test recipes or do not include them.
- [ ] A mystery solved by coincidence: if the protagonist solves the crime by overhearing a confession, the culinary premise never did investigative work. The solution must require culinary expertise.
- [ ] Graphic violence that breaks the cozy register: a body is discovered — that is the genre. Extended descriptions of wounds or gore violate the contract. Horror is kept offstage.
- [ ] Food descriptions that are clinical or generic: "the sauce was good" fails the sensory contract. Readers choose this genre to inhabit the kitchen.
- [ ] Stereotyped culinary characters: the temperamental French chef, the earth-mother baker — stock types substituting occupation for personality. Food-world figures need distinct relationships to their craft.
- [ ] The sleuth has implausible police access: an amateur reading autopsy reports destroys credibility. Access must come through the food world.
- [ ] Motive unconnected to the culinary setting: a murder motivated by a secret unrelated to food could happen anywhere. The food world must generate the motive, not provide only the address.
