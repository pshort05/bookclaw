# Thriller — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

- **A slow start.** No hook, a throat-clearing first chapter, scene-setting before the threat. If the opening doesn't pop, the reader never reaches chapter two.
- **No clock.** Stakes with no time pressure read as a wandering plot; tension collapses without a deadline, literal or figurative.
- **Long, dense chapters.** Multi-page scenes with no break point kill the "just one more chapter" engine and lose the distracted reader.
- **Chapters that end at rest.** Closing on a natural pause instead of a cliffhanger removes the propulsive force across the break.
- **Lyrical or literary prose that slows the pace.** "Artsy," ornate, or long-sentence writing obstructs the momentum the reader came for; the prose must serve the plot, never the reverse.
- **Exposition dumps.** Pages of narrated history, tradecraft, or backstory instead of letting characters reveal it through dialogue and worry.
- **A passive or incompetent protagonist.** A lead who is rescued by luck and coincidence rather than expertise and choice breaks the competence promise.
- **Stakes that don't escalate.** A flat threat, or acts that forget what came before, with danger that never grows past the opening.
- **A telegraphed or cheated twist.** A reveal the reader saw a mile off, or one that contradicts what they were shown (an unfair red herring that breaks the rules) — both feel like a swindle.
- **No resolution.** A conspiracy left unexplained, a threat that fizzles, an ending that withholds the order-from-chaos payoff the genre promised.
- **Implausibility the pace can't cover.** Plot holes so large the reader stops to ask "would that really happen?" — the velocity exists to outrun small gaps, not gaping ones.
- **Cardboard partners.** A sidekick (often the female co-lead) drawn in shallow terms and used only to serve the hero — a flagged, recurring weakness of the form.
