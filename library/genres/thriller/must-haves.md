# Thriller Must-Haves

Non-negotiable craft elements. If any of these are missing, the manuscript is not yet a thriller.

- [ ] **Ticking clock present.** There is a deadline with real consequences — a murder will be committed, a deal will close, an innocent person will be convicted — and the reader knows what happens if the protagonist fails.
- [ ] **Protagonist has something specific to lose.** Not just their life in the abstract, but something concrete and irreplaceable: a child, their reputation, their freedom, a person they love. The reader must feel the weight of the loss that is coming if they fail.
- [ ] **Antagonist is active, not reactive.** The villain is executing a plan. They are not waiting for the protagonist to catch up; they are moving forward and the protagonist is trying to intercept them. Scenes from the antagonist's side (or evidence of their actions) should reinforce this momentum.
- [ ] **Each chapter ends on a propulsive note.** A revelation, a new threat, an unanswered question, a door closing. The reader must feel compelled to continue rather than comfortable stopping.
- [ ] **The protagonist makes a meaningful choice under pressure.** At minimum once per act, the protagonist must choose between two bad options. Thrillers live on forced choices; plots where the protagonist simply reacts to events are not thrillers.
- [ ] **The threat escalates in each act.** Whatever was at stake at the end of Act One is smaller than what is at stake at the end of Act Two. The protagonist's situation worsens before it improves.
- [ ] **At least one major reversal reframes what came before.** This is the genre's signature move — a revelation that reorders the reader's understanding of prior events. It must be planted with clues that are visible in retrospect.
- [ ] **The protagonist solves the central problem through their own agency.** The resolution cannot come from coincidence, a deus ex machina, or information the protagonist happened to stumble across. They must earn the ending through skill, sacrifice, or a decision that costs them something.
- [ ] **The antagonist's logic is internally consistent.** The reader does not need to sympathize with the villain, but they must be able to understand the villain's reasoning. A villain who acts arbitrarily or whose plan collapses under scrutiny deflates suspense retroactively.
- [ ] **The stakes are personal before they are global.** Even in a world-ending-threat thriller, the reader's emotional investment travels through the protagonist's specific personal fear. Ground macro-stakes in micro-consequences.
