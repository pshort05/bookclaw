# Thriller — Must-Haves (non-negotiable checklist)

Skip these and it isn't really a commercial thriller. Treat as a revision checklist.

- [ ] A hook in chapter one: a startling event or the unmistakable implication of one coming. If the opening doesn't grab, nothing after it matters.
- [ ] A ticking clock — literal (deadline, bomb, countdown) or figurative (a truth that must be found before disaster) — established early and tightening.
- [ ] Extremely short chapters that maintain a rapid tempo and give a distracted reader easy exit/entry points.
- [ ] A cliffhanger or open question at the end of nearly every chapter — cut mid-action, not at the natural pause, to launch the reader across the break.
- [ ] Multi-threaded POV with at least one villain/antagonist thread, used for dramatic irony (the reader knows what the hero doesn't) or to seed a red herring.
- [ ] Utilitarian prose: short, clear sentences (aim for low words-per-sentence), low cognitive friction, 7th–8th-grade readability. Clarity and impact over lyricism.
- [ ] Exposition delivered through dialogue and character worry, not lecture — an expert explains the complex material to a partner who stands in for the reader.
- [ ] A competent protagonist out of their depth who survives by expertise and nerve, not luck or coincidence.
- [ ] Escalating stakes: each act raises the threat beyond the last; the danger gets bigger and more personal.
- [ ] At least one genuine twist or reframe (mole, betrayal, or revelation) that the careful reader could *just* have caught.
- [ ] A real, set-piece climax at the deadline where the threads converge and the protagonist out-thinks or out-lasts the antagonist.
- [ ] A resolution that explains the conspiracy and restores order — the puzzle is solved, the threat stopped, the truth made to make sense.
- [ ] A recognizable, high-status world (real cities, agencies, institutions) grounding the concealed layer the plot decodes.
