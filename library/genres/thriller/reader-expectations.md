# Thriller — Reader Expectations

A relentless, plot-first page-turner: a competent protagonist in over their head, escalating
stakes, a ticking clock, and a puzzle the reader gets to "solve" alongside them. The promise
is velocity and resolution — order pulled out of chaos by the final page.

## Tone & Mood
Urgent, propulsive, and lightly "intellectual." The reader should feel both *in danger* and
*in the know* — granted insider access to a concealed world (a secret society, the machinery
of government, a black-budget program) and made to feel they're learning something real while
being entertained. Anxiety is the baseline; competence and momentum are the relief.

## Pacing
Breakneck and non-negotiable. The engine is an interlocking system: extremely short chapters,
a cliffhanger at the end of nearly every one, and frequent POV cuts that let the author break
off mid-action and jump threads. Pace overrides everything — it is deliberately fast enough to
keep the reader in "what happens next?" rather than "would that really happen?". A scene that
slows the plot is a scene that gets cut or compressed.

## Setting Conventions
Real, recognizable, high-status world the reader half-believes: famous cities and monuments,
government agencies (FBI, CIA, Secret Service), courts, museums, corporate or military
power. Ground the exotic in the familiar. The concealed layer underneath — the conspiracy, the
cover-up, the secret — is what's being decoded. Author's-note "this part is real" framing is a
genre signature.

## Character Archetypes
A capable protagonist out of their depth: the scholar-adventurer (an expert dragged into the
field), the haunted professional (a damaged investigator with one extraordinary skill), or the
lone operator. Pair them with a smart, capable partner who facilitates survival and stands in
for the reader during exposition. A powerful, well-resourced antagonist with a plausible plan;
useful institutional allies and traitors.

## Length & Format
70k–100k words, third person, past tense, adult. Short chapters (often 3–6 pages); multi-POV is
standard. Frequently series fiction built around a franchise hero — close this book's case while
leaving the character open for the next. Standalone works too; the contract is the structure,
not the recurring cast.
