## Setting

Post-World War II developments fundamentally reshaped the genre. The 1950s through 1970s witnessed the emergence of sex comedies, driven by broader cultural shifts toward sexual liberation and the social acceptability of discussing previously taboo subjects. This period marked the gradual expansion of romantic comedy beyond purely genteel courtship narratives into more complex explorations of desire, intimacy, and relationship dynamics. By the late 20th century, romantic comedy had diversified significantly, incorporating paranormal elements, high-stakes adventure, workplace settings, and increasingly diverse character representations—laying groundwork for the multifaceted genre landscape of the 2020s.[5]

## Archetypes

The romantic comedy genre traces its lineage back to ancient theatrical traditions, with foundational structures emerging from Shakespeare's comedic works in the late 1590s, particularly A Midsummer Night's Dream, Twelfth Night, and As You Like It. These Shakespearean comedies established enduring narrative patterns: flawed yet lovable characters navigating misunderstandings, female-centric storytelling (with witty female protagonists driving much of the comedy), and ultimately satisfying resolutions celebrating romantic union. The genre's core DNA—incorporating lighthearted humor with romantic longing—was crystallized during this period and continues to define contemporary rom-coms.[2][3]
