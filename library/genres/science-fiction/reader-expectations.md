## Setting

Cyberpunk (1980s) emerged as a direct response to New Wave experimentalism, characterized by the phrase "lowlife and high tech". William Gibson's Neuromancer (1984) epitomized this movement, depicting gritty futures where technology creates new problems rather than solving old ones. The genre explored themes of corporate power, virtual reality, and hackers, proving remarkably prescient about many aspects of our current technological reality. Cyberpunk portrayed humanity becoming increasingly alienated from its natural environment and subsumed by technology.[8][9][10][6]

## Archetypes

The early 20th century brought science fiction into the pulp magazine era, where cheap publications democratized access to speculative stories characterized by their lurid and sensational subject matter. These pulp magazines, reaching their peak in the 1930s with approximately forty-four titles available monthly, provided a platform for emerging writers to experiment with new concepts. John W. Campbell's 1937 assumption of editorship at Astounding Science Fiction marked a pivotal moment, as he championed more literate and analytical approaches that elevated the genre beyond simple adventure tales.[4][5][3]
