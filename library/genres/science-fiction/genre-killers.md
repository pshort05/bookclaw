## Failure Modes

- https://strangerworlds.substack.com/p/the-failure-of-cyberpunk
- Do we repeat Earth's colonial mistakes among the stars?
- Risk of mechanical failure during sleep
- Cultural Impact: "Those who don't learn from history" warning
- Common Pitfalls: Lists the ten most frequent mistakes SF writers make and how to avoid them.
- Avoid: Second-person (except for experimental short fiction) and omniscient narration (which undermines SF's typical focus on limited knowledge and discovery).
- Avoid "As you know, Bob" dialogue where characters explain things they both already know
- Don't:
- Avoid overloading; deploy unfamiliar terms carefully to immerse rather than confuse
- Avoid excessive techno-jargon unless integral to character or plot
- "Show, don't tell" is not an absolute rule—it's a guideline favoring vivid scenes over dry exposition. Science fiction often requires telling efficiently rather than laboriously showing every detail.
- Avoiding tedious dramatization of simple facts
- Common Pitfalls to Avoid
- Show, Don't Info-Dump: Reveal the world through character interaction, dialogue, and action. Instead of explaining how a grav-lift works, have a character complain about its slow speed or marvel at a new, silent model.
- Lived-In Future: The world should feel used. Technology breaks, gets dirty, and is jury-rigged. Corporations plaster ads on everything. People leave clutter in their spaceships. Avoid sterile, perfect environments unless it’s a specific plot point (e.g., a clone factory).
- Clarity is King: The concepts may be complex, but the prose should be clear and accessible. Avoid overly technical or convoluted sentences.
