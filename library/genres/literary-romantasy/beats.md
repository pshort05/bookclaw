# Literary Romantasy — Beats

The dominant structure is the Dual Convergence Arc — two lines of development (the romantic arc and the world-arc) that begin separately, intertwine in the middle, and merge at the climax so that the romantic resolution and the world-crisis resolution are the same event. This distinguishes literary romantasy from standard romantasy, where the HEA is delivered and then the world problem is resolved; here they cannot be separated, because the love itself has political and metaphysical weight. A three-act structure with a strong midpoint pivot is the backbone. Percentage placements are approximate; literary pacing allows breathing room at every phase.

## Phase 1 — World and Self (0–25%)

- **Establish the protagonist's interiority (1–5%)** — render the protagonist's perception of the world in full, specific prose before the plot begins to press; the reader must inhabit their consciousness. Establish the central wound or question they carry without naming it as such.
- **Introduce the world's pressure (5–12%)** — the political, magical, or cultural system that structures the protagonist's life and constrains their choices; show it from the inside, not as exposition but as lived texture.
- **The first encounter (12–18%)** — the romantic counterpart appears; the scene is charged but not conventionally romantic. The protagonist registers something — an intellectual response, an irritation that is too intense, a quality of attention they cannot immediately classify. No declaration of attraction, no internal admission of beauty.
- **The world-problem crystallizes (18–25%)** — the external crisis (political, magical, historical) that will drive the plot becomes unavoidably clear; the protagonist is implicated in it whether they choose to be or not.

## Phase 2 — Proximity and Resistance (25–50%)

- **Forced but plausible proximity (25–32%)** — the world-problem requires the protagonist and the counterpart to occupy the same space, work toward the same (or opposed) end; neither can exit without abandoning the external goal.
- **Accumulation of fragments (32–42%)** — not a single revelation but a sequence of small ones; the protagonist learns something true about the counterpart — a particular silence, an unexpected competence, a cost they are paying — that does not fit the initial reading. The reader accumulates these alongside the protagonist.
- **The unguarded moment (42–48%)** — a scene of involuntary intimacy: exhaustion, danger, or the pressure of the world-problem strips one or both characters' performance; something true passes between them. Nothing is named. The reader feels it before the characters do.
- **Midpoint — the threshold crossed (50%)** — a moment of genuine mutual recognition: they see each other clearly, however briefly. This is not a kiss or a confession but a shift in epistemic register — the protagonist understands that this person is real to them in a way that will not be undone. The world-arc delivers its equivalent midpoint: a discovery, betrayal, or revelation that changes the protagonist's understanding of the crisis.

## Phase 3 — Complication and Cost (50–75%)

- **The doubled stakes (50–58%)** — now both arcs press simultaneously; advancing the romance risks the world-goal, and advancing the world-goal risks the relationship. The characters begin to make small choices that reveal whose side they are on, including from themselves.
- **Intimacy deepens and becomes dangerous (58–65%)** — the romantic arc escalates: a scene of physical or confessional closeness that the characters cannot walk back. Simultaneously the world-arc reveals a complication that makes the romantic alliance newly costly — politically, magically, or personally.
- **The revelation that changes the relationship (65–72%)** — a truth about one character, the world, or the crisis that recontextualizes what has been built; not necessarily a betrayal, but something that forces a choice. The protagonist must decide whether what they now know changes who they are choosing to be.
- **Retreat and silence (72–75%)** — one or both characters pulls back; not a melodramatic break but a withdrawal into the self as the full cost of both arcs becomes clear. The quiet before the climax.

## Phase 4 — Convergence and Reckoning (75–100%)

- **The dark confrontation (75–82%)** — the world-crisis reaches its apex and forces the protagonist into an impossible position; the romantic relationship is the most exposed nerve. This is the scene where the stakes — personal, political, metaphysical — are all simultaneously live.
- **The irreversible choice (82–88%)** — the protagonist acts in a way that cannot be undone, committing to both the romantic relationship and a position in the world-crisis at once. This choice must cost them something real, and the romantic counterpart must witness or be implicated in it.
- **Resolution without false comfort (88–95%)** — the world-arc resolves, but not cleanly: the consequences of the crisis remain, the world is changed rather than restored, the characters bear marks. The romantic resolution is earned and real — a HEA or HFN that the reader believes — but it exists inside a world that is more complicated than when the book began.
- **The last image (95–100%)** — a final scene or paragraph that closes the protagonist's interiority loop from the opening; the reader understands how they have changed. The image should be quiet, specific, and lingering — not a triumphant tableau but a true thing.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)

- **The perception scene** — an early passage of sufficient prose quality that the reader understands this is not standard genre fiction; a description of landscape, city, ritual, or magical phenomenon rendered in language that earns the "literary" designation.
- **The scene of genuine intellectual engagement** — the protagonist and counterpart discuss, argue, or collaborate over something with real stakes (a political theory, a magical problem, a piece of history); readers must believe in their minds before they believe in the romance.
- **The unguarded moment** — the scene where performance drops and something true is exchanged (see Phase 2 above); without it, the romance feels imposed rather than grown.
- **The scene that costs the protagonist something** — not a sacrifice in service of the plot but a moment where the protagonist's adherence to a belief, relationship, or world-picture is tested and they pay a real price; this is the interiority beat that makes the HEA meaningful.
- **The romantic culmination** — the scene that delivers on the long accumulated tension; its register must match the rest of the book; it cannot feel like a different author stepped in.
- **The world rendered in full** — at least one extended passage in which the world's complexity, strangeness, or beauty is rendered without plot obligation; this is the literary romantasy equivalent of the workplace competence scene — proof that the author can do more than move pieces.
