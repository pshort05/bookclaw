# Literary Romantasy — Themes

A menu of thematic territory native to the genre; select 2–3 that your story genuinely explores. Each theme should be present in the world-building, the romantic arc, and the character psychology simultaneously — not as a moral to be stated but as a pressure the narrative applies throughout.

- **Empire and the self** — what is the cost of belonging to, serving, or benefiting from a system of power that depends on erasure, extraction, or violence? The protagonist and the romance exist inside an imperial or hierarchical structure, and the story refuses to let the characters — or the reader — look away from what that structure requires of them. Love is neither a cure for complicity nor impossible inside it, but it demands a reckoning.

- **The politics of identity and naming** — who gets to define a person: their bloodline, their magic, their cultural origin, the empire that conquered them, or themselves? The romantic counterpart often embodies a different answer to this question, and the intimacy between them is partly a negotiation over the terms of selfhood. Language, naming, and the power to narrate one's own story are live forces in the plot.

- **Memory, loss, and the persistence of the dead** — grief as a structural presence rather than a backstory beat; the characters are shaped by what they have lost, and the romance exists in dialogue with that absence. The dead may be literally present (via magic, haunting, or inscription) or their weight may be psychological, but in either case the living must work out what kind of life is possible in their shadow.

- **The body as site of power** — in a world where magic, bloodline, or physical form confers status, the body is political before it is personal; romantic intimacy is therefore always also a negotiation with the world's taxonomy of bodies. The literary execution makes this explicit rather than incidental, and the eroticism — where present — is inseparable from its social context.

- **Loyalty and betrayal** — not as plot twist but as moral problem: to whom does a person owe their truest allegiance, and what do they owe when those allegiances conflict? The romantic relationship forces a test of loyalty — to community, to country, to a cause, to the beloved — and the story does not offer a clean answer.

- **Myth as trap and resource** — the characters exist inside stories the world has already told about people like them (the witch, the monster, the chosen heir, the barbarian scholar); they must navigate those stories, which means sometimes weaponizing them, sometimes dismantling them, and always understanding that other people will read them through the myth regardless of who they actually are. The romance is partly the experience of being truly seen outside the myth.

- **Beauty and devastation** — the co-presence of the transcendent and the terrible; the world is genuinely strange and beautiful, and also genuinely violent and unjust, and the story does not resolve this into a lesson. The romantic experience partakes of both. This is the thematic keynote of the genre at its most distinctive: wonder without innocence.
