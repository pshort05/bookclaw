# Literary Romantasy — Tropes

A menu of genre-defining tropes; select 3–6 that serve your specific story. Each trope is listed with its literary execution — the quality distinction that separates this genre from standard romantasy deployment of the same device.

- **Forbidden by law or empire** — the romance is structurally prohibited by political authority, cultural taboo, or the rules of a magical order; the prohibition must have coherent ideological justification, not arbitrary villainy, and the characters must genuinely understand the cost of defiance. The tension is not "will they get caught" but "what are they willing to sacrifice, and for what."

- **Enemies with ideological conviction** — both characters hold opposing worldviews that are internally coherent and rooted in their histories; neither is simply wrong. The romance does not resolve the ideological conflict by converting one party — it complicates each character's relationship to their own certainties. Readers should not be able to name the villain.

- **The slow uncovering** — attraction precedes understanding; the protagonist accumulates fragments of the counterpart's interiority across many chapters before a scene of genuine mutual recognition. The romantic arc is an epistemological one: coming to know someone rather than falling in love at a prescribed beat.

- **Mythic resonance / the shadow of a story** — the characters exist under the pressure of a myth, prophecy, or narrative the world has already written for them; their love is partly a struggle to exist outside the assigned roles of hero and sacrifice, monster and victim. The literary execution interrogates the myth rather than indulging it.

- **Political marriage or alliance** — the relationship is initiated by necessity or state arrangement; romantic feeling develops against the characters' strategies and better judgments. The literary version refuses easy sentiment: the reader must hold both the political reality and the emotional truth simultaneously.

- **Magic as psychology** — the magical system externalizes interiority; the protagonist's power grows, mutates, or fails in direct correspondence with their emotional state, repressed truths, or relational wounds. The magic is not a plot device but a language the story speaks.

- **The world before the fall** — a romance set in the full knowledge that the world as it stands is ending, already ending, or will be destroyed by the characters' choices; the intimacy is inseparable from loss. The tone is elegiac.

- **Rival minds, reluctant recognition** — intellectual sparring as foreplay; both characters are exceptional at the same or adjacent skill, and mutual recognition of the other's quality is the first crack in the wall. The literary deployment resists making the intellectual content merely decorative — the reader should come away having learned something about the world, magic, or politics.

- **The prisoner and the keeper** — captivity, sanctuary, or enforced proximity where the power dynamic is acknowledged and examined rather than romanticized; the captive character retains interiority, agency, and ethical complexity, and the keeper is implicated in systems they may not fully endorse.

- **The returned exile** — the protagonist re-enters a world that formed them and was lost to them; the romance is entangled with the labor of recovering or reimagining an identity the exile had abandoned. The homecoming is never uncomplicated.

- **The mask and the face** — a character performs a social or political role (court creature, loyal subject, weapon of state) while their interior self diverges further from the performance; the romantic relationship is the only space where the mask slips, and the stakes of that vulnerability are proportionally high.

- **Love as political act** — in a world ordered by hierarchy, bloodline, or magical taxonomy, choosing to love outside sanctioned categories is itself a form of rebellion; the romance has consequences for people beyond the two protagonists.
