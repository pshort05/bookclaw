# Literary Romantasy — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The broken promises, prose failures, and tonal betrayals that trigger DNF reactions and mark a manuscript as misclassified — or, worse, as a standard romantasy that has labeled itself literary to chase a market.

- **Prose that functions as filler** — sentences that exist only to move a character from room to room, summarize the last scene, or announce an emotion without rendering it; generic phrasing, visible scaffolding, or descriptions that could have been generated by averaging the genre. The literary reader came for the prose; absent it, nothing else compensates.
- **A magical system without consequences** — magic that appears when the plot requires it, is limited when the plot requires that too, and whose rules are invented scene by scene; a magical system with no cost, no internal logic, or no relationship to the themes is a sign the world has not been fully imagined.
- **Complexity signaled but not delivered** — a narrative that introduces political intrigue, unreliable narration, or moral ambiguity in its setup, then resolves everything with a clear villain, a correct protagonist, and a world restored to its prior order; readers who signed up for nuance and received tidiness feel cheated.
- **The romantic arc as veneer** — a literary fantasy that relegates the romance to subplot or decoration; the romantic relationship must be structurally load-bearing, not a secondary reward for finishing the real story. Equally damaging: a romance so dominant that the world-building and thematic content are reduced to backdrop.
- **Psychology that is asserted rather than shown** — telling the reader that a character is complex, wounded, or guarded without ever rendering the interior experience that would make this true; characters described as morally grey who behave with uniform consistency; backstory deployed as explanation rather than lived presence.
- **Dual POV head-hopping as a commercial signal** — alternating-chapter dual POV in the commercial romantasy convention, which tells the prestige reader they are in a different genre; the literary romantasy reader expects a primary or single deep perspective that does not flip at chapter breaks to reveal the love interest's feelings on schedule.
- **A world built from genre defaults** — a pseudo-medieval European empire with no cultural specificity; faeries borrowed wholesale from prior series without reimagining; a court that is all political theater and no texture of actual life; settings assembled from recognizable romantasy parts rather than imagined from the ground up.
- **The tidy resolution** — an ending that resolves the thematic question the book has been asking, confirms the characters' goodness, and restores equilibrium; literary romantasy is allowed a happy ending but not a simple one; the world at the end should be different from the world at the beginning in ways that cost something and remain.
- **Romantic conflict manufactured by withholding information** — a sustained act three crisis that exists only because one character does not tell the other something they easily could, with no credible reason for the silence; manufactured misunderstanding is a commercial romantasy device and will be recognized and resented.
- **Intimacy in a different register** — sexual or romantic scenes written in a noticeably different prose style from the rest of the book: more explicit genre-convention language, less imagery, an accelerated pace that abandons the literary quality; signals that the intimate content was inserted rather than written as part of the whole.
