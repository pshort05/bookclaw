A romance with a fully developed thriller or suspense plot running in parallel, where both the happily-ever-after and the resolution of a genuine threat must be delivered by the final page.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is tense and propulsive, with emotional warmth threaded through danger. Readers want the adrenaline of a thriller and the emotional investment of a love story; neither can be ornamental. The suspense plot must generate real fear — credible stakes, not merely inconvenient ones. The romantic arc must generate real feeling — vulnerability and trust earned under pressure, not instalove. Dark moods are expected; the genre can go to violent places as long as the romantic resolution provides catharsis. Grim endings on either track violate reader expectations.

## Pacing

Pacing is tight and relentless. The thriller and romance plots must intercut rather than taking turns. The external threat escalates in parallel with the romantic intimacy: as the characters grow closer, the danger grows worse, until both reach crisis simultaneously at the climax. The black moment and the peak of the external threat should coincide or be in very close proximity. Backstory is woven in through action, not delivered in exposition.

## Setting Conventions

Contemporary settings dominate — law enforcement, intelligence, corporate security, witness protection, small-town threat scenarios. The setting must plausibly generate both the threat and the forced proximity that creates romantic opportunity. Urban environments allow anonymity and surveillance; isolated settings amplify vulnerability. Settings that grant both leads professional competence — police procedural, military, security, legal, or investigative worlds — are preferred because they give each character active agency in resolving the threat. Historical romantic suspense applies the same structural rules to period-appropriate threats.

## Character Archetypes

The leads are competent people under pressure: law enforcement officers, investigators, attorneys, security professionals, military veterans, or civilians with expertise thrust into danger. At least one lead is typically trained for crisis; the romantic tension often comes from the clash between professional detachment and personal attachment. The heroine is not a passive victim — she is an active participant in resolving the threat, even when she is also the target of it. The antagonist must be a genuine threat with real capability and comprehensible motive; an incompetent or cartoonish villain deflates the suspense and by extension the romance.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 80,000–100,000 words. Single-POV (heroine) is common in the romantic suspense tradition, but dual-POV has grown significantly and many subgenre readers now expect it. Series with recurring couples (rare in pure romance) are accepted here — a detective or security team setting allows multiple books with the same protagonists facing new threats. Standalone-with-series-world is also common. Heat level is typically sensual; explicit is present but not dominant. Short chapters with hooks are strongly preferred. Prologues from the antagonist's POV are a recognized convention for establishing the threat early.
