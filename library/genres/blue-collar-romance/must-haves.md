# Blue-Collar Romance — Must-Haves (non-negotiable checklist)

Skip these and the manuscript fails the fundamental requirements of the genre. Treat as a revision pass checklist before submitting or publishing.

- [ ] **Portray the work with accuracy and dignity** — the hero's (and heroine's, if applicable) occupation must be rendered with specific, researched detail. Readers in and adjacent to these trades will abandon a book over a single procedural error; more importantly, the authenticity of the work is the genre's core contract with the reader. If you are writing a welder, know the difference between MIG and TIG. If you are writing a rancher, know the calving calendar.

- [ ] **Establish physical and professional competence early** — within the first three chapters, both leads must be shown excelling at something specific. The reader must respect each character's capabilities before romance complicates their judgment. Competence is the primary form of reader investment in this genre.

- [ ] **Root the external conflict in the occupational world** — a concrete, stakes-bearing conflict that arises from the work itself: a drought threatening the harvest, a safety violation threatening the contractor's license, a firehouse facing closure, a competing developer making an offer on the family land. The romance must complicate this conflict rather than run parallel to it.

- [ ] **Deliver sensory specificity in every setting scene** — the smell of the shop, the weight of the tools, the quality of the light at 5 a.m., the sound the equipment makes when something is wrong. Generic description of physical space kills the authenticity the reader came for.

- [ ] **Guarantee the HEA without a class escape** — the happy ending must not require the hero to be revealed as secretly wealthy, the heroine to abandon her life for his, or either character to leave their working-class identity behind. The resolution affirms that this life — this specific, labor-intensive, rooted life — is a worthy container for love and ambition.

- [ ] **Force the adhesion organically** — the event or condition that locks the characters into sustained proximity must be native to the setting. A blizzard on a ranch is plausible; a contrived business trip to a resort is not. If it would not happen to real people in this profession and geography, revise.

- [ ] **Let the hero's care be expressed through action** — a blue-collar MMC who delivers his feelings in articulate, emotionally fluent speeches from chapter one is not grounded; he is a fantasy of emotional literacy in period costume. His care must be conveyed through what he does: the thing he fixed, the shift he covered, the errand he ran without being asked. The verbal declaration arrives late and means more because of the silence that preceded it.

- [ ] **Earn the vulnerability** — when the self-sufficient character breaks down or admits need, it must feel inevitable rather than convenient. The reader should be able to trace the accumulation of pressure that produced this moment. An unearned vulnerability scene reads as manipulation; an earned one is the emotional climax of the book.

- [ ] **Maintain equal stakes for both characters** — the black moment and its resolution must cost both characters something real. A black moment that only the FMC experiences (she is devastated; he is confused) fails the dual-interiority promise of modern romance. Both characters must have something genuine to lose and a genuine reason to be afraid.

- [ ] **Build the ensemble with series longevity in mind** — introduce at least two or three supporting characters with enough dimension, conflict, and personal history to anchor future standalones. Their roles in this book must be complete and satisfying; their future potential should be implicit, never forced.

- [ ] **Treat the small town as a social system, not a postcard** — community means pressure, history, and accountability. Include the gossip, the grudges, the people who have known the hero since he was a boy making bad decisions. The town's memory is a source of friction and warmth simultaneously; use both.

- [ ] **Mitigate class-gap dynamics with demonstrated respect** — if a class differential exists between the leads (city professional and rural working-class), the professional lead must earn her place in the working-class world through demonstrated capability and genuine curiosity, not through sentiment or a condescending learning arc. The working-class lead must never be positioned as an object of improvement.
