# Blue-Collar Romance — Comparable Titles

Touchstones for calibrating tone, pacing, trope execution, and occupational authenticity — the commercial and critical vanguard of the genre across the 2017–2025 market.

- **Done and Dusted by Lyla Sage (Rebel Blue Ranch, 2023)** — the most recent breakout in the rancher subgenre; a returning-FMC × grumpy-rancher structure anchored in authentic Wyoming ranch life. The setting's seasonal specificity and the hero's wordless care expressed through practical action set the standard for modern blue-collar characterization. Optioned for television (Universal); represents the current commercial ceiling for the subgenre.

- **Things We Never Got Over by Lucy Score (Knockemout, 2022)** — the grumpy barber × sunshine FMC pairing in a rough-edged small town; Score's setting work (Knockemout is a specific, inhabited place with a culture and history) is the reference standard for small-town world-building. A #1 New York Times bestseller demonstrating the mass-market viability of blue-collar romance when setting is treated as a full character.

- **Beard in Mind by Penny Reid (Winston Brothers, 2017)** — mechanic hero in a small Tennessee mountain town; Reid's auto-shop setting is researched and specific, and the grumpy × sunshine pairing (Beau Winston, the unexpectedly warm brother, opposite a prickly female mechanic) illustrates how the archetype functions when the heroine is also the blue-collar professional. The Winston Brothers series is the foundational interconnected-standalone template for this genre.

- **Fix Her Up by Tessa Bailey (Hot & Hammered, 2019)** — a construction-worker hero pivoting from a failed baseball career; Bailey's signature explicit heat combined with an authentic trade background and a grumpy × sunshine dynamic. Demonstrates that explicit heat is fully compatible with blue-collar authenticity and that the hero's competence can be transferred (baseball to construction) without losing its core identity function.

- **Worked Up by Tessa Bailey (Made in Jersey, 2016)** — factory mechanic Duke Crawford is the template for the working-class hero defined entirely by physical and professional competence rather than any residual social aspiration. Small-town setting, protective instinct expressed through action, a heroine who matches his intensity in a different domain. Bailey is described by Entertainment Weekly as the "Michelangelo of dirty talk"; this entry is calibration for Level 4 heat in a blue-collar setting.

- **Wait for It by Mariana Zapata (2016)** — the defining text for the blue-collar slow burn; Diana is a hairstylist managing a household disrupted by grief, and the hero is a police officer (a public-sector blue-collar profession). Zapata's pacing — deliberately weeks-long accumulation, minimal explicit acknowledgment of feelings, maximum tension in small gestures — is the blueprint for the Phase 2 accumulation described in beats.md. Zapata is the reference for "how slow can the slow burn go while retaining forward momentum."

- **Flawless by Elsie Silver (Chestnut Springs, 2022)** — bull rider × PR consultant; the working-class rodeo world rendered with sport-specific authenticity and a grumpy/sunshine inversion (the hero is charming; the heroine is buttoned up). Silver's Chestnut Springs and Gold Rush Ranch series are the current commercial standard for the Canadian-western small-town romance, demonstrating that the blue-collar setting can be athletic and peripatetic, not only rooted and agricultural.

- **Protecting What's Mine by Lucy Score (2019)** — firefighter romance with a female physician heroine; Score's firehouse is specific, inhabited, and funny, with the crew functioning as genuine found family rather than backdrop. The hero's professional competence (he is good at the dangerous thing he does) is depicted with enough authenticity that the physical risk carries real emotional weight. Calibration for firehouse-setting blue-collar romance with a cross-class pairing (working-class MMC, professional-class FMC) handled with respect for both sides.
