Medieval Mystery readers want a crime puzzle embedded in a rigorously realized historical world, where the investigator's methods and the society's constraints are both products of the period.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is contemplative, atmospheric, and intellectually engaged — readers come for a puzzle and stay for the period. The medieval setting is not mere backdrop; the social hierarchy, religious authority, and epistemological assumptions of the era should shape how crimes are committed, concealed, and investigated. A sense of historical texture — the smell of a tallow candle, the sound of Vespers, the weight of ecclesiastical power over daily life — should be woven through the investigation rather than applied as surface decoration. Dark subject matter (murder, plague, institutional corruption) is handled with period-appropriate directness.

## Pacing

Pacing is methodical rather than thriller-fast, reflecting the investigative tools available: observation, questioning, examination of physical evidence without forensic science, and the application of logic and theological reasoning. The investigator must work within the constraints of medieval society — deference to clergy and nobility, restricted access to female spaces, the risk of accusation rebounding on the investigator — and the pacing reflects those constraints. Readers expect a satisfying accumulation of clues and suspects across the middle of the novel, with a resolution that is fair-play: all necessary information was available to the reader before the solution was revealed.

## Setting Conventions

The most successful settings provide institutional structure: a monastery, a cathedral chapter, a guild, a noble household, a pilgrimage route. These enclosed communities create a defined suspect pool, enforce social hierarchies that matter to the plot, and allow the author to develop period texture in depth. The investigator needs a legitimate reason to ask questions — a sanctioned role (monk, physician, magistrate, cunning woman, cathedral canon) that grants access without anachronistic freedom of movement. Legal systems should reflect the period: church courts versus secular courts, trial by ordeal or compurgation as living possibilities rather than historical curiosities, and the role of confession in a world that takes it seriously.

## Character Archetypes

The investigator is typically someone on the institutional margin — a monk whose scholarly habits make him an observer, a woman healer whose outsider status grants access to different social layers, a physician who reads bodies with empirical detachment unusual for the era. They apply a form of reason that is recognizable to modern readers but expressed through period categories (theology, humoral medicine, natural philosophy). Antagonists exploit the period's power structures: corrupt clergy, nobles who expect immunity, guild masters protecting trade secrets. Victims are often marginal figures whose deaths should not matter to the powerful but do to the investigator.

## Length & Format

Novels run 80,000–110,000 words. Series centered on a recurring investigator in a fixed institutional setting are the dominant format (the Ellis Peters model), with readers building detailed knowledge of the world across volumes. Author's notes on historical sources are expected and valued; readers in this genre are frequently history readers as well and will notice anachronisms. Maps of the monastery, town, or estate are common paratextual elements. Standalone novels exist but series commitment is the norm.
