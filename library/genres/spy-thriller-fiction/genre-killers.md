- Avoid ornate descriptions, flowery language, or purple prose that contradicts genre expectations. Each descriptive passage should reveal character perspective or establish atmosphere relevant to the story.[2][5][6]
- Subtext proves essential: what characters don't say matters as much as what they do. Characters with secrets speak guardedly; characters aware of surveillance choose words carefully. Important information is often conveyed obliquely—through euphemisms and references that carry specific meaning within intelligence communities.[2][5][7]
- Avoid exposition disguised as dialogue; resist technical jargon overload; eliminate monologues that feel unnatural. Dialogue should feel like realistic exchange rather than information delivery.[3][2]
- Spy-Thriller Style Guide: What to DO vs. What to AVOID
- Use tradecraft terminology naturally throughout; characters should speak intelligence language as native speakers. Once establishing terminology, maintain consistency. Avoid jargon overload; technical accuracy shouldn't obscure narrative clarity.[7][8][11]
- Avoid resolving tensions prematurely. Build suspense through consistent page-turning momentum, strategic chapter endings, escalating complications that deepen rather than merely delay plot, and payoff resolutions that feel earned.[12][4]
- Avoid melodrama, soap opera emotion, hand-wringing over necessity, and over-the-top stakes. Maintain clarity and directness throughout.[6][2]

- The Imminent Threat: End the chapter just as the danger becomes physical and unavoidable.
- 4.0 Style Elements to Avoid (The "Burn Notice" List)
- Adherence to the subgenre's tone requires discipline. Avoid the following:
- Invincible Heroes: The protagonist must be vulnerable, make mistakes, and suffer consequences. They can be highly competent, but not superhuman. They get hurt, they get tired, and they can be psychologically broken.
- Black-and-White Morality: Villains should have comprehensible, even relatable, motivations. They are the heroes of their own story. Avoid mustache-twirling evil for its own sake.
- Exposition Dumps: Weave backstory and world-building into dialogue, action, and internal thought. Show, don't tell. Instead of saying an agency is ruthless, show them sacrificing an asset for a minor gain.
- At the heart of each story is a complex, multi-dimensional protagonist – a core, relatable hero who is highly skilled yet deeply human, possessing a rich interior life, nuanced motivations, and relatable flaws. Avoid simplistic hero-villain dichotomies; instead, characters should be morally ambiguous, operating in gray areas. Antagonists must be memorable and psychologically complex, often with disturbing motivations. The protagonist's personal life and relationships, particularly family, are crucial, providing emotional grounding and serving as both motivation and vulnerability. Character interactions and decisions should consistently drive the plot forward.
