## Setting

The period between the world wars marked a crucial transformation in spy fiction, moving away from romantic adventure toward psychological realism. W. Somerset Maugham's Ashenden; or, The British Agent (1928) represented a watershed moment, introducing what literary critics recognize as the first realistic espionage narrative. This work depicted ordinary people caught in extraordinary circumstances, abandoning the superhuman capabilities of earlier spy heroes in favor of believable human vulnerability.[2][4]

## Archetypes

This early period was characterized by what scholars term "imperial adventure tales," which merged traditional adventure fiction with emerging espionage themes. These works often reflected the xenophobic anxieties of their era, portraying foreign threats to British imperial interests. Buchan's The Thirty-Nine Steps and Greenmantle exemplified this approach, featuring the archetypal gentleman spy Richard Hannay navigating international conspiracies.[3][4][1]
