Myth retold with punk sensibility — subverted, reclaimed, and reframed from the perspectives of those the original stories silenced, with contemporary political awareness braided into ancient narrative structure.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is sharp, often angry, and reclamatory. Mythpunk does not merely retell old stories; it interrogates who told them, whose suffering was aestheticized, whose agency was stripped to serve a hero's arc. The anger is not abstract — it attaches to specific characters, specific myths, specific injustices embedded in the source material. At the same time, mythpunk holds grief and beauty without contradiction: these are still mythological worlds, still capable of sublimity and terror, but the camera has shifted to the corner that was always cropped out. Voice is everything; a flat or neutral register defeats the genre's central purpose.

## Pacing

Pacing is often fragmentary and nonlinear, reflecting a punk distrust of the clean hero's journey. A mythpunk narrative might deconstruct a myth's structure even as it uses it — circling back to moments the original skipped, inserting the silenced character's experience into gaps in the canonical story. This can produce an elliptical, layered effect that rewards rereading. When the narrative does move linearly, the pacing should feel driven by the marginalized protagonist's urgency rather than by the structure of the myth being subverted. Set pieces of violence, transformation, or divine encounter carry the genre's emotional peaks.

## Setting Conventions

Settings draw from mythologically grounded worlds but are often fractured or doubled: the mythological space exists alongside or within a contemporary or secondary world, with mythological intrusion felt as pressure or rupture rather than simple coexistence. Liminal spaces — thresholds, crossroads, underworlds, spaces between — are privileged locations where the punk element and the mythic element make contact. The aesthetic tends toward the vivid and tactile: sensory specificity as a form of claiming reality for characters who were rendered symbolic or absent.

## Character Archetypes

Protagonists are almost always drawn from the mythological margins: the monster, the betrayed wife, the enslaved craftsman, the silenced prophetess, the divine servant, the foreign woman the hero discards. Their interiority — their anger, desire, grief, and agency — is the novel's subject. The heroes of the original myths appear at a remove, often diminished or reframed without the narrative's endorsement. Gods are structural forces as much as characters: their power is real and dangerous, but their moral authority is interrogated. Mentors and communities of the marginalized replace the traditional heroic support structure.

## Length & Format

Typical length is 70,000–90,000 words, though lyric short fiction and novellas are also common in this genre. Formal innovation is expected and welcomed: fragmented structure, second-person address, mythological intertext woven into the narrative, poetic prose sections. Epigraphs from the source myth or from contemporary feminist/postcolonial theory are conventional. Standalone novels are the norm; the genre's spirit resists the extended series format, though companion volumes revisiting the same mythological world from another silenced perspective do appear.
