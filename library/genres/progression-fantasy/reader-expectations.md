Progression fantasy follows a protagonist's systematic rise through a defined power hierarchy via training, cultivation, or martial discipline — with no requirement for game-system UI elements in the prose.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is driven and purposeful: the protagonist has a goal, the world has a ceiling, and the narrative tension comes from watching someone methodically dismantle both. Introspection is common — readers are inside the protagonist's head as they parse new techniques, absorb setbacks, and recalibrate their path. The emotional register leans toward determination and earned satisfaction rather than discovery or wonder. Unlike LitRPG, there are no stat boxes, system notifications, or numerical readouts in the prose; power growth is conveyed through narrative description, comparison with past capability, and the reactions of other characters. This is the key distinction: progression fantasy can feel like cultivation xianxia, martial arts fantasy, or a magic-training story — but never like a video game with a visible UI.

## Pacing

Structure follows the power ladder: each arc ends with the protagonist reaching a new level or defeating a previously impossible opponent. Early chapters establish the ceiling (what the top of the system looks like) and the protagonist's starting disadvantage. Mid-story accelerates through training montages, trials, and escalating antagonists. Breakthroughs must feel earned — readers track the work, so shortcuts undermine satisfaction. Series pacing matters: book one should advance the protagonist at least two or three tiers and close a major arc even as the larger ascension continues.

## Setting Conventions

The world exists primarily to structure the progression system: cultivation realms, ranked dungeon floors, ranked martial sects, ascension towers, or class-based magical academies all serve this function. Geography and politics are secondary unless they create obstacles or resources relevant to advancement. The power system itself must be internally consistent and introduced early — readers will test it against every encounter. Mentors, rivals, and enemies should be legible within the power hierarchy so readers can calibrate tension.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is typically an underdog or late bloomer with a hidden talent or unusual cultivation path that eventually outpaces orthodoxy. They are disciplined and goal-oriented, sometimes to the point of social narrowness. Key supporting archetypes: the veteran mentor who opens the path then steps aside, the rival who advances in parallel (sometimes becoming an ally), the arrogant mid-tier antagonist who underestimates the protagonist, and the distant peak-level figure whose strength sets the ultimate goal. Character growth tracks power growth — the protagonist's worldview expands as their capability does.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 90,000–150,000 words per volume. Long series (five to fifteen books) are the expectation, not the exception. First-person or close third-person point of view keeps readers inside the protagonist's perception of their own growth. Training sequences, breakthrough scenes, and sparring matches should be detailed and tactile — these are the genre's set pieces, equivalent to action sequences in thriller or explicit scenes in erotic romance.
