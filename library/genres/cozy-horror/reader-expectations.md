Readers expect a horror story in which the threat is genuinely unsettling but the atmosphere is warm, the community is resilient, and the experience of reading feels safe enough to enjoy — horror as comfort rather than ordeal.

## Tone & Mood

The tone holds two things simultaneously: something is wrong here, and we will be all right. The horror is real — readers are not being patronised with fake scares — but the framing ensures they are never overwhelmed or alienated by it. Dread is present but low-level and atmospheric; body horror, extreme violence, and psychological cruelty are absent or heavily subdued. The closest analogy is a ghost story told around a fire: the darkness is acknowledged, but the storyteller and the audience are warm and together. What distinguishes cozy horror from regular horror is that the community does not shatter under pressure — it tightens.

## Pacing

Pacing is measured and atmospheric rather than relentless. The narrative takes time to establish the community, its routines, and the protagonist's relationships before the horror intrudes — this grounding is essential, because the horror's impact depends on readers caring about what might be lost. When the threat escalates, it does so gradually. There are no sudden jump-scare structural equivalents in prose; unease builds slowly. Resolution comes through collective action rather than lone heroism, and survivors are the rule rather than the exception.

## Setting Conventions

Settings favor the cocooned and familiar made uncanny: a small town where something has always been a little off, a cozy bed-and-breakfast with an unusual history, an isolated village that manages its resident strangeness with long-practiced ritual. The physical environment provides comfort — warm kitchens, familiar streets, candlelight — against which the wrongness registers more sharply. The horror element is woven into the setting's texture rather than imported from outside it; the strangeness belongs here. Unlike standard horror, the setting is not primarily a threat environment but a home that must be understood and protected.

## Character Archetypes

The Grounded Protagonist: someone with strong community ties and practical capabilities who faces the strange without losing themselves. The Eccentric Local Expert: a neighbor, a librarian, an elderly resident who has lived with this particular strangeness long enough to understand its rules. The Skeptic Converted: a character who initially refuses the supernatural explanation and must update their worldview before they can help. The Threatened Community as Ensemble: the cast functions collectively; survival is a group achievement. Villains, if present, are often forces or presences rather than human antagonists, keeping violence between people low.

## Length & Format

60,000–80,000 words is typical. Novellas (25,000–45,000 words) are well-suited to the genre's atmospheric concentration. The contained cast and setting favor a tight, focused narrative structure over sprawling multi-plot novels. First-person or close third-person point-of-view sustains the intimate register. An ambiguous or quietly haunting final image is often more appropriate than a fully closed ending — the strangeness remains, but the community has found a way to live alongside it.
