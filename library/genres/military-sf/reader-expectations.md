Science fiction in which military conflict is not backdrop but engine — strategy, command decisions, the soldier's experience, and the human cost of war drive every narrative layer.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is pragmatic, disciplined, and unsentimental. Military SF does not romanticize combat; it examines it. Prose should reflect the mental framework of people trained for high-stakes decision-making under pressure: precise, controlled, with emotional weight delivered through understatement rather than exposition. Moral complexity is expected — the reader should not always know who is right. Unit cohesion, command loyalty, and the ethics of following orders under ambiguous circumstances are recurring emotional undertones. Humor, when present, is dark and brief. Heroism is earned through competence and sacrifice, not bravado.

## Pacing

Pacing alternates between deliberate strategic buildup and intense combat sequences. Readers tolerate — and often expect — long stretches of tactical planning, mission briefing, and institutional maneuvering before action breaks. Battle scenes should be coherent: readers need to track spatial relationships, unit positions, and command decisions even under chaos. Aftermath is as important as the battle itself — the accounting of casualties, the debrief, the psychological cost. Strategic arcs may span multiple books in a series, with individual volumes resolving tactical engagements while the larger war continues.

## Setting Conventions

The setting must feel operationally coherent. Whether the conflict involves near-future terrestrial forces, interstellar fleets, or alien ground campaigns, the military infrastructure should be internally consistent: chain of command, logistics, comms discipline, weapon systems, and unit organization. Readers notice when these break down. Technology should be presented through the eyes of people who use it daily — no exposition dumps, no character explaining what their own equipment does. Radio discipline, abbreviations, and professional jargon used naturally reinforce authenticity without alienating readers.

## Character Archetypes

The competent junior officer thrust into command is a genre staple. The battle-hardened NCO who keeps the unit functional while officers make decisions is equally essential. Protagonists are defined by professional capability first; backstory and personal life arrive as counterpoint to the mission. Antagonists may be enemy commanders deserving of a measure of respect, or political figures whose decisions cost lives at the front. Civilians and politicians are often portrayed skeptically — those who send others to war while insulated from its consequences.

## Length & Format

Novels typically run 90,000–120,000 words, with series common and expected. Single-volume standalone entries are less common; readers often commit to multi-book arcs. Multiple POV is frequent, used to show different levels of the command hierarchy or opposing sides of a conflict. Maps, appendices with order-of-battle information, and dramatis personae lists are conventional supplements in longer series. Prologues set in a prior engagement or strategic crisis are a common opening device.
