*Non-negotiable elements that define commercial fairy tale retellings: recognizable source, romance centrality, mature content.*

Establish a recognizable source text immediately. Readers expect clear connection to a known tale, myth, or folklore—transparency builds trust. You needn't name the source, but enough markers should signal what tale you're retelling. The reimagining's distinctiveness lies in execution, not in obscuring the foundation.

Center romance as equal driver to external plot. The relationship development must matter as much as any war, curse, or political conflict. If you removed the romance, the plot should collapse as thoroughly as if you removed the fantasy elements. Both drive the narrative forward; neither is subplot. Readers prioritize emotional payoff alongside action payoff.

Include explicit sexual content appropriate for New Adult or Adult audiences. "Spice" (intimate scenes) should range from moderately detailed to highly explicit, reflecting protagonist voice and pacing. Multiple intimate moments throughout—not just one culminating scene—demonstrate deepening physical and emotional connection. Frame intimacy as expression of growth and power.

Develop genuine antagonism before romance blooms. Enemies-to-lovers requires authentic enmity, not mere misunderstanding. Start from real opposition and build through forced interaction. The emotional shift from hatred to love should feel earned across the narrative.

Establish a complex love interest with personality beyond "dark and brooding." Give him flaws, history, agency, goals. He should challenge the heroine intellectually and emotionally, not merely provide romantic validation. Morally gray works; one-dimensional doesn't.

Deliver satisfying worldbuilding that serves romance. Create settings that facilitate intimacy: courts, academies, isolated estates, dangerous journeys requiring partnership. Include magical systems with internal logic and sensory richness. Beautiful, atmospheric descriptions matter.

Provide genuine character agency. The heroine must make meaningful choices that shape outcomes—not merely react to events or await rescue. Her agency should extend into intimacy: she chooses her partner, chooses vulnerability, chooses her path forward.

Include found family dynamics. Beyond the central couple, develop secondary relationships (allies, friends, confidants) with authentic care and loyalty. These bonds validate chosen family and provide support infrastructure for healing.
