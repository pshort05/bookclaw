Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do | Don't |
- |---|---|
- | Show the physical and psychological cost of violence. | Glorify combat or make it consequence-free. |
- | Write characters with understandable, if flawed, motivations. | Create cartoonishly evil villains without credible logic. |
- | Use sensory details to create immersion in the grim setting. | Overload with gratuitous shock value without narrative purpose. |
- | Let the world's bleakness affect the plot and character choices. | Have modern, optimistic sensibilities triumph without cost. |
- | Allow subtext and misdirection to drive dialogue. | Have characters openly state their feelings and plans. |
- Template:** [Subtext/Goal] + [Class-Appropriate Phrasing] + [Brevity/Bluntness]
-  **Noble Manipulation:** "Your loyalty is, as always, appreciated. The northern garrisons will need a commander of your... resolve." (Subtext: I'm sending you to a dangerous, possibly fatal post.)
-  **Soldier's Bluntness:** "We take the hill, we die. We don't take the hill, the sarge kills us. Get moving." (Subtext: We're trapped with no good options.)
-  **Peasant Cynicism:** "A lord's 'justice' is just the sword he's holding. Best pray he's holding it at your enemy." (Subtext: The system is rigged; only power matters.)
-  **Pitfall:** "Grimderp"—excessive, senseless bleakness that numbs the reader.
- Fix:** Ground every horror in character motivation or systemic cause. Make the despair purposeful.
-  **Pitfall:** Passive, miserable protagonists who only react.
- Fix:** Give them a clear, desperate goal. Their drive to survive or achieve this goal fuels the plot, even as the world beats them down.
-  **Pitfall:** Modern dialogue breaking immersion.
- Fix:** Use a thesaurus to find archaic or setting-appropriate synonyms. Read dialogue aloud for tonal consistency.
-  **High Nobility:** Formal, indirect, laden with political nuance and veiled threats. Uses titles and honorifics.
- Example: "We must consider the stability of the realm, cousin. Unrest in the south could prove... distracting to our mutual endeavors."
-  **Military/City Guard:** Clipped, professional, jargon-filled, often cynical. Dark humor is common.
- Example: "Another patrol gone. The rats in the dock ward are eating well this month."
-  **Common Folk/Peasants:** Superstitious, direct, rooted in immediate survival needs. Rich in idioms.
- Example: "The witch in the woods takes a tithe of milk or a finger. We pay the milk."
-  **Criminals/Outcasts:** Cryptic, street-wise, trusting no one. Speech is a tool for manipulation or defense.
- Example: "You lookin' for a quiet job, or a profitable one? 'Cause they ain't the same thing."
