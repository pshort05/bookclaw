Ensemble crime fiction where the pleasure is watching a skilled team plan and execute a complex theft or con, with wit and clockwork plotting outweighing moral weight.

## Tone & Mood

Heist-caper fiction skews toward the playful end of the crime spectrum. Even when the stakes are high, the dominant mood is one of confident cool — characters are competent, the banter is sharp, and the reader is invited to root for the criminals. Capers lean fully comedic; heists can carry genuine tension and menace, but neither dwells in the moral darkness of noir. The tone is kinetic and clever, rewarding readers who enjoy being one step behind the protagonist and two steps ahead of the mark.

## Pacing

The narrative has two distinct gears: the planning phase and the execution phase. Planning scenes build anticipation through detail and character interaction; readers want to understand exactly how the job is supposed to work. Execution scenes then deliberately derail that plan, forcing improvisation. The best pacing alternates between these modes — reveal, complicate, adapt — and never lets any single sequence run too long without a reversal or a surprise. Chapters should end on hooks. The finale must pay off every planted element from the planning phase.

## Setting Conventions

Settings are chosen for their value as obstacles. A casino floor, a museum vault, a private auction, a foreign embassy — the setting must impose constraints that make the heist genuinely difficult. Architecture matters: readers expect at least a partial mental map of the target space. The period or geography can vary widely, but the setting should feel researched and specific. Class contrast is a common undercurrent: the crew is usually working-class or outside legitimate society; the target is wealthy, institutional, or corrupt.

## Character Archetypes

The ensemble is the heart of the genre. Core roles recur across nearly every heist: the mastermind or planner, the grifter or con artist, the thief or infiltrator, the tech or safecracker, the muscle, and the inside contact. Each must have a distinct voice and a specific competence that the job cannot succeed without. Tension within the crew — competing loyalties, hidden agendas, past grievances — is as important as the external threat. The antagonist is typically a mark, a rival crew, or law enforcement; they should be credible enough that the crew's victory feels earned.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 70,000–100,000 words. Multiple POV perspectives are common and useful for withholding information from the reader. Non-linear structure — cutting between planning, execution, and aftermath — is a genre convention, not an experiment. Series are viable when the crew recurs; standalone heists are equally common when the job itself is the story.
