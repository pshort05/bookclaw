Readers expect an immersive view from inside organized crime — the codes, loyalties, violence, and corrosive glamour of a criminal hierarchy told with unflinching honesty.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is hard-edged and morally clear-eyed without being moralistic. Readers expect to be seduced by the world alongside the protagonist — the money, the respect, the brotherhood — and to feel the rot spreading beneath it. The genre does not require sympathetic protagonists in any conventional sense, but it requires comprehensible ones: readers need to understand why this life is chosen and why it becomes impossible to leave. Sentimentality is the genre's primary trap; earned emotion lands precisely because the prose does not reach for it.

## Pacing

Pacing typically follows a rise-and-fall arc: early chapters move fast, conveying the intoxicating momentum of ascent; the middle slows as entanglements compound; the final act accelerates into crisis. Violence must feel consequential — neither gratuitous nor sanitized. Readers expect action sequences grounded in logistics and consequence rather than choreographic abstraction. Business scenes (negotiation, territory disputes, tribute collection) should carry as much tension as physical confrontations, because the genre's real subject is power rather than violence.

## Setting Conventions

Gangster fiction is tightly place-specific: the neighborhood, the city, the era. Whether the setting is 1920s Chicago, 1970s New York, or a contemporary port city, the geography of territory and turf must be rendered in concrete detail. The social world — restaurants, social clubs, legitimate-front businesses, family homes — should feel observed rather than generic. Specific ethnic community contexts (Italian-American, Irish, Russian, Mexican cartel, Japanese yakuza) carry distinct codes of conduct, hierarchy structures, and cultural pressures that should be researched and rendered accurately rather than generalized.

## Character Archetypes

The Ambitious Outsider rises through merit and ruthlessness, earning a place in a world that does not easily admit newcomers. The Old Boss holds power through accumulated relationships and reputation; he is usually a figure of both genuine menace and genuine loyalty. The Enforcer does what others prefer not to acknowledge. The Family Member (spouse, sibling, child) represents the life the protagonist claims to be building, and the cost of the life he actually lives. The Betrayer is almost always someone trusted; the genre depends on this revelation landing with genuine devastation.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–110,000 words. Both standalones and series work, but series must track consequences across volumes — a gangster world that resets between books loses credibility. Chapters are typically short to mid-length (2,000–4,000 words), with strong scene-endings that carry the narrative momentum of a genre built on escalation. First-person narration is common and suits the confessional quality of the genre; close third-person is equally viable.
