# Genre-Killers

These failures trigger DNF reviews and one-star ratings. Each represents a breach of the vampire fiction contract—a promise betrayed or a basic competency missing. Avoid these mistakes or use them deliberately with full awareness of reader frustration.

## Predatory Nature Failures

**Sanitized or Absent Predation** — Vampires that don't actually hunt, kill, or feed feel like they're evading the core vampire identity. Readers tolerate many things but not the removal of what makes vampires vampiric. If your vampire has abandoned feeding entirely or never discusses hunger, you've removed the essential tension.

**Guilt-Free Feeding** — Protagonists who feed without any moral reckoning frustrate readers who expect ethical complexity. Whether choosing victims or embracing predatory nature, the choice must have psychological weight. Vampires who kill without hesitation feel like sociopaths, not supernatural beings grappling with nature.

**Humans as Interchangeable Resources** — Treating human lives as generic fuel rather than individuals with value and autonomy cheapens stakes. Readers expect vampire protagonists to at least acknowledge the moral weight of predation, even if they accept it as necessary.

## Character and Relationship Failures

**Passive Vampire Protagonist** — Vampires defined primarily by what happens to them rather than their own choices, moral struggles, or agency feel like plot devices rather than characters. Readers want vampires wrestling with their nature, making difficult choices, pursuing authentic goals—not simply reacting.

**Insta-Love or Love Without Foundation** — Vampire romance demands emotional development showing why these specific characters choose each other despite danger. Instant attraction without character work, vulnerability, or genuine connection frustrates readers expecting earned intimacy.

**Toxic Relationship Presented as Romance** — Compulsion presented as attraction, control framed as protection, and isolation justified as devotion all betray romance readers' trust. If you're writing intentional abuse exploration (like S.T. Gibson), name the toxicity explicitly. If it's accidental, it's a genre-killer.

**Flat Vampire Mentor** — The older vampire who serves only as info-dump or training device without personality, conflict, or authentic relationship feels like narrative scaffolding rather than character. Even secondary vampires need genuine presence.

**Inconsistent Character Voice** — Vampires speaking in modern slang despite centuries of existence, or ancient vampires unable to contextualize modern technology, create credibility breaks. Character voice must reflect their actual age and perspective.

## Worldbuilding and Logic Failures

**Inconsistent or Contradictory Vampire Rules** — Vampires destroyed by sunlight in chapter two but hanging out in partial shade in chapter fifteen; feeding requirement established then forgotten; vampire politics present then absent—these contradictions shatter reader immersion. Rules don't need to be classical; they must be consistent.

**Vampire Powers Solving Every Problem** — Superhuman strength, mind control, and centuries of experience collapse tension if used to solve obstacles. Readers expect vampire abilities to create as many problems as they solve (power temptation, moral compromise, attention from dangerous enemies).

**Implausible Secrecy** — Contemporary settings where vampires remain hidden despite internet, surveillance, and constant communication defy credibility. Either address how vampires stay hidden (technology camouflage, memory manipulation, public denial) or accept public knowledge.

**Missing Immortality Weight** — Vampires unconcerned about centuries passing, unaffected by watching loved ones age and die, or experiencing no existential weariness ignore immortality's core metaphorical power. Even if handled lightly, acknowledgment of age's burden must exist.

## Subgenre-Specific Failures

**Gothic Horror**: Missing atmosphere or psychological dread; villain too sympathetic or insufficiently threatening; atmosphere deflated by comedy or modern-day sass.

**Romance**: No real obstacles or stakes; love existing in vacuum without relationship development scenes; male vampire rescuing passive female rather than genuine partnership; refusal to provide Happily Ever After when readers came for romance.

**Urban Fantasy**: Vampire world completely separate from human world with no meaningful interaction; modern setting but no acknowledgment of how contemporary technology affects vampire existence; investigation abandoned for relationship focus or vice versa.

**Political Intrigue**: Factions without genuine ideological conflict; politics that exist without affecting protagonist's actual choices; power struggles that resolve through luck rather than strategy.

**Paranormal/Cozy**: Trivializing genuine danger by treating predatory vampire threat as cute or humorous without genuine stakes; community that feels like cardboard cutouts rather than lived relationships.

## Technical and Prose Failures

**Inaccurate Vampire Lore Without Explanation** — Deviating from classical vampire rules isn't a problem if you establish your own rules. Swapping vampire traits without justification (Your vampire can fly but the established lore doesn't allow it) confuses readers. Establish rules clearly before breaking them.

**Romanticized Predation Language** — Calling hunting "dancing" or feeding "sharing" risks glossing predatory nature in ways readers find dishonest. Use precise, unflinching language for what vampires actually do.

**Purple Prose About Vampire Sensory Experience** — Over-explaining the transcendent bliss of feeding, the supernatural beauty of vampire form, or the overwhelming allure of vampire presence quickly becomes ridiculous. Restraint serves this better.

## Assumption Failures

**Readers Will Accept Your Vampire Premise Without Justification** — Not explaining how vampirism works, what the world looks like with vampires in it, or how the protagonist fits into vampire society leaves readers confused. Exposition-heavy? No. But readers need foundational clarity.

**Vampire as Default Paranormal Entity** — If your story works identically if you replace "vampire" with "demon" or "werewolf" or "ghost," you haven't written genuine vampire fiction. The vampire's specific nature must matter to plot, character, and theme.

**One-Dimensional Vampire Culture** — All vampires operating identically, with identical values, goals, and perspectives feels unrealistic. Vampires spanning centuries of evolution should have diverse philosophies, traditions, and factional disagreements.

The strongest vampire fiction acknowledges these pitfalls and either avoids them scrupulously or uses them deliberately as part of character psychology or thematic exploration. Awareness transforms potential failures into intentional craft.
