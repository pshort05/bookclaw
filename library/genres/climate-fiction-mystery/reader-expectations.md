Readers expect a mystery where the crime cannot be understood — and cannot be solved — without understanding the climate-changed world that produced it.

## Tone & Mood

Tense, investigative, and morally serious with an undercurrent of ecological grief. The mystery register provides forward momentum and narrative satisfaction (the puzzle, the revelation, the resolution), while the climate fiction register provides weight, scale, and the sense that the crime matters because of what it reveals about systems and power rather than merely about individuals. The tone should avoid treating the environmental setting as colorful decoration; the wrongness of a climate-disrupted world should feel inhabited and real rather than dystopian wallpaper. At the same time, the procedural pleasures of the mystery — the gathering of evidence, the interrogation of suspects, the narrowing of the field — should be genuine and satisfying. Readers who come from a mystery background and readers who come from a cli-fi background must both find what they came for.

## Pacing

Mystery pacing dominates the structure: inciting crime, investigation, escalating revelation, climax, and resolution. Within that structure, the climate-fiction content arrives through the setting, through the motives and backstories of the suspects, and through the investigator's growing understanding that the crime is a symptom of larger forces. Exposition about climate systems or environmental policy should be woven into investigative scenes — discovered through documents, testimony, or physical evidence — rather than delivered as lectures. Red herrings should involve climate-adjacent motives (resource theft, cover-ups of environmental damage, corporate liability) that feel plausible given the world. The climax typically involves a revelation that implicates not just the individual perpetrator but the system that created the conditions for the crime.

## Setting Conventions

The setting must be both a specific, rendered place and a legible future or near-future climate reality. Whether a flooded coastal city, a drought-struck agricultural region, a wildfire corridor, or a water-rights battleground, the environment's specific transformation should be precisely rendered and scientifically plausible. The investigator moves through a world where the climate crisis has already reshaped infrastructure, economy, and governance — and where environmental crime (resource fraud, regulatory corruption, ecological sabotage) is normalized enough to be socially complex rather than simply evil. Physical investigation scenes in damaged or transformed landscapes carry extra narrative weight; the setting itself is evidence.

## Character Archetypes

The Investigator (detective, journalist, scientist, activist, or amateur) who has personal or professional connection to the environmental stakes, making the investigation feel embodied rather than procedural. The Corporate or Government Insider whose culpability is structural as much as individual. The Whistleblower who holds the crucial evidence and is also implicated in some dimension of the cover-up. The Community Member whose loss grounds the crime in human rather than abstract scale. The Skeptic or Obstructor who resists the investigation for reasons that are ultimately as much about climate denial as about the specific crime. The perpetrator should have a motive comprehensible in terms of the climate-changed world's economic and social pressures — desperation, self-preservation, ideological calculation.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 75,000–110,000 words. Series built around a recurring investigator in a consistent climate-future world are a natural fit; the world-building investment pays off across multiple books. Third-person limited close to the investigator's perspective is the dominant POV, maintaining the mystery genre's epistemological discipline (the reader knows what the detective knows). First-person is viable for a confessional or retrospective frame. Author's notes contextualizing the climate science are appreciated but should not substitute for embedding that information in the narrative itself.
