Fantasy set in fully realized secondary worlds where the depth of the world itself — its history, languages, factions, and cosmology — is as much the subject as the plot.

## Tone & Mood

High fantasy tends toward the elevated and the serious. The prose can range from archaic formality to modern clarity, but the register is rarely ironic or self-deprecating about its own conventions. Wonder is the baseline emotional register: the world should feel genuinely alien and genuinely old. Even when the story is tragic or dark, the tone acknowledges that something grand and meaningful is at stake. Readers expect weight — moral, historical, mythological — and a sense that the story they are reading matters within the world that contains it.

## Pacing

Pacing is deliberate and layered. High fantasy earns its slower moments through world revelation: a new landscape, a piece of ancient history, an encounter with a culture the protagonist has never seen. These beats are not interruptions to the plot; they are the genre's primary pleasure. Action sequences and political confrontations provide kinetic contrast, but readers do not expect the relentless forward drive of a thriller. The middle section of a high fantasy novel is often its richest, expanding the world even as it complicates the central conflict.

## Setting Conventions

The secondary world must feel inhabited and historical. Readers expect distinct cultures with coherent internal logic — different languages or naming conventions, different religions, different relationships to magic and power. The world should carry evidence of earlier ages: ruins, dead empires, contested histories, artifacts whose origins are disputed. Maps are a near-universal convention and set reader expectations about scope. Magic systems should be consistent and should carry costs; arbitrary magic undermines the logic the genre depends on.

## Character Archetypes

The chosen hero or unlikely protagonist remains the dominant entry point, but high fantasy increasingly complicates this figure — the chosen one who was chosen for the wrong reasons, the hero whose virtues are also their failures. Mentor figures are common and frequently expendable. Rulers, scholars, and generals populate the supporting cast. Antagonists should be comprehensible — ideologues, rivals with legitimate grievances, ancient powers with their own internal logic — rather than simply evil. Ensemble casts with shifting alliances are the norm in longer works.

## Length & Format

High fantasy runs long: 100,000–200,000 words for a standalone, with series frequently exceeding 300,000 words across volumes. Readers accept and expect extended length. Multiple POV chapters are a genre staple. Appendices — glossaries, genealogies, maps — are conventional and welcomed. Series commitment is high; readers entering a multi-volume work expect the author to sustain world-depth across all volumes, not just the first.
