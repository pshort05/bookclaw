Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do | Don't |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **DO** build tension through silence and subtext. | **DON'T** have characters monologue their plans or feelings. |
- | **DO** make violence sudden, brutal, and consequential. | **DON'T** use cartoonish "gangster" caricatures or excessive slang. |
- | **DO** let the environment (bars, cars, offices) reveal character. | **DON'T** info-dump organizational history or hierarchy. |
- | **DO** have clues arise from character actions and world details. | **DON'T** rely on coincidences or external *deus ex machina* to solve the mystery. |
- | **DO** create moral ambiguity; even "good" characters compromise. | **DON'T** make characters purely evil or saintly. |
- | **DO** make the investigator pay a tangible price for the truth. | **DON'T** provide a perfectly tidy, happy ending. |
- Template:** [Power Move / Assessment] + [Veiled Statement / Question] + [Action Beat implying threat/consequence]
- Examples:**
- 1. **The Subtle Threat:**
- "Nice family you got, Mikey." He didn't look up from polishing his glasses. "Real shame if the school bus was ever late."*
- 2. **The Interrogation:**
- "You were seen with Cardarelli Tuesday." She lit a cigarette, letting the silence stretch. "The same Tuesday his warehouse got light."*
- 3. **The Loyalty Test:**
- "The Boss asks where your cut from the J Street job went." He slid an envelope across the table. "I told him I'd ask the guy who counts the money."*
- 4. **The Betrayal:**
- "We had a deal, Sal."*
- Sal smiled, a thin, cold thing. "Deals change. The weather changes." He nodded to the door. Two men stepped in.*
- | Pitfall | Fix |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **Wooden "Gangster" Dialogue** | Write dialogue for the *character* (a paranoid accountant, a weary boss) first, then layer in minimal, authentic jargon. Read it aloud. |
- | **Predictable "Kingpin" Villain** | Give the antagonist a relatable, if twisted, motive (protecting family legacy, rectifying a past humiliation, enforcing their version of "order"). |
- | **Passive Investigator** | Ensure the investigator is *actively* poking the hornet's nest. Each scene should involve them making a choice that advances the plot and raises stakes. |
- | **Incoherent Power Structure** | Sketch a simple org chart for your fictional crime family/crew. Know who reports to whom. This clarifies loyalties and conflicts. |
- | **Mystery Solved by Luck** | Plant clues early that only make sense in the final context. The investigator should *earn* the answer through deduction, pressure, or sacrifice. |
- | **Bloodless Violence** | Remember the physical and emotional aftermath. A beating leaves broken bones and trauma. A killing haunts the killer and reshapes alliances. |
-  **The Boss / Don**: Measured, quiet, often uses metaphors and stories. Rarely raises voice. Threats are implicit. (e.g., "A tree with rotten roots falls on the whole house.")
-  **The Underboss / Capo**: Direct, pragmatic, focused on business and problem-solving. Dialogue is transactional. (e.g., "What's the damage? Who do we talk to?")
-  **The Soldier / Enforcer**: Terse, literal, uses physicality as punctuation. Dialogue is often short questions or statements of fact. (e.g., "He's here." "Where's the money?")
-  **The Associate / Hanger-on**: Nervous, talkative, tries to impress or ingratiate. Uses more slang, often incorrectly.
-  **The Law Enforcement / Journalist Outsider**: Procedural, probing, tries to impose outside logic on an insular world. May use formal language as a weapon or shield.
-  **The Family Member (Non-Criminal)**: Emotional, pleading, fearful. Dialogue reveals the human cost of the life.
