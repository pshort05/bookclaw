## Essential Checklist

- One of the most persistent and celebrated themes involves the meeting of characters from radically different economic strata. The typical configuration features a billionaire male protagonist meeting a woman of modest or middle-class means: a waitress, administrative assistant, nurse, schoolteacher, or woman recovering from financial hardship. This "opposites attract" framework explores how romantic love supposedly transcends and potentially solves the problems created by economic inequality. Narratively, the billionaire's wealth becomes both the source of initial attraction and conflict—as the heroine must navigate feelings of being "purchased" or controlled through financial dependence versus genuine love.[23][10][24][7][8][14]
- Conversely, romance writers and readers defend the subgenre as providing necessary escapism, pointing out that other romance subgenres featuring mafia bosses or antiheroes face less critical scrutiny despite comparable moral ambiguity. The genre's emphasis on female desire and the heroine's ultimate agency in choosing the romantic relationship distinguishes it from problematic dynamics, some scholars argue. The debate remains live and contested within romance communities.[16]

- Quick Reference Checklist
- Competence Porn: The billionaire must be hyper-competent at his job. We must see him commanding boardrooms. Power is the aphrodisiac.
- Submission/Dominance: While the Hero is dominant in the world and the bedroom, the Heroine must emotionally bring him to his knees outside the bedroom. She is the only one he submits to.
- The Grand Gesture: Money must be used for a romantic purpose eventually (buying her favorite bakery, flying her to Paris for dinner).
- The Grovel: The Third Act Breakup usually involves the Hero choosing business/vengeance over love. He must fix this with a massive apology and sacrifice.
- He Must Work: The Billionaire cannot be an idle heir. He must be a workaholic building an empire. His exhaustion is a plot point she soothes.
