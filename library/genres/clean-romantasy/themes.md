# Clean Romantasy — Themes

The emotional spine. Without a unifying theme the genre devolves into a succession of worldbuilding descriptions and interrupted almost-kisses. Choose two or three to anchor the manuscript.

- **Duty versus desire** — the tension between what the protagonist is obligated to do (save the kingdom, fulfill the prophecy, honor the alliance) and what they genuinely want (a life, a love, a future they chose); the arc proves these are not opposites but must be integrated at great cost.
- **Love as an act of defiance** — in a world designed to keep the leads apart — by law, prophecy, class, or war — choosing to love is itself a political and moral act; the romance is an argument against the world's cruelty.
- **Identity beneath the mask** — the protagonist (and often the love interest) has been shaped by duty, survival, or political necessity into a version of themselves that is not authentic; the romance is the only space where they are seen and known without performance.
- **Redemption and the possibility of change** — particularly in the dark romantasy sub-segment, a morally compromised character is offered a path back through genuine connection; the romance does not excuse past acts but provides a reason to choose differently going forward.
- **Faith and transcendence** — particularly in Christian romantasy and mythology-rooted worlds, love participates in something larger than the human — a divine plan, a sacred covenant, a world made meaningful by grace; the romantic bond reflects a spiritual truth rather than merely a social arrangement.
- **Power and its cost** — magical or political power extracts a price from those who wield it; the love interest or protagonist must reckon with how power has isolated, corrupted, or dehumanized them, and the relationship is the thing that makes them face that reckoning honestly.
- **Trust built under fire** — in a world full of betrayal and political manipulation, the slow construction of genuine trust between two people who have every reason to deceive each other is both the plot and the romance; the climax is often not a kiss but a choice to trust against all evidence.
