# Clean Romantasy — Reader Expectations

The reader's promise is full-intensity romantic fantasy — sweeping worldbuilding, fated bonds, political intrigue, and aching emotional yearning — delivered without explicit sexual content, so that the magic of the story and the heat of the relationship exist entirely above the bedroom door.

## Tone & Mood

Alternate between "epic and mythic" during worldbuilding and action sequences and "intimate and yearning" during romantic beats. The overarching mood is emotionally charged and narratively rich: a fully realized fantastical world with genuine stakes, but one where the romantic connection is expressed through proximity, longing, unspoken words, restrained touch, and charged silences rather than physical consummation. Do not mistake "clean" for "sanitized" — the tone can carry grief, moral ambiguity, violence, political betrayal, and the full weight of dark themes. What it cannot carry is anatomically explicit intimacy. The emotional hunger between leads should feel urgent, even desperate; restraint intensifies rather than diminishes the heat. Forbidden desire is more potent when consummation is withheld.

## Pacing

A dual-track progression: the romantic arc and the external fantasy plot (war, quest, political coup, magical catastrophe) must drive each other forward so that progress in one complicates the other. The slow burn is not optional — it is the genre's primary mechanism. Long chapters of political tension or world-threatening peril punctuated by intimate stolen moments is the rhythm the reader expects. Avoid resolving romantic tension too early; the yearning and almost-touches are the product. External threats compress timelines and force emotional confessions that the characters would otherwise defer indefinitely.

## Setting Conventions

Fully realized secondary worlds or portal-fantasy settings that impose their own rules, hierarchies, and dangers as external obstacles to the romance:

- **Fae courts and political hierarchies** — treacherous high courts with complex allegiance systems, where a romantic bond between rivals or across class lines carries lethal political stakes.
- **Empire and resistance** — a brutal occupying empire versus a scrappy resistance, placing the romantic leads on opposite sides of a moral and military line before forcing alliance.
- **Mythological and deity-touched worlds** — settings drawn from Chinese, Celtic, Norse, Greco-Roman, or West African mythology, where divine intervention, prophecy, and magic complicate mortal love.
- **Academy and training grounds** — a school of magic, a military training compound, or a guild where forbidden relationships between students, rivals, or mentor and pupil carry institutional consequences.
- **Quest and road-fantasy** — a journey narrative where a small group travels across a hostile world, and proximity over weeks or months builds intimacy through shared danger rather than proximity in a closed institution.

The setting must generate its own reasons why the leads cannot simply be together — political prohibition, class disparity, magical fate that brands them enemies, or a mission that demands they stay apart. These obstacles do the work that the closed-door heat level cannot.

## Character Archetypes

- **The morally determined protagonist** — driven by duty, loyalty, or a world-saving mission; fiercely principled but capable of moral complexity; the internal arc is learning that love is not a weakness or a distraction from duty but the thing that makes the duty worth surviving.
- **The guarded, dangerous love interest** — powerful, controlled, often perceived as cold or cruel; the reader discovers the tenderness beneath through small, precisely observed acts of care toward the protagonist alone.
- **The loyal found family** — a small group of allies whose relationships are equally complex and emotionally rich; they provide the comic relief, the sounding board, and the stakes that make the protagonist's survival matter beyond the romance.
- **The morally grey antagonist** — a villain or opposition figure whose motivations are coherent and whose worldview challenges the protagonist's; the best antagonists in this genre have their own coherent love story or loss.

## Heat Levels

Clean romantasy operates exclusively at:

- **Level 1 — Closed door / sweet:** intimacy stops at kissing, cuddling, or a hand held in the dark. The bedroom door is firmly closed. All emotional needs are met; all physical needs fade to black or are simply not depicted.
- **Level 2 — Fade-to-black:** the narrative acknowledges that the characters cross an intimate threshold but cuts away before any description of the act or immediate aftermath. Used at the climax of the arc or in an epilogue.

Level 3 and above is out of genre. A single explicit scene disqualifies the book for the clean romantasy market and violates the contract with the reader. Dark themes, morally ambiguous acts, and violence do not violate the heat-level contract — only explicit sexual content does.

## Length & Format

Aim for **90,000–120,000 words**; the commercial sweet spot is **100,000–110,000** for a standalone or series opener. Clean romantasy readers expect the epic scope of high fantasy alongside the romantic arc, which demands more room than contemporary romance. Under 80,000 words will feel thin in worldbuilding; over 130,000 risks pacing issues without a strong structural skeleton. **Third-person limited dual POV** alternating between the romantic leads is most common, giving the reader ironic access to the mutual pining each character hides from the other. **Past tense** is the dominant convention. Architecture is typically **series** (trilogy or duology) — the romance arc may not fully resolve until book two or three, with each book delivering an emotional milestone rather than a complete HEA, though a satisfying emotional resting point (not a cliffhanger on the relationship itself) is required per book.
