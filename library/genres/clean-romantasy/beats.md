# Clean Romantasy — Beats

The dominant structure is the four-phase slow-burn arc from *Romancing the Beat* (Gwen Hayes), running in continuous alternation with the external fantasy plot so that every romantic advance creates a plot complication and every plot escalation forces an emotional revelation. The fantasy plot (war, quest, magical catastrophe, political coup) and the romance arc must be causally linked, not parallel tracks that happen to share a protagonist.

## Phase 1 — Setup (0–25%)

- **Introduce the protagonist (1–5%)** — establish who they are before the fantasy world demands something from them: their loyalties, their wound, their default coping strategy (duty, isolation, self-sacrifice), and the specific internal flaw that will make love both necessary and terrifying.
- **Establish the world and its stakes (5–10%)** — the magical system, the political situation, the threat. The reader must understand what the world costs to exist in before they can feel the weight of the romance developing within it.
- **The first encounter (10–15%)** — the leads meet under circumstances defined by their opposition: a political confrontation, a moment of violence or rescue, a meeting across an ideological line. The charged atmosphere must be present from the first page they share. Neither is indifferent.
- **No Way #1 (15–20%)** — each lead acknowledges (internally or to a trusted ally) all the reasons an attachment is impossible: political consequence, personal rule against entanglement, a past wound, a mission that requires emotional distance.
- **The forced alliance (20–25%)** — the narrative compels them into unavoidable cooperation: a treaty, a shared enemy, a magical bond, a captivity arrangement. They cannot separate without forfeiting the mission or their lives.

## Phase 2 — Falling (25–50%)

- **No Way #2 (25–30%)** — despite the alliance, they maintain their walls through conflict: political disagreement, ideological argument, carefully maintained hostility that is obviously a shield for something else.
- **The first crack (30–40%)** — one witnesses the other in a moment of genuine humanity: grief, unexpected kindness to someone powerless, a moment of exhaustion beneath the performance of strength. The attraction transforms from adversarial awareness to reluctant admiration.
- **The midpoint of love (~50%)** — a false high: a charged moment that crosses an emotional line (a confession under battle-duress, a kiss that "doesn't count," a night of shared vulnerability that both agree to pretend never happened). They believe they can control what has already been set in motion.

## Phase 3 — Retreating (50–75%)

- **Inkling of doubt (55–65%)** — external stakes escalate: the enemy advances, the political situation worsens, the cost of the mission becomes clear. The romance begins to feel like a threat to the mission and the people they are responsible for protecting. They pull back.
- **Deepening doubt (65–70%)** — a betrayal of trust — not necessarily between the leads, but one that echoes their core wound and makes genuine vulnerability feel catastrophically dangerous. Each blames the other for distraction, or blames themselves for weakness.
- **Retreat and shields up (70–75%)** — they return to the personas they wore in Phase 1: cold, controlled, duty-focused. The distance is more painful than the conflict because it is chosen.
- **The black moment (75–80%)** — the ultimate rupture: one does or appears to do something that destroys the other's trust (sides with the enemy under duress, conceals a mission-critical secret, is revealed to have a prior loyalty that makes the bond impossible). The external plot also reaches its crisis at this point — the world is on the edge.

## Phase 4 — Fighting (80–100%)

- **Dark night of the soul (80–85%)** — separated and moving toward the climax battle or confrontation, each lead confronts what they are willing to sacrifice. The mission without the other feels hollow. The world without both of them alive and known to each other feels wrong at the level of the self.
- **The wake-up (85–90%)** — the recognition that the fear of love is the wound, not the love itself. The internal flaw (self-sacrifice, isolation, duty-above-all) is named and rejected. The protagonist chooses both the mission and the person.
- **The grand gesture (90–95%)** — a public, costly, irreversible demonstration of commitment: defending the love interest before the court, abandoning a political advantage to rescue them, a declaration in the aftermath of the final battle when the outcome is still uncertain. In clean romantasy this gesture is verbal and physical in its bravery but not sexual.
- **Resolution and glimpse forward (95–100%)** — the threat is resolved or held at bay. The relationship is acknowledged, publicly or privately, without the concealment of Phase 3. In a series opener, this is an emotional resting point that delivers satisfaction while leaving the larger world-threat open; the HEA arrives at series close.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)

- **The world-building immersion scene** — early, before the romance has begun to develop, a sequence that makes the reader feel the full texture of the fantasy world: a market, a court ritual, a magical demonstration, a political meeting. The reader must love the world before they love the people in it.
- **The first genuine exchange** — a scene where the performance of hostility or indifference slips and the leads speak to each other as themselves, not as their roles; often late night, often when one is injured or exhausted, often about something small.
- **The almost-moment** — a scene where consummation is clearly available and is not taken; the choice to stop or step back must be motivated by internal character logic, not contrived interruption. This is the clean romantasy's equivalent of the spicy genre's explicit scene — it must be written with the same care and emotional charge.
- **The "I see you" scene** — a moment where one lead demonstrates that they have been observing the other with genuine attention: they know the small habit, the private fear, the thing the other thought they had hidden. This is the scene that converts the reader's investment from conditional to unconditional.
- **The sacrifice or declaration** — the climax of the romance arc: an explicit verbal or acted commitment made at personal cost, in circumstances where survival is not guaranteed. The reader must believe the character means it because it costs them something real.
