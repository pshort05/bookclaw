## Failure Modes

- Upper left (Color Light Red): Low Demand, High Saturation; Label as ‘Avoid These Opportunities’
- Rising action sequences alternate between comedic character moments and action sequences, building toward climactic confrontations that synthesize humor and spectacle. The best action comedies maintain careful pacing equilibrium, where action sequences don't overwhelm the narrative but instead punctuate it, providing high-energy beats that contrast with character-driven sequences.[1][4]
- Redemption Through Partnership: Characters with troubled pasts, compromised values, or professional failures find redemption through partnership and loyalty. This theme provides emotional weight and audience investment.[14][25][3]
- Avoid These Opportunities (Low Demand, High Saturation)
- Negative Constraint: Add a system instruction: "Avoid grimdark descriptions. Avoid realistic medical consequences for minor injuries. Maintain a tone of levity even during danger."
- Punchy & Kinetic: Use active verbs and rhythmic sentence structures. Avoid passive voice in action beats.
- Energetic & Punchy: Sentences should be active and rhythmic. Use strong verbs and avoid passive voice, especially in action sequences.
- Tight Editing: Cut scenes immediately after the punchline or the explosion. Avoid dragging out the aftermath unless it serves a specific comedic purpose (e.g., the awkward silence after a failed stunt).
- The Fish Out of Water: A character dropped into a high-stakes world they don't understand, reacting with relatable panic or confusion.
- The Training Montage: Fast-forwarding through skill acquisition, often ending in failure or a comedic twist.
- 8. Taboos (What to Avoid)
- Forced Jokes: Humor must flow from the situation. Avoid "stopping the movie" to tell a joke.
