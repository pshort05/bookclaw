# Neurodiverse Romance — Tropes

Recurring situational devices that create the conditions for an authentic neurodivergent love
story — the structural promises made to the reader. Pick three to six; using all of them produces
a cluttered manuscript that prevents any single dynamic from reaching full depth.

- **Grumpy / sunshine** — a reserved, routine-dependent character (often autistic) whose defenses
  are no match for a warm, high-energy love interest who genuinely enjoys the grumpy one's
  directness and honesty rather than finding it exhausting.
- **Forced proximity** — a shared apartment, a stranded road trip, a joint project, or a remote
  residency strips away the neurodivergent character's carefully managed routines and forces
  improvisation alongside someone who becomes unexpectedly safe.
- **Enemies to lovers (mutual misreading)** — an antagonistic opening rooted not in genuine dislike
  but in misread social signals: the autistic character's bluntness reads as hostility; the ADHD
  character's interrupting reads as dismissal. The arc is the correction of that misreading.
- **Fake relationship** — a performed arrangement that lets the neurodivergent character practice
  intimacy with someone who has consented to go slowly, only for the practice to become real.
- **Slow burn** — an extended period of friendship, parallel work, or shared space before either
  character names the attraction; suits neurodivergent characters who require trust before
  vulnerability.
- **Special interest as love language** — the neurodivergent character's deep-focus passion (a
  niche subject, a technical skill, a creative obsession) becomes the primary channel through which
  they express care, and the love interest recognizes and receives it as such.
- **The competence reveal** — the love interest witnesses the neurodivergent character operating
  at full capability in their domain — a presentation, a performance, a technical crisis — and
  the reversal of the "odd outsider" perception is the romantic tipping point.
- **Late diagnosis / self-recognition** — one character discovers their neurodivergent identity
  during the story, reframing their past struggles as context rather than character defects; the
  love interest witnesses the discovery and chooses them before and after.
- **Two neurodivergent leads** — both protagonists are neurodivergent (different conditions, or
  different presentations of the same condition); the story explores how two people with non-standard
  wiring build a language uniquely their own.
- **The explicit communication pact** — the leads, having both been burned by subtext and assumption,
  agree to say what they mean; the resulting directness is both a source of comedy and a genuinely
  radical intimacy.
- **Found family as translator** — a tight-knit friend group, chosen family, or supportive community
  provides the scaffolding that makes the neurodivergent character's romantic risk possible; often
  the setting for a future-books ensemble.
- **Second chance (grown into acceptance)** — a prior relationship that failed because one character
  lacked self-knowledge or the tools to communicate their needs; the reunion works because both have
  done the work, not because the other person changed enough.
