# Neurodiverse Romance — Beats

The dominant structure follows four phases — Setup, Falling, Retreating, Fighting — with the
neurodivergent character's internal arc (toward self-acceptance) running in parallel with the
external romance arc. The two arcs must converge at the climax: the protagonist cannot choose
love until they have chosen themselves. Billy Mernit's 8-beat rom-com structure or a three-act
model with a midpoint reversal are compatible alternatives, provided they hit the same emotional
milestones.

## Phase 1 — Setup (1–25%)

- **Introduce the neurodivergent protagonist (1–8%)** — establish the daily management systems,
  compensatory strategies, and interior richness that define this character. Show the cost of
  masking, the comfort of routine, and the domain in which they excel. Root the reader inside
  their perceptual experience before the love interest arrives.
- **Introduce the love interest (8–15%)** — establish why this specific person is uniquely capable
  of perceiving the protagonist clearly; the love interest should demonstrate patience, curiosity,
  and a prior experience of being different that gives them context without making them a
  therapist.
- **The collision (15–20%)** — a first interaction that is almost certainly misread by one or both
  parties (bluntness mistaken for hostility, intense eye contact or its absence misread as
  indifference, a social miscue that creates comedic or dramatic friction).
- **The adhesion (20–25%)** — a structural force locks them together: a shared project, a living
  arrangement, a professional obligation that cannot be escaped without personal cost.

## Phase 2 — Falling (25–55%)

- **The misread lingers (25–35%)** — initial antagonism or awkwardness continues, but the love
  interest begins to reinterpret the neurodivergent character's behavior with curiosity rather than
  judgment. Small moments of genuine understanding accumulate.
- **The special interest scene (35–45%)** — the neurodivergent character reveals their area of
  deep expertise or passion, and the love interest responds with genuine delight rather than
  polite tolerance. This is often the true first-intimacy beat of the genre.
- **Midpoint of love (~50%)** — a false high: a moment of explicit, unguarded connection — a
  conversation that runs hours without the protagonist realizing, a sensory-safe physical intimacy,
  a declaration of a small but real preference — that makes both characters aware this has shifted.
  The protagonist begins, for the first time, not to mask.

## Phase 3 — Retreating (55–80%)

- **The exposure incident (55–65%)** — an external event (a meltdown, a shutdown, an intrusive
  question from a third party, an overstimulating situation) strips away the protagonist's
  composure. The love interest's response in this moment is the genre's highest-stakes scene.
- **The pullback (65–75%)** — the protagonist, convinced they have revealed too much and will now
  be managed, pitied, or left, retreats into masking or avoidance. Simultaneously, a genuine
  misunderstanding (rooted in differing communication styles, not contrived deception) widens the
  distance.
- **The black moment (75–80%)** — the internal conviction crystallizes: "someone like me cannot
  be loved like this without eventually exhausting the person who loves me." The protagonist
  pre-emptively ends or sabotages the connection to spare both of them.

## Phase 4 — Fighting (80–100%)

- **Dark night of the soul (80–88%)** — the protagonist is alone with the full weight of their
  history of exclusion and the specific grief of having let this particular person go. The love
  interest, separately, reckons with whether they communicated their commitment clearly enough.
- **The named choice (88–93%)** — the protagonist chooses themselves explicitly — names, accepts,
  or re-embraces their neurodivergent identity — before returning to the love interest. The romance
  resolution must follow self-acceptance, not replace it.
- **The repair (93–97%)** — an explicit conversation (not a grand gesture performed for an
  audience) in which both parties say precisely what they mean, what they need, and what they are
  choosing. The genre rewards directness here; the reader came for the explicit communication.
- **HEA / HFN and epilogue (97–100%)** — a glimpse of the couple's built life: routines that
  accommodate both people, communication rituals they invented together, and evidence that the
  neurodivergent character is not merely tolerated but genuinely delighted in.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)

- **The competence scene** — early on, the neurodivergent protagonist executes their work or
  expertise at a high level; the reader must admire them before the love interest does.
- **The special interest exchange** — the love interest asks a genuine question about the
  protagonist's passion and stays for the full answer; the first real intimacy of the story.
- **The exposure and aftermath** — a scene in which the protagonist's neurodivergent traits
  become visible and unmanaged (a meltdown, a shutdown, an unmasked stim, a communication
  breakdown), followed immediately by the love interest's response — which must be grounded,
  curious, and non-infantilizing.
- **The explicit-communication scene** — a conversation in which both characters say what they
  actually mean rather than relying on subtext; often the climax of the emotional arc.
- **The "I choose you as you are" declaration** — a verbal acknowledgment from the love interest
  that they are not choosing the masked version, not the aspirational neurotypical version, but
  the full, specific, unfiltered person in front of them.
