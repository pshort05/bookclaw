# Neurodiverse Romance — Comparable Titles

Touchstones for calibrating tone, pacing, heat level, and representation quality — the commercial
and critical vanguard of the genre.

- **The Kiss Quotient by Helen Hoang** — the genre's foundational text; an autistic econometrician
  hires an escort to practice dating, and the arrangement becomes a fully realized adult romance.
  Hoang (diagnosed during the writing of this book) delivers the standard for inside-out autistic
  POV: Stella's hyperfocus, her sensory preferences, and her social processing are specific and
  humane, never caricature. Steamy, smart, and emotionally exact.

- **Act Your Age, Eve Brown by Talia Hibbert** — the gold standard for dual neurodivergent
  representation; grumpy/sunshine enemies-to-lovers where the hero (autistic, openly) and the
  heroine (self-recognizing mid-story) are both fully realized. Hibbert demonstrates how to write
  a character discovering their own neurodivergent identity as an arc of relief rather than tragedy.
  Calibrate to this for heat level (Level 3–4) and tonal warmth.

- **A Brush with Love by Mazey Eddings** — an anxious, ADHD dental student falls for a classmate
  navigating his own neurodivergent experience; debut novel by an autistic-and-ADHD author who
  built her readership on the premise that every brain deserves a love story. Calibrate to this
  for the dual-POV structure and the portrayal of anxiety as a physical, daily experience
  alongside ADHD, not a backdrop condition.

- **Lizzie Blake's Best Mistake by Mazey Eddings** — an unexpected pregnancy forces a woman who
  has just learned she has ADHD into sustained proximity with a cautious, routine-oriented love
  interest; the romance arc and the ADHD self-acceptance arc are structurally inseparable.
  Calibrate to this for the late-diagnosis/recognition beat and the humor that comes from
  ADHD-brained narration without ever punching down.

- **The Bride Test by Helen Hoang** — the companion to The Kiss Quotient, following Khai, who
  has autism and does not believe he is capable of love; his arc is about confronting an
  internalized deficit narrative without the text endorsing it. Calibrate to this for the black
  moment grounded in the specific logic of autistic self-understanding ("I am missing the part
  that loves") rather than generic romantic miscommunication.

- **Tilly in Technicolor by Mazey Eddings** — young adult neurodiverse romance (autistic/ADHD
  leads) demonstrating that the genre's core conventions — explicit communication, special interest
  as love language, the exposure-and-aftermath scene — translate fully to a YA heat level. Useful
  for authors writing lower-heat neurodiverse romance; demonstrates that the emotional beats are
  not dependent on explicit content.

- **When My Heart Joins the Thousand by A.J. Steiger** — a standalone literary romance with an
  autistic protagonist navigating first love; quieter and more introspective than commercial
  romance, but the interior voice and the earned emotional climax are exemplary. Calibrate to this
  for literary-leaning manuscripts prioritizing character depth over plot momentum.

- **Atypical Love (anthology, edited by Alice Riley and Emma Robdale, 2024)** — a short-fiction
  anthology with autistic editors and neurodivergent contributors; useful for surveying the range
  of tones, heat levels, and neurodivergent conditions represented in the contemporary market, and
  for identifying the diversity of what "neurodivergent romance" can mean beyond ASD.
