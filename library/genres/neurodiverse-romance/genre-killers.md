# Neurodiverse Romance — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The misrepresentations, toxic dynamics, and tonal betrayals that trigger DNF reactions and
one-star reviews from the neurodivergent readership this genre is built to serve.

- **The quirky-but-not-really protagonist** — a character whose neurodivergence is aestheticized
  into charming eccentricity (loves spreadsheets, talks fast, forgets things) without depicting
  the genuine challenges of executive dysfunction, sensory overload, or social exhaustion. Readers
  with lived experience will close the book.
- **The love-cures-autism plot** — a romantic arc that frames the neurodivergent character's
  growth as a linear movement toward neurotypical behavior, with love as the treatment. The
  character may grow, but the goal of growth is never "become less autistic."
- **The robot or the chaos machine** — the autistic character who is a cold logic engine with no
  emotional life, or the ADHD character who is a walking comedy of catastrophic impulsivity. Both
  are stereotypes that reduce a complex person to a single trait expressed at maximum caricature.
- **The patient saint love interest** — a love interest framed as heroic, saintly, or self-sacrificing
  for choosing a neurodivergent partner; any narrative that implies the protagonist is inherently
  difficult to love or a burden their partner graciously bears will alienate the core readership.
- **The infantilizing caretaker dynamic** — a relationship in which the love interest manages,
  supervises, or speaks for the neurodivergent protagonist in social situations; protection shading
  into control, no matter how benevolent the intent, reads as dehumanizing.
- **The external fix** — the neurodivergent character's conflicts are resolved when a therapist,
  a medication, a diagnosis, or the love interest's guidance removes the difficult traits; the
  story must be about acceptance and integration, not elimination.
- **Sensory detail used for shock or disgust** — depicting meltdowns, stimming, or sensory
  avoidance behaviors as grotesque, alarming, or comic spectacle rather than as neutral or
  sympathetic aspects of the character's inner life.
- **The "simple misunderstanding" black moment** — a third-act rupture that stems from a
  miscommunication that the neurodivergent character's known communication challenges would
  have predicted, but that a five-minute explicit conversation could solve. If the characters have
  established that they communicate directly, the breakup must earn its stakes through something
  deeper than a misread text.
- **Appropriating neurodivergent identity for plot convenience** — introducing a character as
  autistic or ADHD only when their traits serve a narrative purpose (solves a puzzle, delivers
  an awkward line), then ignoring the condition in all other scenes. Representation must be
  consistent across the entire manuscript, not deployable on demand.
