# Neurodiverse Romance — Reader Expectations

The reader's promise centers on a love story where neurodivergent identity is the lens through which
attraction, intimacy, and connection are experienced — not a condition to be managed or overcome on
the way to a neurotypical-shaped happily ever after.

## Tone & Mood

Warm, specific, and emotionally precise. The dominant mood is one of genuine recognition — the
sensation of being seen in a character whose internal life, social strategies, and sensory
experiences mirror one's own. Humor is present but never at the character's expense; neurodivergent
traits generate situational comedy through friction with a neurotypical world, not through the
character being a punchline. Internal monologue is a primary vehicle for tone: rich, often
hyper-analytical, sometimes racing, always honest. The tension between the character's inner clarity
and their difficulty expressing it externally is the emotional engine. Cozy and safe in the deepest
sense — not saccharine, but a story where the reader trusts the author never to punish the
protagonist for being exactly who they are.

## Pacing

Deliberately slower in the early acts than standard contemporary romance, because the neurodivergent
protagonist requires more scaffolding before trust is established and intimacy is risked. Social
recalibration scenes — decoding mixed signals, processing an unexpected shift in routine, navigating
sensory overload — function as pacing markers, giving readers who recognize these rhythms the
satisfaction of accurate depiction. The midpoint romance beat often arrives via a non-standard
intimacy vector: a shared special interest, a parallel-play scene, or a moment of explicit
communication that neurotypical characters would have handled through subtext. The final act
accelerates sharply once the internal barrier (fear of rejection, masking fatigue, the belief that
love requires becoming someone else) is named and dismantled.

## Setting Conventions

Settings that create sustained proximity and provide a reason for the neurodivergent character's
skills to be indispensable — the social friction is systemic, not personal:

- **Specialized professional environments** — research labs, data science firms, academic departments,
  veterinary practices, museums, libraries, archives; places where deep-focus expertise is an asset
  rather than a social liability.
- **Creative and maker spaces** — recording studios, design agencies, tech startups, artisan workshops;
  environments that reward unconventional thinking and tolerate eccentricity.
- **Closed-community settings** — small towns, rural retreats, shared housing, academic residencies;
  forced proximity without the option of retreat into a crowd.
- **Medical and therapeutic adjacent** — hospitals, counseling practices, occupational therapy clinics;
  settings that normalize neurodivergent experience without pathologizing it.

The setting must provide the neurodivergent character with a context in which their particular brain
is an advantage, not a deficiency.

## Character Archetypes

- **The deep-focus expert** — an autistic or ADHD character whose single-minded mastery in their
  domain is formidable; dismissed by casual observers as odd, treasured by those who take time to
  understand. The romantic arc is about someone choosing to take the time.
- **The high-masking professional** — an autistic or anxious character who has constructed an
  airtight neurotypical persona at enormous daily cost; the love interest becomes the first person
  in front of whom the mask slips naturally.
- **The sunshine chaos agent** — an ADHD character whose warmth, intensity, and hyperfocus-driven
  loyalty disarms a more guarded partner; impulsive but not reckless, spontaneous in ways that create
  genuine joy.
- **The steady anchor** — the love interest (neurotypical or also neurodivergent) who provides
  predictability and explicit communication without condescension; someone who asks rather than
  assumes, whose patience is active rather than passive.
- **The late-diagnosed adult** — a character who spends the first act attributing their social
  difficulties to personal failure, whose diagnosis or self-recognition mid-story reframes everything;
  the romance is inseparable from the journey toward self-acceptance.

## Heat Levels

Signal content clearly. Sensory sensitivity and communication preferences shape intimacy scenes
throughout the spectrum:

- **Level 1 — Clean / Sweet:** closed door; emotional connection, verbal affirmation, and physical
  comfort (hand-holding, pressure, proximity) explored with care for sensory needs; intimacy stops
  at a first kiss.
- **Level 2 — Sensual / fade-to-black:** intimacy approached with explicit verbal consent, sensory
  negotiation, and emotional vulnerability; scenes fade before explicit acts.
- **Level 3 — Steamy / open door (mild):** at least one on-page scene with emphasis on communication
  and emotional intimacy as much as physicality; moderately descriptive.
- **Level 4 — Spicy / explicit:** multiple open-door scenes; consent, communication, and sensory
  preference are core elements of the intimate dynamic rather than interruptions to it.

## Length & Format

Aim for **75,000–95,000 words**; the commercial sweet spot is **80,000–88,000**. Under 70,000
risks under-serving the interior processing scenes that define the genre. **First-person or
close-third singular POV** is most common, rooting the reader inside the neurodivergent protagonist's
distinctive perceptual experience; **dual POV** is effective when both leads are neurodivergent, or
when the love interest's gradual understanding of the protagonist is itself a primary arc. Present
tense suits the immersive, real-time quality of autistic and ADHD inner experience; past tense is
fully acceptable. Format skews toward **standalones with series potential** — the ensemble of
friends, colleagues, or community members provides future-book hooks without requiring an overarching
plot thread to carry between books.
