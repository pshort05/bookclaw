# Folk Horror Comparable Titles

## Harvest Home by Thomas Tryon (1973)

An urban family moves to a New England village and discovers that the community's harvest rituals conceal a matriarchal cult requiring specific sacrifice. The foundational folk horror novel in American fiction; establishes the outsider-integration-then-horror structure.

## The Ritual by Adam Nevill (2011)

Four friends hiking in Scandinavia take a shortcut through old-growth forest and encounter something that has been worshipped there for centuries. Strong example of landscape as active malevolence and the way isolation strips rationalism from the protagonist.

## The Fisherman by John Langan (2016)

A widower fly-fishing in upstate New York hears a story about a fisherman who made a terrible bargain with something in the water. Nested narrative structure; demonstrates how folk horror can integrate mythology and grief without weakening the dread.

## We Have Always Lived in the Castle by Shirley Jackson (1962)

Two sisters live in isolation after most of their family was poisoned at dinner. Not traditional folk horror, but the rural insularity, community hostility toward the outsider, and the protagonist's genuine belief in her rituals make it an essential antecedent.

## The Little Stranger by Sarah Waters (2009)

A country doctor becomes entangled with a decaying manor house and the family within it; what follows may be haunting or psychological deterioration. Demonstrates how folk horror can operate through social class and landscape without requiring a cult or a ritual.

## Plain Bad Heroines by Emily M. Danforth (2020)

Dual timeline connecting a cursed girls' school in the early twentieth century to a film production at the same location in the present. Folk horror through institutional memory and the persistence of local malevolence.

## Brother by Ania Ahlborn (2015)

A young man raised in a rural West Virginia family of killers attempts to escape. American Gothic shading into folk horror; effective for the way community and family become indistinguishable from the horror.

## The Corn Maiden and Other Nightmares by Joyce Carol Oates (2011)

Short story collection anchored by a novella about a child's abduction ritualized by a disturbed teenager. Demonstrates folk horror operating through a single character's mythologised violence rather than a community; useful for writers working at shorter length.
