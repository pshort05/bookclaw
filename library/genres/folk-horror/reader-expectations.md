# Folk Horror Reader Expectations

Folk horror locates dread in the land itself and in the communities shaped by it — the countryside is beautiful and predatory simultaneously, and the horror emerges from what people do to remain part of it.

## Tone & Mood

Dread accumulates through the pastoral. The natural world — its rhythms, its beauty, its indifference — is presented as something that pre-dates human morality and will outlast it. The tone is atmospheric and slow-building, rooted in specific landscape and specific custom. There should be no comfort in the rural setting; instead, its remoteness and antiquity make rescue and reason inaccessible.

## Pacing

Pacing is slow atmospheric immersion in the first half, accelerating toward horror as the protagonist's understanding of the community's true nature becomes impossible to deny. The reader may understand what is happening before the protagonist does, which creates unbearable dramatic irony. The turn should feel inevitable in retrospect — not a twist but a revelation that the dread was always pointing here.

## Setting

Rural and isolated communities are essential: villages with long memories, farms operating under old rules, seasonal landscapes where the calendar marks ritual rather than weather. Ancient landscapes — moors, fens, hills, forests — carry the weight of previous sacrifices and older beliefs. The isolation is geographic and cultural; the protagonist cannot leave easily, and even if they leave physically, the community's logic follows.

## Archetypes

The outsider who intrudes — often urban, educated, and rational — encounters a community whose coherence depends on beliefs the outsider cannot accept. The community with hidden beliefs presents a normal face that gradually reveals its shape; they are not individually malevolent, only faithful to something the outsider's world abandoned. The convert is the character, often from within the community, who chooses or is compelled toward belief; they are the most disturbing figure because they show what the protagonist might become. The sacrifice is the role the outsider may not know they are filling until too late.

## Length & Format

70,000–100,000 words is the typical range. Third-person or close-first narration works best for maintaining the outsider's limited perspective. Present tense is less common than in psychological thriller; past tense with a retrospective quality suits the genre's interest in deep time. Short story collections are also commercially viable in this genre.
