Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- The Journey: This is the core of the novel. The protagonist struggles with internal conflicts (identity, morality, self-doubt) and external ones (societal pressure, family expectations, personal failures). They make mistakes.
- Nostalgic, Not Sentimental: A reflective tone is common (e.g., an adult looking back), but it should be grounded in the raw, often painful, reality of the experience. Avoid overly romanticizing the past.
- The "Firsts": First love, first heartbreak, first major failure, first rebellion, first moral compromise.
- Pitfalls to Avoid
- The Preachy Moral: The "lesson" should be learned by the protagonist through their experience, not stated overtly to the reader. Show the change, don't explain it.
- Anachronistic Voice: Avoid giving a young protagonist the wisdom and vocabulary of a seasoned adult before they've earned it through the story's events.
- Common Pitfalls - Ten pitfalls to avoid, from condescension to rushed resolutions
- Avoid sustained monotone pacing—modulate rhythm throughout
- Avoid condescension toward protagonist or youth
- Avoid anachronistic slang or technology references
- Avoid heavy-handed explanations
- Avoid over-explanation of symbolic meaning
- Common Pitfalls to Avoid
- Trust your reader: Avoid explaining symbolism or moral lessons explicitly
- Learning through failure and experience
- Teaches through experience and mistakes rather than instruction
- First Sexual Experience: Often significant moment of integration (or failure to integrate) physical and emotional selves; may be positive, negative, or ambivalent
