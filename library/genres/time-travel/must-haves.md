# Time Travel Must-Haves

Non-negotiable elements for time travel SF. If these are missing, the manuscript is not genre-ready.

- [ ] The temporal mechanics are established and internally consistent from their first use. The rules need not be explained in exposition, but they must be stable: whatever the mechanism does on page 30, it must do on page 280.
- [ ] Paradoxes are given narrative weight. When the protagonist creates a contradiction — meets themselves, prevents their own departure, alters a cause — the text treats it as a genuine crisis, not a hand-wave or a throwaway joke.
- [ ] The protagonist is changed by what they witness and do in other times. Temporal displacement is not tourism; returning unchanged, or changed only superficially, is a character failure and a thematic void.
- [ ] At least two distinct historical (or future) periods are rendered with specific, researched texture — sensory detail, social architecture, distinct idiom — not generic costume-drama backdrop.
- [ ] The cost of time travel is established and enforced. Cost may be physical, psychological, temporal (missed time in the home era), or relational — but something must be at stake each time the mechanism is used.
- [ ] Cause-and-effect consequences are dramatised, not summarised. When the past changes, readers must see the altered present, not simply be told it occurred.
- [ ] The protagonist has a clear and believable reason for using time travel rather than accepting the present. Curiosity alone is insufficient; the motivation must be legible and personal.
- [ ] Secondary characters in other time periods are fully realised people, not props. They have their own agendas, knowledge limits, and emotional responses to the protagonist's presence.
- [ ] The ending resolves the central temporal question — whether history can be changed, whether the protagonist can return, whether the loops can be broken — on terms the narrative has earned. It need not be happy; it must be coherent.
- [ ] The narrative distinguishes between what the protagonist knows and what is knowable. Omniscient foreknowledge of historical outcomes should create dramatic irony, not just convenience.
