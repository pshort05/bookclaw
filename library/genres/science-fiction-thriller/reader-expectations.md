A thriller driven by a science fiction premise — near-future technology, artificial intelligence, biotech, surveillance, or extraterrestrial threat — where the speculative element is not backdrop but the direct source of the danger the protagonists must survive or defeat.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is tense, often paranoid, and intellectually engaged. Readers want the propulsive fear of a thriller and the intellectual provocation of SF: the best SF thrillers make the technology frightening precisely because they make it plausible. The mood tends toward the cold and institutional — conspiracies, systems, corporate or governmental actors — though individual antagonists with ideological commitments are also common. A sense of dread that the world has already changed in ways the protagonist does not yet understand is a tonal hallmark. Dark endings are more acceptable here than in genre romance, but most readers expect the protagonist to survive and the specific threat to be defeated or contained.

## Pacing

Pacing is relentless. Revelations arrive faster than the protagonist can process them, and each answer opens new questions. The SF premise must be introduced early enough to establish the rules before the twist exploits them. Action sequences must be grounded in the SF setting's specific capabilities — a fight in a zero-gravity corridor is not interchangeable with a conventional thriller set-piece. Exposition about the SF world should be distributed through action, dialogue, and discovery rather than concentrated in early chapters.

## Setting Conventions

Near-future Earth is the dominant setting — near enough to feel recognizable, distant enough for the speculative technology to be plausible. Specific locations — research facilities, orbital stations, corporate campuses, underground bunkers — are preferred because they create the physical logic that drives escape, pursuit, and confrontation. The technology should feel like a logical extension of current trends: AI become autonomous, biotech escaped containment, surveillance infrastructure weaponized. The setting must convey that normal institutions are inadequate to address the threat, forcing protagonists outside normal channels.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is typically a specialist thrust outside their domain of control: a scientist who created the technology turned against humanity, a security analyst who discovers the breach goes to the top, a journalist whose evidence cannot be safely published. Competence is assumed; the threat works because it is beyond normal competence to address. The antagonist is often institutional — a corporation, an agency, an AI system — though a human face on the conspiracy is useful for confrontation. Supporting characters are frequently expendable; SF thrillers kill secondary characters to establish that the threat is real.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 80,000–100,000 words. Single-POV following the investigation is traditional; multiple-POV thrillers showing different facets of a conspiracy are also effective. Series are viable when the protagonist's expertise makes them a recurring threat-resolver; standalone is equally common. No romance requirement — a subplot may be present but is not expected, and forced romance reads as padding. Short chapters with hooks are the structural standard. Technical language should be present but not impenetrable.
