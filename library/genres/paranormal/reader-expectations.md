Fiction in which paranormal elements — ghosts, psychics, demons, possession, haunting, the unquiet dead — are central to the narrative without the romantic relationship being the primary genre engine.

## Tone & Mood

The tone depends on the paranormal register being deployed, but across subvariants the genre trades in the uncanny: the feeling that the world is not fully knowable, that something exists at the edge of rational accounting. Horror-adjacent paranormal leans into dread and the violation of domestic or bodily safety. Supernatural thriller paranormal leans into urgency and the protagonist's race to understand and survive. Literary paranormal leans into grief and the thin membrane between the living and the dead. What remains constant is a respect for the paranormal element as genuinely threatening or genuinely mysterious — not a backdrop, not an aesthetic choice, but a narrative force with real stakes.

## Pacing

Pacing in paranormal fiction tracks the gradual incursion of the supernatural into the mundane. The early chapters establish normal life against which the paranormal intrudes; readers should feel the texture of ordinary reality before it begins to distort. Revelation is staged: partial manifestations, ambiguous events, and dismissed perceptions precede full acknowledgment of the supernatural. The protagonist's resistance to accepting what they are experiencing is a classic pacing mechanism. Once the paranormal is fully acknowledged, the story shifts from revelation to escalation — the entity, ability, or supernatural situation becoming more dangerous or complex until crisis.

## Setting Conventions

Setting is load-bearing. Haunted houses, liminal spaces, small towns with occult histories, hospitals and institutions where many have died, archaeological sites disturbing the dead — these locations are not generic; their specific history and geography shape what kind of paranormal activity is possible and what it means. The paranormal should feel native to its environment rather than imported. Urban settings allow for institutional skepticism and the isolation of a believer surrounded by rationalists. Rural or isolated settings intensify the vulnerability of the protagonist and remove the social infrastructure that might otherwise explain or contain the supernatural.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist who can perceive what others cannot — the psychic, the medium, the accidentally sensitized — is the genre's central figure. Their gift is also their burden: social isolation, difficulty distinguishing visions from reality, institutional disbelief. The skeptic who is converted by events they cannot explain provides both counterpoint and a reader surrogate for the revelation process. The entity or supernatural force itself is a character with history and motivation; generic hauntings are less compelling than those tied to specific, knowable injustice or grief. Institutional authority — the priest, the academic, the law enforcement officer — often represents the failure of official frameworks to account for what the protagonist knows.

## Length & Format

Length varies by tone: horror-adjacent paranormal typically runs 80,000–100,000 words; supernatural thriller runs 85,000–100,000 words; literary paranormal may be shorter. Standalone novels are more common than in genre romance, though series built around a recurring psychic investigator or supernatural operative are a distinct and popular subformat. First-person narration is common, intensifying the reader's alignment with the protagonist's uncertain perception. Ambiguity about the reality of the paranormal elements is a legitimate and popular structural choice, particularly in literary entries.
