Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- Mystery and Revelation: This genre thrives on secrets. Don't reveal everything at once. Use a slow, deliberate unveiling of the supernatural world, its rules, and its history. Let the reader discover the truth alongside the protagonist.
- Give them Motives: Avoid purely "evil" creatures. What drives them? Is it hunger, a code of honor, a twisted form of love, or survival?
- Lore & History: Your world should feel lived-in. Create a basic history for the supernatural factions, their ancient wars, alliances, and prophecies. Weave this lore into the narrative through dialogue, artifacts, and old texts—show, don't just tell.
- Avoid camp unless intentional: Take supernatural elements seriously within your world's logic
- Show, don't info-dump: Reveal rules through action and conflict
- Avoid "As you know, Bob": Characters don't explain what they already know unless teaching someone
- Avoid Clichés or Subvert Them
- People don't speak in perfect grammar
- Avoid Common Pitfalls
- No exposition dumps: Characters don't explain what they both know
- No naming emotions: Don't have characters say "I'm terrified"—show through dialogue and action
- Avoid on-the-nose dialogue: Use subtext—what's unsaid matters
- Characters often don't say what they mean
- Don't reveal everything at once
- Avoid "lol magic" cop-out feeling
- Show, Don't Tell Techniques
- Supernatural characters sense things humans don't
- Make failure matter
- Avoid clichés unless subverting them
- Avoid appropriation
- Seeking immortality to avoid death
- Immortals seeing same mistakes repeatedly
- Terror of what we don't understand
- Fated mates who genuinely don't like each other
