- Mystery and Revelation: This genre thrives on secrets. Don't reveal everything at once. Use a slow, deliberate unveiling of the supernatural world, its rules, and its history. Let the reader discover the truth alongside the protagonist.
- Atmosphere is King: The feeling of your story is paramount. Whether it's spine-chilling dread, forbidden romantic tension, or the thrill of discovery, the atmosphere should be a character in its own right.
- Your tone dictates the reader's emotional experience. While subgenres vary, these are the primary tonal palettes to work with.
- Dread & Horror: When aiming for horror, focus on psychological fear and vulnerability. The threat shouldn't just be physical; it should challenge the character's sanity, identity, and perception of reality.
- The New Normal: The resolution doesn't return the world to how it was. The protagonist is irrevocably changed. Conclude the main conflict but often leave a thread or a new mystery dangling, especially for a series.
- The Insider: A character who is already part of the supernatural world (a witch, a half-vampire, etc.). Their conflict comes from their place within that world—navigating its politics, dangers, and moral complexities.
- Their goals should be clear and, from their perspective, rational. The most compelling villains believe they are the heroes of their own story.
- Consistency is the key to reader buy-in. Your world must have clear, consistent rules.
- Establish the Cost of Magic: Magic should never be easy. What is the price? Does it require physical energy, rare components, emotional sacrifice, or a piece of one's soul? Cost creates stakes.
