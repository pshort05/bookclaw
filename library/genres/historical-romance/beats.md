## Obligatory Scenes

- Historical romance stands as one of the most enduring and beloved genres in contemporary publishing, captivating millions of readers worldwide with its intoxicating blend of passionate love stories and meticulously researched historical settings. This comprehensive guide explores the evolution, characteristics, subgenres, themes, and prominent authors that define this multifaceted literary field, offering both aspiring writers and devoted readers an in-depth examination of what makes historical romance such a compelling form of escapism and emotional engagement.
- The latter half of the twentieth century witnessed a revolution in historical romance driven by authors like Julia Quinn, Lisa Kleypas, and Tessa Dare, who modernized the genre while maintaining its essential appeal. These contemporary authors infused historical settings with more contemporary sensibilities regarding relationships and character psychology, making the genre more accessible to modern readers while still honoring historical authenticity. The success of television adaptations, particularly the phenomenally successful Bridgerton series (2020-present), based on Julia Quinn's novels, has catapulted historical romance into mainstream popular culture, introducing the genre to millions of viewers worldwide.[7][6]
- The twenty-first century has witnessed unprecedented growth in historical romance, with particular emphasis on increasing diversity and representation. Pioneering authors like Beverly Jenkins, the acknowledged Queen of Black historical romance, established the viability of centering Black characters in historical narratives set in the nineteenth century. More recent authors including Alyssa Cole, Courtney Milan, Adriana Herrera, and Stacy Reid have expanded the genre to include protagonists of color, queer characters, neurodivergent individuals, and protagonists from non-Western cultures, challenging long-standing assumptions about who could inhabit these historical spaces and deserved happy endings.[8][9][10]
- Major Historical Romance Subgenres and Their Characteristics
- Tudor romances transport readers to a period of intense political intrigue and royal drama, centered on the reigns of monarchs like Henry VIII and characterized by court politics, religious upheaval, and power struggles. These novels often feature or reimagine historical figures, creating fictionalized love stories around real people and events. The Tudor period provides exceptional material for romance because genuine historical events—royal marriages, betrayals, and scandals—naturally contain the seeds of dramatic romantic conflict.[12][11]
- Georgian romance occupies a fascinating position as the immediate predecessor to the more popular Regency period. Set during the reign of George I through George IV (until the Regency began), Georgian novels feature greater diversity in character types and professions than their Regency counterparts—including scientists, artists, reformists, politicians, and landowners. The Georgian era witnessed the flourishing of high culture, the emergence of Romantic poetry, and significant social changes, providing a rich backdrop for romance narratives. Georgian romances offer readers an opportunity to explore stories set in an era of artistic achievement and intellectual ferment, often featuring less rigid social hierarchies than strict Regency narratives.[14][2][15]
- Traditional Regency Romance adheres closely to the conventions established by Georgette Heyer, emphasizing intelligent, fast-paced dialogue between protagonists, meticulous attention to historical detail to satisfy famously exacting readers, and traditionally featuring little explicit sexual content or discussion of sexuality. These novels typically have shorter lengths, focus tightly on the romance plot, and employ the voice and style of the era. The conventions include social etiquette centered on the Ton (the aristocratic elite in London), marriage of convenience tropes, forced compromises, the governess romance, rakes and rogues seeking redemption, and the threat of scandal to female reputation.[2][17][14][3]
- Regency Historical Romance diverges significantly from the traditional form, developing fuller, more complex worlds surrounding the main characters, incorporating more extensive historical detail and contemporary historical events, and typically featuring much more explicit sexual content. These novels often span longer page counts and may develop secondary character arcs and subplots more extensively than traditional Regencies.[7][2]
- Victorian romance encompasses the long reign of Queen Victoria, a period often conflated with Regency in casual discussion but possessing distinctly different characteristics. The Victorian era emphasized moral earnestness, propriety, industrial progress, and scientific advancement, creating a different atmosphere than the Regency. Victorian romances frequently feature rakes and rogues, marriage of convenience plots, compromising situations leading to forced marriages, and themes of social class conflict. The era's strict morality provides natural conflict, while the mechanization and industrialization of society add complexity to romantic narratives set against this transforming landscape.[17][15][14]
- Viking romances represent a subgenre that has significantly declined in popularity since its height in the 1990s, featuring warriors from the Norse culture during the Dark and Middle Ages. These novels characteristically present heroes as stereotypically masculine, often described as "tall, blonde, and strikingly handsome," who are "tamed" by their heroines through love. The Viking culture allows for extensive travel and adventure plots, as Vikings were explorers founding and conquering colonies throughout the globe. However, Viking romances have been subject to considerable scholarly critique regarding their romanticization of sexual violence and the use of Viking warriors as symbols of pre-modern, uncontrolled masculine aggression allowed to dominate within historical settings. The genre's decline reflects both changing reader preferences and evolving critical consciousness regarding the problematic tropes employed.[18][11]
- Western historical romance encompasses stories set in the American frontier before 1945, featuring cowboys, sheriffs, and settlers navigating the challenges of building civilization in untamed landscapes. These novels often explore themes unique to the American experience: mail-order brides, marriages of convenience born from necessity, frontier justice, and the survival of strong, independent women in an unforgiving environment. Western romances often feature practical heroines who can ride, shoot, and manage homesteads as effectively as their male counterparts, though they still experience the universal romance arc of falling in love against seemingly insurmountable odds. Authors like Rosanne Bittner and Linda Broday have become celebrated for their meticulous historical research and authentic portrayal of frontier life, winning devoted reader followings for works that educate readers about American history while delivering compelling love stories.[19]
- Native American historical romance represents an important subcategory of American frontier romance, featuring Native American protagonists or significant Native American characters navigating romantic relationships during the colonial and frontier periods. These romances must carefully balance authentic representation of tribal cultures with romantic fantasy, a challenge that has produced both celebrated works and problematic narratives. Authors like Beth Trissel and Catherine Anderson have authored significant works in this subgenre, with Red Bird's Song and Comanche Moon receiving particular recognition for their attention to cultural authenticity alongside romantic storytelling. This subgenre has historically struggled with problematic tropes romanticizing conquest and cultural appropriation, though contemporary authors are increasingly centering indigenous voices and perspectives.[20][19]
- Celtic and Irish historical romance draws inspiration from both historical periods and mythological traditions, creating narratives rich with cultural identity and passionate characterization. Stories set during various Irish historical periods—from pre-Christian tribal Ireland through the independence movement—offer opportunities to explore themes of cultural resistance, political conflict, and national identity alongside romance. Authors like Edward Rutherfurd (The Princes of Ireland) have created epic multi-generational sagas exploring Irish history through romantic narratives, while contemporary authors blend historical romance with paranormal and fantasy elements inspired by Celtic mythology. These romances often emphasize the fierce, passionate nature attributed to Celtic and Irish people, creating characters whose intensity matches the turbulent histories of their homelands.[21]
- Historical romance relies upon a constellation of narrative tropes that recur across time periods and subgenres, providing readers with the satisfying familiarity they expect while allowing authors creative variations within established patterns. The marriage of convenience stands among the most prevalent, appearing across medieval, Regency, Victorian, and American frontier romances as a mechanism forcing protagonists into proximity and creating natural conflict between contractual obligation and genuine emotion. This trope gains power from the historical period's actual marital practices, where economic, social, or political considerations frequently superseded romantic attraction in spouse selection.[17][24]
- Forced proximity represents another foundational trope, trapping characters in enclosed spaces or circumstances that force reliance on one another and build sexual tension. In historical romance, this might take the form of a carriage journey, a country house visit, or an arranged engagement creating inescapable daily contact. The historical settings enhance this trope's power, as characters lacking modern freedom of movement find their circumstances feel genuinely inescapable.[25]
- Compromising situations appear repeatedly across historical romance, particularly in Regency and Victorian narratives where a woman's reputation could be irreparably damaged by being alone with a man without a chaperone. These situations—a woman caught alone with a man, a woman whose reputation is publicly linked to a man's, or a woman who commits some scandalous act that threatens her marriage prospects—create natural conflict within the historical context. The modern reader understands these consequences as culturally constructed rather than inevitable, yet the historical setting allows readers to suspend disbelief and accept them as genuine threats motivating character behavior.[17]
- The rake or rogue hero seeking redemption through love appears persistently across historical romance subgenres. This character archetype—a man of uncertain morals, extensive romantic experience, and guarded heart who is transformed by genuine love—appeals to readers' desire to believe in redemptive love while providing exciting sexual tension and dramatic backstory. The historical setting legitimizes the rake's behavior to some extent, as certain periods' social codes allowed greater latitude for aristocratic men's sexual behavior.[17]
- Friends to lovers and enemies to lovers remain staple tropes, with the former emphasizing slow-burn intimacy built on genuine connection and the latter featuring passionate opponents whose arguments mask attraction.[25]
- Ruins, decay, and gothic settings draw from Romantic symbolism suggesting melancholy beauty, the passage of time, and spiritual connections with history. Historical romance frequently employs gothic elements—mysterious estates, hidden chambers, ancestral secrets—as both setting and symbolic representation of hidden emotional depths and secrets between characters.[27]
- Letters and written correspondence function as significant symbolic elements in historical romance, serving as mechanisms for revelation, misunderstanding, or expressions of love and honesty that characters struggle to communicate verbally. Historical settings make letters naturally important plot devices, as they represent the primary form of long-distance communication.[28]
- Historical romance follows patterns adapted from the broader romance genre but modified by historical period constraints. Most historical romance novels range between 70,000 and 100,000 words, allowing sufficient space for both historical world-building and character development. The narrative typically follows the journey of two protagonists from initial meeting, through conflict (external and internal), to the recognition of love and commitment to a shared future. The historical context shapes what that journey entails—a Regency heroine cannot simply leave her family without support to pursue an independent life with her lover, creating conflict impossible in contemporary romance.[30][31][32]
- Meeting cute and romantic tension remain essential elements, though historical periods shape their expression. In Regency romance, a heroine and hero might meet at a ball, with witty banter replacing more casual contemporary interactions. In Western romance, the meeting might occur through necessity—a rancher hiring a mail-order bride, or a woman arriving alone in a frontier town requiring protection.[7]
- However, this balance generates ongoing debate within the genre. Some argue that historical romance should scrupulously adhere to period-appropriate attitudes, values, and behaviors, maintaining verisimilitude with actual history. Others contend that expecting authors to strip their work of modern perspective is unrealistic, particularly given that romance novels are written by and for twenty-first-century readers seeking emotional connection to protagonists. The most critically acclaimed historical romances typically employ what scholars term "verisimilitude"—creating worlds that feel meaningful, internally consistent, and realistically complex without necessarily matching every detail of actual history. As one scholar notes, "emotional authenticity in the development of the relationship is far more important to the genre than strict fidelity to a historical or geographic setting".[29][33]
- Readers of historical romance hold varied expectations regarding accuracy, with some noting that anachronisms particularly jar their reading experience (such as a bride in 1812 walking down an aisle to Mendelssohn's "Wedding March," composed in 1842), while others prioritize emotional authenticity and character coherence over perfect historical detail. The contemporary practice of including diverse characters in historical romance raises particular questions about accuracy, as historical racism and social exclusion genuinely existed while also reflecting some historians' emphasis on only certain populations. Authors increasingly embrace creative liberty regarding which stories to center, arguing that diversity in historical romance reflects historical reality (Black people lived in Regency London, sapphic couples existed in history, people of color inhabited multiple historical periods) while also serving modern readers' desires for representation.[8][29]
- Historical romance gains much of its emotional resonance from societal structures and restrictions that create genuine stakes for characters' romantic choices. A woman in Regency society who is unmarried past her prime finds herself economically vulnerable and socially diminished. A woman caught alone with a man risks her reputation and marriageability. A lower-class woman developing feelings for an aristocratic man faces structural impossibility without intervention by fate or wealth. These historical restrictions are not merely window-dressing but central to creating meaningful conflict and emotional investment in character outcomes.[17][32][30]
- Julia Quinn has emerged as perhaps the most commercially successful historical romance author of the contemporary era, best known for her Bridgerton series beginning with The Viscount Who Loved Me (2000) and Bridgerton: An Offer from a Gentleman (2001). Quinn's novels feature witty banter, lovable characters, and emotional depth that has appealed to millions of readers, with her works forming the basis for the massively popular Bridgerton television series (2020-present).[7][12]
- Tessa Dare represents another major contemporary voice, celebrated for her sparkling dialogue, emotionally complex characters, and inventive plots. Her Spindle Cove series and Castles Ever After series have garnered devoted followings, with The Duchess Deal and Do You Want to Start a Scandal frequently recommended for their romantic tension and memorable characterization. Dare's recent work includes Romancing the Duke (part of Castles Ever After).[34][35][36]
- Mary Balogh stands as a legend within historical romance, with her Bedwyn Saga and Slightly series achieving classic status. Works like Slightly Married and Slightly Tempted exemplify her ability to create complex, wounded characters who heal through love, with emotionally sophisticated narratives that reward rereading. Her works consistently appear on reader recommendation lists as exemplifying the genre at its finest.[35][7][34]
- Loretta Chase has maintained prominence since beginning her career in 1987, with more than fifty published books and numerous Romance Writers of America RITA awards. Known for witty characterization and inventive plotting, Chase's works include her popular Scoundrels series.[37]
- Courtney Milan has established herself as an innovative voice writing Regency and Victorian romance with diverse characters, including Asian protagonists in The Duke Who Didn't and Chinese characters in The Devil Comes Courting. Her Brothers Sinister and Worth Saga series have achieved critical acclaim and devoted reader followings, with her work notable for combining commercial appeal with progressive politics and complex characterization.[41][9][10][37]
- Adriana Herrera represents another important contemporary voice, writing multicultural historical romance including Latina protagonists. Her recent work A Tropical Rebel Gets the Duke (2025) features a rebellious physician and a newly titled duke in 1889 Paris, exemplifying her ability to combine diverse characters with compelling romance. Her A Caribbean Heiress in Paris (2022) similarly centers an Afro-Latina protagonist navigating 1880s Paris.[42][43][9]
- Eloisa James maintains prominence with emotionally complex romance, frequently recommended for readers seeking sophisticated characterization and emotional satisfaction.[7]
- Recent releases garnering significant reader enthusiasm include Alexandra Vasti's Earl Crush (January 2025), featuring a reclusive earl and radical pamphleteer in a marriage of convenience complicated by hidden identities and espionage. Joanna Lowell's A Rare Find (2025) provides queer Victorian romance with a 4-star rating. Adriana Herrera's A Tropical Rebel Gets the Duke (February 2025) combines multicultural characters with steamy Parisian intrigue.[43][41]
- Lucinda Brant, an Australian author, continues producing acclaimed 18th-century historical romance through series including the Roxton Family Saga and Roxton Foundation Series, with her characters described as leaping off the page with drama, intrigue, and power.[37]
- The contemporary historical romance landscape increasingly features writers centering marginalized perspectives and previously excluded identities. Vanessa Riley writes Black heroines in Regency settings with historical authenticity and emotional depth. Jeannie Lin writes Tang Dynasty China historical romance through her Pingkang Li Mysteries series, bringing non-Western historical settings to the genre. Mimi Matthews brings diverse characters to Victorian romance, including South Asian protagonists, exemplified by her work The Siren of Sussex with an Indian male main character.[9]
- Erica Ridley notably includes Black leads in her dashing Regency romps, including Nobody's Princess (2022) and The Perks of Loving a Wallflower (2021), with her Wild Wynchesters series intentionally centering unconventional protagonists including queer characters, neurodivergent individuals, and people of color. Ridley's work reflects historical reality—Black people lived in Regency London, sapphic couples existed—while creating contemporary representation through historically grounded narrative.[8]
- Within romance's broader market dominance, historical romance maintains prominence, though exact market share figures by subgenre remain difficult to isolate. Romantasy has experienced triple-digit growth in 2025, with some publishers and industry analysts predicting historical romantasy as an emerging growth area. The popularity of the Bridgerton television series has significantly boosted sales of Julia Quinn's original novels and similar historical romance works.[42][45][44]
- One of the most significant contemporary trends involves increasing diversity in historical romance representation. Where Beverly Jenkins long "reigned alone" as the primary author centering Black protagonists, she is now gratifyingly joined by Alyssa Cole, Vanessa Riley, and others. Similarly, Adriana Herrera and Liana De la Rosa have introduced Latina heroines to historical romance, while Courtney Milan consistently features Asian protagonists. Contemporary historical romance increasingly features disabled protagonists (such as protagonists with clubfeet, as in The Painter Takes an Earl ), queer protagonists across all sexual orientations and gender identities, neurodivergent characters, and protagonists from diverse cultural and ethnic backgrounds.[8]
- This diversification reflects both contemporary readers' desires for representation and scholarly and activist insistence that historical romance acknowledge that diverse populations inhabited historical periods previously depicted as uniformly white. As scholar Erica Ridley notes, "Black people have lived in Britain for centuries, and there was in fact a large Black population in London during the Regency". Similarly, scholars point to documented sapphic couples in history, such as the Ladies of Llangollen, establishing that queer relationships in historical settings are not anachronistic inventions but reflections of historical reality.[8]
- An emerging trend involves increased reader interest in darker historical romance exploring difficult emotions and complex moral landscapes. Publishers and industry analysts observe increased interest in psychological aspects of romance, with readers seeking stories that safely explore challenging emotions like anger, sadness, and anxiety. This trend extends to darker subjects within historical romance, with authors increasingly willing to explore morally ambiguous characters, trauma, and complex emotional landscapes rather than exclusively emphasizing lightness or idealization.[44]
- Historical romance represents one of contemporary publishing's most vital, evolving, and beloved genres, combining readers' universal hunger for romantic connection with fascination with the past and desire to understand how love transcends historical periods and social structures. From Georgette Heyer's pioneering Regency novels to contemporary works centering diverse characters and previously marginalized narratives, historical romance has demonstrated remarkable adaptability while maintaining its essential appeal: the promise that two people can find genuine, transformative love despite—or because of—the obstacles their historical moment creates.[30]
- The genre's evolution reflects broader cultural shifts, with contemporary historical romance increasingly questioning whose stories deserve telling and what histories deserve centering. The expansion beyond predominantly white, heterosexual, able-bodied characters to include people of color, queer individuals, disabled protagonists, and characters from non-Western cultures represents both ethical progress and artistic enrichment, bringing previously untold stories to readers hungry for diverse representations of love across time.
- The commercial success of historical romance, evidenced by booming sales figures, television adaptations, and sustained reader enthusiasm, demonstrates that audiences continue craving the escapism, emotional satisfaction, and sense of historical immersion that well-crafted historical romance provides. Whether readers seek the witty banter of Regency salons, the passionate intensity of medieval knights, the frontier independence of Western heroines, or the complex emotions of Civil War-era divided loyalties, historical romance offers pathways into fully realized worlds where love provides meaning, transformation, and ultimately, the promise of happily-ever-after despite all historical odds.
- The genre's future appears vibrant, with emerging authors continuing to expand what historical romance can encompass in terms of time periods, cultural contexts, character identities, and thematic complexity. As long as readers continue seeking connection to the past and celebration of love's transformative power, historical romance will remain one of literature's most vital and beloved genres.
