## Failure Modes

- Separated lovers reunited represents a classic trope wherein separated protagonists must rediscover their love, often with improved understanding and commitment. The trope gains emotional force from protagonists' recognition of what they lost and determination not to repeat that mistake.[17]
- The Scar: A physical manifestation of backstory; a reminder of a past failure or a lesson learned.
- The "Show, Don't Tell" Rule: Do not lecture the reader on history. Weave period details (clothing, architecture, etiquette) seamlessly into action and interaction.
- Flavor over Accuracy: Use a "historicized" style that feels authentic but remains readable to modern audiences. Avoid "Forsooth" or "Thee/Thou" unless specifically Medieval/Puritan.
- When generating text, strictly avoid modern slang and 21st-century psychological terminology. Assume the social constraints of the era (e.g., Regency, Victorian) regarding gender roles and reputation are absolute law, unless the character is explicitly rebelling against them.
- Character Archetypes and Development — Hero and heroine types, character authenticity, and avoiding stereotypes
- Common Pitfalls and Solutions — Real problems writers face with practical fixes
- DON'T:
- Avoid modern similes or comparisons that break historical immersion
- Avoid modern slang, profanity, or casual speech patterns
- Avoid making characters uniformly progressive or modern in values—class, education, and era shape perspective
- Avoid anachronistic sexual attitudes or practices specific to modern sexual culture
- Avoid anachronisms (objects, words, concepts that didn't exist)
- Importance of reputation and scandal avoidance
- Avoiding Worldbuilding Pitfalls
- Don't info-dump historical details through dialogue or narration
- Avoid excessive technical detail not serving story
- Don't make setting feel like museum backdrop rather than lived reality
- Ensure sensual/sexual scenes don't dominate—romance includes emotional intimacy too
- Avoid purple prose: "he bellowed," "she whispered seductively"
- Let tension exist in what characters don't say
- "Tis" and "'twas" contractions (avoid unless historically appropriate and character-specific)
