Readers expect a cozy mystery in which an animal companion is not a decoration but a genuine participant in the investigation — contributing clues, perspective, or capability that the human protagonist could not access alone.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is warm, often gently humorous, and deeply affectionate toward the animal at the centre of the story. The mystery follows cozy conventions — bloodless crime, amateur sleuth, tight community of suspects — but the animal's presence shapes the narrative texture in distinct ways: scenes are filtered through the animal's perceptions, the protagonist's bond with the pet is an emotional anchor, and the humor that arises from the animal's misunderstandings or instincts is a structural pleasure, not just a flourish. What distinguishes cozy pet mystery from standard cozy mystery is that removing the animal would make the mystery genuinely harder to solve — the pet's role is load-bearing, not decorative.

## Pacing

Investigation pacing follows cozy mystery conventions: crime discovered, suspects circled, clues accumulated through community interaction, red herrings landed, resolution reached. The animal companion adds a distinctive beat: scenes in which the pet's behavior provides a clue — insistent attention to a particular person or object, unease in a specific location, a retrieved item — which the protagonist must interpret. These beats should be plausible given the animal's established capabilities, not magical. Domestic scenes between the protagonist and pet slow the pace productively, providing warmth and character grounding between investigation sequences.

## Setting Conventions

A small, stable community where the protagonist is known and trusted: a village, a small town, a narrowly defined urban neighborhood. The setting should accommodate the animal's presence naturally — a protagonist who runs a pet shop, a veterinarian, a dog walker, a cat-sanctuary volunteer — so the animal's access to places and people feels plausible rather than contrived. Sensory details relevant to the animal are welcome: what the cat observes from the window seat, what the dog smells at the scene. The domestic space — home, shop, garden — should feel warm and specific.

## Character Archetypes

The Investigative Human: an amateur sleuth with a profession or circumstance that gives her access to the community and its information networks, and whose relationship with the pet is the emotional core of the book. The Animal Companion: the central animal — typically a cat or dog, occasionally a bird or other creature — whose personality, capabilities, and perspective are rendered consistently and with affection. The Standard Cozy Suspects: neighbors, business rivals, relatives, community figures with motive and access. The Skeptical Official: a constable or detective who correctly disbelieves the protagonist's crime theory until events prove otherwise.

## Length & Format

65,000–80,000 words for a standalone; series strongly favored, with the animal companion as the series anchor and continuity. The animal's capabilities should be established early and remain consistent across the series — readers will notice if the pet's intelligence or perceptions are inflated to serve a plot point. Point-of-view is typically close third-person following the human protagonist, with filtered glimpses of the animal's perspective rather than full interior monologue. An epilogue scene featuring the protagonist and pet in domestic contentment is a genre-standard closing beat.
