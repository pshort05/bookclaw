The spy thriller has a set of obligatory scenes that the genre demands — not optional flourishes but structural requirements. Every scene in this list must appear in some form; omitting any one of them leaves a recognizable gap.

## Obligatory Scenes

**1. The Briefing with the Hidden Agenda**
The protagonist receives the mission. The briefing appears straightforward; something about it is wrong. The handler's phrasing is slightly off. The stated objective doesn't quite match the political moment. The reader should sense the gap even if they cannot name it. This scene establishes the genre's governing tension: the protagonist is being deployed by an institution that is not being fully honest with them. Write it so that a second read, after the truth is revealed, confirms the tells were always there.

**2. The Tradecraft Scene (Competence Demonstration)**
Early in the book, show the protagonist doing their actual job with precision and skill. This is not an action scene — it is a procedure scene. A surveillance detection run. Servicing a dead drop. Making contact with an asset using recognition signals and exfiltration protocols. The scene exists to establish credibility: the reader must believe this person knows what they are doing before they will believe in the danger when things go wrong. Generic spy-speak is not tradecraft; specific, procedurally accurate detail is.

**3. The Asset-Handling Scene**
The protagonist meets and works with a human source. The asset is a real person with their own fears, motivations, and moral complexity. This scene must establish the asset's humanity — the reader needs to care about them as a person, not just as an operational resource. The professional requirement (maintain the asset's usefulness) should be in visible tension with the human response (this person is in danger because of me). The asset-handling relationship is where the genre's moral weight is most concentrated.

**4. The Identity Crisis or Burn**
Something happens that threatens or destroys the protagonist's cover, their operational position, or their institutional standing. They are blown, burned, or made. This is the structural reversal that forces the second-half action — the protagonist must operate without the resources and protections they relied on in the first half. The burn should feel earned, not arbitrary: the reader should be able to trace the chain of events that led to it.

**5. The Mole Revelation (or Equivalent Betrayal)**
The hidden enemy inside the protagonist's organization is identified. This revelation must reframe events the reader has already witnessed — on reflection, the mole's behavior in earlier scenes was consistent with their allegiance. The best revelations name someone the reader considered a plausible candidate but did not fully commit to. Shock alone is insufficient; the revelation must feel retrospectively inevitable. If there is no mole, there must be an equivalent institutional betrayal: the operation was a deception from the start, or the protagonist was the pawn, not the player.

**6. The Moral Compromise**
The protagonist must do something they find genuinely wrong in order to advance the mission or protect an asset. Not a tactical compromise but an ethical one — a decision that will cost them something internally and that the narrative does not let them off the hook for. They may deceive someone who trusted them. They may allow a death they could have prevented. They may execute an order they believe is unjust. The scene should have weight; the protagonist should feel it; the reader should feel it. This is not the moment for internal monologue that absolves the character. Let the wrongness sit.

**7. The Dark Night (Institutional Doubt)**
The protagonist reaches a point where the mission, the institution, and the personal cost are all in crisis simultaneously. They question not just whether they can complete the mission but whether the mission should be completed and whether the institution that assigned it deserves their loyalty. This is the emotional nadir. It should not be resolved by pep talk or convenient information — it should be resolved by the protagonist making a choice under genuine uncertainty.

**8. The Ending That Costs Something**
Spy thrillers do not end cleanly. The mission may succeed while the personal cost is irreversible — the asset is dead, the relationship is destroyed, the protagonist is more compromised than they were at the start. Or the mission fails but something true has been recovered. What the ending cannot do is return the protagonist to where they started. The intelligence world in this genre is corrosive; contact with it changes people and the ending must reflect that. Readers will accept an ambivalent ending; they will not forgive a cheap one.

## Structural Framework

**Act 1 (opening — 20%):** Establish the protagonist's world and professional competence. Deliver the briefing with its hidden agenda. Initiate the mission.

**Act 2 first half (20–50%):** Infiltration and tradecraft. Asset handling. Complications begin to accumulate. Establish candidate suspects for the betrayal.

**Midpoint (50%):** A major discovery that changes the nature of the mission — what appeared to be the objective is not the real objective, or the mole becomes a live suspicion rather than a background possibility.

**Act 2 second half (50–75%):** Complication cascade. The burn or identity crisis. Moral compromise. Institutional trust collapses.

**Act 3 (75–100%):** Mole revelation. The protagonist acts on incomplete intelligence. Resolution at cost. Ambivalent ending.
