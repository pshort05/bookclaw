These are the non-negotiable elements of the spy thriller. A book missing any of these is not a failed spy thriller — it is not a spy thriller at all.

- [ ] **Tradecraft that feels earned, not described.** Readers must see the protagonist performing specific espionage tradecraft with procedural authenticity: surveillance detection runs, dead drops, recognition signals, cover legend maintenance, asset communication protocols. Generic references to "spy stuff" do not satisfy. If you cannot describe the tradecraft specifically, research it until you can. Jason Matthews (a former CIA officer) set the standard in Red Sparrow — appendices on tradecraft are optional, but the operational specificity throughout is not.

- [ ] **A hidden agenda in the original briefing.** The mission the protagonist is sent on is not quite what it appears to be. The institution or the handler has a layer of purpose that the protagonist does not have access to at the start. This may be revealed gradually or all at once, but it must be there from the beginning, and it must reframe the events of the first act when it surfaces.

- [ ] **A planted betrayal that pays off.** The mole, the double agent, the institutional betrayal, or the handler's hidden agenda must be planted in the text before it is revealed. Plant the seeds of suspicion across multiple plausible candidates. The reveal should be retrospectively inevitable — readers who flip back should find the evidence was there.

- [ ] **Geopolitical stakes with personal consequences.** The mission matters at the geopolitical level — assassination, weapons transfer, intelligence compromise, diplomatic crisis. But the protagonist must also have personal stakes that are distinct from and in tension with the mission stakes. Both must be in play and both must be resolved (partially or fully) by the ending.

- [ ] **Moral compromise with real cost.** The protagonist must do something ethically wrong — not just tactically difficult — and live with the consequence. This is not optional in the genre. The spy thriller's argument is that this work costs the person doing it; a protagonist who acts without cost has not fully inhabited the genre.

- [ ] **An asset who becomes human.** The protagonist must run, interact with, or be responsible for a human source whose wellbeing is in genuine jeopardy. The reader must care about this person as a person, not just as an operational resource. The asset's fate must have weight in the resolution.

- [ ] **Institutional ambiguity — the agency is not simply good.** The intelligence service the protagonist works for must have its own agenda, its own institutional survival instincts, and its own capacity to sacrifice the individual for the program. It does not need to be villainous, but it cannot be straightforwardly trustworthy. This is the genre's foundational moral position.

- [ ] **International scope rendered with specificity.** The story must move across at least two countries, and the locations must be rendered through the operational eye — not tourist description but tradecraft geography. What does this city look like to someone running a surveillance detection route through it? Where are the embassy's security cameras? What is the social protocol of this contact environment?

- [ ] **An ending that costs the protagonist something irrecoverable.** The resolution must exact a real price. Relationships, beliefs, psychological wholeness, institutional standing — something must be spent that cannot be recovered. A spy thriller that ends with the protagonist restored to their pre-mission state has broken the genre's implicit contract with the reader.

- [ ] **Competence demonstrated before it is tested.** The reader must believe in the protagonist's professional competence before they will believe in the danger. Show the protagonist performing their craft successfully — at least one scene of tradecraft executed correctly — before the complications arrive. Competence under pressure reads differently from unexplained luck.
