Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do | Don't |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **DO** show the friendship first through specific, mundane shared moments. | **DON'T** just tell us "they're best friends" in narration. |
- | **DO** use the characters' deep knowledge of each other as foreplay and conflict. | **DON'T** have them act like strangers on a first date after getting together. |
- | **DO** make the "glitch" moment sensory and subtle (a look, a touch, a laugh). | **DON'T** use a contrived, over-the-top event (e.g., saving them from a burning building) as the inciting incident. |
- | **DO** let the friendship strain under the weight of unspoken feelings. | **DON'T** keep the friendship perfectly static until the confession. Show the cost. |
- | **DO** have the confidant call it out early—it's a genre staple. | **DON'T** have the protagonists be the last two people on Earth to realize it. |
- | **DO** make the first real kiss feel like a culmination, charged with history. | **DON'T** make the physical intimacy generic. It should reflect their unique bond. |
-    **Pitfall:** Instant attraction from page one.
-    **Fix:** Ensure the first chapter(s) are purely platonic. Build the friendship foundation first.
-    **Pitfall:** The friendship feels shallow or based only on convenience.
-    **Fix:** Give them a shared history (flashbacks, anecdotes), inside jokes, and demonstrate mutual support in a non-romantic context.
-    **Pitfall:** Lack of real conflict; they're just perfect for each other.
-    **Fix:** Amplify the *risk*. Make the friendship genuinely vital to both their lives. Show what they stand to lose.
-    **Pitfall:** The "get-together" scene is underwhelming.
-    **Fix:** The confession must be vulnerable and specific to *them*. It should reference their history and explicitly acknowledge the crossed line.
-    **Pitfall:** After they get together, all tension evaporates.
-    **Fix:** Explore the *new* tensions: adjusting to physical intimacy, defining the new relationship for friends/family, navigating conflict as lovers instead of friends.
- While not strictly necessary, dialogue can reflect shared social/professional backgrounds that cement the friendship.
-    **Working-Class Friends:** Dialogue may include shared workplace jargon, humor born of shared struggle, and direct, unpretentious speech. Confessions are likely blunt and heartfelt.
-    **Professional/Academic Friends:** Dialogue may feature intellectual sparring, shared professional shorthand, and a more measured, analytical style that breaks down during emotional moments.
-    **Friends from Different Backgrounds:** Their dialogue may highlight how they've influenced each other's speech patterns, adopting each other's phrases, showing the depth of their connection.
