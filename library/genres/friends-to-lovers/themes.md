## Central Themes

-    **Ease and Banter:** Early dialogue should flow effortlessly, filled with shared references, playful teasing, and comfortable silences. It should sound like two people who know each other better than anyone else.
-    **The Fracture in Familiarity:** As attraction grows, dialogue should show subtle disruptions: a joke that falls flat because one is distracted, a conversation that suddenly feels too intense, a pause that lingers a beat too long.
-    **Speaking the Unspoken:** The most critical dialogue moments are when the subtext of the romance almost, but not quite, becomes text. Use hedging ("I just... you mean a lot to me"), accidental admissions quickly covered by a joke, or loaded questions ("Have you ever thought about us?... Never mind.").
-    **The Confession:** The romantic confession must acknowledge the dual risk/reward: the threat to the friendship AND the promise of something more. It rarely involves flowery, generic romantic language. It's specific, vulnerable, and references their unique history (e.g., "I'm terrified of ruining what we have, but I'm more terrified of never knowing.").
