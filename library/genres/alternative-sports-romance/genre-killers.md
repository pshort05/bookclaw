# Alternative Sports Romance — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The broken promises, tonal betrayals, and authenticity failures that trigger DNF reactions and
one-star reviews in this genre.

- **Generic sport backdrop** — using "sports" as wallpaper rather than architecture. If the setting swapped from soccer to any other profession without changing a scene, the manuscript has failed the genre's core contract. Readers come for the specific world; give it to them.
- **Retirement as the price of love** — resolving the romantic conflict by having one lead quietly retire or walk away from their sport so the relationship can work. Unless the exit is explicitly framed as the character's own hard-won autonomous decision (not the love interest's ask), this reads as punishing ambition for daring to want both.
- **Stripping athletic agency** — the MMC/FMC solves the athlete's career problem from the outside: buys the club, speaks to the coach, pulls strings with the federation. The athlete must win their own fights.
- **The "simple misunderstanding" black moment** — an Act III rupture that a single honest conversation would resolve in under three minutes. The break must be rooted in a genuine collision of competitive identity with emotional need, not a withheld text or an overheard remark.
- **Incompetent athlete protagonist** — a lead who is supposed to be elite but performs like a hobbyist. Readers come to admire athletic excellence; a protagonist who loses every match, makes every beginner's mistake, and needs constant rescue from the love interest fails the competence promise of the genre.
- **Ignoring the physical cost** — presenting athletic life as glamorous with no acknowledgment of training exhaustion, injury risk, dietary discipline, or performance anxiety. The hard physical reality of elite sport is where the genre's emotional authenticity lives; sanitizing it reads as hollow.
- **Power imbalance mishandled in coach/athlete dynamics** — a coach-athlete romance is viable only if the athlete maintains full agency, the relationship is disclosed appropriately within the sport's governing structure, and the coach does not leverage position for access or compliance. Any coercive dynamic in this setup reads as exploitation, not romance.
- **The sport disappears after the midpoint** — front-loading athletic authenticity and then dropping competition entirely in the back half of the book as the romance takes over. The sport must remain present and consequential through the black moment and resolution.
