# Alternative Sports Romance — Comparable Titles

Touchstones for calibrating tone, pacing, and sport authenticity — covering the range of non-mainstream
disciplines and heat levels across the current market.

- **Kulti by Mariana Zapata (2015, soccer)** — the definitive slow-burn model for this genre: a female professional soccer player whose childhood idol becomes her team's coach. Zapata's signature is 400+ pages of earned intimacy before the first romantic breakthrough; demonstrates that the sport setting can carry a long build when the athletic world is rendered with enough specificity and affection to sustain reader investment on its own.
- **Cleat Cute by Meryl Wilsner (2023, soccer)** — sapphic rivals-to-lovers on the US Women's National Soccer Team during a World Cup campaign; an exemplar of how to embed the romance inside a team's competitive arc so that athletic stakes and emotional stakes rise in lockstep. The dual POV structure mirrors both the rivalry and the growing respect; demonstrates that queer rep and mainstream sports romance can occupy the same commercial space.
- **Deep End by Ali Hazelwood (2024, competitive swimming/diving)** — Hazelwood's STEM-romance sensibility applied to elite aquatics; an injured platform diver and a world-champion swimmer navigate recovery, rivalry, and forced-proximity in a high-performance training environment. Shows how injury vulnerability functions as the sport-specific analog to forced-proximity in workplace romance — competence admired across the pool deck is this genre's equivalent of the boardroom.
- **Waking Olivia by Elizabeth O'Roark (2016, track and field)** — college sports romance centering a troubled star runner and her demanding coach; an IBPA gold medal winner that demonstrates how athletic identity and emotional damage can be wound together so tightly that the sport itself becomes the therapy arc. Calibrate here for new-adult heat level and for depicting a coach/athlete dynamic with clear, earned resolution of the power tension.
- **Just This Once by Rosalind James (2012, rugby)** — Book 1 of the *Escape to New Zealand* series; introduced All Blacks rugby to the romance market and built a nine-book interconnected franchise on it, demonstrating that readers will follow a niche sport across an entire backlist if the team world is rendered with warmth and specificity. The international relocation element (American protagonist in New Zealand) is a workable template for cross-cultural sports settings.
- **Against the Ropes by Sarah Castille (2013, MMA)** — a traditional-publisher MMA entry from Sourcebooks Casablanca; benchmarks the combat-sport variant of the genre where the physical danger of the discipline raises the romantic stakes. Use for calibrating how to render the MMA/boxing training-gym subculture authentically while maintaining genre romance conventions, particularly the balance between the sport's physical aggression and the emotional vulnerability required for the romance arc.
- **Head Over Heels by Hannah Orenstein (2020, gymnastics)** — a former elite gymnast returns as a coach and navigates a forbidden romance inside a competitive gym; published by Simon & Schuster and positioned as rom-com. Demonstrates the second-chance-athlete structure, how to use a protagonist's past competitive failure as the primary internal wound, and how the coaching role creates the adhesion that keeps the leads in proximity.
- **Real by Katy Evans (2013, underground boxing)** — a *New York Times* and *USA Today* bestseller that set the commercial ceiling for high-heat combat-sports romance; a sports-rehab therapist and an underground boxing champion. Use for calibrating Level 4–5 heat within a sport setting, and for the tension between a male athlete's public physical dominance and private emotional need as the central romantic engine.
