# Alternative Sports Romance — Beats

The dominant structure runs two interlocked arcs — athletic competition and romantic development — so
that every milestone in one raises or lowers the stakes in the other; use *Romancing the Beat* (Gwen
Hayes) four-phase architecture as the spine, anchoring the phases to competition calendar beats
(qualifiers, season openers, championships, finals) rather than corporate timelines.

## Phase 1 — Setup (0–25%)
- **Establish the athlete (1–5%)** — ground the protagonist in their sport before the love interest
  appears: a training session, a qualifier heat, a first-day-of-camp scene. Show skill, obsession,
  and the cost of the sport on daily life.
- **Establish the sport's world (5–10%)** — introduce the specific community, culture, and stakes of
  this discipline (the team hierarchy, the governing body, the funding reality, the career window).
  This is the world the romance will live inside.
- **The collision (10–15%)** — the leads meet in a context rooted in the sport: rival lane draw,
  shared training facility, same team bus, a coach-athlete introduction. The chemistry ignites
  against a backdrop of athletic tension.
- **No Way #1 (15–20%)** — an explicit internal acknowledgment that entanglement is impossible:
  they're rivals for the same spot, a coach/athlete dynamic complicates things, or a competition
  schedule makes commitment irrational.
- **The adhesion (20–25%)** — the sport locks them together: co-captains on the same squad, forced
  to train under the same coach, assigned as climbing partners, paired doubles team, or injury
  recovery with only one physio on staff.

## Phase 2 — Falling (25–50%)
- **No Way #2 (25–30%)** — attraction is now undeniable but the competitive pressure intensifies;
  both deflect with athletic drive, banter, or performance rivalry.
- **The competence revelation (30–40%)** — one witnesses the other's mastery at close range and the
  admiration crosses into desire. This is the genre's equivalent of the competence scene: watch them
  do the sport at full capacity and feel something crack open.
- **Midpoint of love (~50%)** — a false high outside the competitive arena: a post-match celebration,
  a hotel room at a road tournament, a quiet moment on the mountain. They cross a physical or
  emotional line and believe they can contain it.

## Phase 3 — Retreating (50–75%)
- **Inkling of doubt (55–65%)** — the sport reasserts its claim: a key qualifier looms, a coach
  notices the distraction, a rival capitalizes on a focus lapse. One or both pulls back.
- **Deepening doubt (65–70%)** — an external athletic event (injury, selection announcement,
  sponsorship dispute, team conflict) is blamed on the relationship rather than examined honestly.
- **Retreat & shields up (70–75%)** — they revert to professional athlete mode: cold, focused, sport
  first. The emotional intimacy disappears behind athletic identity.
- **Black moment (75–80%)** — peak competition and romantic collapse arrive simultaneously. One
  performs to protect the other's shot at selection; or a public athletic failure is attributed to the
  relationship; or a competitive secret betrays trust.

## Phase 4 — Fighting (80–100%)
- **Dark night of the soul (80–85%)** — the competitive win (or loss) lands hollow without the
  partner. Athletic success without emotional connection fails to satisfy.
- **Wake up (85–90%)** — recognition that it was the internal wound (fear of vulnerability, fear of
  losing athletic identity to love) that caused the collapse, not the sport itself.
- **The grand gesture (90–95%)** — a public, sporting-context declaration: defending the partner on
  the field, forfeiting a competitive advantage, showing up at the final event.
- **HEA / HFN + epilogue (95–100%)** — both sport and relationship resolved; show the integration of
  athletic ambition and romantic partnership as coexisting rather than competing.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)
- **The athletic-competence showcase** — both leads demonstrate excellence in their sport early;
  readers must respect the athlete before rooting for the lover.
- **The sport-specific intimacy scene** — a moment only possible inside this discipline: taping each
  other's hands before a climb, strapping on soccer boots in an empty locker room, sharing a lane
  during an early-morning swim. The sport creates the closeness.
- **The injury or performance crisis** — athletic vulnerability forces emotional exposure; the other
  lead chooses to show up rather than capitalize on the weakness.
- **The "sport is enough" false resolution** — one or both convinces themselves that athletic
  achievement will fill the void; the lie must be shown clearly before it can be dismantled.
- **The sacrifice** — one willingly compromises a competitive outcome or career opportunity to
  protect, support, or choose the other person.
