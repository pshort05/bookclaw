Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do's | Don'ts |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **DO** root the horror in real-world science or plausible extrapolation. | **DON'T** rely on supernatural evil or ancient curses as the primary cause (unless blended carefully). |
- | **DO** personify the environment and give it agency. | **DON'T** make the threat a clearly defined monster with animalistic intelligence. |
- | **DO** use the setting as an active, constantly pressing force. | **DON'T** treat locations as passive backdrops or simple set pieces. |
- | **DO** create moral ambiguity; blur the line between victim and perpetrator. | **DON'T** create purely evil corporate villains or purely saintly eco-warriors. |
- | **DO** focus on the visceral, sensory experience of ecological decay/transformation. | **DON'T** explain the threat fully via technobabble or exposition dumps. |
- | **DO** end on a note that reflects a shift in the world's balance, not a return to status quo. | **DON'T** provide a neat victory where the threat is permanently destroyed. |
- Template:** [CHARACTER TYPE] reacting to [STAGE OF THREAT].
- 1. The Dismissal (Act I):**
- > **Scientist:** "The accelerated fungal growth is fascinating, but it's just a nutrient spike from the runoff. Isolate sample F7 and schedule a scan for Monday."
- > **Local:** "My granddad said not to build here. The soil remembers."
- 2. The Realization (Act II):**
- > **Scientist:** "It's not consuming cellulose... it's consuming the silica in the glass. It's learning. We have to burn everything."
- > **Corporate Rep:** "The evacuation order is a precaution. Media is calling it 'Kudzu 2.0.' We need a statement that protects the project."
- 3. The Desperation (Act II/III):**
- > **Protagonist:** "The radio's dead. The roads are gone. It's in the water supply. Do you understand? It's *in the water*."
- > **Any Character:** *(Whispering, interrupted by environmental sound)* "Did you hear that? No, not the wind. The sound *under* the—" *(Crunching, snapping sounds)*
- 4. The Acceptance/Elegy (Act III):**
- > **Survivor:** "It doesn't hate us. It just... doesn't need us. We were a season, and it's moving on."
- > **Final Log Entry:** "Day 24. The birds are returning. They sound different. I think I'll stay."
- | Pitfall | Fix |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **The Threat is Too Small/Contained** (e.g., one angry bear). | Reframe the threat as systemic. The bear is a symptom; the cause is the contaminated forest that drives *all* animals mad. |
- | **Preachy or One-Dimensional Morality.** | Give the "antagonistic" humans believable motivations (jobs, family security). Make the "good" characters flawed or partially responsible. |
- | **Slow, Boring Buildup.** | Ensure every scene has an **environmental pressure**. Even quiet scenes should be tense with unnatural sounds, smells, or subtle transformations. |
- | **The Threat is Defeated Too Easily.** | The solution should be costly, temporary, or come with a terrible trade-off (e.g., stopping the mutant plants requires salting the earth, making the land barren). |
- | **Characters Make Stupid Decisions.** | Base poor decisions on believable human traits: greed, denial, institutional loyalty, or desperation to save a loved one trapped in the danger zone. |
