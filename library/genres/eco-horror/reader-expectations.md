Eco-horror generates dread from ecological systems themselves — nature, environment, and the consequences of environmental collapse become the source and engine of horror.

## Tone & Mood

Creeping and inexorable, with an undercurrent of guilt and grief beneath the fear. Unlike supernatural horror, eco-horror implicates the reader in the threat — the monster is often a consequence of human action, which creates a specific moral unease. The tone should resist the comfort of clean evil: there is rarely a villain to defeat, only a system responding to what was done to it. Body horror is a natural register here — the ecological threat frequently manifests through decay, infestation, mutation, or the violation of bodily boundaries by natural forces. The mood should feel inevitable rather than arbitrary: the horror was always coming.

## Pacing

Slow build to visceral acceleration. The ecological threat should reveal itself gradually through anomalies — a bloom of fungi where there should be none, water that tastes of something wrong, animals behaving with uncharacteristic coordination. The first act establishes the environment and the protagonist's relationship to it. The middle act accelerates the anomalies while characters attempt rational explanations. The final act abandons rational containment and delivers the full horror. Corporate or governmental negligence revealed mid-story reframes the threat as man-made and deepens the dread.

## Setting Conventions

Isolated or semi-isolated environments are most effective: a research station in old-growth forest, a coastal fishing village where something is changing in the water, a farm at the edge of an industrial waste corridor, a rewilded estate where the introduced species are not behaving as expected. The setting must have a specific ecology that readers can track deteriorating — generic wilderness is less effective than a named, described ecosystem with particular flora, fauna, and seasonal behavior. Urban eco-horror works when the natural world intrudes on the built environment: roots cracking foundations, mold colonizing sealed buildings, insects appearing where they have no business being.

## Character Archetypes

The Scientist or Expert whose methodical skepticism gets stripped away one observation at a time — their capitulation to the irrational is the genre's central conversion experience. The Local Who Knows — a farmer, fisherman, or longtime resident — whose folk knowledge is dismissed and then vindicated. The Corporate or Institutional Representative whose presence establishes the chain of negligence. The True Believer — environmentalist, naturalist, or researcher — whose ideology is tested by encountering nature as genuinely hostile rather than simply injured. Protagonists who survive eco-horror typically do so by accepting loss rather than achieving victory.

## Length & Format

75,000–100,000 words. Third-person close or first-person; both work. First-person present tense is particularly effective at conveying the immediacy of encroaching threat. Ecological detail should be accurate and specific — readers knowledgeable about the real environment will catch errors, and specificity is what separates eco-horror from generic nature-menace.
