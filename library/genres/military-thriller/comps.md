The first tradition traces its roots to invasion narratives, a subgenre that gained prominence following George Chesney's 1871 novella The Battle of Dorking, which depicted a fictional German invasion of England and combined futuristic war drama with political commentary. These invasion stories flourished through the early twentieth century, providing readers with speculative scenarios of large-scale military conflict set against contemporary geopolitical tensions. The second tradition originated from spy fiction, which began to mature as a distinct genre in the years preceding World War I, focusing on espionage, intelligence operations, and covert activities conducted by state agents.[1][2]
