## Essential Checklist

- This guide is designed to act as a "Series Bible" or "Style Directive" entry in your Codex. It focuses on the specific tone, prose, and psychological beats required for the genre.
- Redemption: Not required to become "good," only to become "devoted."
- Conflicting Emotions: The narrative must constantly juggle the protagonist's struggle between logic (I should run) and desire (I want to stay).
- Dubious Consent (DubCon): Situations often involve coercion or blackmail initially. The shift from "No" to "Yes" must be psychologically motivated, not instant.
- No "Instant Fluff": Tenderness must be earned through trial/tribulation.
- The HEA is Mandatory: Even in Dark Romance, there must be a Happily Ever After (or Happy For Now), though it may be a "Twisted HEA" where they are happy together but isolated from normal society.
- Dark romance explores a concentrated set of psychological and emotional themes that distinguish it from lighter romance subgenres. Obsession and possession function as dominant themes, with protagonists frequently displaying unhealthy fixation on their love interests. Characters treat romantic partners as objects to control and protect, often justified through twisted logic of love. Trauma and redemption appear consistently, with characters bonding over shared dark experiences and attempting to heal one another despite their damage. Power imbalance and control constitute another central theme, portrayed through master-servant dynamics, kidnapping situations, or relationships between powerful and vulnerable characters. Forbidden love remains perpetual, whether involving class differences, enemies from rival families, step-relations, or other socially transgressive pairings. Moral ambiguity permeates the genre: protagonists refuse clear moral categorization, making choices readers must grapple with rather than judge easily.[18][34][17]
