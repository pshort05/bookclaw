Readers expect a romance built on genuine danger, power imbalance, and transgressive intensity — with full awareness that this is the specific experience they came for.

## Tone & Mood

Dark romance operates under an explicit genre contract: the reader has chosen a story where the love interest may be a genuine threat, power is distributed unequally, and the emotional stakes are extreme. This is not romance with a difficult patch; the darkness is structural and sustained. Readers expect the author to commit fully — hedging the love interest's menace, softening the power imbalance, or apologizing for the content all break the contract. The emotional register is possessive, obsessive, and heightened. Jealousy, control, and moral complexity on the part of the love interest are features. Genuine warmth eventually emerges, but from within the darkness rather than instead of it.

## Pacing

Pacing is intense and slow-building. The tension between threat and attraction is the engine; readers expect it to be sustained and escalated rather than resolved early. Scenes of power imbalance, confrontation, and near-consummation should be drawn out. The pacing pulls readers forward through wanting — resolution, safety, the dangerous thing the protagonist also wants. External plot, when present, should pressure the central dynamic rather than dilute it. Dark romance readers are patient with internal conflict and impatient with anything that pulls focus from the central pairing.

## Setting Conventions

Settings tend toward the enclosed and controlled: a compound, an estate, a criminal organization's territory, a ship, a campus. The geography often reflects the power dynamic — the love interest controls the space the protagonist inhabits. Contemporary settings dominate commercially, though dark historical and dark fantasy romance are both viable. The setting functions primarily as the container for the central relationship rather than as a world to be explored. Sensory detail should prioritize physical proximity, confined space, and the protagonist's acute awareness of the love interest's presence.

## Character Archetypes

The love interest is the genre's defining creation: dangerous, possessive, capable of harm, and eventually revealed to harbor something that makes the reader understand — if not excuse — the behavior. Common types include the crime lord, the morally corrupt billionaire, the cult leader, the captor, and the supernatural entity with alien values. The protagonist is typically not naive about the danger; she may resist, bargain, or be complicit in her own captivity, and her interiority carries the novel's emotional weight. The central arc moves toward the love interest's attachment becoming protective rather than merely possessive — the darkness does not disappear but gains a direction.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–120,000 words. Series are commercially dominant. Dual point-of-view — alternating between the protagonist and the love interest — is standard, giving readers direct access to the love interest's obsessive interiority. Explicit sexual content is the commercial norm; its absence should be clearly signaled. Cliffhangers between series installments are accepted. Trigger warnings in front matter are a genre convention that readers appreciate as navigation tools rather than caveats.
