## Rising Tropes

- Exaggeration operates as parody's most visible formal strategy, amplifying specific features of the original work to highlight absurdities or conventions the parody wishes to interrogate. In Cassandra Gannon's Not Another Vampire Book, exaggeration extends to the extreme, as the book presents itself as an editor's nightmare—a vampire romance so clichéd and poorly written that it becomes a parody of romance novel conventions themselves. The titular "bad" vampire book within the novel contains every romance trope imaginable presented with such enthusiasm and thoughtlessness that the tropes become visible as constructed devices rather than inevitable narrative elements.[17][18][19][16]
- Spoof occupies a position that can be lighter and more playful than parody, often focusing on "simple silliness" while targeting broader genre conventions rather than specific works. Scary Movie exemplifies spoof—it exaggerates and ridicules the conventions of horror movies generally rather than parodying one specific horror film. While Scary Movie may reference particular films like Scream and I Know What You Did Last Summer, its primary target remains horror film tropes and conventions writ large. This distinction proves crucial: spoofs can remain entertaining even for audiences unfamiliar with the specific films being referenced, provided they understand the broader genre being mocked.[6][8][14][16][4]
- Romantic comedy parody represents one of the most commercially active subgenres in contemporary parody fiction. These works simultaneously parody romance novel conventions (excessive emotional intensity, formulaic plot structures, hyperbolic passion) while delivering satisfying romantic narratives with happy endings. Cassandra Gannon's work exemplifies this subgenre's approach: Not Another Vampire Book functions as a comprehensive parody of paranormal romance clichés while also providing a genuine love story between the protagonist Karalynn and the morally complex antagonist Damien. The genius of paranormal parody romance lies in its ability to maintain romantic satisfaction while critiquing the very tropes that generate that satisfaction.[24][17][25][18]
- Horror film spoof, exemplified by the Scary Movie franchise, represents the most commercially successful contemporary spoof subgenre, with multiple installments achieving both critical recognition and substantial box office returns. These spoofs target horror film conventions—including the "final girl," the masked killer, supernatural curses, and various monster tropes—by exaggerating and absurdifying them to the point of ridiculousness. The upcoming Scary Movie 6 (2026) demonstrates continued audience appetite for horror parody, as it will target contemporary elevated horror films and recent franchises like Scream, Get Out, and Nope.[4][8][13][14][15]
