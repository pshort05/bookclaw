Comedy that targets a specific genre or work by exaggerating its conventions to absurdist extremes, requiring reader familiarity with the source material for the jokes to land.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is comic throughout, but effective parody operates on a precise register: the characters must treat the absurdity with complete seriousness. If the protagonist winks at the audience or acknowledges they are in a comedy, the tension that makes the jokes work collapses. The best parodies are played entirely straight — a vampire who obsesses over spreadsheet logistics, a chosen one who complains about the physical demands of prophecy. Readers expect the humor to arise from the collision between heightened genre conventions and mundane reality, not from characters commenting on how silly everything is.

## Pacing

Parody pacing mirrors the target genre's rhythm but with comic beats inserted at pressure points. A fantasy parody follows the same quest structure its source material would; a romance parody hits the same emotional beats. The jokes need breathing room — cramming gags into every sentence exhausts the reader. The rhythm should feel recognizable to fans of the source genre, with comedic subversions placed where readers expect the genre's most earnest moments. Escalation matters: the absurdity should build as the story progresses, not plateau in the first act.

## Setting Conventions

The setting is the target genre's world rendered with exaggerated fidelity and then punctured by realistic inconvenience or logic. If parodying epic fantasy, the setting has all the correct furniture — taverns, prophecies, maps with dramatic names — but the magic system has bureaucratic inefficiencies or the quest involves terrible logistics. The world's rules must be consistent within the parody; random chaos is less funny than rigidly consistent absurdity. Readers enjoy recognizing conventions and then watching them fail in plausible ways.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is typically a genre archetype who behaves exactly as the archetype demands — except that the real-world implications of that behavior become visible. The Chosen One is exhausted and under-resourced. The Hard-Boiled Detective refuses to accept obvious explanations on principle. The Love Interest exists as a genre function and periodically notices this. Supporting characters often represent other stock genre types, each played straight, so that the comedy emerges from their interactions rather than any single character being the butt of the joke.

## Length & Format

Parody novels run 60,000–90,000 words. Shorter is often stronger — the joke has a half-life, and overstaying degrades the effect. Chapter titles and epigraphs are frequently used as secondary joke delivery mechanisms. Series parodies work when each installment targets a different subgenre within a larger genre (e.g., a fantasy parody series that moves from epic quest to court intrigue to chosen-one academy). Standalone is the safer default unless the concept has clearly demonstrated series legs.
