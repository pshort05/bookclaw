A young protagonist must forge their identity and moral code while the world they were born into collapses or reveals itself as a lie.

## Tone & Mood

Readers expect a dual emotional register: the claustrophobic dread of a society that has failed, paired with the stubborn, often naive hope that defines adolescence. The tone should feel urgent but not nihilistic. Grief is present — for lost parents, lost normalcy, lost innocence — but it exists in tension with defiance. The emotional core is almost always indignation: the protagonist slowly realizes the world is wrong in a way that demands a response. Darkness must be earned, and hope must be hard-won rather than handed over. Readers in this genre are unusually alert to false comfort; they want genuine stakes and real costs. Unlike general apocalyptic fiction, the coming-of-age arc is not subordinate to the civilizational stakes — both must move in parallel, and the story is ultimately about the person the protagonist becomes as much as about the world they survive.

## Pacing

External threat drives pacing, but internal transformation must keep pace with it. Readers expect fast opening chapters that establish the broken world quickly, followed by a rhythm that alternates action sequences — raids, escapes, confrontations with the regime — with quieter scenes in which the protagonist processes what they are becoming. The coming-of-age arc requires breathing room: a chapter of pure tension followed by a chapter of character reckoning. Mid-book pacing often slows as the protagonist grapples with a moral dilemma that mirrors the world's corruption, forcing them to choose between what they were raised to believe and what they are learning to see. The climax should resolve both the external threat and the internal question of who the protagonist chooses to be.

## Setting Conventions

The setting must do double duty: it is both a physical world the protagonist must survive and a symbol of the failed adult order they are inheriting. Collapsed infrastructure, repurposed buildings, and rationed resources are standard. Underground spaces — tunnels, bunkers, hidden communities — carry special resonance as spaces of both refuge and imprisonment. Nature frequently reclaims or punishes. Readers expect the dystopian society, even if largely destroyed, to have a logic: a traceable history of how it failed, visible in architecture, propaganda remnants, and the generational trauma of adults who remember the before. The world should feel like a place with a past, not a game level. The contrast between what the protagonist's generation inherited and what adults lost gives the setting its emotional depth.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is typically sixteen to nineteen, female-coded or gender-diverse in contemporary market terms, and defined by a gap between what they believe at the opening and what they accept by the end. Key supporting archetypes: the Mentor Who Falls (an adult who prepares the protagonist and then dies or betrays them, forcing the protagonist to proceed without guidance), the Loyal Friend Who Pays a Price, the Ideological Antagonist (a true believer in the broken system, not merely a sadist), and the Love Interest whose presence must never eclipse the protagonist's self-discovery. Avoid reducing the antagonist to cruelty alone; the most resonant villains believe they are saving something.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–110,000 words. Series are the dominant commercial format; a standalone is viable but readers will expect a complete arc with no dangling threads. First-person present tense remains the dominant POV convention — it creates immediacy and places the reader inside the protagonist's evolving perception. Multiple first-person POVs (alternating chapters) are accepted in series books. Chapter lengths are typically short to moderate (1,500–3,000 words), maintaining narrative velocity even during quieter scenes.
