## Failure Modes

- Common Mistakes to Avoid – Authenticity failures, character pitfalls, pacing issues, and context errors
- This guide is specifically formatted for quick reference during the writing process, with practical examples, dos and don'ts, and actionable advice. It balances genre conventions with authenticity requirements, making it ideal for both new military romance writers and those looking to strengthen their craft in this specialized subgenre.
- Disciplined efficiency: Military characters speak with economy and precision; avoid excessive flowery language for military POV
- Best Practice: Integrate jargon naturally in dialogue; avoid acronym dumps or glossaries unless extensive.
- Weapons terminology: Use realistic weapons names (M4 carbine, Glock 19, etc.); avoid generic "guns" unless showing civilian ignorance; brief, functional descriptions work better than technical specifications.
- Don't exaggerate acronym usage; one per 500 words is typically sufficient
- Don't invent ranks or confuse military branches' rank structures
- Avoid "Roger that" or "Copy that"—just "Roger" or "Copy"
- Don't treat military personnel like civilian office workers with flexible scheduling
- Avoid Hollywood weapon misconceptions (magazine capacity, recoil, auditory ranges)
- Give heroes specific fears beyond typical "losing the heroine" (failure on mission, letting down team, unable to protect family)
- Avoid cardboard alpha; the strongest military heroes feel conflicted about their own protective instincts
- Avoid:
- Keep scenes relatively brief: Don't describe every movement like a technical manual
- Honor the realistic grief: Deployment separations genuinely hurt; don't minimize
- Soldiers still have personality: Don't make all military characters sound identical.
