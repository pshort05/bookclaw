Readers expect a procedural investigation where physical evidence drives the plot and scientific method functions as the primary mode of detection.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is clinical, precise, and coolly intelligent, with emotional weight delivered through what the evidence reveals rather than through overt sentiment. Readers want a protagonist who finds meaning in the objective truth that physical evidence provides — often as a counterpoint to institutional dysfunction, political pressure, or a justice system that fails to cooperate. There is room for dry wit and professional camaraderie, but the dominant register is focused and unsentimental. Horror, when it appears, is rooted in the specifics of what violence does to the body; readers in this genre are not squeamish, and sanitizing the forensic detail undermines credibility.

## Pacing

Pacing follows an investigative rhythm: discovery, analysis, interpretation, complication. Each forensic finding should open new questions rather than simply confirming existing theories, maintaining forward momentum through intellectual tension rather than action alone. Readers expect regular procedural scenes — laboratory work, autopsy suites, crime-scene reconstruction — balanced with confrontations: interviews with suspects, clashes with supervisors, or field sequences where the protagonist applies forensic insight in real time. The final act typically pivots on a single overlooked piece of evidence that reframes everything prior.

## Setting Conventions

The laboratory and the crime scene are the two poles of the genre's setting. Morgues, evidence rooms, and forensic suites must feel technically accurate and detailed; these spaces are characters in their own right. Exterior settings (cities, rural crime scenes, institutional corridors) exist to provide context and contrast for the forensic work. Readers in this genre pay close attention to procedural accuracy — sloppy science breaks immersion immediately. Jurisdictional friction (FBI versus local PD, civilian expert versus department authority) is a reliable and expected source of dramatic conflict.

## Character Archetypes

The Forensic Expert — pathologist, crime-scene analyst, DNA specialist, or trace evidence examiner — is typically the protagonist, defined by intellectual rigor and social friction with authority. The Skeptical Detective is a partner or rival who distrusts the science until it proves him wrong. The Institutional Obstacle (administrator, prosecutor, or police chief) prioritizes closure over truth. The Reluctant Witness has information readable in their physiology that they will not voluntarily provide. The second victim appears mid-story to escalate stakes and provide comparative forensic data that breaks the case.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–100,000 words. Standalones are viable; series built around a recurring forensic specialist are more common, since readers invest in the protagonist's expertise accumulating across cases. Chapters are short (1,500–3,000 words), with frequent POV returns to the crime-scene or lab to maintain procedural momentum. Technical detail should be accurate but digestible — one well-explained forensic mechanism per chapter is more effective than extended scientific exposition.
