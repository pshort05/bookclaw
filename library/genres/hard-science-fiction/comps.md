# Hard Science Fiction — Comparable Titles

Touchstones and *why* they work — a source for deriving obligatory scenes and calibrating
the rigor-versus-velocity balance. Fill with live comps during book planning's
market-analysis step.

- **Andy Weir, *The Martian*** — competence as the engaging trait; "Man vs. Physics"; airtight problem-solving within physical limits against a ticking clock. The gold standard for gamified technical puzzle-solving.
- **Kim Stanley Robinson, *Mars trilogy* / *The Ministry for the Future*** — cascading societal and climate effects traced rigorously; world-building that drives politics rather than decorating it.
- **James S. A. Corey, *The Expanse*** — physics-grounded space opera; drive mechanics, gravity, and resource depletion shape the geopolitics and the military action.
- **Neal Stephenson, *Seveneves*** — high technical density and intellectual depth; the model for "insider access" detail (keep the digressions immediately relevant to the threat).
- **Dan Brown, *Angels & Demons* / *The Da Vinci Code*** — the commercial chassis: short chapters, relentless cliffhangers, multi-threaded POV for dramatic irony, gamified specialist knowledge.
- **David Baldacci** (political thrillers) — the "Big Pop" opening, sustained ticking-clock pressure, and conspiracy mechanics that interlock with the central problem.
- **Genre hybrids** — James Rollins' *Sigma Force* and Steve Berry's *Cotton Malone* for blending hard-science "insider access" into thriller velocity.
- **Research touchstones** (for getting the tech right): Commonwealth Fusion / SPARC and HTS magnets for compact fusion; the SENS framework for genetic life extension; published AGI-safety and fault-tolerant-QC work for plausible 2040s capabilities.
