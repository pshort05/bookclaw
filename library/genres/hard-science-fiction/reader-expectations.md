Readers expect scientific plausibility as a load-bearing structural element, not a decorative backdrop — the science must be accurate or rigorously extrapolated, and the story's conflicts should emerge from it rather than despite it.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is intellectually serious and curious, committed to ideas as dramatic material in their own right. Readers in this genre actively enjoy lengthy passages of scientific reasoning and problem-solving; they do not need these to be disguised as something else. The dominant emotional register is one of wonder bounded by consequence — the universe is vast and indifferent, human ingenuity is remarkable, and the gap between those two facts is where the genre lives. Sentiment is not absent, but it is earned through the logic of the situation rather than imposed from outside. Irresponsible or technologically incoherent resolutions read as failures of craft, not just accuracy.

## Pacing

Pacing is typically problem-driven: a scientific or engineering challenge is established, the investigation and attempted solutions drive the middle, and the resolution turns on correct scientific reasoning under pressure. Readers tolerate and enjoy extended problem-solving sequences, technical digressions, and what other genres would consider excessive explanation — provided these are presented as the characters' active engagement with a genuine puzzle rather than authorial lecturing. Action sequences exist but are typically constrained by physical and biological reality (no sound in space, no instant acceleration, no magical medical recovery), which requires creative staging rather than genre-convention shortcuts.

## Setting Conventions

Setting must be physically consistent: orbital mechanics, atmospheric chemistry, radiation environments, distances, and time scales should be accurate enough that a scientifically literate reader finds nothing egregious. The physical environment is an active antagonist in this genre — vacuum, radiation, resource scarcity, communication lag, relativistic time dilation — rather than mere backdrop. Readers expect the consequences of setting to be taken seriously: communication delays proportional to light-speed distance, weight and atmosphere constraints, the actual lethality of space environments. Near-future Earth, the solar system, and interstellar space each carry distinct conventions that should be understood before writing in them.

## Character Archetypes

The Scientist-Protagonist defines herself through her relationship to her discipline: her personal stakes are inseparable from her intellectual commitments. The Engineer is pragmatic where the scientist is theoretical; the two in conflict or collaboration is a genre staple. The Mission Commander manages human factors (morale, resource allocation, political pressure from Earth) while depending on specialists for technical judgment. The Institutional Obstacle (government agency, corporation, funding body) imposes constraints that are not always wrong but are often poorly timed. The Non-Specialist POV character (a journalist, a politician, a family member) allows scientific concepts to be explained at reader level without condescension.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 90,000–130,000 words, with longer entries common in the tradition (400-page novels are not unusual). Standalones and series are equally viable. The genre has a strong culture of acknowledgments and author's notes explaining which elements are accurate and which are extrapolated — readers expect and appreciate this transparency. Infodump is a technical term of art in this genre rather than a pure insult; integrated exposition through dialogue and character observation is preferable, but readers will accept direct technical narrative if it is interesting enough.
