# Hard Science Fiction — Reader Expectations

Scientific rigor foregrounded and a world that obeys its own rules — every technology has
limits, every rule gets tested by the plot, and the science *is* the conflict. Unlike
mundane SF, hard SF may use FTL, aliens, AGI, or far-future tech — but only when
extrapolated rigorously, with consequences traced to the third order. Plausibility is the
contract; "because plot" breaks it.

## Tone & Mood
Intellectually charged and confident. Awe comes from understanding how a system works (and
why it fails). Readers want to feel smart while being entertained — "insider access" to
specialized knowledge (fusion physics, biogerontology, orbital mechanics), delivered as
puzzle-solving, never as a lecture. Often a thriller's urgency layered over an engineer's
precision.

## Pacing
Velocity is a plausibility defense: rapid forward motion keeps readers curious ("what
happens next?") rather than analytical ("would that really happen?"). A ticking clock —
literal (reactor meltdown in 48 hours) or figurative (a closing window) — drives the book.
Technical exposition arrives in urgent beats between scene cuts, never in stalled
info-dumps.

## Setting Conventions
A world built as a coherent system: a defined tech roadmap with dates, supply chains,
failure modes, and second-order social effects. The setting is a character with more
integrity than most of the cast — established rules constrain the plot and stay constrained.
Near-future (2030s–2040s tech convergence), hard space opera, or post-collapse all qualify
as long as the rigor holds.

## Character Archetypes
Competence-defined specialists — the engineer, the medical researcher, the systems analyst,
the knowledge-keeper — who solve problems through expertise and cleverness within the rules.
The antagonist is rarely a cartoon villain; more often a misguided technocrat acting on
flawed data or a scientifically unsound ideology. Avoid AI-default names (Sarah/Marcus Chen,
Dr. Chen, an all-"-a" female cast) — they read as un-revised and break credibility instantly.

## Length & Format
90k–110k words for a commercial hard-SF thriller — tight enough to sustain velocity, long
enough for the technical world-building. Multi-threaded third person across 2–4 specialists
(POV shifts create dramatic irony — the reader knows more than any single character). Short
chapters (3–7 pages), each ending on a hook. Resolves a standalone crisis while seeding a
larger systemic threat for the series.
