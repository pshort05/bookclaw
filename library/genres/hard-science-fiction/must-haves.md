# Hard Science Fiction — Must-Haves (non-negotiable checklist)

Skip these and it isn't really hard SF. Treat as a revision checklist.

- [ ] Internal coherence: the world obeys its own rules in chapter 22 exactly as it did in chapter 2 — no technology that quietly changes behavior for convenience.
- [ ] The "tested by plot" principle: every rule you establish is eventually stressed by the story, and it holds without a "because plot" exception.
- [ ] Scientific plausibility: core technologies are rooted in real physics, engineering, or biology — extrapolate aggressively, but show the math is done even if the reader never sees the spreadsheet.
- [ ] Specified limits and failure modes: every technology has constraints and a realistic way it breaks; the drama comes from exploiting those limits, not ignoring them.
- [ ] Timeline coherence: character ages, travel times, and tech-development arcs are mathematically consistent (a 2020-born character is 23 in 2043, not "30-something").
- [ ] Cascading consequence: major events produce shown second- and third-order effects on infrastructure, economy, society, and individual behavior.
- [ ] Competence-driven problem-solving: protagonists win through specialized knowledge and clues planted earlier, never luck or a rule-break.
- [ ] A "Big Pop" opening: chapter one delivers an immediate high-stakes technical crisis.
- [ ] Structural velocity: short chapters (3–7 pages) ending on hooks, with a ticking clock driving urgency throughout.
- [ ] Integrated exposition: science delivered via urgent dialogue, in-action thought, or immediate consequence — never a multi-paragraph info-dump.
- [ ] Sensory grounding: failing systems screech, spark, vibrate, and smell of ozone — abstract technical problems have concrete, felt consequences.
- [ ] Original character names: no AI-default tells (Sarah/Marcus Chen, Dr. Chen, a cast of "-a" endings) — they signal un-revised work to agents and readers.
