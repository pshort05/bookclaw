Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- Avoid: "He was as free as a bird," "The wind in the trees."
- Avoid "Star Trek" Tech: No teleporters, replicators, or shields. Technology is industrial, mechanical, and failing.
- Balance: Avoid relentless grimness. Counterbalance the harsh realities of ship life with small, humanizing moments like friendships, communal rituals, and found family dynamics.
- Show, Don't Tell: Demonstrate the realities of life in a closed system. Show how people eat, work, and manage waste. Illustrate how limited space, energy, and resources shape social status and create conflict.
- Narrative Rhythm: Alternate between intimate "slice of life" scenes, high-stakes crisis episodes (e.g., system failures, political revolts), and transitional passages that mark the passage of time.
- Long-Term Change: Avoid a static world. When the narrative jumps forward, show how the ship, its culture, and even its language have evolved. Long periods of normalcy are as important as crises for showing how life is truly lived.
- 10. Do & Don't Summary
- Avoid constant grimdark; balance hard realities with small, human joys (friendship, rituals, running jokes, found family).
- Short, punchy lines for emergencies, airlock failures, fights, or mutinies.
- Crisis episodes: hull breaches, system failures, political upheaval, discoveries.
- Avoid constant catastrophe. Let long stretches of relative normalcy show how people truly live in a sealed world.
- Avoid cheap nihilism; even in dark moments, preserve some thread of meaning or hope, whether in personal loyalty, stubborn curiosity, or quiet acts of care.
- The Skeleton in the Suit: Finding the corpse of an original crew member. Symbolizes the connection to the past and the failure of the original mission.
