Readers expect a contained civilization under existential pressure, where the ship itself is society, and the original mission has become myth competing with survival.

## Tone & Mood

The dominant tone is claustrophobic and introspective, shadowed by inherited obligation and institutional decay. This is a genre about what happens to human societies when they are stripped of external context — no horizon, no seasons, no outside — and compressed into a closed system for generations. Readers expect a mood that oscillates between the quietly tragic (people living and dying without ever seeing what the journey is for) and the occasionally transcendent (finding meaning inside radical constraint). Paranoia and collective delusion are genre-appropriate and expected; so is genuine community and solidarity forged by shared isolation.

## Pacing

Pacing tends toward the deliberate rather than the propulsive. The genre's central conflicts are systemic and social — resource allocation, institutional corruption, the erosion of purpose — rather than episodic action. Readers expect slow revelations: the discovery that the mission parameters have been distorted, that the original logs have been altered, that the destination may not exist or may not be what the founders promised. Crisis sequences (mechanical failure, disease outbreak, faction violence, resource collapse) punctuate the slower social narrative and should feel like the consequences of accumulated institutional failure rather than external intrusions.

## Setting Conventions

The ship is the world, and it must be rendered as a complete society: distinct decks or zones with different class connotations, maintenance corridors that only the technically initiated can navigate, agricultural bays, recycling systems, archives of a planet no living person has seen. Readers expect the ship's architecture to be meaningful — spatial geography should reflect social hierarchy and historical strata. The original founders' design choices should be legible in the current society's distortions. Characters who have never seen a sky, ocean, or horizon perceive reality differently; their metaphors and reference points should reflect the ship's sensory world rather than Earth's.

## Character Archetypes

The Archivist or Historian preserves records of the original mission that the institution would prefer to suppress. The Engineer or Maintenance Worker has an intimate relationship with the ship's physical systems that grants a kind of authority the social hierarchy does not officially recognize. The True Believer maintains absolute faith in the mission's purpose, often violently. The Reformer attempts to adapt mission parameters to the reality of the current generation's needs. The Outsider-Within (someone born in a marginal zone, outcast, or with unusual access) sees the social structure from angles that native insiders cannot.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 90,000–120,000 words. The genre supports both standalones and series; series typically track the ship across multiple generations rather than a single protagonist's arc, which requires careful continuity management. Multiple POV characters drawn from different ship strata are common and useful for conveying the full social picture. Time-jump structures — moving forward decades or centuries between sections — are a genre convention that readers accept and expect.
