Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do | Don't |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **DO** fuse romantic and thriller plot beats. | **DON'T** have the romance and thriller exist in separate plot silos. |
- | **DO** let characters make questionable choices driven by desire/fear. | **DON'T** protect your protagonist from their own bad decisions. |
- | **DO** use setting to reflect psychological states (a gilded cage, a storm). | **DON'T** use generic, inert settings. |
- | **DO** write dialogue where power shifts and subtext reigns. | **DON'T** use dialogue for simple exposition or pure niceties. |
- | **DO** balance lyrical, sensory prose with sharp, punchy action beats. | **DON'T** maintain a single, monotonous prose style throughout. |
- | **DO** make the physical intimacy a continuation of the power dynamic. | **DON'T** write love scenes that feel disconnected from the characters' fraught dynamic. |
- Template:** Provocation/Question -> Evasion/Retort -> Revelation/Physical Escalation
- Example 1 (Power Play):**
- > "You're afraid of me." (Provocation, stating a claim of power)
- > "I'm afraid of what happens when I stop being afraid of you." (Evasion/Retort, flips the power dynamic, reveals her own dangerous potential)
- > He went very still, his eyes darkening. "Now that is a threat I'd like to see you make good on." (Physical/verbal escalation, acknowledges the shift, raises the stakes)
- Example 2 (Seductive Threat):**
- > "Tell me where the files are, and this can be over." (Question wrapped in a false promise)
- > "And what if I don't want it to be over?" (Evasion, introduces desire into the danger)
- > His smile was a razor's edge. "Then, my dear, we move to the more persuasive part of the evening." (Revelation of intent, fusion of threat and seduction)
-    **Pitfall:** The "Stalker-to-Lover" pipeline without accountability.
-    **Fix:** Ensure the morally ambiguous love interest demonstrates genuine growth, sacrifice, or is forced to confront their actions. The protagonist must not simply be a passive victim who "fixes" them.
-    **Pitfall:** The thriller plot stalling for romance, or vice-versa.
-    **Fix:** Use the romance to complicate the thriller (e.g., hiding evidence for the love interest). Use thriller beats to force romantic intimacy (e.g., sharing a hiding place).
-    **Pitfall:** Weak external threat that doesn't justify the extreme romance.
-    **Fix:** Make the antagonist a dark mirror or direct result of the central theme (obsession, corruption). Their threat should challenge the core of the protagonists' bond.
-    **Pitfall:** Telling, not showing, the dark attraction.
-    **Fix:** Instead of "She was drawn to his darkness," show her noticing the calm precision of his violent acts, or feeling a thrill when his protective instincts turn possessive.
