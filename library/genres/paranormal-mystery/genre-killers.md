## Failure Modes

- Upper left (Color Light Red): Low Demand, High Saturation; Label as ‘Avoid These Opportunities’
- The contemporary paranormal mystery audience is significantly influenced by cozy mystery traditions, with most paranormal mystery readers expressing preference for narratives that avoid graphic violence, explicit sexual content, and traumatic psychological content. The cozy mystery context has elevated reader expectations for close-knit community settings, sympathetic protagonists, quirky supporting characters, and resolutions that preserve community bonds even when murders occur. Paranormal mystery readers increasingly expect diverse representation in characters and cultural settings, moving beyond the traditional English village or American small-town conventions that dominated earlier mystery fiction.[13][14][15][16][6][17]
- The Liminal Space Between Worlds: Paranormal mysteries frequently employ the theme of existing in liminal spaces—between living and dead, between magical and mundane, between insider and outsider status. Settings like haunted houses, magical small towns hidden from ordinary perception, or paranormal investigation agencies that operate in shadow all emphasize this liminal positioning. The theme creates narrative tension by placing protagonists in territories where established rules don't apply and where investigation must account for multiple realities operating simultaneously.[10][15]
- The upper-left quadrant ("Avoid These Opportunities") contains traditional hardboiled paranormal mysteries and pure occult horror, indicating low current reader demand combined with existing supply—suggesting these categories show declining market viability and represent poor opportunities for new author entry.
- Limitations: Define what the protagonist cannot do. If they can talk to ghosts, why can't the victim just name the killer? (e.g., The ghost is confused, stuck in a loop, or speaks in riddles).
- Avoid:
- The Twist: A reveal that changes the nature of the threat (e.g., the "ghost" is actually a psychic projection, or the "human" killer is possessed).
- The Climax: Confronting the killer requires both wit/deduction and a supernatural binding/banishing.
- One-Sided Conversations: If the protagonist sees ghosts but others don't, include the awkwardness of them talking to thin air.
- System Instruction: Add a constraint: "Do not allow magic/psychic powers to instantly reveal the killer's name. Clues must be earned. Maintain the structure of a mystery novel."
- The protagonist knows who the killer is via supernatural means but cannot tell the police because they will be institutionalized.
- Concept: Legal justice puts the killer in jail; spiritual closure allows the ghost to move on ("cross over").
- The Point: Sometimes you can't have both. The detective may have to let the killer go to save the ghost's soul, or vice versa.
