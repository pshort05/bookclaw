# Dark Romantasy Reader Expectations

Dark romantasy places romantic tension and genuine fantasy threat in equal balance — neither element is decoration for the other, and both must escalate toward a resolution that costs the protagonist something real.

## Tone & Mood

Tone is dark, morally complex, and lush; the world is dangerous and beautiful in the same breath. The romantic element carries real heat and genuine emotional risk, not comfortable fantasy fulfilment. The fantasy element carries genuine menace — power imbalances are not metaphors, they are structural conditions the protagonist must navigate. Moral greyness is expected and desired: the love interest should have done genuinely terrible things, and readers should be conflicted about wanting the relationship to succeed.

## Pacing

Pacing runs two engines simultaneously: the romantic pull between protagonist and love interest, and the fantasy threat that endangers both of them or that one of them embodies. Both must escalate together. A chapter that advances the romance without advancing the threat, or vice versa, loses tension. The middle act must tighten both screws; the climax must resolve or transform both.

## Setting

Secondary worlds with visible corruption and entrenched power imbalances are the genre's native environment: fae courts, dark kingdoms, underworlds, empires built on systematic cruelty. The setting should be immersive and specific — readers expect world-building detail. The power structure of the setting must matter to the plot; it cannot be backdrop.

## Archetypes

The morally grey love interest holds genuine, dangerous power and has used it in ways the protagonist cannot simply excuse. The protagonist chooses dangerously — she is not rescued; she makes an active choice that exposes her to the love interest's world, often knowing the risk. The court or dark society provides the social framework within which the romance is either forbidden, politically consequential, or structurally coerced.

## Length & Format

90,000–120,000 words is the commercial range; some titles run longer. First-person narration from the female protagonist is predominant. Sensual tension is high across the genre; heat levels vary from closed-door to explicit. Series are the norm — standalone dark romantasy is rare. Maps, glossaries, and chapter epigraphs are common paratextual features.
