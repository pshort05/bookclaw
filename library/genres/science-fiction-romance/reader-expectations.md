Romance set in a science fiction world — space opera, near-future Earth, alien contact, or far-future civilization — where the SF setting is central to both the conflict and the relationship, and a happily-ever-after or happy-for-now is required.

## Tone & Mood

The tone blends romantic warmth with SF wonder and physical danger. Readers want the emotional intensity of a romance novel and the immersive thrill of a science fiction setting; neither is decoration for the other. The SF world must generate genuine stakes — isolation, cultural difference, survival pressure, species conflict — that make the romance feel hard-won. Optimism about human (and alien) connection is the dominant register even in dark or action-heavy stories. Humor has a strong presence; the fish-out-of-water potential of cross-cultural or cross-species romance is frequently mined. The HEA/HFN is non-negotiable: a dark or ambiguous ending on the relationship reads as a genre violation.

## Pacing

Pacing matches the romance structure but is driven forward by SF plot events. Forced proximity — on a ship, in a survival situation, on an alien world — accelerates the emotional connection. The SF worldbuilding must emerge through action and character interaction rather than exposition; readers tolerate far less info-dumping in SFR than in mainstream SF because the romance momentum takes priority. The middle of the book must develop both the relationship arc and the SF plot simultaneously. The black moment typically involves both a relationship rupture and a SF-world crisis, resolved together at the climax.

## Setting Conventions

The SF setting must be internally consistent and specific enough to feel real, but worldbuilding must not pause the story. Technology is shown functioning in scenes rather than explained in narration. Space opera — starships, alien planets, galactic civilizations — is dominant. Near-future Earth, colony worlds, generation ships, and alien contact scenarios are all common. Alien species require genuine otherness, not humans with cosmetic differences, while remaining emotionally comprehensible.

## Character Archetypes

The leads must be individually capable — SFR does not permit the passive heroine. Both characters should have expertise and agency in the SF setting: pilots, engineers, scientists, warriors, diplomats, or traders. Emotional vulnerability is the required counterweight to physical or professional competence; characters who are capable in every external way but closed-off emotionally are the standard setup. Alien heroes with human-incompatible cultures are a beloved character type, as are alien heroines. The romance must establish genuine interdependence — each character needs what the other provides — without collapsing either into dependency.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 75,000–100,000 words. Third-person limited is dominant, though first-person has grown. Dual-POV is strongly preferred. Series are very common — a shared universe or starship crew generates multiple books. Standalone SFR requires a complete SF-world story alongside the complete romance. Heat level is most commonly sensual to explicit. Chapter lengths run short to medium (1,500–3,000 words). Alien language and social structures should be introduced gradually, anchored in sensory or emotional experience rather than technical summary.
