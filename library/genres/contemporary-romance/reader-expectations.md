# Contemporary Romance — Reader Expectations

A central love story between two people, and an emotionally satisfying, optimistic
ending — HEA (happily ever after) or HFN (happy for now). The relationship is the spine
of the book, not a subplot; everything else serves it. No fantasy or magic, and a
present-day setting — modern jobs, phones, apps, and the texture of contemporary life.

## Tone & Mood
Emotionally immersive and ultimately hopeful. Readers came for the relationship, so the
register lives in yearning, warmth, banter, and the ache of wanting someone you can't yet
have. Angst is welcome but earned; the contract promises the reader will feel safe that
love wins in the end. Match the heat promise (sweet ↔ explicit) to the cover and blurb.

## Pacing
Driven by relationship escalation, not external plot. The love interest must appear by
~10% or engagement collapses; attraction is obvious by ~30%. At least one relationship
beat per chapter — every scene advances romantic tension or emotional intimacy. Both leads
get roughly equal page time and development. Use *engineered proximity* (a contract, a job,
forced cohabitation, a bet) to keep the leads colliding; random chance meetings read as
dated.

## Setting Conventions
The present-day real world — a workplace, a small town, a big city, a coffee shop, a
shared apartment building, a snowed-in cabin. Place is a felt, sensory character that
shapes the romance, never inert backdrop. No magic system (that's romantasy); a historical
period setting is its own genre (historical romance) — this is the modern day.

## Character Archetypes
Two leads readers actively *like* and want together: relatable flaws plus appealing
traits, never annoying or deal-breaking. Both have agency and solve their own problems —
no passive damsels, no controlling "alpha" who removes the other's choices. Memorable
quirks beat generic perfection. Common supporting roles: the meddling best friend, the
ex, the rival, the disapproving family, the matchmaker.

## Length & Format
~60k words for category romance, 80k+ for single-title; first kiss lands roughly by the
midpoint (chapter 8–12 of ~20–25). Third person, dual POV is the workhorse (give the
reader inside both heads); first-person single or dual also common. Adult or upper-YA.
Heat level set early and held consistent throughout. Standalone with a closed arc, or
series where each book closes its own couple's arc.
