Readers expect a love story set in the recognisable present day, where two people navigate real-world obstacles — emotional, professional, or logistical — on the way to a guaranteed emotionally satisfying ending.

## Tone & Mood

Contemporary romance spans a wide tonal range — from sharp-tongued banter comedy to emotionally raw women's fiction — but the baseline expectation is warmth, emotional honesty, and optimism about love. The reader must believe the protagonists deserve happiness and that the relationship, once secured, will last. Humor is common and welcomed; even emotionally heavy books typically have moments of levity. The tone should never undercut the sincerity of the emotional stakes. Cynicism is a character flaw to be worked through, not a narrative stance.

## Pacing

Contemporary romance moves in waves: attraction, complication, deepening, crisis, resolution. The romantic tension should be established early (within the first few chapters) and maintained through the obligatory dark moment — the point where the relationship appears to fall apart — before the earned resolution. Subplots (professional conflict, family dynamics, friendship) run alongside the romance but must not displace it. The reader's primary question at every chapter break should concern the relationship, not the subplot.

## Setting Conventions

The setting is contemporary and recognisable: cities, small towns, workplaces, or specific milieu communities (sports teams, publishing, food industry, academia). No paranormal, historical, or speculative elements — this is the genre baseline that distinguishes it from paranormal romance, historical romance, or romantasy. Setting should feel lived-in and specific; vague or interchangeable locations dilute reader investment. The cultural moment matters — social media, work culture, and contemporary anxieties about relationships are frequently present and should feel accurate, not dated.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is typically a woman navigating competing demands (career, family, self-worth) who holds a wound or belief about herself that the relationship will ultimately challenge. Common tropes: enemies-to-lovers, forced proximity, second-chance romance, grumpy/sunshine contrast, friends-to-lovers. The love interest must be a full character, not a fantasy object — readers need to understand why this specific person unlocks growth in the protagonist. A cast of supportive secondary characters (best friend, sibling, colleague) grounds the romance in community.

## Length & Format

75,000–100,000 words for a standalone; series installments often run 70,000–90,000 words. Dual point-of-view (alternating chapters between both protagonists) has become the genre standard over the past decade. Heat level varies from sweet/closed-door to explicit; the market expectation should be matched. An epilogue — showing the couple's future — is strongly expected by readers and its absence is frequently noted in reviews.
