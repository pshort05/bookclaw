Readers expect warmth, wonder, and human connection in speculative settings where the universe is strange but fundamentally safe.

## Tone & Mood

The prevailing tone is optimistic and unhurried. Science fiction elements exist to enable human stories, not to threaten them. Readers expect a sense of wonder that is soft-edged rather than awe-inspiring — the alien planet is beautiful, the AI is a little eccentric, the space station feels like a village. Humor is gentle and character-driven. Emotional warmth is the genre's primary currency; readers pick up a cozy SF novel to feel better, not to feel afraid. Melancholy is permitted as a seasoning — a character's loneliness, a lost homeworld, a grief not yet processed — but dread, body horror, and existential threat undercut the contract. The narrator's voice is typically intimate and inviting, often with a confessional or journal-like quality.

## Pacing

Pacing is deliberate and scene-oriented. Readers are not here for plot momentum; they are here for texture and relationship. Chapters are organized around small events: a delivery run goes sideways, a neighbor needs help, something unexpected turns up in the cargo hold. Macro conflict exists but unfolds slowly and is rarely violent. Climaxes are emotional rather than action-driven. Readers expect breathing room — scenes of domestic comfort, shared meals, idle conversations — and reward authors who resist the urge to accelerate toward external threat.

## Setting Conventions

Settings are almost always community-sized: a generation ship, a remote research outpost, a small trade station, a colony town, a single strange planet with a handful of inhabitants. Speculative elements are integrated into daily life rather than foregrounded as marvels — the sentient houseplant is just a houseplant, the FTL drive is just how you get to work. Technology tends to be benign or at least manageable. Dystopian mega-corporations, surveillance states, and ecological collapse are absent or background noise. The setting often functions as a character, with geography and community infrastructure given careful, affectionate detail.

## Character Archetypes

Found family is the defining social structure. Characters tend to be competent in their domains but emotionally uncertain — good at their jobs and bad at asking for help. Common types include the burned-out expert seeking a quieter life, the accidental community anchor everyone relies on, the odd-one-out who gradually belongs, and the ancient or alien presence (AI, elder, long-lived species) who provides gentle wisdom without condescension. Antagonists, when present, are usually misguided rather than malevolent. Villains who threaten physical harm to the cast are rare and feel like tonal violations.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 70,000–100,000 words. Shorter works — novellas in the 30,000–50,000 word range — are commercially accepted and common. Series structures built around a recurring cast and setting, rather than a single escalating arc, are the norm. Episodic chapter structure serves the genre well. First-person and close third-person narration dominate; omniscient narration is uncommon. Prose is accessible rather than literary; description is moderate in density, emphasizing sensory comfort over technical world-building detail.
