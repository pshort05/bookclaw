A thriller in which the protagonist discovers or suspects that reality is a constructed simulation, generating paranoia, existential dread, and the horror of a universe without inherent meaning.

## Tone & Mood

The dominant emotional register is paranoid unease that escalates into full existential terror. Readers expect a creeping sense that the rules of the world cannot be trusted — small inconsistencies accumulate until the protagonist can no longer distinguish genuine perception from manufactured experience. Dread should feel philosophical as well as visceral: not just "someone is watching" but "nothing I do matters." Moments of apparent relief are suspect; the genre trains readers to distrust resolution. A cold, clinical atmosphere often contrasts with the protagonist's increasingly frantic internal state, reinforcing isolation. Dark humor is occasionally present but never defuses the underlying horror.

## Pacing

Pacing mirrors the protagonist's psychological deterioration. The opening establishes a normal world with subtle wrongness — glitches, repeated events, scripted-feeling people. The middle escalates through revelations that each feel like the final truth but open into deeper uncertainty. Tension builds through constraint rather than action: the walls close in, options narrow, and the horror of inaction can outweigh the horror of any particular threat. Liminal or underground sequences — tunnels, server rooms, featureless corridors — slow the pace to create claustrophobic pressure. The climax rarely delivers clean resolution; the most effective examples end in ambiguity or recursive doubt.

## Setting Conventions

Urban contemporary settings are standard, chosen to make the simulation's cracks more visible against a familiar backdrop. Readers expect: repeated environments (the same alley twice, a room that has changed without explanation), boundaries the protagonist cannot cross, and layered spaces suggesting architecture hidden beneath architecture. Digital and technological motifs permeate the world even when the setting predates the internet. The "observer effect" is a recurring device: the world behaves differently when watched, and the protagonist's attention itself seems to alter events. Settings often feel slightly too perfect or too empty, populated only when needed.

## Character Archetypes

The **Doubting Everyman** is the core protagonist — not a specialist but an ordinary person whose specific perception has made them notice what others ignore. Their credibility is perpetually in question, including to themselves. The **Architect or Administrator** antagonist may be malevolent, indifferent, or simply doing a job — the last is often most disturbing. **NPCs** (in spirit if not in name) populate the edges: background figures who behave strangely when examined, testing moral boundaries when they seem to lack genuine consciousness. A **Believer** who has accepted the simulation and adapted serves as foil, representing the road the protagonist fears taking.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 70,000–100,000 words. Shorter works are common; the genre's claustrophobia suits tight, compressed narratives. First-person or close third-person point of view is strongly preferred, keeping the reader inside the protagonist's unreliable perception. Multiple timelines or nested narrative frames are acceptable when they serve the paranoia rather than obscure it as a stylistic exercise.
