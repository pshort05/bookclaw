**1.3 Enter the Simulation Hypothesis**

Nick Bostrom's 2003 simulation argument brought a new level of philosophical and popular visibility to the idea that we might inhabit a computer simulation. He proposes a trilemma: either civilizations go extinct before reaching "posthuman" simulation capability, or they choose not to create ancestor‑simulations, or we are almost certainly living in a simulation because such simulations would vastly outnumber base‑level realities.[^[\[26\]]^](#fn26)[^[\[1\]]^](#fn1)[^[\[27\]]^](#fn27)

This argument migrated quickly into media and public discourse, reinforced by *The Matrix* (1999), which dramatized humanity unknowingly living in a machine‑generated virtual reality. Popular treatments of simulation theory, from non‑fiction to think pieces and podcasts, have since reframed older SF motifs in explicitly ontological terms.[^[\[28\]]^](#fn28)[^[\[18\]]^](#fn18)[^[\[1\]]^](#fn1)[^[\[22\]]^](#fn22)[^[\[29\]]^](#fn29)

In fiction, Greg Egan's *Permutation City* (1994) and later novels like *Diaspora* and *Schild's Ladder* dramatize simulated consciousnesses, artificial universes, and the mathematical nature of reality, arguing that a self‑consistent computational universe is as "real" as any physical one.
