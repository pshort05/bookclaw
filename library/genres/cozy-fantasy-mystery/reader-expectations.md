Readers expect a puzzle to solve in a warm, magically rendered world, where the detective work is intellectually satisfying and the community around the sleuth is as important as the crime itself — low graphic violence, high charm.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is warm, curious, and gently suspenseful. The mystery creates forward momentum — who did it, and why — but never generates genuine dread or graphic horror. Death and wrongdoing exist in this world, but they are handled with the bloodless restraint of classic cozy mystery: a victim is found, a community is disturbed, an amateur investigator steps in. The fantasy layer adds whimsy and wonder without darkening the atmosphere. What separates cozy fantasy mystery from standard fantasy with a detective element is the sustained warmth: the community is trustworthy, the protagonist has allies, and the world, though troubled by the crime, is fundamentally benign.

## Pacing

Pacing follows the cozy mystery rhythm: discovery of the crime, investigation through community interaction, the accumulation of clues and suspects, a misdirection or two, and a satisfying resolution. The investigation advances through conversation and observation rather than action or violence. Meals, market days, and social gatherings are not filler — they are where clues are dropped and character relationships are deepened. Unlike standard fantasy, there is no B-plot involving war, prophecy, or world threat; the mystery is the whole narrative.

## Setting Conventions

The setting is a contained magical community: a guild town, a witch's village, a magical academy, a trading port where several fantastical species coexist. The scope is deliberately small so every suspect is plausibly known to the protagonist. Magic is woven into daily life in ways that affect how clues are discovered and how the crime was committed — enchanted objects, spells that leave traces, magical witnesses. The world should feel specific and lived-in without requiring the reader to absorb extensive lore before the mystery begins.

## Character Archetypes

The Amateur Magical Sleuth: a protagonist whose everyday magical occupation (potion-maker, librarian, hedge witch, cartographer) gives them access to information and locations a professional investigator would not have. The Skeptical Official: a guard captain, magistrate, or investigator who initially dismisses the protagonist but comes to rely on their insight. The Loyal Magical Companion: a familiar, a magical being, or a close friend who helps reason through clues and provides emotional support. The Tight-Knit Suspects Community: neighbors, colleagues, and rivals who all had motive — and all of whom the protagonist is personally connected to, raising the emotional stakes.

## Length & Format

65,000–85,000 words for a standalone entry; series are the dominant format. Each installment should feature a self-contained mystery with a full resolution, while the community relationships and any overarching series arc develop across books. Close first-person or close third-person point-of-view sustains the intimate detective perspective. A cast list or glossary of magical terms is helpful in series entries but should not be necessary for following the mystery.
