Action fiction delivers relentless physical conflict driven by a competent protagonist facing clear, escalating stakes with no room to stop moving.

## Tone & Mood

Urgent, propulsive, and confident. The prose should feel kinetic — readers are not here for ambiguity or introspection; they want to feel the heat of the chase, the crack of the fight, and the certainty that something is always on the line. The tone stays lean and controlled even in high chaos. Avoid melodrama. The protagonist may be grim or wry, but never whiny. Danger must feel real: injuries have consequences, plans go wrong, and victories cost something.

## Pacing

Fast and unrelenting. Chapters are short. Action sequences use short sentences and tight paragraphs to control tempo. Quiet scenes — exposition, character bonding, tactical planning — exist only to reload tension before the next escalation. Every scene must push forward: either the threat grows, the protagonist's position worsens, or a new complication arrives. The midpoint raises the stakes sharply, and the climax demands the highest physical and psychological output from the protagonist.

## Setting Conventions

Settings are chosen for conflict potential: confined spaces that limit escape, hostile environments that add physical pressure, urban terrain that forces improvisation. The geography matters — readers want to understand the layout so the action is legible. Locations shift frequently. Iconic set-pieces (the warehouse, the highway, the rooftop, the facility under lockdown) are expected, but must be deployed with fresh tactical logic. The world outside the immediate conflict is kept spare; detailed world-building is not the point.

## Character Archetypes

The Competent Protagonist: skilled, resourceful, decisive. May be military, law enforcement, a specialist, or a trained civilian — the specific background matters less than the demonstrated ability to solve problems under fire. The Antagonist must be a genuine threat, ideally with a coherent plan and comparable competence. Supporting characters serve a function: the ally who covers the protagonist's blind spot, the handler who controls mission scope, the obstacle who complicates extraction. Avoid sidekicks who exist only to admire the hero.

## Length & Format

60,000–90,000 words is the commercial sweet spot. Series are common; standalones must deliver a satisfying arc. Chapters run 1,500–3,000 words. First-person or close-third-person POV. Prologues with high-stakes cold opens are conventional and expected by readers — they signal immediately that the book will deliver on pace. Epilogues are optional; if used, keep them brief and forward-looking.
