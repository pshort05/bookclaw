## Failure Modes

- The Legal Mind: The protagonist views the world through the lens of liability, statutes, and strategy. They don't just see a car crash; they see negligence and insurance caps.
- Tropes Management: Clear guidance on which tropes to embrace (exculpatory discovery, systemic corruption, moral dilemmas) and which to avoid or subvert (generic villains, magical trial resolution, simplistic morality).
- Moral Complexity: Avoid simplistic good-versus-evil binaries; embrace ethical gray areas
- Experience moral compromise through professional practice; avoid simple righteousness
- Avoid purely evil characters; explore how systems, pressures, or genuine belief drive antagonistic behavior
- Avoid predictable resolutions; surprise through fair evidence-planting
- Avoid overly theatrical or unrealistic questioning patterns
- Tropes to Avoid or Subvert
- Generic Villain: Avoid purely evil antagonists; develop complex motivation
- Magical Trial Resolution: Avoid unrealistic courtroom epiphanies; employ credible legal mechanisms
- Avoid excessive legal explanation that interrupts narrative flow
- Avoid purple prose or excessive metaphor
- Moral Complexity: Embrace ambiguity; avoid simplistic resolution
- Upper left (Color Light Red): Low Demand, High Saturation; Label as ‘Avoid These Opportunities’
- Moral Ambiguity Without Clear Resolution: Readers increasingly appreciate legal narratives avoiding simplistic good-versus-evil binaries, instead exploring situations where multiple parties possess legitimate claims, where legal rights diverge from moral justice, and where procedural requirements conflict with truth discovery. This reflects broader reader sophistication and decreased appetite for simplistic resolution.[23]
- The "Avoid These Opportunities" quadrant—low demand combined with high saturation—identifies subgenres characterized by both limited reader interest and substantial existing competition. Perry Mason-style episodic procedurals, sleepy small-town trials, overly academic legal process narratives, and generic betrayal-by-lawyer tropes populate this space. Unless authors possess exceptional platforms or innovative approaches fundamentally differentiating their work, entries into this quadrant face significant commercial headwinds.
