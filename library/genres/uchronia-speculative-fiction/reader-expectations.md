Speculative fiction in the alternate history tradition that foregrounds philosophical and literary inquiry over action — asking not just "what if history changed?" but "what does that change reveal about power, identity, and contingency?"

## Tone & Mood

The tone is contemplative and idea-driven, often melancholy. Readers come not primarily for adventure but for the cognitive pleasure of exploring a world where history bent differently, and for what that illuminates about the one we inhabit. A sense of historical weight is essential: the alternate timeline should feel as consequential and lived-in as our own. Irony is a frequent register — the altered world often recapitulates the same structures and injustices as real history in new configurations, making those structures newly visible. The mood ranges from elegiac (mourning what was lost when history forked) to satirical (exposing the contingency of arrangements we treat as inevitable).

## Pacing

Pacing is measured rather than urgent. The genre prioritizes revealing the alternate world's texture: how ordinary life works, how people understand their own history, what categories of thought are available to characters who grew up in a different timeline. Plot is important but secondary to exploring divergence. Readers accept extended passages of world-building delivered through character experience and dialogue rather than action. When thriller elements appear, they accelerate the pace significantly, but the baseline rhythm is literary rather than genre-thriller tempo.

## Setting Conventions

The alternate world is the central setting, and its construction is the genre's primary craft challenge. The **point of divergence** — the specific historical event that changed — must be identified clearly, either explicitly or through accumulated evidence, and its consequences must be plausible. Readers expect internal consistency: its own political geography, technology level, cultural norms, and ideological vocabulary. The **Explainer** character — a historian, archivist, or well-read viewpoint character — is a conventional device for delivering world-building without breaking narrative. Physical settings range across the full alternate timeline; the genre is not confined to the moment of divergence.

## Character Archetypes

The **Traveler or Stranger** — arriving from our own timeline or with unusual knowledge of the real timeline — provides an outsider perspective that lets readers experience the world's strangeness directly. The **Native of the Alternate Timeline** protagonist is equally valid and often more literarily interesting: someone who accepts their world as normal and must have its contingency revealed. The **Historian or Archivist** embodies the genre's core subject: the knowledge that history could have been otherwise. Antagonists are typically institutional rather than personal — ideological systems, political orders, the weight of an alternate history's momentum.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–120,000 words. Literary standalones are more common here than in action-oriented alternate history; series are not required. Multiple POV is common but not universal. Prose style is typically more literary than genre-thriller standard: careful with language, attentive to historical detail, willing to use the alternate world's vocabulary and idiom. Author's notes explaining the historical basis and point of divergence are conventional and valued.
