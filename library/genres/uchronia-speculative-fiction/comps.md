## Comparable Titles

- Narrative voice should convey confidence in the altered world. Use precise terminology for historical objects, titles, and social roles. When introducing anachronistic elements (a technology developed earlier due to the POD), describe them with the same matter-of-factness as historical elements. The prose itself can subtly reflect the era's literary conventions (e.g., a more formal sentence structure for a 19th-century setting).
- Weave historical and alternate-world context organically into action and dialogue. Avoid “infodumps.” Use character perspectives to reveal differences: a character might note how a common object in our timeline is considered a luxury in theirs, or vice-versa. Trust the reader to recognize historical touchstones, but clearly signpost the divergences.
- Dialogue must serve two masters: **Character** and **World-Building**.
- Use vocabulary and syntax appropriate to the social class, education, and era of the character. Research colloquialisms and slang of the period, but adapt judiciously for readability. Avoid outright archaic speech (“thee,” “thou”) unless strictly warranted by setting and character. Instead, suggest antiquity through word choice and rhythm.
- Characters should discuss their world’s politics, technology, and social norms as mundane reality. A soldier might complain about the reliability of his “Babbage-powered rifle.” A politician might reference “the Treaty of New Amsterdam” without explanation. This embedded exposition makes the world feel lived-in.
- Dialogue should reflect the period's ideologies (colonialism, class hierarchy, scientific racism) as they have been reshaped by the POD. Characters will hold beliefs foreign to modern readers; their dialogue should reveal this worldview without the narrator necessarily endorsing it.
- Uchronia narratives typically follow a modified version of classic plot structures, with heightened emphasis on discovery and consequence.
-    **Opening Hook:** Introduce the world through a scene that highlights a key difference from our timeline. Show, don't tell.
-    **Inciting Incident:** Often tied to the discovery of the POD's ongoing consequences, a threat emerging from the altered timeline, or a character gaining knowledge of the “real” history (if that is part of the plot).
-    **Protagonist's Dilemma:** The protagonist is forced to engage with the altered world's central conflict, which is always a direct or indirect result of the POD.
