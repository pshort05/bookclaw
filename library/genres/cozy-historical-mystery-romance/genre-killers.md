Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

-  **Do:** Develop a relatable protagonist with flaws and growth; use historical research to enrich setting; weave romance into the mystery plot; provide a clear, logical resolution.
-  **Don't:** Include graphic violence, explicit sexual content, or modern sensibilities without context; neglect romantic subplot; overload with historical facts; make the mystery too complex or the solution unjust.
-  **Template for Clue Revelation:**
- Character A: [Observation or question about mystery]
- Character B: [Response that hints at clue, possibly with historical reference]
- Example: "Lady Clara noticed the locket's engraving. 'This emblem resembles the one from the old scandal, doesn't it?' Mr. Thorne nodded, his eyes softening. 'Indeed, but let us speak elsewhere.'"
-  **Template for Romantic Tension:**
- Character A: [Witty or teasing remark]
- Character B: [Playful or heartfelt reply]
- Example: "'You seem to attract trouble, Miss Evans,' he said with a smile. 'Only the interesting kind, Lord Ashton,' she retorted, her pulse quickening."
-  **Pitfall:** Pacing issues—too slow on romance or too fast on mystery. **Fix:** Alternate scenes; use chapter breaks to balance both plots.
-  **Pitfall:** Anachronistic dialogue or details. **Fix:** Research period language; beta readers for historical accuracy.
-  **Pitfall:** Weak romantic chemistry. **Fix:** Build tension through dialogue, shared goals, and emotional vulnerability.
-  **Pitfall:** Mystery solution feels contrived. **Fix:** Plant clues early; ensure logical progression.
-  **Upper Class:** Formal, restrained, with polite phrasing. Example: "I daresay, your insight is most valuable, Miss Bennet."
-  **Middle Class:** Slightly less formal, practical. Example: "We must look into this matter carefully, Mr. Harris."
-  **Working Class:** Direct, colloquial, but respectful. Example: "Begging your pardon, sir, but I seen something odd."
