Satire fiction readers expect to be made to laugh at something real — and to feel, underneath the comedy, that the target deserved it.

## Tone & Mood

The register depends on where the novel sits between Horatian and Juvenalian satire. Horatian satire (Adams, Pratchett, early Heller) is warm, absurdist, and invites the reader to enjoy the joke alongside the author; the target is real but the emotional temperature is affectionate. Juvenalian satire (Vonnegut's bleaker work, Sinclair Lewis, R.F. Kuang's darker modes) is sharper and more indignant, implicating the reader rather than comforting them. Readers come to both expecting that the comedy is not accidental — it is controlled, purposeful, and pointed. The mood should feel like wit deployed as a weapon, not as decoration.

## Pacing

Satirical scenarios must build and escalate: the joke must deepen, not repeat. The first act establishes the premise and its absurdity; the middle acts follow the logic further than the reader expected and show what the system costs the people inside it; the final act brings the critique to its sharpest point. A satirical novel that finds its rhythm in act one and sustains it at the same pitch for three hundred pages becomes exhausting. The pacing requirement is escalation — each development should reveal that the target is worse, stranger, or more pervasive than it appeared.

## Setting

Satire fiction typically operates in a version of reality that is recognizable but heightened — not the documentary realism of literary fiction, but a world where one element of actual reality has been followed to an exaggerated or logical extreme. The setting's relationship to the real world is often part of the joke: the military bureaucracy in Catch-22, the publishing industry in Yellowface, the police state in a Pratchett Watch novel. The heightening must be consistent; a setting that sometimes behaves realistically and sometimes absurdly without intention produces incoherence rather than satire.

## Archetypes

The naive protagonist who reveals the system's absurdity: a character who engages with the target sincerely and whose sincerity makes the system's dysfunction visible — Candide, Billy Pilgrim, Moist von Lipwig. The system personified: an institution, a bureaucracy, a social set rendered in sufficient detail that it becomes a character the reader recognizes. The true believer: someone who defends the system without irony, who exists to show that the absurdity is not an accident but a feature. The knowing insider: a character who sees the system clearly and has made a settled peace with it, which is often more disturbing than the true believer.

## Length & Format

60,000–100,000 words is common; the genre rewards compression. First person is frequent when the comedy depends on the protagonist's unreliable or earnest self-presentation. Third person works when the satirical target is the world rather than a single consciousness. Series are less common than in other genre fiction; the satirical premise tends to be fully exploited in one volume. Pratchett's Discworld is the notable exception, where the world is the satirical engine and individual novels work independently.
