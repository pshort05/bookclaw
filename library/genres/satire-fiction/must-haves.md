Every book in this genre needs these essential elements.

## Essential Checklist

- [ ] A satirical target that is clearly identifiable — a system, institution, ideology, or human tendency — without being stated didactically; the reader should be able to name the target without the author naming it first.
- [ ] Comedy that emerges from situation and character logic, not from authorial intrusion — the humor must arise from what happens when satirical premises are followed to their conclusions, not from the narrator winking at the reader.
- [ ] An internally consistent satirical logic — the world's absurdity must follow rules, even if those rules are inverted or exaggerated versions of real ones; inconsistency breaks the satirical contract.
- [ ] Human cost felt through characters, not just observed — the satirical target must damage or constrain actual people the reader cares about; abstract critique without embodied consequence produces essays, not novels.
- [ ] Escalation of the satirical premise — the core absurdity must deepen and complicate across the narrative, not simply repeat; each act should reveal a new layer of what the target does to people.
- [ ] At least one character who takes the system entirely seriously — the true believer who defends the absurdity without irony is essential; they are often funnier and more disturbing than the protagonist's resistance.
- [ ] A protagonist whose relationship to the satirical target is dynamic — they begin somewhere on the spectrum between complicity and opposition, and the novel moves them; static observers produce commentary, not story.
- [ ] Moments of genuine pathos that the comedy doesn't undercut — Heller, Vonnegut, and Pratchett all contain grief; satire that cannot hold sadness alongside the joke stays shallow.
- [ ] A register choice that is deliberate and consistent: Horatian (gentle, affectionate, inviting the reader to laugh with) or Juvenalian (biting, indignant, implicating the reader as part of the problem); mixing registers without intention muddies the satirical effect.
- [ ] A resolution that does something with the critique — it need not fix the target, but the ending must reflect on what the satire revealed; a finale that shrugs is an abdication.
