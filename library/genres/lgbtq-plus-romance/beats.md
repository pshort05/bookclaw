# LGBTQ+ Romance — Beats

LGBTQ+ romance runs a dual arc — the central love story and the identity arc — woven together so
that growth in one propels and complicates the other; the HEA or HFN must resolve both.

## Phase 1 — Setup

- **Introduce the protagonist (1–5%)** — establish their queer identity as it currently stands: fully
  out and at ease, closeted, questioning, or navigating a recent transition. Show the specific
  internal weight they carry — internalized shame, isolation, chosen invisibility, or the freedom of
  authenticity — before love is a possibility.
- **Introduce the love interest (5–10%)** — give them their own distinct position on the
  out/identity spectrum. The contrast with the protagonist is a pressure point, not a hierarchy.
- **The recognition scene (10–15%)** — the charged first moment of awareness that is both romantic
  and identity-resonant: a glance that lasts too long, a pronoun that lands differently, an
  unmistakable signal of shared experience. This is not the meet-cute; it is the moment the
  protagonist registers *possibility*.
- **The stakes of the identity arc established (15–20%)** — the internal or external obstacle is
  named: a family that doesn't know, a community that would reject them, an identity still being
  formed, a world (historical, political, fantastical) with a specific cost for queerness. The reader
  must understand what this person risks.
- **Adhesion (20–25%)** — circumstances force sustained proximity: shared community, event, job,
  voyage, or magical bind.

## Phase 2 — Falling

- **Attraction complicated by identity (25–35%)** — desire deepens while the identity obstacle
  presses harder. A closeted character panics. A questioning character rationalizes. An out character
  fears loving someone not yet free.
- **The inkling of safe harbor (35–45%)** — the love interest becomes the first person around whom
  the protagonist's armor slips. A vulnerability is offered and received without weaponization.
- **Midpoint: the first act of claiming (~50%)** — a kiss, a confession of feeling, a public act of
  acknowledgment. A tentative "yes" to both the relationship and, implicitly, to their own identity.
  False high: they believe this is manageable.

## Phase 3 — Retreating

- **The identity crisis hits hardest (55–65%)** — external pressure (family ultimatum, community
  exposure, historical danger, internalized shame) collides with the relationship; the protagonist
  retreats behind the wall they built before the love interest existed.
- **Forced reckoning (65–75%)** — the love interest either withdraws out of self-protection or
  issues an ultimatum — not as cruelty but as a refusal to be a secret. The protagonist faces what
  full authenticity actually costs.
- **The dark night of the soul (75–80%)** — alone, the protagonist confronts the identity arc
  directly: who they are, what they want, and whether they are willing to pay for it. The romance is
  secondary here; the self-reckoning is primary.

## Phase 4 — Fighting

- **The identity claim (80–85%)** — the protagonist acts on who they are, independent of whether
  the love interest comes back: comes out to the person who mattered most, draws the line with the
  hostile parent, stops hiding. This beat must precede the romantic resolution — the reader needs to
  believe the growth is real and not contingent on the reward of love.
- **The coming-back (85–92%)** — the protagonist goes to the love interest with the change
  demonstrated, not promised. The love interest's response is measured against their own arc.
- **Chosen-family acceptance beat (92–96%)** — the community that matters witnesses or affirms the
  relationship. This may be small (a single trusted friend, a found-family group) or large (a public
  moment), but LGBTQ+ readers expect community to have a role in the resolution.
- **HEA/HFN and identity integrated (96–100%)** — the couple is together *and* the protagonist is
  more fully themselves than they were on page one. Both arcs land.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)

- **The recognition scene** — the wordless moment of mutual identification as queer and as
  *possible*; specific to LGBTQ+ romance and cannot be replaced by a generic meet-cute.
- **The safe-harbor scene** — one character is vulnerable about their identity (not their backstory,
  their *identity*) and the other holds it without flinching.
- **The isolation scene** — the protagonist faces a moment of genuine aloneness within their
  identity arc; the reader must feel what is at stake before the resolution means anything.
- **The identity-claim beat** — the protagonist acts for themselves, not for the relationship. If
  this beat is missing, the HEA reads as a rescue rather than a choice.
- **The chosen-family moment** — community or chosen kin witness, celebrate, or anchor the couple;
  this is structurally load-bearing in LGBTQ+ romance in a way it is not in heterosexual romance.
