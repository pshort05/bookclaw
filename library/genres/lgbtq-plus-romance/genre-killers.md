# LGBTQ+ Romance — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The representation failures, craft betrayals, and tonal missteps that collapse reader trust and
mark an LGBTQ+ romance as written from outside the community.

- **Queerbaiting without delivery** — building unmistakable queer tension between main characters
  and resolving it as a straight pairing, a tragic misread, or a "they were just very close friends"
  ending. Readers tracking queer subtext are not wrong; payoff is mandatory.
- **Bury Your Gays / tragedy as default** — killing a queer protagonist or love interest at the
  climax, or ending with permanent separation, illness, or exile rather than an HEA or HFN.
  LGBTQ+ romance readers are specifically choosing this genre for affirmation; tragedy is a genre
  promise broken unless the book is marketed and structured as a queer tragedy from page one.
- **Identity as the only personality** — a queer character whose entire inner life is their
  LGBTQ+ identity and nothing else. Real people have jobs, fears, ambitions, aesthetics, senses of
  humor, and obsessions that have nothing to do with their orientation or gender. Flat representation
  is not positive representation.
- **The coming-out arc resolved for the love interest's comfort, not the protagonist's growth** —
  forcing a closeted character out because the love interest "deserves" honesty, or framing the
  protagonist's unreadiness as moral failure. The identity arc belongs to the person living it.
- **Tokenism and isolation** — a single queer character in a world that otherwise reads as
  universally heterosexual and cisgender, with no queer community, no chosen family, and no
  acknowledgment of the social context. Isolation is a condition of the story's world; it is not a
  default worldbuilding choice.
- **Miswriting trans and non-binary characters** — deadnaming without narrative purpose, defining a
  trans character primarily by dysphoria and medical procedures, using incorrect pronouns in
  narration (not as a character's error but as an authorial error), or treating non-binary identity
  as a quirk or phase. These are not nuance; they are errors.
- **The "gay best friend" as protagonist trick** — the queer character begins as sidekick or
  comic-relief support for a heterosexual protagonist, then gets promoted to lead mid-series without
  the structural rework required. Readers can tell when a POV character was not originally written
  as queer.
- **Suffering as a prerequisite for love** — the narrative requiring a queer character to endure
  sustained trauma, abuse, family rejection, or hate crimes before they are "allowed" the HEA.
  Stakes and conflict are necessary; mandatory suffering before joy is a different thing.
- **Sanitizing identity for palatability** — removing bisexuality, fluidity, or asexuality from
  a character once they enter the central pairing ("she was confused, but now she's with him and
  everything makes sense"). Identity does not resolve into a previous one; it is what it is inside
  the relationship as much as outside it.
- **No queer joy** — an LGBTQ+ romance that spends its page budget entirely on homophobia, family
  rejection, internalized shame, and political danger, with no room for desire, delight, humor,
  chosen-family warmth, or the specific pleasure of queer recognition. Acknowledging difficulty is
  craft; centering only difficulty is a failure of genre.
