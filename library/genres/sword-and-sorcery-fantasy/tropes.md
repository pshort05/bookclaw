- Part Three: Key Themes, Motifs, Tropes, and Symbols
- The Corruption of Civilization: A recurring S&S theme posits civilization as inherently corrupting, with rural, martial, or "primitive" cultures representing vitality, authenticity, and moral clarity. This problematic romanticization of primitivism reflects historical context (particularly Robert E. Howard's ideological commitments) but remains remarkably persistent within S&S, even in contemporary works. However, modern S&S increasingly complicates this dichotomy, presenting civilization as neither purely corrupting nor entirely desirable.[4][7][16]
- Slavery, Tyranny, and Liberation: Within S&S narratives, particularly those influenced by Howard's work and contemporary writers like Charles R. Saunders, slavery and institutional oppression function as recurring thematic concerns. S&S heroes frequently confront slavers and oppressive systems, with personal liberation mirroring broader struggles for freedom.[1][4]
