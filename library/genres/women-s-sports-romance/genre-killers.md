# Women's Sports Romance — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The broken promises, competence failures, and tonal betrayals that trigger DNF reactions and
one-star reviews in women's sports romance specifically.

- **Sidelining her for the romance** — the heroine's athletic goals quietly evaporate as the relationship intensifies; by Act III she is functionally no longer competing. Readers picked up the book for a female athlete, not a woman who used to train. Her ambition must remain on the page at the same intensity from page one to the final scene.
- **The incompetent athlete** — grounding her early scenes in errors, self-doubt, and physical struggle without establishing genuine excellence first. Readers cannot root for a competitor they have never seen compete well. Vulnerability is earned by credibility, not substituted for it.
- **Green-screen sport** — a hollow backdrop that names a sport without depicting it. If the heroine is a climber, the reader must feel the chalk, the fall, the beta. Generic "she practiced really hard" prose signals the author did not research the discipline; readers who love the sport will close the book.
- **The love interest who rescues her career** — a coach who writes her into the lineup through favoritism, a romantic partner who funds her sponsor, a rival who throws a match to preserve her ranking. She must win her athletic battles herself. Romantic support is welcome; career rescue is not.
- **Punishing her for choosing her sport** — framing her athletic ambition as emotional unavailability, selfishness, or the cause of the romantic conflict in a way that requires her to apologise for wanting to compete. The sport is not her flaw; the reader is there to celebrate it.
- **The jealous love interest who can't handle her success** — a love interest threatened by her talent, her media attention, her income, or her better performance. Readers of women's sports romance are explicitly seeking a partner who champions female excellence; a sulking or undermining love interest is a category violation.
- **Collapsing athletic stakes at the black moment** — relieving the competition pressure just before the emotional climax so the heroine does not have to carry both at once. The genre's power comes from dual-arc pressure. If the championship is quietly postponed or the injury miraculous resolves before the dark night of the soul, the book loses its structural argument that love and ambition are compatible.
- **Misrepresenting queer culture in women's sport** — treating the heroine's sport as entirely heterosexual without acknowledgment of the real landscape of women's athletics. This does not require a queer romance, but it does require that the world feel accurate; an all-straight locker room with no awareness reads as obliviousness to readers embedded in that community.
