Cozy-dystopia readers expect the genre's signature contradiction to be honored in full: the warmth is real, the threat is real, and neither cancels the other.

## Tone & Mood

The defining quality is simultaneous comfort and unease — not alternating, but genuinely concurrent. The community life must feel genuinely warm: the shared meals, the neighborly systems, the small kindnesses are not ironic or hollow; they are what makes survival possible and human. At the same time, the dystopian context must be felt as real constraint, not as wallpaper. The reader should feel both the pleasure of the community and the awareness of what it exists within. The mood is not hopeless — cozy-dystopia is not grimdark with better lighting — but it is honest about cost.

## Pacing

The rhythm is domestic and slow, structured around the textures of daily life under constraint: what can be grown in a hidden garden, what can be bartered, what cannot be said in front of certain people. This slow domestic pulse is punctuated by system intrusions — a census, a new regulation, a stranger arriving with outside information, an enforcement action — that remind the community and the reader of the world beyond the community's warmth. These intrusions should escalate in frequency or severity as the novel progresses, tightening the tension between the cozy interior and the dystopian exterior without resolving it.

## Setting

A small-scale community — a village, a neighborhood, a workplace, a compound — contained within a larger controlled society. The dystopia operates at a remove from daily life: it is the background hum, the bureaucratic intrusion, the distant capital's regulations rather than the immediate foreground. The community space is the foreground. The reader should understand the larger system clearly enough to feel its weight while spending most of the novel in the human warmth of the smaller world. The contrast between these two scales is the genre's primary structural feature.

## Archetypes

The community as protagonist: the genre's emotional investment is distributed across the community rather than concentrated in a single heroic individual; the protagonist is typically a member of the community whose actions and perceptions represent it rather than transcend it. The system's local representative: an official, an inspector, or a neighbor who enforces or reports on the community — not cartoonishly evil, but performing their function in a way that reveals how the system works at human scale. The disruptive newcomer or event: an outsider arrival or an external development that forces the community to make explicit the accommodation it has been maintaining, and to decide whether and how to defend it.

## Length & Format

70,000–100,000 words is common; the genre lends itself to series in which the community is the constant and the systemic pressure evolves across volumes. Third person works well for distributing focus across multiple community members; close third person or first person work when the novel emphasizes a single character's navigation of the contradiction. Prose style should be warm and specific — the material details of daily life are load-bearing, not decorative.
