These failures do not merely weaken portal SF — they reclassify it as something else or make it unreadable to the audience that sought out the genre specifically for its scientific grounding and consequential transit.

## Anti-Checklist

- [ ] **The portal has no stated mechanism.** If the gateway operates through will, destiny, magic, or unspecified dimensional energy with no theoretical framework, you have written portal fantasy. Portal SF readers come specifically for a mechanism that can be understood, violated, and weaponized. Calling it a "wormhole" and then treating it as a magic door does not fulfill the contract — the mechanism must be rule-governed and those rules must matter to the plot.

- [ ] **The rules of transit change without explanation.** The portal can do something in chapter 12 that was established as impossible in chapter 4, and no in-world reason is given. This kills portal SF faster than almost anything else because the genre's reader pleasure depends on watching rules hold under pressure. Unexplained rule changes signal that the mechanism was never real — it was just a plot device wearing scientific clothing.

- [ ] **Crossing is free.** The protagonist moves between worlds as often as the plot requires, at no cost in energy, biology, time, identity, or political exposure. The genre's central tension is the weight of transit — what it takes from you to cross, and whether what is on the other side is worth it. Remove the cost and you remove the genre.

- [ ] **The destination world is arbitrary.** The other side of the portal has strange things in it, but they follow no coherent logic and cannot be mapped through observation. The protagonist encounters events that are simply different without any internal consistency. This is a genre kill because portal SF promises a universe that can be understood — not necessarily easily or safely, but in principle through rational effort. Arbitrary strangeness is horror or surrealism, not portal SF.

- [ ] **The doppelganger has no inner life.** When the protagonist meets their counterpart from another world, that counterpart is simply a villain, a prop, or a mirror with no independent perspective. The genre's deepest theme — the contingency of identity, the question of what makes you uniquely you — requires that the other-self be as fully realized as the protagonist. A flat counterpart wastes the genre's most powerful device.

- [ ] **Infinite worlds mean death has no stakes.** The multiverse is described as genuinely unlimited, and the protagonist or the reader understands that a version of every character persists in some branch regardless of what happens in this one. Without formal constraints on the multiverse — a rule about what dies in all worlds at once, a limit on which branches can be reached, a cost that makes caring about this particular branch rational — character death loses meaning and tension collapses. The fix is always constraint, not infinite freedom.

- [ ] **The controlling institution is simply evil.** The government program or corporate entity that controls portal access exists only to be opposed and eventually dismantled. No institutional logic, no understandable reason for the secrecy, no competing legitimate interests. This kills the genre's political dimension: the most interesting version of "the portal is controlled" is one where the control has defensible reasons that the protagonist must weigh against the need to cross. Pure villainy is a thriller convention; portal SF needs institutional complexity.

- [ ] **The first crossing is skipped or summarized.** The protagonist crosses the threshold between a paragraph break or in a single sentence of reported experience. This is the genre's most obligatory scene, and skipping it signals either a craft failure or a fundamental misunderstanding of why readers come to portal SF. The transit moment — sensory, psychological, disorienting — is the genre's core aesthetic experience. It cannot be cut.

- [ ] **Return restores the protagonist to their original state.** The crossing happened, the protagonist survived the destination, they crossed back — and are unchanged. Same beliefs, same relationships, same sense of what is real and possible. Portal SF is built on the irreversibility of knowing. A protagonist who returns fully restored is a protagonist for whom the portal was merely a plot obstacle, not a transformative encounter. The genre's promise is broken.

- [ ] **The science is decoration.** Character mentions quantum mechanics, wormhole geometry, or parallel universe theory in dialogue to signal that this is SF, then the novel proceeds with no connection between that scientific vocabulary and the plot's actual mechanics. The science must load-bear — it must generate the constraints, the costs, the crises. Scientific language used only for flavor is not portal SF; it is fantasy with a glossary.
