Portal SF structures its narrative around the discovery of a mechanism, the cost of using it, and the irreversible transformation that results from knowing what lies on the other side.

## Dominant Structure

Portal SF most commonly follows a three-act arc built around access: gaining it, using it, and surviving what using it revealed. The middle act is usually the longest, because the heart of the genre is mapping — of the destination world, the portal's rules, and the protagonist's own contingency. The final act is not a return to normal; it is a reckoning with the fact that normal no longer exists.

### Phase 1: Before the Crossing

**Establish the origin world in enough detail that leaving it costs something.** The protagonist's normal life — work, relationships, what they believe about how the universe operates — needs to be legible before it is disrupted. This is not an opportunity for extended setup; one or two scenes anchoring the protagonist in their world are sufficient if they are specific.

**Introduce the mechanism's existence before the first crossing.** Whether this is a classified facility, a theoretical discovery, a prototype device, or a threshold the protagonist stumbles across — the reader should know the portal exists before the protagonist crosses it. The discovery of the portal and the decision to cross (or the event that forces the crossing) are separate beats.

### Phase 2: The Obligatory Scenes

These are the scenes readers of portal SF are contractually owed. Deliver them in full.

**Scene 1 — The discovery of the gate.** The moment the protagonist first understands that the portal is real and functional. This beat carries the genre's first wave of wonder. It is often also the moment of rational disbelief: the character who understands physics knows this should not exist. The scene should give the reader the mechanism's basic parameters — what it opens to, what it requires, what the controls are, who else knows it exists.

**Scene 2 — The first crossing (or the failed first crossing).** The threshold moment. This is the genre's single most obligatory scene. It must be rendered in full sensory and psychological detail: what does crossing feel like, what does the crosser perceive between worlds, what is the first thing they register on arrival? A failed first crossing — where something goes wrong before full transit, or the protagonist cannot complete the passage — is also a valid scene if the failure reveals something about the mechanism's rules.

**Scene 3 — The cost revealed.** The moment when the protagonist (and reader) learn that crossing is not free. The cost may be biological (the body changes with each transit), temporal (time moves differently on each side), psychological (cumulative crossings degrade memory or identity coherence), political (someone now knows the protagonist crossed and will not permit it again), or ecological (something was brought back). The cost should be revealed through event, not exposition — the protagonist should experience it, not be told about it.

**Scene 4 — Mapping the destination's rules.** A sequence, not a single scene, in which the protagonist observes the destination world systematically and forms working hypotheses about how it differs from the origin. This is the genre's signature rationalist pleasure: watching a scientifically literate character try to understand a world on its own terms. One or more hypotheses should be tested and disproven — the destination world should resist clean mapping at least once.

**Scene 5 — The point of no return.** The moment when the protagonist cannot simply choose to go back. Either the portal closes, a rule has been violated that makes return impossible, the protagonist has been seen or captured, or returning would require abandoning something that cannot be left. This beat is structural: it commits the protagonist to the destination and forces the reader out of any expectation of easy resolution.

**Scene 6 — The destination's rules versus the protagonist's assumptions.** A high-stakes confrontation in which the protagonist acts on an assumption imported from the origin world and the assumption proves wrong in a costly way. This beat tests the mapping done in Scene 4 and usually requires the protagonist to revise their mental model of how this world works. The scene establishes that the destination has its own logic and that logic will not bend for the protagonist.

**Scene 7 — The reckoning.** The final confrontation with what the portal revealed — not just about the destination but about the protagonist, the mechanism, and the origin world. This is the beat where the genre's thematic payload is delivered. The reckoning may take the form of a climactic action sequence, but underneath the action it is epistemological: what the protagonist now knows that they cannot unknow.

### Phase 3: After the Crossing

**Return, if it happens, is not restoration.** The protagonist may cross back to the origin world. They will find it unchanged. They will be changed. The final beat of portal SF is not triumph over the destination but the protagonist's reckoning with their own transformation — and with what knowing about the other side means for how they live in this one.

## Structural Variants

**Thriller-paced portal SF** (Crouch model) compresses the mapping phase and repeats the crossing beat many times, using each repetition to escalate stakes. The mechanism's rules are established early and then systematically violated, with the violations generating plot. The protagonist crosses dozens of times; each crossing is shorter on the page but higher in cost.

**Literary portal SF** (VanderMeer, Johnson model) extends the mapping phase and treats the cost as psychological and social rather than physical. Single crossings or small numbers of crossings carry enormous narrative weight. The point-of-no-return may be reached before the first crossing even occurs.

**Network-scale portal SF** (Simmons, Pratchett/Baxter model) distributes the beats across multiple POV characters, each encountering different aspects of the mechanism or the destination. The discovery of the gate's full architecture — who built it, what it is actually for — is the structural spine of the entire series.
