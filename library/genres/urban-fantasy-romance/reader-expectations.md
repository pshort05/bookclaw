A romance set in a contemporary urban world with a hidden supernatural layer, where the emotional arc of a central relationship develops alongside — and is complicated by — genuine magical and physical danger.

## Tone & Mood

The tone balances gritty atmospheric tension with romantic warmth, and readers expect both in substantial measure. The urban setting should feel real and specific — particular neighborhoods, familiar social textures — with the supernatural hidden just beneath, surfacing in ways that feel dangerous rather than whimsical. Wit and banter are expected, but the genre does not tip into romantic comedy; the danger is real, the emotional wounds are real. Found family and belonging themes run through nearly every successful work in the genre; protagonists are usually outcasts who build chosen communities. Moral complexity and grey-area decisions are expected.

## Pacing

The narrative carries two simultaneous escalating arcs — the supernatural conflict and the developing romantic relationship — and both must advance at each significant plot beat. Slow-burn romantic development is strongly preferred: emotional and intellectual compatibility should be established before physical intimacy, and attraction should be complicated by genuine conflict rather than mere misunderstanding. The pacing is faster than literary fiction but more emotionally layered than pure urban fantasy action; readers expect to feel the relationship developing in real time.

## Setting Conventions

The contemporary city is not merely backdrop; it is a character with geography that matters. Readers expect specific, sensory urban detail — landmarks, neighborhood atmospheres, the texture of particular nights — that grounds the fantasy in physical reality. The supernatural world exists in parallel with its own politics, territories, and history. Magic systems should have clear rules, costs, and limitations; magic that solves problems too easily is a genre failure. The supernatural population typically includes multiple species or factions with established hierarchies and rivalries. The protagonist usually exists at the intersection of human and supernatural worlds, giving readers access to both.

## Character Archetypes

The **Competent Scarred Protagonist** is capable and independent, with emotional damage specific enough to explain their defensiveness without defining them entirely. They drive the plot and the relationship rather than waiting to be rescued. The **Dangerous Romantic Lead** is typically powerful within the supernatural hierarchy, carrying their own wounds and moral complexity — attraction to them involves risk. **Mentor or Found-Family Figures** provide support, information, and stakes beyond the central relationship. Antagonists are often figures within the supernatural power structure whose removal serves both the plot and the romance.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 85,000–110,000 words. Series built around the same central pairing or a shared world with different couples per book are both common. The romance arc must conclude with a Happily Ever After (HEA) or Happily For Now (HFN) in each installment; open-ended romantic conclusions are not acceptable in the genre. Dual POV — alternating between both romantic leads — is strongly preferred. A substantial external plot (40–50% of page time) is expected; pure romance with thin supernatural scaffolding will disappoint genre readers.
