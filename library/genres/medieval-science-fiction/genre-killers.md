# Medieval Science Fiction Genre Killers

What causes DNF (Did Not Finish) and one-star reviews.

## Failure Modes

- [ ] Technology that contradicts its own rules: a finite power source in chapter four cannot be inexhaustible in chapter fourteen. Inconsistency signals the SF substrate is decorative.
- [ ] The medieval world feels like a Hollywood set: generic castles, interchangeable peasants, anachronistic dialogue. Without a credible surface, the SF revelation has nothing to shatter.
- [ ] The SF explanation is revealed too early: if readers know the "magic" is technology before committing to the world, the mystery — the genre's engine — is gone with nothing to replace it.
- [ ] The protagonist is too modern in outlook: a medieval character questioning authority with contemporary rationalist framing destroys the epistemological premise the genre depends on.
- [ ] The concealing institution has no coherent motive: a conspiracy without self-interest is a plot device, not a system.
- [ ] The SF and medieval elements do not causally connect: if the story works equally well as pure fantasy or pure SF, the hybrid premise is not doing narrative work.
- [ ] The outsider who knows the truth arrives and explains everything: infodump replaces mystery with lecture, removing the protagonist's agency.
- [ ] The resolution destroys the medieval world without exploring human cost: upgrading everyone to SF technology immediately skips the actual story — what does it mean to these people that everything they believed was fiction?
