# Medieval Science Fiction Reader Expectations

Readers come to medieval science fiction for the slow, wonder-saturated discovery that the world they thought they understood — knights, prayers, hand-forged iron — is built on a buried science-fictional substrate that changes everything.

## Tone

The dominant emotional register is wonder laced with dread. The surface world feels historically grounded and credible, so the incremental revelation of the SF substrate carries genuine shock. Readers expect intellectual rigor: the technology must be internally consistent, follow discernible rules, and reward attention. This is not fantasy with chrome — it is science fiction wearing a convincing medieval disguise.

## Pacing

Moderate to deliberate. The mystery of the technology is the engine, so revelation must be rationed. Each chapter should advance both the adventure plot and the epistemological one: what does the protagonist now suspect about the true nature of their world? Pacing accelerates as the SF substrate becomes harder to ignore, but early sections earn their patience by making the medieval surface world genuinely interesting.

## Setting

A society outwardly resembling medieval civilization — feudal hierarchy, agrarian economy, religious authority — with a hidden SF substrate beneath. The medieval veneer must be textured and specific. The hidden layer might be a dome housing a post-collapse remnant, a generation ship's interior, or a society deliberately kept pre-technological by those who control the buried infrastructure.

## Archetypes

The scholar who suspects the truth: an intellectual whose questioning makes them the reader's guide and a political threat. The authority protecting the secret: an institution — church, guild, court — whose power depends on the SF substrate being mistaken for divine law. The outsider with technological knowledge: a figure who introduces genuine SF context and forces the protagonist to choose between the world they know and the truth.

## Length and Format

70,000–120,000 words. Multi-volume series are the norm — the world-building required to sustain a credible medieval surface and a coherent SF substrate typically needs more room than a standalone allows. Third-person limited is most common, keeping the reader inside the protagonist's constrained point of view.
