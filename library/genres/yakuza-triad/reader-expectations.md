Crime fiction set within Japanese yakuza or Chinese triad organized crime hierarchies, where loyalty codes, ritual violence, and the conflict between duty and humanity generate both plot and moral drama.

## Tone & Mood

The genre's defining tension is between the surface beauty of its world — the elaborate codes, aesthetics, and rituals of organized crime culture — and the brutal reality those codes enable and conceal. Readers expect a tone that takes the organizations seriously as social structures while never romanticizing their violence. Honor and betrayal exist in close proximity; the same loyalty that creates deep brotherhood makes betrayal catastrophic when it occurs. A fatalistic quality pervades the best work: characters serve hierarchies older than they are, and the price of belonging is surrender of autonomous moral judgment. The atmosphere is sumptuous and dark — expensive restaurants, tattoo parlors, back rooms of clubs — with violence that arrives without warning and ends quickly.

## Pacing

Pacing follows the rhythms of organizational life: long periods of institutional routine (enforcing territory, navigating internal politics, managing criminal enterprises) punctuated by sudden crises of loyalty, violence, or exposure. Readers accept extended sequences of character and relationship development between action sequences; the drama is primarily interpersonal and hierarchical rather than chase-driven. The central moral conflict — giri-ninjo, duty versus humanity — requires time to develop its full weight. Climaxes are often not action sequences but confrontations between characters who know each other deeply, where the choice to act or refuse carries the entire accumulated weight of the narrative.

## Setting Conventions

Settings are urban, specific, and culturally grounded. For yakuza fiction: contemporary or near-contemporary Japan (Tokyo, Osaka, Kobe) with attention to the kobun-oyabun hierarchy, front businesses, and the territorial geography of organized crime. For triad fiction: Hong Kong, Taiwan, Macau, mainland Chinese cities, or diaspora communities in major global cities, with attention to rank structures and the commercial operations that fund the organizations. Both settings demand cultural authenticity: rituals, language, food, aesthetics, and social customs must be rendered with specificity rather than generic atmosphere.

## Character Archetypes

The **Young Initiate** entering the organization is a classic entry-point protagonist whose journey allows the world to be revealed naturally. The **Mid-Rank Lieutenant** torn between organizational loyalty and a specific human obligation is the genre's core moral protagonist. The **Boss or Patriarch** embodies the organization's philosophy with genuine charisma alongside capacity for ruthlessness; the best examples are neither pure villain nor idealized father figure. The **Outsider or Half-Blood** — a character existing between the criminal world and legitimate society — provides moral perspective without requiring an ignorant viewpoint character.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–110,000 words. Series following a character's rise through an organization are a strong fit; standalone novels work best when structured around a single operation or betrayal. Literary quality and moral seriousness are as valued as plot momentum. Violence should be rendered precisely rather than spectacularly; its significance is social and moral, not cinematic.
