Alternate-reality romance puts the central relationship into a parallel world, divergent timeline, or "what if" version of life where the speculative premise creates both the wonder and the obstacles the couple must overcome.

## Tone & Mood

Emotionally immersive with a strong undercurrent of longing. The alternate reality is not just a backdrop — it is the source of the romantic tension. Characters are displaced from the world they know, which makes them vulnerable, observant, and hungry for connection in ways that feel authentic. The tone balances the strangeness of the speculative world with the familiar pull of falling in love. Avoid allowing the world-building to overwhelm the emotional core: readers are here for the relationship. The alternate setting should illuminate, complicate, or transform the romance — not replace it.

## Pacing

Dual-track movement: the plot follows both the character's navigation of the alternate world and the progression of the romance. Early chapters establish the reality displacement and its emotional cost; the romance develops as the protagonist finds footing in the new reality. The midpoint typically marks either the deepest emotional connection or the moment the protagonist discovers that returning home would mean losing what they've found. The climax resolves the central speculative question (can they stay, do they choose to, what is the cost) in a way that is inseparable from the romantic resolution.

## Setting Conventions

The alternate world must be clearly differentiated from the protagonist's origin world, and that difference must matter to the romance. A parallel world where everything is slightly better is not enough — the divergence should create specific obstacles, misunderstandings, or opportunities that drive the romantic arc. Common conventions: a world where the love interest exists in a different form or circumstance, a world where social rules around relationships differ, a world where the stakes of love are raised by the fragility of the protagonist's place in it. Ground the speculative elements in sensory, concrete detail so the world feels inhabited, not sketched.

## Character Archetypes

The Displaced Protagonist: arrives in the alternate world without a map, which creates genuine vulnerability and justifies the emotional openness that accelerates the romance. The Love Interest is native to the alternate world and serves as the protagonist's guide, which creates a structural power imbalance that the romance must level over time. The protagonist should bring something the love interest lacks or needs, establishing mutual dependency. Supporting characters serve as mirrors: versions of people the protagonist knows from home, showing what could have been or should have been.

## Length & Format

80,000–100,000 words. Standalone or series; if series, each book should feature a new reality displacement rather than stalling the central couple across multiple volumes. Dual POV or single POV depending on whether the love interest's perspective on the protagonist's strangeness adds value. Chapter length: 2,500–4,000 words. The HEA or HFN must be emotionally earned — the reader must feel that the choice to stay (or return, or find a third path) was the right one and cost something real.
