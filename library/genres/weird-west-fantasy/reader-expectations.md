Fantasy set in the American Western frontier where supernatural and cosmic horror elements intrude on cowboy culture, blending the mythology of the West with the unsettling logic of weird fiction.

## Tone & Mood

The genre's defining tension is between the Western's mythology of human mastery over the landscape and weird fiction's insistence that the universe is indifferent and full of forces beyond human understanding. Readers expect a tone simultaneously atmospheric in the Western mode — dust, heat, the moral clarity of range justice — and vertiginous in the cosmic horror mode, where the ground can open onto something ancient and wrong. The frontier setting amplifies the horror: isolation, distance from institutions, and existing violence create conditions where the supernatural can operate without easy rescue. Dark folklore — regional mythology treated with respect rather than as generic ancient evil — enriches the atmosphere.

## Pacing

Pacing blends Western genre rhythms (the slow build of a frontier journey punctuated by sudden violence) with horror genre escalation (dread accumulating through strangeness before breaking into open threat). The opening should establish the Western world firmly before the supernatural intrudes; the contrast is essential. Investigation sequences — tracking something wrong across open country, examining evidence of an impossible event — suit the genre well. Climaxes are often simultaneously physical (a confrontation with weapons) and supernatural (requiring knowledge or sacrifice beyond gunplay). The genre resists tidy resolution; weird fiction conventions favor survival over victory.

## Setting Conventions

The historical American West of roughly 1850–1900 is the standard setting, though alternate-history and secondary-world variants exist. Key elements: small frontier towns where social order is thin and communal fear spreads quickly; open country that is genuinely vast and uncharted; mines, excavation sites, and archaeological digs that serve as portals to what should not be disturbed; and the contested nature of the frontier itself — land taken, displaced peoples, broken promises — which the supernatural often literalizes as consequence. The weird elements should feel indigenous to the setting rather than imported: ancient things disturbed by settler expansion, curses rooted in the specific history of the land, monsters that embody the frontier's real violence.

## Character Archetypes

The **Veteran Gunslinger or Marshal** is already accustomed to violence but must confront threats their competence cannot fully address. The **Occultist or Scholar** who understands but cannot physically handle the supernatural pairs naturally with the gunslinger. The **Indigenous Wisdom Keeper** — handled with cultural specificity and respect — provides knowledge of what the land contains and what settlers have disturbed; this character must not be reduced to exposition delivery. The **Outlaw or Drifter** brings moral ambiguity and freedom of movement.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 75,000–100,000 words. Standalone novels are more common than in other fantasy subgenres; the genre's horror elements suit contained narratives. Series tend to follow a recurring protagonist through separate encounters rather than a single escalating arc. Prose should be atmospheric and deliberate; spare, precise writing makes every strange detail count.
