# Comedy Thriller Comparable Titles

## One for the Money by Janet Evanovich (1994)

Stephanie Plum becomes a bounty hunter to pay her rent and immediately pursues a dangerous skip she once dated. The definitive template for the accidental amateur sleuth: incompetent by training, lucky by disaster, consistently funny and consistently in peril.

## Smokin' Seventeen by Janet Evanovich (2011)

Later in the Plum series, demonstrating the escalating stakes and established comedic rhythm that sustain a long-running comedy-thriller franchise. Useful for pacing and ensemble management.

## The Big Over Easy by Jasper Fforde (2005)

A detective investigates the death of Humpty Dumpty in a world where nursery rhyme characters are real citizens. Absurdist premise with a tightly constructed thriller plot; shows how far the comic frame can stretch without losing structural integrity.

## Skinny Dip by Carl Hiaasen (2004)

A woman thrown overboard by her husband survives and plans revenge with the help of an ex-cop. Florida noir with consistent comic energy and real menace; Hiaasen's work defines the regional comedy-thriller with serious ecological and criminal stakes.

## Squeeze Me by Carl Hiaasen (2020)

A nuisance wildlife trapper in Palm Beach becomes entangled in a political disappearance. Demonstrates how comedy-thriller can engage satirical targets without losing narrative momentum.

## The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy by Douglas Adams (1979)

The Earth is demolished to make way for a hyperspace bypass. Primarily comic science fiction, but the sustained threat to Arthur Dent's survival qualifies it as comedy-thriller in structure. Essential reference for timing, comic escalation, and the treatment of absurdist stakes as real stakes.

## Confess, Fletch by Gregory Mcdonald (1976)

Irwin Fletcher investigates a murder while pursuing a story, trading wisecracks with the detective investigating him as the primary suspect. Sharp, economical, and genuinely suspenseful; Fletch established the wisecracking-amateur-in-real-danger template.

## Hot Water by P.G. Wodehouse (1932)

An American millionaire and a British man-about-town become entangled in blackmail and theft at a French chateau. Wodehouse works at the light end of the comedy-thriller range; useful as a reference for social comedy generating genuine stakes without violence.
