Farce fiction generates extreme comedy through escalating situational misunderstandings, where characters pursuing incompatible goals in the same space produce chaos that is both inevitable and completely avoidable.

## Tone & Mood

Breathless, absurd, and committed — the author must play the chaos completely straight while readers laugh. The moment the narrative winks at the audience about how ridiculous this is, the spell breaks. Characters must believe, at every moment, that their chosen course of action is the only reasonable option available to them. The emotional stakes — embarrassment, exposure, the collapse of a social reputation — must feel enormous to the characters even as readers find them comic. The tone is light but relentless; there is no genuine cruelty in true farce, only the comic suffering of people undone by their own commitment to avoiding admission of a mistake.

## Pacing

Accelerating and rhythmically precise. Farce runs on timing: a beat too early or too late collapses the joke. Early chapters establish the misunderstanding's architecture and introduce all the characters whose incompatible goals will collide. The middle escalates through a series of near-misses, failed coverups, and complications that each make the situation geometrically worse. The final act brings every thread into simultaneous collision — the farcical climax works only when all the incompatible goals and mistaken assumptions are present in the same physical space at the same moment. Resolution must be fast once the knot is cut; prolonged denouements after the chaos peaks read as anticlimax.

## Setting Conventions

A single bounded location that forces all characters into proximity: a country house over a weekend, a hotel during a conference, a theater on opening night, a restaurant during a family dinner, a cruise ship without escape. The setting must have specific physical architecture that enables the farce — doors that slam, rooms that are adjacent, corridors that allow close-call escapes, hiding places that are both plausible and absurd. The social rules of the setting generate the stakes: what would be scandalous here, what is forbidden, who has power and who is trying to preserve an appearance of it. The more respectable the setting's social norms, the more comic the violation of them.

## Character Archetypes

The Preservation-of-Face Character whose entire behavioral logic is driven by the need to avoid exposure or embarrassment — they generate the plot by choosing to cover up rather than confess. The Innocent Bystander who becomes entangled through no fault of their own and whose attempts to extricate themselves make everything worse. The Authority Figure whose presence raises the stakes of exposure — the boss, the parent, the inspector — and who is always just one corridor away. The Confidant who knows the truth and whose attempts to help are well-intentioned disasters. The Catalyst — a letter, an arrival, an overheard conversation — that sets the misunderstanding in motion.

## Length & Format

70,000–90,000 words; farce that runs long loses its tension. Chapters should be short (1,500–2,500 words) and end at moments of maximum comic complication, not resolution. Third-person close, usually following the primary Preservation-of-Face character, with occasional chapters from other perspectives to show the audience information the protagonist lacks. Avoid extended interiority — farce lives in action and dialogue, not reflection.
