Dieselpunk delivers alt-history industrial adventure saturated in diesel-era aesthetics, where the machinery of the 1910s–1940s is both backdrop and protagonist.

## Tone & Mood

The dominant register is gritty optimism undercut by fatalism — characters believe in human ingenuity while living inside systems that grind them down. Expect moral ambiguity over clean heroism: the detective is corrupt, the revolutionary has blood on her hands, the engineer built the war machine he now regrets. Art deco beauty coexists with industrial squalor. Political cynicism is the default worldview; idealism is something characters struggle toward, not where they start. The atmosphere skews noir in its shadows and pulp in its energy — violence is blunt, romance is fast, and authority is almost always crooked.

## Pacing

Mid-to-fast, with the mechanical urgency of a diesel engine turning over. Action sequences carry real physical weight — crashes, explosions, and hand-to-hand fights have consequence. Exposition is delivered in motion: world-building emerges from what characters use, fix, sabotage, or avoid, not through infodumps. The middle act typically involves a reversal of allegiance or a discovery that reframes the opening premise. Chase sequences, infiltrations, and siege scenarios are genre staples that readers expect at regular intervals.

## Setting Conventions

The world diverges from ours somewhere between 1910 and 1945 — atomic power either never arrived or was suppressed, so diesel and coal remain the pinnacle of industrial achievement. Cities are vertical and smoky: elevated rail, zeppelin docks, neon signs over diesel-stained cobblestones. Art deco architecture signals prestige; corrugated iron and riveted steel signals the working class. Oceans are crossed by airship or diesel liner, not aircraft. Empires persist longer than in our timeline — colonial power structures are present and should not be treated as neutral backdrop. The technology ceiling matters: no computers, no transistors, no radar. Mechanical ingenuity substitutes for all of them. Readers will notice anachronistic technology more readily than social anachronisms, though retrofitting 1940s politics without critical framing reads as endorsement.

## Character Archetypes

The Grease-Stained Engineer who understands machines better than people and whose technical knowledge becomes the plot's engine. The Noir Operator — detective, spy, or fixer — navigating corrupt institutions with pragmatic ethics. The Idealist Revolutionary whose cause is just but whose methods force the protagonist to choose sides. The Corporate Magnate or Imperial Official who embodies the system's seductive power and its casual cruelty. The Outcast Pilot or smuggler who operates in the margins and knows where the bodies are buried. Female characters carry agency proportional to their narrative weight — the genre has moved well past the lone-dame-in-distress template.

## Length & Format

80,000–110,000 words is the commercial sweet spot. Series are common; the world-building investment rewards multi-book payoff. Close third-person limited or first-person works best, keeping the reader inside the grime and noise. Chapters run 2,500–4,000 words. Short prologues establishing the alternate-history divergence point are acceptable but should not exceed two pages.
