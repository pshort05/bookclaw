Romance featuring protagonists aged 50 and older, where lived experience, loss, mortality awareness, and the possibility of reinvention replace the coming-of-age arc of traditional romance with something harder-won and more complex.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is emotionally mature and reflective without being elegiac. Silver romance acknowledges what time does to people — grief accumulated, adult children present, the body's different relationship to desire, the awareness that this may be the last chance at happiness — but holds this weight against genuine joy at second chances. These characters have evidence that things go wrong, which makes choosing to risk love again more meaningful, not less romantic. Readers want tenderness, humor, and heat alongside the seriousness; a silver romance that is merely sad is not doing its job. The HEA/HFN is required and often more powerful than in younger-protagonist romance because the reader understands exactly what the characters are risking.

## Pacing

Pacing is more measured than category romance — these characters resist attraction with hard-won self-knowledge rather than falling headlong. Internal obstacles (fear of loss, loyalty to a former partner, adult children's disapproval) drive more tension than external plot events. External events — a health crisis, a family rupture, a professional disruption — serve as catalysts that force protagonists to stop deferring. The black moment often involves one character reverting to the protective withdrawal their experience has taught them.

## Setting Conventions

Contemporary settings are dominant, anchored in the textures of mid-life and later: retirement communities, second homes, long-established neighborhoods, family gatherings that reveal new cracks. The setting should reflect lives that have accumulated — houses with histories, friendships of decades, careers at their peak or just past it. Small towns where everyone knows the characters' first marriages are a common setting because community memory creates social pressure. The physical world of aging should be present without becoming medicalized or grimly focused on decline.

## Character Archetypes

Protagonists are typically 50–70. Common figures: the widow or widower who has rebuilt a life but closed off love; the divorcee whose marriage revealed what they actually need; the person who chose career over relationship and faces that choice again with different information. Secondary characters — adult children who complicate or support the romance, long-standing friends, grandchildren — are a defining feature and must be rendered with specificity. Former spouses, whether deceased or co-parenting, require careful handling; they must feel real without dominating the story.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 70,000–95,000 words. Dual-POV is increasingly preferred as it lets readers see both characters' emotional resistance from the inside. Series set in the same community — a retirement town, a long-standing friend group, a family across generations — allow multiple standalone romances in a shared world. Heat level varies significantly; the subgenre spans from sweet/clean to explicit and readers are particular about this. Explicit scenes must be written with the same specificity as other aspects of mature characterization — not defaulted to tropes from younger-protagonist romance.
