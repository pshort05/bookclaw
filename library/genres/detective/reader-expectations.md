Readers expect a crime, a methodical investigation, and a resolution that feels earned through evidence and intelligence rather than luck or revelation.

## Tone & Mood

Detective fiction's tonal range runs from the cool and clinical (forensic procedural) to the atmospheric and morally weighted (literary detective fiction) to the cheerful and puzzle-oriented (cozy mystery). Across all registers, readers expect a baseline of intelligence: the detective is smart, the crime has been constructed with care, and the reader is being given real information rather than being misdirected arbitrarily. Hard-boiled fiction is cynical and morally complex; classical detective fiction maintains ironic distance and formal game-play; contemporary literary detective fiction often scrutinizes the investigator's psychology as closely as the crime. The tone should be consistent; tonal inconsistency registers as a craft problem.

## Pacing

The detective narrative is organized around information release. Readers are in a structured game of revelation, and they expect the structure to be honored: clues appear, the investigator reasons from them, red herrings are introduced and resolved, the picture gradually assembles. Each scene should add something to the reader's understanding, even if that something is a complication or false lead. Readers are patient with methodical procedure when the method feels real. The climax — confrontation, arrest, or explanation — must feel like the logical product of everything preceding it.

## Setting Conventions

Setting in detective fiction is closely tied to access and territory. The investigator must move through spaces — the crime scene, the victim's home, the suspect's workplace, the criminal neighborhood — and readers expect these spaces to feel real. The violation of space (the body in the library, the murder in the locked room) is a formal element; readers appreciate the contrast between a familiar setting and the crime committed there. Urban environments dominate, but the country house, the small town, and the closed community all have strong traditions. Place should carry social meaning; the geography of a detective novel is almost always a map of power and class.

## Character Archetypes

The detective protagonist is the genre's fixed point: observationally acute, tolerant of the disturbing, methodical, and more interested in truth than comfort. Classic archetypes include the brilliant eccentric, the hard-bitten private investigator, the female amateur whose social access exceeds the official investigator's, and the police detective navigating institutional constraints. Supporting cast is organized around the investigation: the loyal Watson-figure who represents the reader's bewilderment, the skeptical official investigator, the circle of suspects, and the killer who is typically introduced early and given genuine characterization.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 70,000–100,000 words. Series with recurring detective protagonists are the commercial norm; standalone novels are viable in literary detective fiction. Chapter endings that deliver or withhold key information are a genre convention. Multiple suspect viewpoints are used selectively; the detective's POV should dominate to preserve the investigative experience. The solution must be retrospectively fair — all necessary clues available to the attentive reader before the revelation.
