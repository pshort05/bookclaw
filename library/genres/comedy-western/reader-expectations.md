Readers expect a Western that plays every frontier cliche for laughs, using the genre's own conventions as the setup for jokes, absurdist reversals, and broadly drawn comic characters.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is wry, irreverent, and deadpan rather than cartoonish. The humor comes from the gap between the gravity Western characters assign to their own rituals — the showdown, the code of honor, the silent nod — and the mundane or ridiculous reality beneath them. Slapstick is used sparingly and earns its place; understatement and situational irony are the primary tools. The West is simultaneously affectionately rendered and gently mocked. Readers should feel the genre knows exactly what it is doing and is delighting in the subversion rather than dismissing the source material.

## Pacing

Scenes alternate between snappy, banter-heavy dialogue and bursts of chaotic comic action. The plot rarely stops moving for long; comic timing demands that set-up and payoff stay close together. That said, individual scenes — especially showdowns or negotiations — can stretch out for comic effect, letting the absurdity build before the puncture. Chapters run shorter than in straight Westerns, keeping energy high.

## Setting Conventions

The setting is the American Frontier, broadly 1860–1910: dusty main streets, saloons with swinging doors, cattle drives, railroad towns, and the open prairie. The physical environment is rendered faithfully enough that subverting it lands — readers need to recognize the cliche before it can be deflated. Geography and period detail are present but not exhaustive; the aim is flavor, not historical accuracy. Small, eccentric frontier towns are the most common stage, giving the ensemble cast a fixed arena where everyone knows everyone.

## Character Archetypes

The Incompetent Lawman: wears the badge with enormous self-importance while consistently making things worse. The Gruff Gunslinger with an Embarrassing Secret: stoic facade, mortifying hobby or fear. The Villain with a Reasonable Grievance: menacing in posture, thwarted by circumstance or their own poor planning. The Pragmatic Outsider: a newcomer (journalist, city lawyer, travelling salesperson) whose ordinary logic is treated as heresy by locals. Archetypes are deliberately archetypal; the comedy depends on the audience recognising the template being sent up.

## Length & Format

60,000–80,000 words is the comfortable range for a standalone comic Western novel; series installments sometimes run shorter (45,000–60,000 words) to maintain pace across multiple books. Chapters should be short to medium length — four to eight pages — sustaining comic momentum. Epilogues and chapter-heading epigrams in the style of dime-novel blurbs are welcome period-appropriate flourishes.
