Historical romance set in the Scottish Highlands, where clan loyalty, rugged landscape, and the particular tensions of Highland culture are as central to the story as the love affair itself.

## Tone & Mood

The tone blends romantic intensity with historical grounding. Readers expect passion that feels proportionate to the danger and instability of the setting — clan conflict, political betrayal, English encroachment, and forced marriages create stakes that make the romance urgent rather than decorative. The mood can range from sweeping and lyrical to darkly tense, but the emotional register is always high. Humor appears in banter and in the culture clash between hero and heroine, but it undercuts neither the danger nor the desire. A sense of place — wind, stone, heather, rain — is essential to the genre's atmosphere.

## Pacing

Pacing follows the dual engine of romance: the emotional arc between the leads, and the external conflict generated by clan politics or historical threat. These two tracks should accelerate together toward the midpoint crisis and the dark moment before resolution. Readers expect a slow-burn tension in the early sections — barriers are established before they begin to fall. Scenes of Highland life (feasts, raids, gatherings, negotiations) provide pacing texture and world-grounding without stopping the romantic momentum. The climax typically resolves both the external threat and the final emotional barrier in close sequence.

## Setting Conventions

The Scottish Highlands are not generic medieval backdrop — readers expect specificity. Clan names, castle geography, regional loyalties, and the political realities of the period (most commonly the 17th or 18th century, including Jacobite conflict) should be accurately rendered or at least plausibly invented. The landscape is a character: the moors, the lochs, the mountain passes, and the isolation all shape what is possible in the story. Interior settings — great halls, chambers, stables — should feel functional and period-appropriate, not anachronistically comfortable.

## Character Archetypes

The hero is almost always a Highland warrior or clan leader: physically imposing, bound by codes of honor and loyalty, guarded but capable of fierce devotion. He is protective without being a tyrant — or he begins as a tyrant and is remade by the heroine. The heroine is typically an outsider (English, Lowland Scots, or from a rival clan), which generates the central tension. She is brave, principled, and refuses to be reduced to a political pawn. Secondary characters — loyal clansmen, scheming relatives, wise old women — flesh out clan society and provide the external plot.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 80,000–100,000 words. Single-POV (heroine) and dual-POV structures are both common; dual-POV is increasingly the expectation in the contemporary market. Series set within a single clan or region are the dominant form, allowing readers to follow secondary characters into their own books. Explicit sexual content ranges from closed-door to very explicit depending on the author's line — readers select accordingly.
