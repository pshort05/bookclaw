## Failure Modes

- Upper left (Color Light Red): Low Demand, High Saturation; Label as ‘Avoid These Opportunities’
- The popularity of grimdark reflects broader cultural skepticism about institutional goodness and a reader appetite for narratives that don't offer false comfort. Grimdark acknowledges that real-world power dynamics are often savage and that survival frequently requires moral compromise.
- Avoid These Opportunities (Low Demand, High Saturation) includes Dark Academia. The combination of high saturation and plateauing demand makes this category increasingly competitive for new market entrants. While innovation continues and successful dark academia titles continue selling, the category's growth phase has matured, and competition from established properties is intense. New entrants face difficulty achieving visibility in an increasingly crowded marketplace without significant marketing resources.
- Sharp & Grounded: Avoid overly flowery "purple prose" unless describing eldritch magic. Action should be brutal, fast, and heavy.
- Avoid: Whimsy without danger, consequence-free magic, and black-and-white morality.
- Third Person Multiple POV: Switch between characters to build political intrigue and reveal hidden agendas. Maintain consistent voice/tone per POV character; don't shift voice between their chapters.
- Avoid: Third person omniscient softens dark atmosphere by providing godlike perspective. Exception: use sparingly for cosmic horror or prophecy revelation.
- Avoid academic tone; let ideas emerge through character struggle and world logic.
- Immerse Without Overwhelming: Use rich, evocative descriptions focused on sensory details (visual, tactile, olfactory). Avoid purple prose.
- Avoiding Tonal Pitfalls
- Don't be gratuitously dark: Darkness should serve narrative/thematic purpose, not shock for shock's sake.
- Don't overuse gore: Strategic brutality impacts more than excessive gore. One precise detail often horrifies more than pages of graphic description.
- Don't abandon moments of small grace: Brief respites from darkness (companionship, small victories, humor) make darkness more impactful by contrast.
- Don't sacrifice clarity for atmosphere: Atmosphere enhances readability; it shouldn't obscure meaning.
- Avoid Archaic Language: Unless world-building necessitates specific linguistic conventions, write natural, modern dialogue. Characters use contractions, interruptions, and subtext like real speakers.
- Subtext Over Exposition: Characters reveal information through what they don't say. Avoid dialogue explaining plot; let conflict drive revelation.
- Avoid Elaborate Tags: Tags like "he averred," "she proclaimed," "he explained tersely" draw attention away from dialogue substance.
- Avoid Magic-As-Solution: Magic in dark fantasy creates problems as often as solving them. Emphasize consequences and corruption rather than magical convenience.
- Avoid Glorification: Dark fantasy portrays violence realistically—brutal, consequential, sometimes pointless. Don't make violence feel heroic or righteous unless deliberately subverting that expectation.
- Philosophical Dark Fantasy: N.K. Jemisin (The Broken Earth Trilogy), Patrick Rothfuss (The Kingkiller Chronicle)
