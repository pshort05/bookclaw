Speculative fiction set in ancient Mediterranean or Near Eastern civilizations — Greece, Rome, Egypt, Persia — where anachronistic or advanced technology is layered onto a historically grounded world to explore power, ingenuity, and the voices history suppressed.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is intellectually adventurous and politically charged. Sandalpunk critiques ancient power structures — empire, slavery, patriarchy, priestly authority — while celebrating the perspectives of those the historical record erased: enslaved engineers, women philosophers, colonized peoples. The mood combines the grandeur of the classical world with a punk sensibility: irreverence toward authority, sympathy for the marginal, skepticism toward received narratives of progress. Wonder at the technology is balanced by awareness of who controls it and who is harmed by it. The tone ranges from epic to intimate, but the political consciousness is constant.

## Pacing

Pacing varies by mode — political intrigue is deliberate; adventure and escape run fast. Speculative technology should be introduced through use and action; readers need to see an invention operating before being told how it works. Philosophical questions — free will in a world with functioning oracles; the ethics of technological advantage in warfare — are live plot stakes, not dialogue asides. The ancient world's relationship to fate creates structural opportunities: a prophecy subverted, a historical disaster approached or averted.

## Setting Conventions

The ancient world must feel authentic in its material culture — architecture, food, clothing, social hierarchy, religious practice — even as the speculative elements disrupt it. The technology intrudes on an otherwise plausible ancient setting rather than replacing it wholesale; readers should feel the cognitive dissonance of seeing advanced mechanics in a world that also uses oil lamps and papyrus. Common settings include Greek city-states, Hellenistic Alexandria, the Roman Republic or Empire, Ptolemaic Egypt, and Achaemenid Persia. The sense of historical scale — empires colliding, generations of tradition — should be present even in intimate storylines.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is often a skilled practitioner operating outside the elite: an artisan, a physician, a slave, a foreign-born adviser, a woman who works within or around gender restriction. Inventors and engineers are central; the narrative values making things and solving problems. Scholars, priests, and oracles appear as both allies and antagonists, often representing institutional power that the protagonist must navigate. Political figures — generals, senators, pharaohs — function as forces that redirect the plot. Antagonists often embody the conservatism of the ancient world: those who suppress knowledge, exploit the speculative technology for conquest, or enforce hierarchies the protagonist threatens.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 80,000–110,000 words. Series are well-suited to the genre since ancient empires provide a vast canvas — one character's career can span multiple books, or different protagonists inhabit the same world in different eras. Standalone novels are viable. Maps adapted for the speculative setting are welcomed. The genre sits at the intersection of historical fiction, science fiction, and fantasy readerships; books marketed as any of the three need clear signals about the others.
