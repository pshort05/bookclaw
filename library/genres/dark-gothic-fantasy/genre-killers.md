## Failure Modes

- Language & Vocabulary provides specific words to favor (shadowed, crumbling, tainted) and avoid (literally, dark used generically), ensuring consistent gothic diction throughout your narrative.
- Do's and Don'ts provides a quick reference checklist for maintaining style consistency.
- Tone: Formal, literary, atmospheric. Maintain an undercurrent of dread beneath composed prose. Avoid casual or overly modern phrasing. The narrative voice itself should carry gothic sensibility—refined yet haunted.
- Metaphor & Symbolism: Use sustained metaphors connecting decay, corruption, and darkness to internal states. Mirrors, shadows, blood, water, and architectural elements carry layered symbolic weight. Avoid explaining symbolism; allow it to resonate beneath surface meaning.
- Avoidances:
- Avoid casual contemporary speech patterns and slang
- Rhythm: Read prose aloud. Gothic writing should move with a stately, sometimes funereal pace. Avoid excessive alliteration, but employ consonant repetition and assonance to create sonic atmosphere.
- Avoid: dark (use specific alternatives), evil (show through action), literally (modern intrusion)
- DO'S AND DON'Ts
- DON'T:
- Point of View: Maintain consistency. Avoid head-hopping. If using multiple POV characters, ensure each carries distinctive voice.
- Dialogue Tags: Use simple attributions (said, whispered, murmured). Avoid excessive adverbs or creative tags. Let dialogue itself convey tone.
- Formal diction avoiding contemporary speech patterns
- Death and Decay stand as the most omnipresent thematic concern, with the genre exploring not merely death as narrative event but as atmospheric condition—a constant presence that saturates every page and character interaction. Death in Dark Gothic Fantasy rarely offers redemption or closure; instead, it frequently represents transition, contamination, or the failure of boundaries between life and undeath. Corruption and Pollution extend this preoccupation beyond individual mortality to encompass institutional decay, moral degradation, and the systematic poisoning of land, society, and soul. These elements function as both literal and metaphorical expressions of how systems designed to protect instead destroy, and how individual moral compromise accumulates into civilization-wide rot.[20][9][2][6]
- The Undead and Living Dead—ghosts, vampires, zombies, and creatures suspended between life and death—represent the failure of proper endings and the impossibility of closure. These beings embody both literal and metaphorical resurrection; past sins and traumas refuse to stay buried but instead return in corrupted, monstrous forms. Blood functions as a primary symbol of transgression, violation, family legacy, and the animal nature lurking beneath civilization. Bloodlines carry curse and doom; blood spilled marks indelible moral corruption; vampiric blooddrinking represents addiction, predation, and the violation of bodily autonomy.[21][8]
- Purple Prose is Permitted: Use lush, ornate, and sensory-heavy language. Don't say "he was sad"; say "he was consumed by a hollow, gnawing grief."
- The Anti-Hero: Protagonists are flawed, often morally grey, traumatized, or seeking redemption. Avoid "pure good" paragons.
- No Modern Slang/Quipping: Avoid irony, sarcasm, or "Marvel-style" banter. Humor should be dark, dry, or gallows humor only.
- Avoid "High Fantasy" Tropes: No clear distinctions between Dark Lords and Chosen Ones. No "fireball-slinging" wizards.
- Don't Rush the Reveal: Do not show the monster too early. Describe the shadow, the sound, or the aftermath first.
