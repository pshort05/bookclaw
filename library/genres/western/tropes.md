These tropes define the Western genre and power reader expectations.

## Required Tropes

The frontier setting must exist—whether 1800s historical frontier, contemporary ranch landscape, or speculative-displaced frontier, the essential liminal space between civilization and wilderness is non-negotiable. Include a moral code governing character behavior; the Code of the West establishes that a person's word is their bond, hospitality is sacred, fair play matters, and one never shoots a woman regardless of circumstance.

A protagonist who is essentially "free"—unbound by civilization's conventions, able to move through frontier with autonomy—is fundamental to the genre. An inciting incident occurs when this free-spirited protagonist encounters a victim threatened by antagonist, forcing moral dilemma. Even outlaw protagonists operate according to moral code making it impossible to abandon innocent victims.

The showdown or final confrontation is expected—typically depicting violent face-off between protagonist and antagonist. Whether quick-draw duel, siege, psychological confrontation, or legal reckoning, some climactic resolution of core conflict is required.

## Common Tropes

The revenge quest structure (initial injury → pursuit of perpetrator → confrontation → ambiguous resolution) appears frequently. The town-tamer narrative—lone hero or group confronting corrupt leadership in isolated town—recurs across traditional, revisionist, and contemporary Westerns. Cattle drives or extended journey quests depict protagonists tested by hardship across vast distances. The rescue mission (innocent captured → hero pursues → climactic rescue) drives numerous subgenres. The love triangle creates romantic tension between protagonist and two potential interests, though contemporary versions increasingly decenter this trope. The "coming storm" foreshadows inevitable danger—approaching villain, natural disaster, or civilization encroaching on wilderness—creating sustained narrative tension.

## Tropes to Avoid

Anachronistic language or technology shatters immersion—modern phrases, attitudes, or devices destroy Western authenticity. One-dimensional "black hat" villains and "noble savage" stereotypes flatten the genre; strive instead for complex characters with believable motivations. Excessive sentimentality cheapens earned emotional moments—the West was harsh; emotions must be deserved, not manufactured. Over-dialectalization renders text unreadable; suggest period speech rather than phonetically render it. Head-hopping between POVs within scenes breaks Western intimacy conventions. Clichéd violence that feels spectacular rather than consequential violates the genre's realism. Nostalgia without critique fails contemporary readers; modern Westerns must interrogate frontier mythology rather than uncritically celebrate it. Passive pacing or static settings undermine the genre's dynamic tension and landscape-as-character imperative.
