# Superhero Action Genre Killers

What causes DNF (Did Not Finish) and one-star reviews.

## Failure Modes

- [ ] Action that does not escalate: if the fight in chapter twenty feels identical in stakes, scale, and consequence to the fight in chapter two, the reader has no reason to keep turning pages. Every confrontation must raise the bar or change the terms.
- [ ] The protagonist wins every fight too easily: a hero who is never in genuine danger removes all tension from the action. Readers need to believe loss is possible; near-losses that cost something are essential.
- [ ] Civilian casualties mentioned then forgotten: a building collapses in chapter five killing dozens, and no character ever references it again. This tells readers that the world does not take its own stakes seriously.
- [ ] Action described so vaguely the reader cannot picture it: "they fought intensely for several minutes" is not an action scene. Without spatial clarity — who is where, what is happening to their body — the reader cannot engage.
- [ ] Power inconsistency between fights: a hero who cannot punch through concrete in chapter three cannot punch through a mountain in chapter eight without explanation. Unexplained escalation breaks the internal contract with the reader.
- [ ] Humor that punctures genuine stakes: comedy during action is a tool, not a shield. If every dangerous moment is deflated with a quip, readers stop believing anything is at risk.
- [ ] Ensemble members who are interchangeable: if any team member could be removed and the plot would proceed identically, the team is not a team — it is set dressing.
- [ ] Slow pacing between action scenes with no compensating tension: downtime must still build pressure. Quiet chapters that simply pause the story rather than accelerate toward the next confrontation drain momentum and invite readers to stop.
