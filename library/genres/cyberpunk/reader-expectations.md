Readers expect a rain-slicked near-future where corporate power is absolute, the body is hardware, and resistance costs everything.

## Tone & Mood

Cyberpunk's tone is cynical, saturated, and urgent. The world is broken in specific, systemic ways — not chaotically but by design, by capital, by the deliberate choices of institutions that benefit from the current arrangement. Readers expect a narrator who sees this clearly, without sentimentality or false hope, but who still acts — or watches protagonists act — because inaction is also a choice. Atmosphere is as important as plot; the neon-soaked wet street, the corporate tower blotting out the sky, the implant that hums at the base of your skull are not decoration. They are the thesis. The genre permits dark humor but not optimism that hasn't been earned at significant cost.

## Pacing

Pacing is fast, fragmented, and high-pressure. Action sequences are kinetic and chaotic; the reader should feel the sensory overwhelm of a hack-and-run or street firefight. Investigation sequences — because cyberpunk borrows from noir — slow down for atmospheric detail before snapping back to urgency. The genre supports parallel chapter structures cutting between the street operative, the corporate insider, and the AI watching everything. Readers expect escalation; the world should feel more dangerous as the story progresses.

## Setting Conventions

The canonical cyberpunk setting is a stratified mega-city: corporate arcologies above, street-level squalor below, and a gap protagonists must traverse. Environmental collapse is ambient — acid rain, polluted air — but the city's machine keeps running. The digital infrastructure mirrors and extends the physical city's power dynamics. Readers expect sensory density: the smell of street food and synthetic oil, the visual noise of advertising overlaid on everything, the tactile difference between augmented flesh and the original. Brand names, corporate logos, and proprietary slang are legitimate world-building tools.

## Character Archetypes

The hacker or netrunner — skilled, underpaid, morally compromised — is the genre's central figure. Alongside them: the street samurai, the fixer, the corporate defector who knows too much, the AI of uncertain allegiance, and the underground journalist maintaining truth in a world of manufactured reality. Protagonists are rarely heroes; they survive, resist, and sometimes win on narrow terms. Antagonists are usually institutional — the corporation, the security state, the algorithm — embodied in human agents who are themselves products of the system. Tragic arcs are genre-appropriate and common.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–120,000 words. Standalone novels are viable; series allow the political geography of a city or corporation to be mapped across multiple storylines. Short fiction and novellas have strong roots in the genre — the form originated partly in short-story magazines. Third-person limited and first-person are both common; the latter suits the genre's noir inheritance. Dense, sensory prose is expected; minimalism feels wrong in a genre defined by information saturation. Chapters are often short and punchy, built for momentum. Unreliable narrators and fragmented information delivery are genre-appropriate techniques.
