# Swashbuckler Must-Haves

Non-negotiable elements for this genre. A manuscript missing two or more of these is probably not swashbuckler.

- [ ] The protagonist must be charming, not merely competent. Physical skill alone is not enough — readers need to be won over by personality. The hero earns loyalty from other characters (and the reader) through wit, warmth, and a particular kind of confidence that never tips into arrogance.

- [ ] Swordplay and action scenes must reflect character, not just choreography. A duel should reveal something: who controls their temper, who fights dirty, who holds back out of mercy. Action that is purely mechanical — blow by blow, no interiority — reads as filler.

- [ ] A romantic thread, even if it is not the central plot. Swashbuckler without romantic tension loses half its energy. The romantic interest should be withheld, complicated, or endangered — never simply won at the outset and then set aside.

- [ ] Wit under pressure. Dialogue must sparkle, especially in high-stakes scenes. A character who is eloquent in the drawing room and silent in the dungeon has failed the genre. The best swashbuckler lines are delivered with a sword at someone's throat.

- [ ] The antagonist must be genuinely worthy of being outwitted. A stupid villain cheapens every victory. The corrupt governor, the scheming cardinal, the rival captain — they need enough intelligence and power that the protagonist's triumph is earned, not inevitable.

- [ ] Period or setting texture that makes the world feel inhabited. The clothes, the social codes, the political stakes, the price of a public insult — the setting should create real friction and real opportunity for the protagonist, not just serve as backdrop.

- [ ] At least one sequence of physical bravado that is larger than life. The impossible rope swing, the three-against-one duel, the ship boarded in full sight of a harbour fleet. Readers arrive expecting something that cannot happen in real life and happens beautifully here.

- [ ] A code of honour the protagonist actually lives by. It need not be simple or conventional — pirates can have codes — but the protagonist must believe in something beyond personal survival. The code creates the moments where self-interest and principle collide.

- [ ] A loyal companion or ensemble who are more than furniture. The sidekick earns their moments. Whether comic, tragic, or steadfast, they should have a perspective distinct from the protagonist's and at least one scene that is entirely theirs.

- [ ] Momentum: the story must always be moving toward the next complication. Swashbuckler has no tolerance for introspective interludes that stop the plot. Even a scene of rest or romance should shift some element of the situation before it ends.
