# Swashbuckler Genre Killers

What causes DNF (Did Not Finish) and one-star reviews. Each item below has ended manuscripts in beta or books on shelves.

- [ ] A protagonist who is grim and humourless. This is the single most common swashbuckler failure. The genre's contract with the reader is that adventure is exhilarating, and the hero embodies that feeling. A brooding, self-pitying, or relentlessly serious protagonist kills the spirit of the genre entirely — no matter how skilled they are with a blade.

- [ ] Combat that is brutal rather than elegant. Realistic gore, detailed suffering, and visceral injury descriptions belong to grimdark, not swashbuckler. Action in this genre is stylised — think cinematic choreography, not field surgery. Crossing into extended brutality signals a genre mismatch that readers will not forgive.

- [ ] Period setting without period texture. A story set in 1680 Tortuga that could equally be set in a modern city (with swords instead of guns) has wasted its setting. Readers come for the world. Social rank, religious authority, the specific political tensions of the era, the physical reality of ships and courts and cobblestones — these are not optional colour, they are the medium through which the story moves.

- [ ] A romantic interest who is merely passive. The love interest who waits, hopes, and is eventually rescued signals that the author has not understood the genre. Swashbuckler's most memorable romantic figures (Milady de Winter, Buttercup, Elizabeth Swann) are active, capable, and capable of surprising the protagonist. A passive prize-figure drains tension from the romance and insults half the readership.

- [ ] Wit that disappears when the stakes rise. If the dialogue sparkles in tavern scenes but goes flat in duels and dungeons, the author is only half-delivering the genre. Pressure is when swashbuckler characters are most themselves. A hero who becomes silent and mechanical under threat has stopped being a swashbuckler hero.

- [ ] A villain who is merely an obstacle, not a worthy adversary. If the antagonist could be replaced by a locked door and the plot would feel the same, the story lacks a true villain. The antagonist must be smart enough and powerful enough that outwitting them means something — and ideally charming or interesting enough that readers almost enjoy watching them scheme.

- [ ] Pacing that stalls for long internal monologue. Swashbuckler externalises everything. Introspection is brief, purposeful, and quickly replaced by the next event. A protagonist who spends three pages examining their feelings in a moment of action has stopped the genre dead. The internal life must be revealed through choices and quips, not reflection.

- [ ] An ensemble that vanishes after the first act. If the loyal companions are present for the opening and then functionally disappear or become reactive passengers, the story loses the camaraderie that makes swashbuckler work. Brotherhood and loyalty are structural, not decorative.

- [ ] A climax that is purely physical. If the final confrontation is won by strength or luck alone, without wit, cunning, or the specific skill the protagonist has been developing throughout the book, the ending fails the genre. The blade wins the duel, but the brain won the war — swashbuckler endings should honour both.
