Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do | Don't |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **DO** root darkness in character choice and consequence. | **DON'T** rely on gore or shock value for its own sake. |
- | **DO** let your characters be morally compromised and flawed. | **DON'T** create purely evil villains without a trace of logic or tragedy. |
- | **DO** make the physical and emotional toll of the journey visible and lasting. | **DON'T** let characters heal quickly or without scars. |
- | **DO** use vivid, sensory details to build a palpable mood. | **DON'T** over-describe with meaningless adjective chains. |
- | **DO** ensure dialogue is layered with subtext, ritual, and hidden power plays. | **DON'T** have characters state their feelings or plans plainly. |
- | **DO** structure your story around a pivotal, costly bargain or curse. | **DON'T** resolve major conflicts with unearned luck or deus ex machina. |
- Template for a Bargain/Contract Scene:**
- 1.  **Ritual Opening:** A formal recognition of the act. ("You have come to barter. Speak your desire.")
- 2.  **Stated Desire:** The protagonist states what they want, usually in simple, dire terms. ("I need my sister's soul returned.")
- 3.  **The Offered Terms:** The other party states the explicit price. ("I will weave her a new soul from moonlight and memory. For this, I require your voice.")
- 4.  **Clarification & Trap:** The protagonist seeks to understand. The terms are clarified, often revealing a hidden catch. ("For how long?" / "For as long as she draws breath. And know this: the soul will be pure, but it will not remember you.")
- 5.  **The Acceptance:** The protagonist agrees, often with a ritual phrase or action. (Elara nodded, a single tear cutting through the grime on her cheek. "By my breath, I agree.")
- Dialogue By Social Class/Species:**
-    **Peasant/Villager:** Direct, pragmatic, laced with superstition and local idiom. ("Ain't right, them lights in the marsh. Granda said they lead you in to drown, just to steal your shadow.")
-    **Nobility/Courtier:** Flowing, politic, metaphorical. Indirect insults and flattery. ("Your radiance outshines the court's tarnished silver, my lord. A beacon, though some moths may find it... blinding.")
-    **The Fae/Witch/Ancient Being:** Archaic, rhythmic, literal-minded with words. Speaks in riddles and binding statements. ("I will grant you the strength of the river's rush. But rivers only flow one way, little stone. They never return to their source.")
-    **Pitfall:** A setting that's generically "dark" (perpetual rain, gloomy castles) without specific, symbolic detail.
-    **Fix:** Tie the environment to the central conflict or a character's psyche. Is the forest overgrown because of a sleeping curse? Are the castle's stones weeping black sap from a buried sin?
-    **Pitfall:** A protagonist who remains a passive victim of the plot.
-    **Fix:** Ensure they make active, difficult choices—even bad ones—that drive the story forward. Their agency is key to the tragedy or triumph.
-    **Pitfall:** Magic that solves problems without cost, weakening stakes.
-    **Fix:** Before any magic is used, define its limit and its price. Show the payment being extracted, visibly and painfully.
-    **Pitfall: ** A "twist" that merely inverts a trope (the princess is the villain) without deeper exploration.
-    **Fix:** Ask *why*. What trauma, logic, or compelling philosophy drives the inversion? Make their path understandable, even if not condoned.
-    **Pitfall:** Dialogue that is either too modern/slangy or stiffly archaic.
-    **Fix:** Use a restrained, timeless vocabulary. Avoid contemporary slang. For ancient beings, use slightly inverted syntax ("Forgotten I had not that promise") and concrete, natural metaphors.
