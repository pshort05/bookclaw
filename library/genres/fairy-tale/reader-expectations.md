Fairy-tale fiction operates inside a simplified moral universe where archetypal characters, rule-following magic, and earned transformation deliver the emotional satisfaction of the oldest story structures.

## Tone & Mood

Timeless and declarative — the prose should have the cadence of something told aloud. Emotional states are large and clear rather than nuanced and ambiguous: grief is total, joy is radiant, cruelty is unmistakable. The tone sits between solemnity and wonder; whimsy without weight reads as parody, while weight without wonder reads as allegory. The moral universe is simplified but not simplistic — good and evil are legible categories, but what constitutes goodness is tested rather than assumed. Darkness is present and should not be sanitized; fairy tales have always been about the real dangers of the world rendered in archetypal form.

## Pacing

Structured and episodic, following the logic of task and consequence rather than psychological realism. Events occur in threes — three trials, three gifts, three nights, three wrong choices before the right one. The pace is steady and purposeful: no scene exists without advancing the protagonist's transformation or revealing the rules of the magical system. Digressions and subplots are minimal. The ending arrives with narrative inevitability — readers should feel it was the only possible conclusion once the logic of the tale is established.

## Setting Conventions

A secondary world that is geographically vague but ecologically specific: deep forest with its own rules, a castle where every room has significance, a market town where magical commerce operates openly, a sea with a bottom that is a kingdom. The setting should feel like a place that exists outside historical time — no dates, no clocks that matter, no reference to real-world geography. Nature is animate and responsive: forests conspire, rivers have preferences, mountains remember. Rules of the world must be consistent and discoverable — magic is a system with costs and limits, not arbitrary intervention. The setting is not incidental; it is the moral architecture of the story made physical.

## Character Archetypes

The Protagonist-Who-Must-Earn, typically the youngest, the overlooked, or the apparently least suited — their virtue is demonstrated through action rather than stated. The Donor or Helper who provides the key object or knowledge but only to one who demonstrates worthiness. The Antagonist whose cruelty or greed is the generative force of the plot — often a stepparent, a sorcerer, or a ruler who has violated the natural order. The Trickster Figure who operates outside the protagonist/antagonist axis and whose assistance or hindrance reveals the tale's deeper logic. Transformation — of form, station, or understanding — is the central event that resolves the narrative.

## Length & Format

40,000–80,000 words for standalone novels; shorter novellas are common and respected in the form. Simple declarative sentences and active verbs. Past tense, third-person limited or omniscient. Avoid interiority that is too elaborate — fairy-tale characters act and speak rather than ruminate. Chapter breaks should feel like the natural pauses of oral telling.
