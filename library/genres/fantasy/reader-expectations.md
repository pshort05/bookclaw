Fantasy fiction builds worlds where the impossible operates by discoverable rules, and the protagonist's journey through that world illuminates something true about power, identity, or what it means to be human.

## Tone & Mood

Varies enormously by subgenre, but the foundational fantasy register is one of wonder held in tension with danger — the world is larger and stranger than ordinary life, and that strangeness cuts both ways. Readers come to fantasy for immersion: the prose must make the invented world feel lived-in and consequential, not decorative. Moral complexity is the contemporary expectation: clean good-versus-evil frameworks are acceptable if the story earns them, but flat heroism without cost reads as juvenilia to adult readers. The emotional core — however much magic surrounds it — must be rooted in recognizable human experience: grief, ambition, loyalty, the desire to matter.

## Pacing

Variable by scope, but the opening must establish the rules of the world and the protagonist's place in it before the central conflict fully activates. World-building delivered through action and dialogue lands better than dedicated exposition chapters. The middle act should complicate whatever the protagonist believed at the start — about the world, about their allies, about their own nature. Fantasy readers tolerate longer page counts than most genre readers, but length must be justified by genuine narrative density, not repeated world-building or circular character processing.

## Setting Conventions

Secondary world with a coherent internal geography: named regions with distinct cultures, climates, and political structures. Magic systems must have discoverable rules and consistent costs — arbitrary magic that solves whatever problem arises destroys narrative tension. Economies matter: who grows food, who controls trade routes, who funds armies shapes what the conflict is actually about. Religion and cosmology should be internally consistent and have real influence on how characters behave, not be window dressing. The world should feel as though it existed before the protagonist arrived in it and will continue after they are gone. Maps are conventional and expected in physical editions; readers will use them.

## Character Archetypes

The Reluctant or Chosen Protagonist whose special capacity is a burden as much as a gift. The Mentor whose knowledge is partial and whose death or failure transfers responsibility to the protagonist. The Trickster or Morally Ambiguous Ally whose loyalties are unclear and whose assistance comes with hidden cost. The Antagonist who believes, from within their own logic, that they are right — flat villainy is a genre liability. The Community or Found Family who represent what the protagonist is fighting for and whose vulnerability raises the stakes of every decision.

## Length & Format

90,000–120,000 words for standalone; 100,000–150,000 per book in a series, where the world investment amortizes across volumes. Third-person close or omniscient; multiple viewpoints are common in epic-scope work. Series are the commercial norm; standalone fantasy must deliver complete thematic closure even if world elements remain open.
