## Essential Checklist

- A succinct, practical style guide for writing fantasy fiction has been created, covering the essential elements writers need to craft authentic, immersive fantasy narratives.[1][2][3]
- Third-person close/limited is highly immersive—essentially first-person with third-person pronouns. It provides deep access to one character's thoughts per scene while maintaining narrative flexibility. This is extremely popular in contemporary fantasy.[26][28]
- Highly immersive—essentially first-person with third-person pronouns. Provides deep access to one character's thoughts, feelings, and perceptions per scene while maintaining narrative distance. This is extremely popular in contemporary fantasy. The reader experiences the story very intimately but you maintain flexibility to switch POV characters between scenes or chapters.
- Destiny versus free will creates productive tension in countless fantasy narratives. Prophecies suggest predetermined futures, yet heroes typically must choose to fulfill or resist their fates. This theme speaks to universal human questions about agency, purpose, and whether our lives follow scripts or our own authorship. Contemporary fantasy often subverts prophecy tropes, questioning whether apparent destiny actually represents self-fulfilling cycles rather than cosmic inevitability.[32][33][45][46]
- The Mentor/Sage guides heroes, providing wisdom and often magical training. Gandalf, Dumbledore, and Obi-Wan Kenobi exemplify this archetype, though the mentor's death or departure often catalyzes the hero's growth toward independence. The Shadow/Villain represents the hero's opposite, embodying qualities the protagonist must overcome externally and internally. Effective villains possess comprehensible motivations rather than existing as pure evil—Magneto's extremism grows from trauma, making him simultaneously sympathetic and dangerous.[49][50][51][53]
- Magic must feel both wondrous and consequential.
- Clarity of Effect: Whether your magic system is hard or soft, its effect on the world and characters should be clear. The reader must understand the stakes of a spell being cast.
