- Skip greetings, weather talk, and "How are you?" exchanges unless they serve character development or plot advancement. Readers don't need to see characters say hello and goodbye. Keep dialogue brief, impactful, and always moving the story forward or revealing character.
- In medieval-style fantasy, characters should use general rather than technical terms. They ask the healer for "something for the pain," not for specific pharmaceutical compounds. This keeps your worldbuilding coherent and prevents jarring modern intrusions.
- Too much description bogs down your narrative and causes readers to skim. Too little leaves them spatially lost or unable to visualize your world. Focus on story-relevant details that establish mood, advance plot, or reveal character. Not every room needs a full inventory—give readers just enough to ground them in the scene.
- Setting as Character

Don't just describe a tavern—show your character wiping spilled ale off the sticky table, hearing the crackle of the hearth fire, smelling roasting meat. Readers need recovery time to process events and connect emotionally with characters. Think of it like a rollercoaster—the thrill comes from the contrast between the climb and the drop.

Give readers scenes that explore relationships, internal thoughts, memories, and backstory between the sword fights and magical battles. Even in "slower" scenes, maintain tension through unresolved conflict, unanswered questions, or looming threats.

- Fantasy is a genre of speculative fiction characterized by supernatural or magical elements that do not exist in our known reality. Unlike science fiction, which grounds its extraordinary elements in technological or scientific plausibility, fantasy embraces the impossible—magic, mythical creatures, alternate worlds, and forces beyond natural law. The defining feature of fantasy is that authors need not rely on history or natural laws to create coherence in their narratives, allowing for complete imaginative freedom within the internal logic of their created worlds.[3][4][5][6]
- The early twentieth century saw fantasy find a new home in pulp magazines, particularly Weird Tales (founded 1923), which published fantasy, science fiction, and horror alongside one another. This period produced Robert E. Howard's Conan the Barbarian stories, establishing the Sword and Sorcery subgenre with its focus on muscular heroes, episodic adventures, and visceral action. Fritz Leiber coined the term "Sword and Sorcery" in 1939 to describe this style of fantasy.[1][7][8][11]
- This era witnessed fantasy's diversification beyond Tolkienesque high fantasy. Anne McCaffrey's Dragonriders of Pern (beginning 1968) blended fantasy with science fiction elements. Robert Jordan's Wheel of Time series (beginning 1990) expanded epic fantasy to unprecedented length and complexity. The genre demonstrated increasing sophistication in characterization, moral ambiguity, and thematic depth.[7][8][1]
- The rise of "romantasy"—fantasy with prominent romantic elements—represents one of the most significant recent developments. Sarah J. Maas's A Court of Thorns and Roses series and Rebecca Yarros's Fourth Wing (2023) have achieved massive commercial success, with Fourth Wing selling over 2.7 million copies and staying on the New York Times bestseller list for 18 weeks. These works blend epic fantasy worldbuilding with romance novel emotional intensity, attracting readers who might not have previously engaged with fantasy.[5][19][20][21]
- Social media, particularly TikTok's BookTok community, has transformed how fantasy reaches audiences, creating viral phenomena and democratizing book discovery beyond traditional publishing channels. This digital ecosystem has enabled rapid fanbase building and direct author-reader interaction, fundamentally changing fantasy's cultural circulation.[19][20]
- The Fantasy Genre Family Tree: Nine major fantasy subgenres and their defining characteristics, from High Fantasy's elaborate fictional worlds to Romantasy's blend of magic and romance.
- Quest narratives remain central to fantasy's form, providing clear dramatic momentum as characters journey toward specific goals—whether retrieving artifacts, defeating dark lords, or discovering personal identity. The physical journey often parallels internal character development, with external obstacles reflecting psychological challenges. Portal fantasy adds another layer, using the transition between mundane and magical worlds to explore themes of belonging, identity, and the relationship between reality and imagination.[3][30][31][32][33][23][24][34][22]
- Fantasy worldbuilding generally follows either top-down or bottom-up approaches. Top-down worldbuilding begins with broad strokes—continents, climate, historical epochs—then zooms into specifics. Bottom-up worldbuilding starts with a specific location or character, gradually expanding outward as the story requires. Many authors employ hybrid methods, developing broad frameworks while focusing detail on story-relevant elements.[35][36]
- Successful worldbuilding establishes consistent internal rules even when depicting impossible phenomena. If characters breathe on an airless moon, readers need plausible explanations within the story's logic. This consistency enables suspension of disbelief—readers accept fantastical elements because they operate according to reliable principles. The "primary world" details (gravity, weather, biology) that match our reality help ground the "secondary world" fantastic elements (magic, monsters, divine intervention).[36][35]

Contemporary fantasy often subverts prophecy tropes, questioning whether apparent destiny actually represents self-fulfilling cycles rather than cosmic inevitability.[32][33][45][46]

"Found family" narratives, where characters create non-biological kinship bonds, have become increasingly prominent, reflecting contemporary values around chosen relationships and community. These themes resonate particularly strongly with readers who feel marginalized or displaced in their own lives.[33][45][47][46][32]
- Character Archetypes
- Fantasy draws heavily on archetypal character types rooted in Jungian psychology and mythological traditions. The Hero/Heroine archetype embodies courage, honor, and the drive to protect others, though contemporary fantasy often complicates this with flaws and moral compromises. Aragorn in The Lord of the Rings represents the archetypal hero, while more recent protagonists like Kaz Brekker in Six of Crows embody the morally ambiguous anti-hero variant.[32][48][49][50][51][52][29]
- The Trickster injects humor and disruption, using wit to expose hypocrisy and challenge authority. Merry and Pippin in The Lord of the Rings provide comic relief while also demonstrating unexpected courage. The Orphan archetype resonates deeply in fantasy, representing vulnerability, resilience, and the search for belonging. Harry Potter's orphanhood shapes his entire character arc, from his humble beginnings to his ultimate confrontation with a villain who similarly lost his parents.[50][51][29][32][49]
- The Magician archetype seeks knowledge and power, often becoming manipulative in pursuing understanding. This archetype can manifest as mentor (Gandalf) or villain (Saruman), depending on whether wisdom serves others or selfish ambition. Contemporary fantasy increasingly features complex characters who embody multiple archetypes or subvert archetypal expectations entirely.[29][54][49][50]
- The quest narrative provides clear structure and purpose, driving characters through challenges toward specific goals. Whether seeking artifacts, rescuing captives, or overthrowing tyrants, quests create narrative momentum and opportunities for character development through trials. The journey itself often matters more than the destination, with characters transformed by their experiences.[22][33][23][24][34][32]
- Tragic backstories motivate characters, explaining their drives and vulnerabilities. While sometimes deployed superficially, effective use of trauma explores how past pain shapes present behavior without defining characters entirely by suffering. The "ancient evil awakening" trope creates existential stakes, positioning characters against threats that transcend personal conflicts to endanger entire worlds.[33][45][32]

Leigh Bardugo's Six of Crows features diverse casts where race, disability, and sexuality inform but don't solely define characters. This evolution reflects both changing social attitudes and recognition that diverse perspectives generate richer storytelling.[16][55][58][59][60][61][52]
