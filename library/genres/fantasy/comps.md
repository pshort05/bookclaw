## Comparable Titles

- Engage multiple senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) to create immersion. Use specific, meaningful details—quality over quantity. Avoid overwhelming readers with excessive description.[6][8][9][5]
- Use archaic language sparingly and consistently. While it can enhance immersion, it can also alienate readers if overdone. If uncertain about maintaining archaic patterns, stick to coherent modern prose.[3][11][12]
- Balance descriptive detail carefully. Too much bogs down narrative; too little leaves readers lost. Focus on story-relevant details that establish mood or advance plot.[18][19][20][17][5][6]
- Vary rhythm between high-tension action and quiet reflection. Create ebb and flow, not constant intensity. Readers need breathing room to process events and connect with characters.[19][20][17][18]
- Third-person multi-POV is most common in epic fantasy. It allows multiple perspectives, moral complexity, and vast scope while showing different viewpoints of the same events.[25][26][27]
- First-person creates maximum intimacy and immediacy. It limits knowledge to narrator's perspective, making it effective for mysteries, character-driven stories, and single-arc narratives. Worldbuilding is more challenging in first-person because you can't pull back to explain things the character already knows.[27][28][29][25]
- Read dialogue aloud—every line. Speaking reveals awkwardness eyes miss.[2]
- Trust reader intelligence. You don't need to explain everything—imply, suggest, and let readers fill gaps.[15]
- Fantasy permits heightened, slightly poetic style that might feel excessive in other genres. Use this freedom in service of story and reader experience, not to showcase vocabulary or worldbuilding prowess. Authenticity matters more than archaism. Clarity matters more than cleverness. And your unique voice matters more than following any rules.[34][35][4][1]
- Start simple. Avoid trying to be "fancy" in your initial drafts. Beautiful language should emerge naturally from your mastery of metaphor and rhythm, not from forced ornamentation. The best style flows from your authentic voice—when readers compliment your language choices, you should think "Really? I didn't plan it that way...it just came to me."
