## Failure Modes

- Timelessness is fundamental to fantasy writing. Use simple, strong subject-verb-object constructions that create authenticity without flowery archaisms. As demonstrated in HBO's ROME, you don't need antiquated language to evoke otherworldly settings—just avoid expressions that feel distinctly modern.[1]
- Show, don't tell through sensory details and character actions that display emotion rather than narrating it directly. Don't write "He was scared"—write "His pulse thundered in his ears as his trembling hand fumbled for the key".[5][6][7]
- Engage multiple senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) to create immersion. Use specific, meaningful details—quality over quantity. Avoid overwhelming readers with excessive description.[6][8][9][5]
- Avoid anachronisms rigorously. Characters shouldn't say "screw you" in medieval settings or reference modern concepts. Match language to your world's development level.[2][10][3][11][1]
- Employ non-specific vocabulary for world elements. Characters request "medicine" not "penicillin," visit "the apothecary" not "the pharmacy". Avoid referencing Earth-based historical events or figures.[3]
- Avoid small talk unless it serves character or plot. Skip greetings and weather discussions. Keep dialogue brief, impactful, advancing story or revealing character.[15][16]
- Use figurative language strategically. Employ simile and metaphor purposefully, but don't overdo it. Know instinctively when to be figurative versus literal.[24][4][1]
- Vary scene length to control pace. Short scenes create urgency; longer ones allow detailed exploration. Mix both to avoid monotony.[18][19][17]
- Avoid "telling" words like "I heard," "she saw," "he smelled". Write "Thunder cracked overhead" not "She heard thunder".[5][6]
- Trust reader intelligence. You don't need to explain everything—imply, suggest, and let readers fill gaps.[15]

- Start simple. Avoid trying to be "fancy" in your initial drafts. Beautiful language should emerge naturally from your mastery of metaphor and rhythm, not from forced ornamentation. The best style flows from your authentic voice—when readers compliment your language choices, you should think "Really? I didn't plan it that way...it just came to me."
- Show, Don't Tell
- Provide sensory details and character actions that display emotion and situation rather than narrating them directly. Make readers experience the story, not just read about it. Don't tell us "He was scared"—show us "His pulse thundered in his ears as his trembling hand fumbled for the key."
- Engage multiple senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) to create immersion. Use specific, meaningful details—quality over quantity. Avoid overwhelming readers with excessive description. Pick the sensory details that matter most to the scene's mood or the character's experience.
- Avoid Anachronisms
- Avoid contemporary slang like "sup," "cool," or "OK" in medieval-style fantasy unless you're deliberately writing an alternate modern world. These terms instantly remind readers they're reading a book rather than experiencing another world.
- Use non-specific terms for your world's elements. Have characters request "medicine" rather than "penicillin" or visit "the apothecary" rather than asking for "aspirin." Avoid referencing Earth-based historical events, figures, or specific modern inventions that wouldn't exist in your fantasy world.
- Avoid Small Talk
- Skip greetings, weather talk, and "How are you?" exchanges unless they serve character development or plot advancement. Readers don't need to see characters say hello and goodbye. Keep dialogue brief, impactful, and always moving the story forward or revealing character.
- "Don't lie to me."
- "Don't lie to me." She stepped closer, studying his face.
- Use worldbuilding through description by showing how characters interact with their environment. Don't just describe a tavern—show your character wiping spilled ale off the sticky table, hearing the crackle of the hearth fire, smelling roasting meat. Let your setting pose challenges (treacherous mountain terrain, political unrest) and reveal culture (how people dress, what they value, their social hierarchies).
- Use simile and metaphor strategically—they're your friends in fantasy, but don't overdo it. Know instinctively when to be figurative ("her voice sounded like smoke and honey") and when to be literal ("she crossed the room"). Highlight your favorite metaphors from writers you admire, analyze why they work, then let that knowledge sink into your subconscious.
- Short scenes and chapters create urgency and quicken pace—use them during high-stakes moments. Longer scenes allow for detailed exploration of setting, emotion, and character—use them when you want readers to slow down and absorb. Vary scene length throughout your narrative to avoid monotony and control the reading experience.
- Avoid "telling" words: "I heard," "she saw," "he smelled." Instead: "Thunder cracked overhead" not "She heard thunder."
- Trust your reader's intelligence. You don't need to explain everything. Imply, suggest, and let readers fill in gaps.
- Hard magic systems feature clearly defined rules, costs, and limitations. Brandon Sanderson's Allomancy in Mistborn, where specific metals grant specific powers when ingested and "burned," exemplifies hard magic. Patrick Rothfuss's Sympathy in The Kingkiller Chronicle, which requires energy transfers between similar objects, similarly operates according to quasi-scientific principles. Hard magic enables authors to use magical solutions to plot problems without feeling like cheating—readers understand the rules and can anticipate possibilities and limitations.[41][42][18][17]
- Soft magic systems remain mysterious and undefined, emphasizing wonder over explanation. J.R.R. Tolkien's magic in The Lord of the Rings works this way—Gandalf's abilities remain vague, contributing to the sense of ancient, unknowable power rather than a tool to be mastered. George R.R. Martin's magic in A Song of Ice and Fire similarly resists systematic explanation, appearing unpredictable and dangerous. Soft magic works well for creating atmosphere and avoiding the problem of "why doesn't magic solve everything?".[43][18][17][41]
- LGBTQ+ representation has similarly expanded, with authors like T.J. Klune creating fantasy worlds where queer identities are normalized rather than exceptional. Leigh Bardugo's Six of Crows features diverse casts where race, disability, and sexuality inform but don't solely define characters. This evolution reflects both changing social attitudes and recognition that diverse perspectives generate richer storytelling.[16][55][58][59][60][61][52]
- Sense of Wonder: Use evocative, sensory language. Describe the smell of ancient stone, the hum of latent magic, the shimmer of a dragon's scales. Don't just state that something is magical; make the reader feel it.
- Avoid Anachronisms: Steer clear of modern slang, idioms, and concepts that would break immersion.
- Avoid: "It is what it is," "literally," "on the same page," "no-brainer."
- High/Epic Fantasy: Use contractions (don't, can't, it's) sparingly to create a more formal or timeless tone.
