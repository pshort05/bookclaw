# Workplace Romance — Reader Expectations

The reader's promise centers on the intersection of professional competence and romantic
vulnerability. The genre guarantees a narrative where career ambition and romantic pursuit are
inextricably linked — a relatable, immersive escape that balances the intellectual rigor of a
career with the emotional intensity of a developing relationship.

## Tone & Mood
Alternate between "sharp, witty, and competent" during professional sequences and "vulnerable,
heightened, and immersive" during romantic beats. The overarching mood is aspirational but
grounded: a tangible modern workplace — deadlines, HR protocols, office politics — but slightly
glossier, far more banter-heavy, and more emotionally charged than reality. Never veer into
genuine corporate dystopia or grim realism; the tone is an antidote to real-world anxiety. Humor
is snappy and dialogue-forward, letting characters show intelligence and mutual respect through
verbal sparring.

## Pacing
A dual-track progression: alternate romance beats with plot and career beats, operating on
problem-solution hinges — a professional crisis forces romantic proximity, or a romantic
complication threatens a professional milestone. Avoid slow, contemplative decay; readers expect
a brisk, forward trajectory propelled by external deadlines (a looming product launch, a
promotion announcement, a board meeting, the end of a joint business trip) that compress the
timeline and force the characters to confront their feelings faster than they would in a vacuum.

## Setting Conventions
Closed-loop professional ecosystems that force interaction and establish clear hierarchies and
rules:
- **Corporate empires & billionaire playgrounds** — glass-walled executive suites, high-stakes boardrooms, competitive assistant desks, sprawling family conglomerates.
- **STEM & academia** — research labs, engineering firms, university departments, biotech startups; grant-funding competition and intellectual posturing.
- **Specialized & blue-collar industries** — hospitals, professional sports organizations, architecture firms, public radio, luxury lifestyle brands; fertile ground for women breaking barriers in male-dominated spaces.

The setting must impose rules, codes of conduct, and professional risk that serve as the primary
external obstacles. The threat of HR violations or public scandal supplies the friction that
sustains a slow burn.

## Character Archetypes
- **The competent professional (often the FMC)** — driven, organized, fiercely ambitious; masks personal vulnerability with professional perfectionism, and holds distinct goals outside the relationship.
- **The stoic / grumpy executive (often the MMC)** — hyper-focused, guarded, cynical, demanding, but harboring a hidden capacity for loyalty and protectiveness unlocked only by the romantic lead.
- **The golden-retriever / sunshine colleague** — optimistic, emotionally intelligent, capable; disarms the stoic archetype through persistence, humor, and warmth.
- **The meddling colleague / found family** — the supporting cast supplying comic relief, emotional sounding boards, and the interconnected network that will star in later standalones.

## Length & Format
Aim for **70,000–100,000 words**; the commercial sweet spot is **85,000–90,000**. Under 70,000
feels underdeveloped for a dual-arc narrative; over 100,000 flags pacing issues in a contemporary
setting (YA workplace/school romances run shorter, 60,000–80,000). **First-person dual POV**,
alternating chapters, is the dominant expectation — it lets the reader hold the dramatic irony of
mutual pining each lead hides from the other. **Present tense** is the trend-forward default for
immersive intimacy; past tense is acceptable but less common. Architecture is **interconnected
standalones**: each book gives a new couple a complete arc within the same workplace or friend
group, with prior leads recurring as side characters to maximize backlist retention.

## Heat Levels
Signal sexual content clearly and market it accurately. The genre spans:
- **Level 1 — Clean / Sweet:** closed door; emotional connection and banter; intimacy stops at kissing.
- **Level 2 — Sensual / fade-to-black:** intimacy implied and approached, but the scene fades before explicit acts.
- **Level 3 — Steamy / open door (mild):** at least one on-page scene, emotion-forward, moderately descriptive.
- **Level 4 — Spicy / explicit:** open door, high detail, multiple scenes with explicit terms — common in mainstream adult workplace romance.
- **Level 5 — Erotic:** highly explicit and frequent, often with kink or power-exchange; the plot frequently serves the intimacy.
