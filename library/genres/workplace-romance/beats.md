# Workplace Romance — Beats

The dominant structure is *Romancing the Beat* (Gwen Hayes), four phases, continuously
alternating the romance arc with the professional plot arc so advancement in one complicates the
other. Billy Mernit's 8-beat rom-com structure or a three-act-with-midpoint-hinge work too,
provided they hit the same emotional milestones.

## Phase 1 — Setup
- **Introduce Protagonist 1 (1–5%)** — establish career goals, daily professional life, and the internal flaw blocking real emotional connection.
- **Introduce Protagonist 2 (5–10%)** — the counter-perspective; if they're rivals, show them excelling at the exact skill Protagonist 1 lacks.
- **The meet-cute / collision (10–15%)** — they clash over a professional objective, or share a charged first interaction before learning they must work together.
- **No Way #1 (15–20%)** — each states (internally or to a colleague) why an entanglement would be professional suicide or personally catastrophic.
- **The adhesion (20–25%)** — the narrative forces them together (shared office, mandatory joint presentation, a CEO's order); they can't walk away without sacrificing their careers.

## Phase 2 — Falling
- **No Way #2 (25–30%)** — despite proximity they fight the attraction, usually as charged, sarcastic banter.
- **The inkling of desire (30–40%)** — professional respect morphs into physical awareness; one witnesses the other's hidden competence, kindness, or vulnerability.
- **Midpoint of love (~50%)** — a false high: they cross a physical or emotional line (a first kiss on a business trip) and foolishly believe they can manage a secret arrangement without consequence.

## Phase 3 — Retreating
- **Inkling of doubt (55–65%)** — the secret begins to threaten their primary career goals; paranoia about HR, bosses, or perception sets in.
- **Deepening doubt (65–70%)** — external pressure mounts (a promotion announced, a project fails, a rumor spreads); they blame the relationship as a distraction.
- **Retreat & shields up (70–75%)** — they pull back to their Phase 1 professional facades to protect themselves.
- **The breakup / black moment (75–80%)** — the ultimate conflict: one is perceived to have betrayed the other for advancement, or they break up to save the other's job.

## Phase 4 — Fighting
- **Dark night of the soul (80–85%)** — both achieve their professional goals and find success hollow without the partner.
- **Wake up (85–90%)** — love is worth the risk; their internal flaws, not the job, caused the separation.
- **The grand gesture (90–95%)** — a public, career-risking demonstration of loyalty: defending the partner in a boardroom, resigning from a toxic firm, confessing in front of the office.
- **Whole-hearted & epilogue (95–100%)** — a healthy work-life integration and a glimpse of the shared future, confirming the HEA.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)
- **The professional-competence scene** — early on, both leads execute their jobs flawlessly; readers must respect them as professionals before rooting for them as lovers.
- **The forced-vulnerability scene** — an event strips the corporate armor (a supply-closet panic attack, a ruined presentation, a family emergency) and the rival/colleague offers comfort instead of weaponizing the weakness.
- **The first intimate moment** — the irrevocable shift from antagonistic/platonic to romantic, often outside the office but triggered by a work event.
- **The confession** — an explicit verbal acknowledgment that this is no longer a convenience, a fake relationship, or a purely physical arrangement.
- **The sacrifice / grand gesture** — the climax: one willingly risks standing, wealth, or reputation to protect the other's career or prove devotion.
