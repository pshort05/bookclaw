Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do | Don't |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **DO** structure fights with a beginning (assessment), middle (escalation), and end (climax). | **DON'T** write chaotic, indefinite "blurs of motion" where spatial logic is lost. |
- | **DO** intercut action with the fighter's internal thoughts, strategy, and fatigue. | **DON'T** have characters deliver long monologues during active combat. |
- | **DO** use specific, anatomical language for techniques (e.g., "palm-heel strike to the solar plexus"). | **DON'T** overuse exotic technique names without clear description. |
- | **DO** establish clear rules for any supernatural or internal energy (*qi*) systems. | **DON'T** introduce unexplained power-ups or violate your own system's rules (*deus ex machina*). |
- | **DO** give the antagonist a credible philosophy and a reason for their actions. | **DON'T** create villains who are evil just to be a punching bag. |
- | **DO** include scenes of recovery, introspection, and daily life between action set-pieces. | **DON'T** maintain relentless, exhausting action without narrative breathers. |
- 1. The Formal Challenge / Pre-Fight Exchange**
- > **Structure:** [Greeting/Honorific] + [State of Grievance/Objective] + [Offer of Non-Violent Resolution] + [Ultimatum].
- > **Example (Wuxia):** "Elder Zhao of the Iron Sword Sect. You have poisoned the wells of Mudwort Village to seize their land. Withdraw your men and provide the antidote. Do this, and you may leave with your life. Refuse, and I will enforce the *xia* code."
- 2. The Master's Enigmatic Lesson**
- > **Structure:** [Student's Direct Question] + [Master's Indirect Answer/Parable/Koan] + [Student's Frustration] + [Later Revelation].
- > **Example:** *Student:* "Master, how do I defeat a faster opponent?" *Master:* "Fill this bowl from the river using only this sieve." *[After days of failure, the student realizes the lesson is about defense—the sieve doesn't hold water, but it can deflect the river's flow.]*
- 3. In-Fight Taunt/Realization**
- > **Structure:** Keep it short. [Observation] + [Psychological Needle/Insult].
- > **Example:** "You favor your left leg. That old wound still aches, doesn't it?" or "Your style is rigid, like your father's. And he died on his feet, too."
- 4. Post-Victory Dialogue**
- > **Structure:** [Acknowledgement of the Fight] + [Philosophical Point/Warning] + [Consequence].
- > **Example:** "You fought well. But strength without compassion is a barren mountain. Remember that, as you sit upon your new, lonely throne."
-    **Pitfall: The Invincible Protagonist.** No tension.
-    **Fix:** Give them a meaningful flaw—a technical gap, a moral blind spot, a physical limitation, or an emotional trigger. Their growth must involve overcoming this.
-    **Pitfall: Training Montage as Power-Up.** Unearned progression.
-    **Fix:** *Show* the repetitive, painful, boring, and frustrating work. Focus on one specific skill being honed. Connect the training directly to a later application.
-    **Pitfall: Incoherent Fight Scenes.**
-    **Fix:** Use a "camera" mindset. Establish the space. Track the positions of key combatants. Use paragraph breaks for significant moves. Prioritize cause-and-effect: "Because she ducked the roundhouse kick, she was inside his guard for a rapid series of palm strikes."
-    **Pitfall: Bland Antagonist.**
-    **Fix:** Give them a coherent philosophy that contrasts with the hero's. Make them a dark mirror. What righteous path did they abandon? What pain drives them? Their fighting style should reflect their personality.
-    **Master to Student:** Formal, cryptic, uses parables. Heavy with honorifics ("Disciple Lin"). Speech is often about principles, not techniques.
-    **Student to Master:** Respectful, often frustrated. Uses titles ("Shifu," "Sifu"). Questions are direct; answers are rarely so.
-    **Xia/Knight-Errant to Commoner:** Protective, reassuring, humble. May downplay their own skill. Speech is straightforward and kind.
-    **Xia to Xial (Rival Heroes):** Highly formal, full of ritual challenge language. Acknowledges each other's school and reputation even while preparing to fight.
-    **Underworld Enforcer to Target:** Direct, brutal, laced with threat. Minimal formality. May use street slang or criminal jargon.
-    **Tournament Fighter to Opponent:** Can range from respectful sportsmanship to arrogant trash-talk, depending on character. Often includes a pre-fight bow or salute.
