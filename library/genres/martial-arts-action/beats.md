## Obligatory Scenes

- For a Fight Scene:**
-  [ ] Have I established the LOCATION and any environmental hazards/opportunities?
-  [ ] Have I established the COMBATANTS' initial positions and mindsets?
-  [ ] Does the fight have a clear TRIGGER (first move)?
-  [ ] Am I using short sentences for speed and long ones for impact/setup?
-  [ ] Am I engaging multiple SENSES (sound, smell, touch)?
-  [ ] Am I tracking INJURY and FATIGUE realistically?
-  [ ] Does the fight end with a clear, decisive ACTION (not just stopping)?
-  [ ] What is the IMMEDIATE AFTERMATH (breathing, pain, consequences)?
- For a Training Scene:**
-  [ ] What SPECIFIC SKILL or principle is being worked on?
-  [ ] What is the PHYSICAL and MENTAL strain on the protagonist?
-  [ ] Is there a measurable PROGRESSION or repeated failure?
-  [ ] Does the MASTER (or method) provide a key insight, however oblique?
-  [ ] How does this link to a future story need or character flaw?
- For a Dialogue/Philosophy Scene:**
-  [ ] Does this conversation reveal character MOTIVATION or WORLDVIEW?
-  [ ] Does it advance the PLOT (provide information, make a decision)?
-  [ ] Does it touch on a core THEME of the story?
-  [ ] Is the speech style appropriate to the characters' relationship and context?
- Martial Arts Action is a genre that dramatizes the physical, philosophical, and spiritual journey of mastering combat arts. The narrative core is the development and application of martial prowess against worthy adversaries. It is defined by the centrality of physical conflict, the importance of a personal code (a *Way*), and the transformative power of discipline. Key subgenres include:
-    **Wuxia (Heroic Martial Arts):** Set in a fictionalized ancient or historical China. Characters are often *xia* (knights-errant) who follow a chivalric code (*xia yi*), wield *qi* (vital energy), and perform superhuman feats (*qinggong*). Morality and honor are paramount.
-    **Kung Fu/Crime:** Often set in modern or early 20th-century settings (e.g., Hong Kong crime dramas). Focuses on grounded, stylistic fighting (Wing Chun, Drunken Fist) used for personal justice, protection, or underworld conflict.
-    **Tournament Arc:** Narrative driven by a structured competition, allowing for varied opponents, escalating stakes, and clear progression.
-    **Revenge Tragedy:** A classic structure where the protagonist's quest for vengeance drives the plot, often leading to moral compromise and a pyrrhic victory.
-    **Progression Fantasy/Cultivation:** Focuses on systematic power growth through training, meditation, and overcoming bottlenecks, often within a hierarchical world of martial arts schools and sects.
