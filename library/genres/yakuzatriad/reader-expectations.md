Crime fiction set within Japanese yakuza or Chinese triad organized crime hierarchies, where loyalty codes, ritual violence, and the conflict between duty and personal conscience drive both plot and moral drama.

## Tone & Mood

The genre's defining tension is between the surface beauty of its world — the elaborate codes, aesthetics, and rituals of organized crime culture — and the brutal reality those codes enable and conceal. Readers expect a tone that takes the organizations seriously as social structures while never romanticizing their violence. The same loyalty that creates deep brotherhood makes betrayal catastrophic when it occurs. Characters serve hierarchies older than they are, and the price of belonging is surrender of autonomous moral judgment. The core theme across both yakuza and triad traditions is giri-ninjo: duty versus humanity, the protagonist torn between following orders and doing what is morally right. The atmosphere is dark and sumptuous, with violence that arrives abruptly and ends fast.

## Pacing

Pacing follows the rhythms of organizational life: extended periods of institutional routine — enforcing territory, navigating internal politics, managing criminal operations — punctuated by sudden crises of loyalty, violence, or exposure. The drama is primarily interpersonal and hierarchical rather than chase-driven. Readers accept long sequences of character and relationship development between action beats; this is where the moral weight accumulates. Climaxes are often confrontations between characters who know each other deeply, where the choice to act or refuse is the story's true climax rather than any physical event.

## Setting Conventions

Settings are urban, specific, and culturally grounded. For yakuza fiction: contemporary Japan (Tokyo, Osaka, Kobe) with attention to the kobun-oyabun hierarchy and front business networks. For triad fiction: Hong Kong, Taiwan, Macau, mainland China, or diaspora communities in global cities, with attention to rank structures and territorial operations. Both require cultural authenticity: rituals, language, aesthetics, and social customs rendered with specificity rather than generic "Asian crime" atmosphere. The organizations should feel like functioning institutions with their own internal logic, not simple criminal gangs.

## Character Archetypes

The **Young Initiate** entering the organization lets the world be revealed naturally through their learning curve. The **Mid-Rank Lieutenant** caught between organizational loyalty and a specific human obligation is the genre's core moral protagonist. The **Boss or Patriarch** carries genuine charisma and wisdom alongside capacity for ruthlessness; neither pure villain nor idealized father. The **Outsider or Half-Blood** — living between the criminal world and legitimate society — provides moral perspective without requiring an ignorant viewpoint character. Romantic subplots frequently involve someone from outside the organization, embodying the giri-ninjo tension at the personal level.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–110,000 words. Series following a character's rise through an organization work well; standalone novels are best structured around a single operation, betrayal, or moral test. Translation fiction in this tradition has shaped reader expectations toward literary quality and moral seriousness alongside plot momentum. Violence should be precise rather than spectacular.
