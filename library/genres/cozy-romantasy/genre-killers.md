Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do's | Don'ts |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | Focus on internal emotional conflict. | Create high-stakes, life-or-death external plots. |
- | Build a rich cast of supportive secondary characters. | Leave the protagonists isolated without a community. |
- | Weave magic into daily, domestic life. | Design complex, hard-magic systems that require extensive explanation. |
- | Use dialogue for witty banter and heartfelt reveals. | Rely on miscommunication as the primary long-term conflict. |
- | Describe sensory details that evoke warmth & comfort. | Use graphic, grim, or horrifying descriptions. |
- | Let the romantic relationship be the central plot engine. | Make the romance a subplot to a larger fantasy quest. |
- | Resolve the Dark Moment through communication & growth. | Use betrayal, violence, or cruelty to create separation. |
- Template: [Context] + [Character Voice] + [Emotional Subtext] + [Cozy/Magical Detail]**
-    **Playful Banter:**
-    *"You've got flour in your hair," he said, his voice low.*
-    *"It's a professional hazard of baking confrontation muffins," she retorted, not meeting his eye.*
-    *"Here." He reached out, gently brushing a strand away. His fingertips were surprisingly warm. "The storm outside is making the oven-ghost restless. Might need a calming song."*
-    **Vulnerable Confession:**
-    *"I'm not good at... this," she whispered, gesturing between them. The only light came from the softly glowing moss on the cottage walls.*
-    *"At what?" he asked, his teacup hovering midway to his lips.*
-    *"Letting people in. My magic tends to overgrow things. Literally. I'm afraid of tangling someone up in my thorns."*
-    **Pitfall:** The conflict feels too trivial or boring.
-    **Fix:** Ensure the internal stakes are deeply personal and emotional. The fear (of rejection, of not being enough) must feel real and relatable.
-    **Pitfall:** The love interest is too perfect (a "Gary Stu" or "Mary Sue").
-    **Fix:** Give them a relatable flaw tied to their emotional wound—they're messy, overly cautious, stubborn, or socially awkward. Their "perfection" should be seen through the protagonist's loving gaze.
-    **Pitfall:** The world lacks tension or any conflict.
-    **Fix:** Introduce small, local tensions—a competing business, a stubborn council member, a mischievous magical pest, a brewing village feud over the Harvest Festival decorations. Keep it community-scale.
-    **Pitfall:** The magic is an afterthought or a deus ex machina.
-    **Fix:** Integrate magic into character and setting. Let it be imperfect, character-specific, and used in daily life. The solution to the Dark Moment should not be "a new spell," but magic used in a creatively emotional way.
-    **The Gentry / Noble:** Polite, slightly formal, but not archaic. May use more complete sentences. Their softening is shown by dropping titles and using colloquialisms. *"Miss Brightwood, your assistance was... remarkably effective."* → *"Elara, that was brilliant."*
-    **The Artisan / Tradesperson:** Practical, direct, with vocabulary related to their craft. Humor is dry. *"The enchantment on your door hinge wasn't failing; it was bored. Gave it a more interesting pattern to monitor."*
-    **The Scholar / Librarian:** Precise, prone to tangential facts, but deeply passionate about their interest. Rambles when excited. *"Ah, your phoenix cat is exhibiting standard fledgling teleportation anxiety. According to Grimoire's Bestiary, page 442, a weighted blanket often helps... may I?"*
-    **The Earthy Community Elder (Baker, Gardener, Innkeeper):** Wise, folkloric, uses metaphorical language related to their trade. *"Love's not a loaf you can rush, dear. It needs to proof in its own time."*
