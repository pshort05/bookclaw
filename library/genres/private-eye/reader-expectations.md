Mystery narrated from the perspective of a private investigator who operates outside official channels, in a morally complex world where the truth costs something and the law is rarely the same as justice.

## Tone & Mood

The defining tone is cynical and atmospheric. Readers expect a narrator who has seen enough of human nature to be unsurprised by its worst expressions but who still, against their better judgment, cares about getting it right. The prose style is typically spare, observational, and dry-humored — the PI's inner monologue is as important as the dialogue, offering a running commentary on a world viewed with detachment, irony, and earned pessimism. The mood tends toward the nocturnal, the rain-soaked, the overlit and underlit. Glamour is always adjacent to corruption. The atmosphere should feel lived-in and specific, not generic.

## Pacing

The investigation drives the pacing through a rhythm of lead, complication, and dead end. Readers expect the PI to pursue a thread, have it cut, and find another — not through stupidity but because the people with answers are actively withholding them. Interviews and confrontations are the primary scene types; action sequences exist but should feel dangerous rather than exciting, because the PI is one person without backup. The mid-story revelation that reframes everything — the client lied, the victim wasn't innocent, the case connects to something larger — is a genre expectation. Resolution brings truth but not necessarily justice.

## Setting Conventions

Setting is atmosphere made physical. Classic private-eye territory includes urban environments — Los Angeles, Chicago, New York, or a fictional equivalent — rendered at street level, from cheap offices to expensive addresses that reveal how the money flows. Modern iterations extend the setting to suburbs, small towns, or international locations while retaining the social stratification that is central to the genre's critique: the PI moves between social classes that do not otherwise intersect, and that mobility is both professional skill and source of alienation. The physical environment should reflect the moral environment — decay, surface gloss, hidden rot.

## Character Archetypes

The PI protagonist is defined by operating alone, outside institutions, with a personal code that sometimes conflicts with the law but rarely with justice as they understand it. They are not a hero in any conventional sense — they are effective and principled within limits. The client is rarely fully honest. The police contact is either an obstacle or an ally with their own constraints; the relationship is never simple. Supporting characters serve as windows into different social strata: the wealthy with secrets, the desperate with nothing to lose, the mid-level corrupt who make the system function.

## Length & Format

Private-eye novels run 70,000–90,000 words. First-person past tense is the genre's most established voice — it positions the reader inside the PI's head while the "past" framing implies the narrator survived to tell it. Series built around a single PI are the dominant commercial format, with readers developing long-term investment in the protagonist's recurring relationships and slow-burn character arc across books. Standalones work but typically require a stronger hook to compensate for the absence of series continuity.
