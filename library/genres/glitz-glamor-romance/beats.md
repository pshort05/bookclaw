- Glitz romance succeeds through the intersection of escapism and emotional authenticity. - Glitz and glamor romance represents one of the most indulgent and escapist subgenres within the broader romance fiction landscape, centered on the lives and loves of the wealthy elite and jet-set socialites. This subgenre emerged as a distinctive category of romance literature in the late twentieth century, evolving from earlier "bonkbuster" traditions to become a sophisticated exploration of luxury, power dynamics, and romantic connection among characters who inhabit worlds of extraordinary wealth and privilege. Unlike some romance subgenres that focus primarily on emotional intimacy or personal growth, glitz romance prioritizes the aesthetic and material dimensions of its characters' lives—their penthouses, yachts, designer wardrobes, exotic destinations, and the complex social hierarchies of the ultra-wealthy—while maintaining the genre's core commitment to romantic fulfillment and emotional resolution. This guide examines the historical development, defining characteristics, thematic elements, key tropes, important authors, and contemporary works that define this glamorous segment of romance fiction, as well as its relationship to the closely related but distinct billionaire romance subgenre.
- The glitz and glamor romance subgenre emerged as a distinctive category during the 1980s, a transformative decade for romance fiction more broadly. This period saw the rise of professional career authors and the expansion of category romance, alongside the iconic "clinch cover" era dominated by the image of Fabio, whose muscular physique became synonymous with romance publishing. However, glitz romance distinguished itself by shifting focus from purely intimate encounters to the opulent settings and social status of its characters. The genre drew inspiration from earlier literary traditions, particularly from the "bonkbuster" novels that had gained popularity in Britain and America during the 1970s and early 1980s.[1][2][3]
- The foundational texts of glitz romance emerged during this period, with authors like Judith Krantz and Danielle Steel establishing the template for the subgenre. Krantz's novel Glitz became emblematic of the category, following three heiress nieces who stood to lose their inheritance when their uncle remarried, immersing readers in a world of wealth and familial intrigue. These novels established the essential formula: glamorous characters navigating romantic entanglements against backdrops of extraordinary luxury, often involving wealthy families, prestigious institutions, and high-stakes financial considerations. Steel's prolific output further cemented the subgenre's popularity, with her novels featuring wealthy protagonists and complex romantic relationships set within luxurious environments.[4][2]
- Understanding glitz romance requires contextualizing its relationship to the broader "bonkbuster" tradition, a term applied to several British and American authors who wrote sexually explicit, drama-filled romance novels during the late twentieth century. Authors including Jilly Cooper, Jackie Collins, Judith Krantz, and Shirley Conran became recognized as key writers of the bonkbuster novel, creating lengthy, plot-heavy narratives filled with numerous characters, sexual encounters, and social intrigue. Jilly Cooper's Riders, in particular, became a foundational text, selling over one million copies and establishing many conventions that would persist in glitz romance. Cooper's work is noted for its humor, explicit sexuality, and sophisticated depictions of upper-class life, with characters such as Rupert Campbell-Black becoming iconic figures in the genre.[2][3]
- The transition from the 1980s bonkbuster tradition to contemporary glitz romance has involved both continuities and significant evolution. While the emphasis on wealthy, glamorous characters remains constant, modern glitz romance has incorporated contemporary concerns including workplace dynamics, social consciousness, and more nuanced character development. The emergence of the Romance Writers of America (RWA) and the professionalization of genre writing contributed to increased attention to craft, character consistency, and emotional authenticity, even within escapist narratives.[1]
- Contemporary authors working in glitz romance have built upon the foundations established by Krantz and Steel while incorporating influences from other literary and entertainment traditions. Victoria Fox, Rebecca Chance, and other modern practitioners bring literary sophistication and contemporary social awareness to the subgenre, while maintaining its essential appeal to readers seeking escapism and luxury. The rise of self-publishing platforms and the democratization of book distribution has also allowed new voices to enter the subgenre, creating diverse representations of wealth, romance, and social status.[2]
- Defining Characteristics: Form, Content, and Context
- At its core, glitz romance fiction is fundamentally defined by its setting and the socioeconomic status of its protagonists. The subgenre focuses on "the jet-set elite and celebrity-like characters", whose lives are characterized by extraordinary access to resources, social privilege, and cultural capital. These characters inhabit penthouses in major metropolitan centers, maintain residences in exclusive vacation destinations, travel via private aircraft, and participate in high-profile social events such as charity galas, gallery openings, and destination weddings. The materiality of luxury functions not merely as backdrop but as central to the narrative's emotional landscape; the possession of beautiful objects, access to exclusive spaces, and membership in elite social circles constitute fundamental aspects of character identity and romantic aspiration.[5][6]
- The settings of glitz romance typically include major international cities—New York, Los Angeles, London, Paris, Milan—as well as exclusive resort destinations and the homes of the ultra-wealthy. These spaces are rendered in precise, sensory detail: the aesthetic qualities of designer fashion, the atmosphere of exclusive nightclubs, the amenities of luxury resorts, and the architectural grandeur of significant properties. The jet-set lifestyle invoked in the subgenre's definition originates in the historical emergence of commercial jet travel in the 1950s, which transformed the meaning of elite socialization. The term "jet set" itself was coined by journalist Igor Cassini in 1949, describing a social group of wealthy and fashionable people who traveled the world to participate in social activities unavailable to ordinary people. Though the term has evolved with the democratization of air travel, the concept remains central to glitz romance: characters move fluidly between exclusive destinations, participating in social worlds inaccessible to the non-wealthy.[7]
- Unlike many romance subgenres that focus primarily on the relationship between two protagonists, glitz romance frequently employs ensemble casts and multiple narrative perspectives. This structural choice reflects the influence of the bonkbuster tradition and allows authors to explore multiple romantic storylines, family dynamics, and social networks simultaneously. A single glitz romance novel might follow several characters through interweaving plotlines, each with distinct romantic arcs, though typically with one central couple receiving primary narrative focus.[4][2]
- This narrative structure creates several effects. First, it allows for the exploration of how wealth and social status influence romantic possibilities across different contexts and for different characters. A protagonist who is a self-made billionaire experiences romance differently than one who inherited generational wealth; a character navigating entry into elite circles has distinct romantic challenges compared to one born into privilege. Second, the ensemble structure permits extended exploration of social hierarchies and the ways status functions within relationships. Third, it enables the incorporation of secondary romance plots that provide additional escapism and complexity to the overall narrative.
- Despite the expanded narrative scope characteristic of glitz romance, romantic love remains central to the genre's appeal and ultimate resolution. However, the integration of romance differs from some other contemporary romance subgenres in its subordination to larger narrative arcs involving career ambitions, family intrigue, and social maneuvering. Romance functions as a significant plot element but not necessarily the sole organizing principle of the narrative. This distinction matters because it allows glitz romance to maintain literary sophistication and narrative complexity while still satisfying genre expectations for romantic fulfillment and emotional resolution.[5]
- The romantic relationships depicted typically occur between characters of similar or complementary social status, though increasingly glitz romance incorporates "rags-to-riches" dynamics where a protagonist from a more ordinary background enters the world of wealth through romantic connection. When such class differences exist, they frequently generate both romantic tension and opportunities for social commentary on the nature of wealth, privilege, and love's capacity to transcend material circumstances.
- One emerging theme within contemporary glitz romance, particularly as the subgenre has evolved away from purely escapist narratives, is the concept of "luxury as intimacy". In this framework, wealth functions not merely as a setting or character trait but as the primary means through which characters express devotion and understanding toward one another. A character with unlimited resources demonstrates love through their capacity to fulfill their partner's dreams, solve intractable problems, and provide material security—not through coercion or power, but through genuine recognition of what their beloved needs. This transformation of wealth from a source of power imbalance into an expression of care and intimacy represents an important evolution in how contemporary glitz romance engages with economic disparity.[8]
- The luxury-as-intimacy concept suggests that when characters possess "this much power, this much influence, this much at stake" and consciously "choose each other," that choice becomes transformative. Rather than money solving problems through plot convenience, it becomes a vehicle for exploring how characters with extraordinary means use them to demonstrate commitment. This thematic development allows glitz romance to interrogate wealth and its meanings while maintaining the genre's fundamental appeal.[8]
- A persistent theme throughout glitz romance involves the relationship between social status and personal identity. Many glitz romance narratives explore how characters navigate the elite social worlds they inhabit—whether born into them or newly arrived through romantic connection, business success, or other means. These narratives frequently examine the question of authentic belonging: can one truly belong to a community of the ultra-wealthy if one lacks the historical lineage, inherited expectations, and social conditioning that typically characterize membership? This theme gains particular intensity in narratives featuring protagonists who enter elite circles as outsiders, creating romantic tension that derives not only from interpersonal attraction but from social positioning and the anxieties associated with class transition.
- Glitz romance also explores the ways that maintaining status requires performance and careful management of image. Characters must navigate complex social rituals, understand unwritten rules of elite behavior, and strategically present themselves to media, family, and competitors. The romantic relationship often becomes a space where characters can potentially drop this performance and reveal authentic selves—though this itself creates dramatic tension when partners from different social backgrounds must negotiate what authenticity looks like and what it means to love someone across such stark material divides.
- Power dynamics feature prominently in glitz romance, reflecting both the genre's heritage in the bonkbuster tradition and its engagement with realistic aspects of wealth and influence. Characters with significant economic resources typically possess correlate power in their professional lives and romantic relationships; the romantic arc often involves these powerful characters becoming vulnerable in their emotional lives, allowing love to soften defenses and reshape priorities. This dynamic permits exploration of the psychological and emotional costs of power while maintaining the fantasy element of extraordinary wealth and privilege.
- Given glitz romance's engagement with exclusive social worlds, themes of scandal and exposure frequently appear. Characters with significant public profiles navigate the threat of paparazzi attention, tabloid speculation about their romantic lives, and the ways that intimate information can become public spectacle. The anxiety surrounding exposure—whether of past indiscretions, family secrets, or genuine romantic feelings—generates dramatic tension. For characters accustomed to controlling their public image, the vulnerability of having personal information exposed or romantic feelings revealed represents a distinctive form of jeopardy.
- This thematic element connects glitz romance to broader contemporary anxieties about privacy, celebrity culture, and the difficulty of maintaining intimacy in a culture of surveillance and documentation. Characters must negotiate between authentic emotional connection and the performative aspects of public visibility.
- Many glitz romance narratives incorporate themes of redemption and personal transformation, particularly for characters who have built their wealth through ethically questionable means or who have allowed material ambition to override personal relationships and emotional growth. The romantic relationship often catalyzes this transformation, as a character learns to value intimate connection, family loyalty, or personal integrity over continued accumulation of wealth and power. This theme reflects enduring romance genre conventions while engaging with specific anxieties about the moral costs of ambition and the capacity of love to redeem and transform even the most morally compromised individuals.
- One of the most prevalent tropes in glitz romance involves characters entering into marriages for practical reasons—business advantages, inheritance requirements, visa status, or the consolidation of family empires—only to develop genuine romantic feelings as the narrative progresses. This trope permits exploration of the boundary between contractual arrangement and authentic emotion while maintaining the material stakes of wealth and power. A marriage undertaken to access an inheritance, secure a business merger, or maintain family harmony becomes genuinely transformative when the characters discover unexpected emotional and sexual compatibility.[9][10]
- Closely related to the marriage of convenience trope, the fake relationship requires characters to present themselves as romantically involved to achieve some external goal—preventing unwanted romantic pursuit, satisfying family expectations, managing public perception, or securing a business opportunity. The dramatic tension emerges when the performance gradually becomes indistinguishable from genuine feeling. This trope generates particular intensity in glitz romance contexts because the public nature of elite circles means that maintaining the pretense requires performance across multiple social contexts and before discerning observers attuned to social nuance and potential romance.[9]
- Glitz romance frequently incorporates the enemies-to-lovers trope, wherein characters who initially regard each other with antagonism or contempt experience a gradual recognition of compatibility and attraction. In the context of glitz romance, enmity might derive from business competition, family rivalry, social tension, or personality clash within elite circles. The movement from hostility to romantic connection generates particular narrative intensity when coupled with the material stakes of wealth and power.[9]
- The trope of a character known for casual romantic conquests and emotional unavailability undergoing transformation through meeting "the one" who finally changes everything persists across many romance subgenres but appears with particular frequency in glitz romance. Often the hero is a wealthy CEO, entrepreneur, or inherited-wealth heir known for his string of short-lived relationships and reluctance to commit, only to have meeting the heroine fundamentally alter his priorities and desires. This trope permits exploration of the fantasy that love possesses the power to redeem even the most resistant individuals while maintaining the underlying anxieties about fidelity and the genuine possibility of authentic connection within highly stratified social contexts.[10][9]
- While glitz romance and billionaire romance are related subgenres that share numerous characteristics and often overlap considerably in contemporary publishing, important distinctions differentiate their emphases and narrative priorities.
- Narrative Focus and Scope: Glitz romance typically employs wider narrative scope, incorporating multiple characters, subplots, and perspectives. The subgenre inherited this characteristic from the bonkbuster tradition, which valued expansive narratives with numerous characters and plotlines. Billionaire romance, by contrast, generally maintains tighter focus on the central romantic relationship, often employing a dual-perspective narrative structure that alternates between the hero and heroine. The billionaire romance protagonist's wealth is typically far more central to the narrative than in glitz romance, where wealth functions as one element among many.[10]
- The Source and Nature of Wealth: Billionaire romance typically features protagonists who have accumulated extraordinary wealth through entrepreneurial activity or business acumen—the "self-made" billionaire represents an archetypal figure in the subgenre. These characters possess not only money but also the power and agency to shape their world through decision-making and strategic action. Glitz romance, while certainly featuring self-made wealthy individuals, more frequently incorporates characters with inherited wealth, old money, family dynasties, and generational privilege. The moral implications and narrative possibilities differ significantly between self-made wealth (which often permits redemption arcs and narratives of personal transformation) and inherited wealth (which generates different anxieties about authenticity and deservingness).[11][9][10]
- Thematic Priorities: Billionaire romance emphasizes the fantasy of being rescued by wealth, of having all problems solved through the hero's resources and power, and of the hero's vulnerability and emotional need offsetting his practical dominance. The subgenre explores the allure of power and the fantasy that power can be wielded for love rather than domination. Glitz romance, drawing on the bonkbuster tradition, emphasizes social intrigue, complex character networks, scandal and exposure, and the navigation of elite social worlds. While billionaire romance asks "what would it be like to be loved by someone with complete material power over the world?" glitz romance asks "what is it like to live among the ultra-wealthy, and how do authentic relationships form within such communities?"[10][9]
- Sexual Content: Billionaire romance typically features more explicit sexual content and often emphasizes the erotic dimensions of power imbalance and the hero's possessiveness and desire. Glitz romance, while certainly featuring sexual content, often subordinates explicit scenes to broader narrative concerns about social positioning, family dynamics, and romantic complication. Billionaire romance's emphasis on the erotic dimensions of wealth and power means that sexual scenes often carry thematic weight within the genre, while glitz romance's sexual content, while important, functions alongside other narrative elements.[12][10]
- The foundational figures who established glitz and glamor romance include Judith Krantz, whose novels such as Glitz and The Jewels of Tessa Kent epitomize the subgenre's focus on wealthy characters, family intrigue, and glamorous settings. Danielle Steel produced prolifically within the subgenre throughout the 1980s and beyond, with her novels featuring wealthy protagonists navigating complex romantic and family relationships. Jackie Collins brought a grittier sensibility to the glamor romance tradition, with works like American Star exploring love, ambition, and scandal against Hollywood and international backdrops. Jilly Cooper established herself as the queen of the British bonkbuster with Riders and subsequent novels featuring upper-class characters, sexual adventure, and complex family networks, making her one of the key writers recognized as defining the bonkbuster tradition. Shirley Conran contributed significantly to establishing the subgenre through her own novels featuring wealthy, glamorous characters.[13][14][3][4][2]
- Victoria Fox has become a significant contemporary voice in glitz romance, with novels such as Hollywood Sinners and Glittering Fortunes following wealthy, glamorous characters through scandal, intrigue, and romance. Fox's work maintains the emphasis on social intrigue and multiple character perspectives characteristic of glitz romance while incorporating contemporary concerns and more sophisticated character development.[2]
- Rebecca Chance represents another important contemporary practitioner of glitz romance, with novels such as Mile High and Killer Diamonds featuring wealthy, glamorous characters navigating scandal, intrigue, and complex relationships within exclusive settings. Chance's novels incorporate themes of celebrity, social status, and the ways that public visibility complicates intimate relationships.[15][2]
- Emily Henry, celebrated for her contemporary romance across multiple settings and scenarios, incorporates glitz romance elements in various works, particularly through her depictions of wealthy characters and luxurious vacation settings. Henry's success in the contemporary romance market demonstrates the continuing popularity of narratives featuring glamorous settings and the intersection of personal wealth and romantic connection.[17][19]
- Talia Hibbert and Jasmine Guillory, prominent contemporary romance authors, bring important diversity perspectives to romance fiction, including works that feature characters of color navigating romantic relationships sometimes in contexts involving varying degrees of wealth and social status. Their contributions represent the genre's increasing attention to diverse representation while maintaining romance's core commitments to emotional authenticity and satisfying romantic resolution.[19][17]
- Christina Lauren - The Paradise Problem (2024): This recent publication exemplifies contemporary glitz romance, featuring an ordinary woman (Anna) and a wealthy heir (West/Liam) whose marriage of convenience was supposed to be temporary but becomes complicated when he requires her to accompany him to a family wedding on a private island resort. The novel incorporates classic glitz romance elements: luxurious settings, wealthy family dynamics, the outsider perspective of the ordinary woman encountering ultra-wealthy social circles, and the complications that arise when authentic feeling develops within an arrangement initially conceived as transactional.[18][16][17]
- Sadie Kincaid - Made and subsequent titles in her billionaire romance series represent contemporary dark romance with glitz elements, featuring alpha male protagonists with extraordinary wealth and the complex, often possessive romantic dynamics characteristic of this subgenre variation.[20][21]
- Emily Shepherd, Greer Hennessy, and other emerging contemporary authors contribute to the ongoing evolution of glitz and glamor romance, often incorporating contemporary social consciousness and diverse character representation while maintaining the subgenre's commitment to escapism through wealth and luxury.
- Glittering Fortunes by Victoria Fox: Features glamorous characters navigating wealth, privilege, and romantic complexity[2]
- Killer Affair by Rebecca Chance: Explores the world of reality television with glamorous characters engaging in scandal and intrigue[2]
- An emerging variation incorporates darker psychological elements, moral ambiguity, and sometimes morally questionable character behaviors within glitz/glamor romance frameworks. These narratives maintain the luxury settings and wealth focus of traditional glitz romance while incorporating psychological complexity, power dynamics that verge on the coercive, and less tidy moral resolutions. This variation appeals to readers seeking more sophisticated engagement with power, wealth, and romance while maintaining the escapism associated with glitz settings.
- A significant subgenre within glitz romance focuses specifically on characters within entertainment industries—actors, musicians, directors, producers, and others navigating Hollywood, the music industry, or international entertainment sectors. These narratives often incorporate themes of fame, paparazzi attention, the pressure to maintain public image, and the difficulty of genuine intimacy within contexts of constant observation and potential exposure.
- While sometimes classified separately, royal and aristocratic romance shares considerable overlap with glitz romance, particularly in its emphasis on inherited wealth, exclusive social worlds, and the complications of romantic relationships within contexts of public visibility and dynastic obligation. The distinction between glitz romance featuring characters within aristocratic families or royal circles and "pure" royal romance can be somewhat blurred, particularly in contemporary works.
- Some glitz romance emphasizes settings and situations involving luxury travel, exclusive resorts, destination weddings, and the romantic possibilities that emerge when characters from different worlds encounter one another in glamorous travel contexts. These narratives exploit the escapist fantasy of luxury vacation settings while maintaining glitz romance's interest in wealthy protagonists and elite social worlds.
- Glitz and glamor romance fiction continues to appeal to readers seeking escapism, luxury fantasy, and romantic fulfillment set within worlds of extraordinary wealth and social privilege. The subgenre's appeal derives not primarily from the literary quality of its prose or the psychological depth of its character development—though contemporary practitioners increasingly deliver sophisticated work in both dimensions—but rather from its commitment to the fantasy of admission to exclusive worlds, the romance that flourishes among the ultra-wealthy, and the emotional authenticity possible within contexts of material luxury. Readers turn to glitz romance to imagine what their lives might be like if money were no object, if access to extraordinary experiences were unlimited, if romantic love could transcend class and social boundaries to create genuine intimacy.
- The subgenre has evolved considerably since its foundational texts in the 1970s and 1980s. While the bonkbuster tradition emphasized sexual content, dramatic plot twists, and broad social satire, contemporary glitz romance increasingly emphasizes character development, emotional authenticity, and more nuanced engagement with the moral and psychological dimensions of wealth. Contemporary authors working within glitz romance traditions remain committed to the fundamental appeal of luxury and privilege while incorporating more sophisticated exploration of how wealth shapes identity, relationships, and the possibilities for authentic connection.

- Recurring plot devices, character dynamics, and narrative patterns.
- The Revenge Makeover: The heroine is scorned or ruined by a powerful man in Act 1. She reinvents herself (plastic surgery, new name, new career) and returns years later to destroy him.
- Sleeping Your Way to the Top (Subverted): Characters accused of this usually prove they have the talent to back it up. Alternatively, the antagonist does this and fails because they lack substance.
- Ambition vs. Happiness: The central question: "Is it lonely at the top?" Characters often sacrifice love for success, only to spend the third act trying to reclaim it.
- The Mirror: Characters constantly checking their reflection. Applying lipstick as armor. The fear of aging (wrinkles are enemy combatants).
- Objects or concepts representing abstract ideas.
- The Gown: The armor of the glitz heroine. The scene of zipping up a couture dress before a confrontation symbolizes preparing for battle.
