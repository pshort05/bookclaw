Readers expect a romance where the luxury setting is aspirational fantasy made tangible, and the emotional journey happens against a backdrop of wealth, celebrity, or high society rendered in convincing and pleasurable detail.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is warm, indulgent, and knowing — readers understand they are participating in wish-fulfillment, and the best entries honor that contract without condescension. The glamour must feel real enough to be enjoyable and excessive enough to be escapist; the skill lies in making conspicuous wealth feel like texture rather than spectacle. Beneath the surface glitter, readers expect genuine emotional stakes: the protagonist's access to this world is typically conditional, contested, or psychologically costly. Wit and sharp social observation are assets; snobbishness directed at readers is a liability.

## Pacing

Pacing follows standard romance structure — meet, attraction, complication, black moment, resolution — with scene work designed to maximize the pleasures of the setting. Gala scenes, fashion moments, travel sequences, and luxury-environment details should be written with sensory richness because they are load-bearing genre elements, not decoration. The romantic tension should escalate through social situations specific to the world: the public performance of a relationship that is not yet real, the class or background gap made visible at a formal event, the moment when the protagonist's outsider status creates vulnerability.

## Setting Conventions

The setting is aspirational by definition: the celebrity world, old-money society, fashion industry, luxury hospitality, or international wealth circuit. The specific milieu should be rendered with authentic detail — insider knowledge of how these worlds actually function matters to readers who consume them extensively. Locations should include at least one iconic luxury environment (a particular city, estate, event, or institution) that becomes associated with the central relationship. Season and social calendar can drive plot: the Met Gala, awards season, the racing circuit, the summer house circuit all carry built-in stakes and deadlines.

## Character Archetypes

The Outsider Protagonist enters the glamorous world through talent, circumstance, or relationship — her position is earned but precarious, which generates natural tension. The Established Figure (celebrity, heir, established industry power) has access to everything the protagonist desires but is isolated by it. The Social Gatekeeper enforces the rules of the world and often opposes the central relationship. The Loyal Friend grounds the protagonist in her original identity when the glamorous world threatens to absorb her. The Rival provides social and romantic competition; she may be sympathetic or not but should be competent.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 75,000–95,000 words. Standalones are common; series featuring recurring social worlds (a fashion house, a celebrity family, a luxury hotel chain) work well when each volume resolves one couple's arc while maintaining ensemble continuity. The HEA (happily ever after) or HFN (happy for now) ending is a genre requirement, not optional. Sensory and visual detail throughout the manuscript should be higher than genre average — readers expect to feel immersed in the setting, not merely informed of it.
