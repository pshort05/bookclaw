## Failure Modes

- Common Pitfalls: Seven key mistakes to avoid when writing in the subgenre
- Complexity: Avoid one-dimensional wealthy characters; provide psychological depth, conflicting motivations, and genuine vulnerability
- Avoid wealth serving purely as plot convenience; show realistic complications and constraints that even unlimited money cannot solve
- Brand/Designer Names: Strategic use of specific luxury brands adds texture; avoid excessive name-dropping that feels gratuitous
- Tone: Sophisticated but accessible; avoid purple prose while maintaining sensory richness
- Pacing: Build romantic tension across narrative; don't rush genuine emotional connection even within fast-paced plots
- Multiple subplots should interconnect and resolve thematically; avoid purely tangential plotlines
- Common Pitfalls to Avoid
- Passive Heroine: Avoid characters who exist primarily to be rescued; ensure protagonists drive action and make meaningful choices
- Unearned Resolution: Don't use wealth as plot convenience; show how characters' choices, not money alone, resolve conflict
- Stilted Dialogue: Wealthy characters should sound sophisticated but natural; avoid overly formal or archaic speech patterns
- Shallow Characterization: Avoid reducing characters to their net worth; provide psychological depth and genuine motivation
- Moralizing about Wealth: While social commentary is acceptable, avoid heavy-handed lessons about wealth's corrupting influence

- Adjectival Richness: Don't be afraid of modifiers. This genre thrives on lush, specific descriptions.
- Don't say: "She drank champagne." -> Say: "She sipped vintage Dom Pérignon."
- Don't say: "He drove a nice car." -> Say: "He slid into the leather bucket seat of the Aston Martin."
- Avoid "Cozy": Nothing should feel small or quaint. Even a quiet dinner is a seven-course meal at a Michelin-star restaurant.
