## Failure Modes

- Upper left (Color Light Red): Low Demand, High Saturation; Label as ‘Avoid These Opportunities’
- Paranormal Western Romance integrates supernatural elements—ghosts, witches, shapeshifters, or magical beings—into western settings. These stories blend frontier mythology with paranormal elements and romantic tension. Christian Western Romance centers faith as a significant element in characters' lives and the storyline, incorporating themes of forgiveness, redemption, and divine guidance while maintaining Christian values and avoiding explicit content. LGBTQ+ Western Romance focuses on romantic relationships between LGBTQ+ characters in western settings, bringing representation and diversity to a historically heteronormative genre.[29][36]
- The Low Demand, High Saturation quadrant ("Avoid These Opportunities") contains properties with limited reader appeal despite market presence, including Classic Ostern, Space Western, and Acid Western. These subgenres represent cautionary positions—continued investment may not yield adequate returns despite existing market presence.
- Dialect: Accessible but period-flavored. Avoid heavy phonetic accents that slow reading.
- Avoid: Anachronistic modern slang, overly pithy "action movie" one-liners, or excessive profanity (unless gritty realism is the sub-goal).
- III. Dialogue and Character Voice — Provides specific guidance on creating authentic frontier dialogue without caricature, maintaining regional authenticity while avoiding phonetic distortion. Emphasizes sparse, purposeful speech where silence carries meaning and character voice remains immediately distinguishable through distinct patterns.[7][8][9][10]
- XI. Common Pitfalls — Lists twelve critical mistakes to avoid, from clichéd dialogue to unearned morality.[10][23][9]
- Limited Information: Characters know only what they've experienced. Avoid authorial omniscience that reveals information beyond character knowledge.
- Avoid Caricature: Don't render every frontier character as "yonder" and "kinda slow." Modern readers recognize cliché.
- Period-Appropriate Language: Research historical terminology and avoid anachronisms (characters don't "get coffee" in 1870; they "get some coffee"). Blend Spanish phrases naturally into dialog near Mexican borders.
- Avoid Dialogue Tags: Minimize "he said," "she asked." Use physical action instead: "You leaving?" He poured another drink.
- Sexualized Violence: Avoid using sexual assault as casual plot device. If included, treat with gravity and psychological realism
- XI. COMMON PITFALLS TO AVOID
- Clichéd Dialogue: Avoid stereotypical "Western speak" that feels inauthentic to contemporary sensibilities
- Excessive Explanation: Trust readers to infer subtext; avoid authorial intrusion explaining character motivation
