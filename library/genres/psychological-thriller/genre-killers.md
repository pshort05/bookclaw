## Failure Modes

- Upper left (Color Light Red): Low Demand, High Saturation; Label as ‘Avoid These Opportunities’
- Authors employ various mechanisms to create unreliability: head trauma and cognitive impairment (as in Annie Ward's Beautiful Bad and Tana French's The Witch Elm), addiction and altered consciousness (exemplified in The Girl on the Train), mental illness and dissociation (creating fractured perspectives and reality distortion), and deliberate deception (where narrators knowingly mislead while maintaining convincing personas). The key element is that readers often don't realize they're experiencing an unreliable perspective until late-stage revelations fundamentally reframe earlier events.[7][3]
- Dark psychological thrillers intensify the psychological elements with heightened menace, dread, and disturbing subject matter. These variants often feature serial killers or deeply disturbed individuals as antagonists, with protagonists as worn-out investigators or ordinary people trapped in violent situations. Thomas Harris's The Silence of the Lambs (1988) exemplifies this subgenre, presenting a "dangerous psychological game" between protagonist and cannibal killer, where the boundary between hunter and hunted blurs.[1]
- A significant evolution within the genre involves protagonists who are morally compromised, villainous, or operating in profound moral ambiguity. These narratives present serial killers, con artists, manipulators, and narcissists as point-of-view characters, forcing readers into uncomfortable psychological alliance with deeply disturbing individuals. Michelle Bomer's The Stalker features a predatory con artist as narrator; Alex Michaelides' The Silent Patient (2019) involves a woman accused of murdering her husband; and Lisa Jewell's recent work Don't Let Him In (2025) features a confidence man running a sophisticated con.[14][15][16]
- Don't Let Him In by Lisa Jewell (2025): A #1 New York Times bestseller featuring a sophisticated con man manipulating women through charm and psychological exploitation, this thriller exemplifies Jewell's evolution toward darker, more disturbing psychological exploration and represents the trending rise of con artist thrillers and predatory male protagonists.[14]
- Lisa Jewell: The #1 New York Times bestselling author with over 30 published novels, Jewell has pioneered the domestic psychological thriller and mastered the combination of family drama with bone-chilling suspense. Her recent release Don't Let Him In (2025) demonstrates her continued evolution toward darker, more explicitly disturbing psychological territory, exploring con artistry, psychological manipulation, and predatory behavior.[20][14]
- Con artist and predatory male protagonist thrillers, including Lisa Jewell's Don't Let Him In (2025) and Michelle Bomer's The Stalker, where sophisticated manipulators and psychologically disturbed men serve as protagonists. This trend reflects reader interest in morally complex, disturbing characters and the psychology of manipulation.[14][15]
- Formulaic serial killer procedurals: Traditional police procedurals featuring serial killers following predictable patterns have declined as readers seek greater psychological complexity, character development, and moral ambiguity than procedural structures typically allow.
- Con Artist/Sophisticated Manipulator Protagonists: Lisa Jewell's Don't Let Him In and similar titles center on intelligent, charming con men or psychological predators, reflecting reader appetite for understanding disturbing psychology rather than simply condemning it.[14][15]
- Literary psychological thrillers: Works aspiring to both commercial thriller success and literary merit remain relatively uncommon, with most literary fiction avoiding genre conventions. Authors successfully bridging this gap (like Tana French and Liz Moore) demonstrate reader appetite for sophisticated prose and literary ambition within thriller structures.
- Upper Left Quadrant: Avoid These Opportunities (Low Demand, High Saturation)
- Over-explained plot mechanisms: Thrillers that laboriously justify implausible plot mechanics through psychological explanations (the killer had face blindness, etc.) have diminishing market impact as readers seek grounded psychological motivation.
- Traditional serial killer procedurals: The formulaic structure of investigating serial killers following predictable patterns has become overdone, with readers showing preference for psychological complexity over procedural investigation patterns.
- Con artist and sophisticated manipulator thrillers: The rising success of narratives centering intelligent, charming con men (Lisa Jewell's Don't Let Him In) indicates reader appetite for exploring the psychology of manipulation and predation.[14]
