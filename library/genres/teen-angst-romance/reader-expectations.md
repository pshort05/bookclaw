Teen-angst romance readers want the full emotional spectrum of adolescence delivered through a central love story where the coming-of-age arc and the romantic arc are inseparable.

## Tone and Mood

The defining emotional register is **sincere intensity**. This is not ironic YA rom-com territory. Readers expect to feel the protagonist's longing, humiliation, hope, and devastation in their bodies — the prose should carry emotional weight without melodrama. The tone is earnest, vulnerable, and often achingly self-aware. Moments of humor are welcome but never undercut genuine pain. The mood oscillates between anticipation and dread; readers are here for the full-spectrum experience, not a comfortable ride.

**What to avoid:** Detached or sardonic narration that keeps emotion at arm's length. Adult-toned wit that signals the narrator has already processed what they're living through.

## Pacing

The internal emotional clock drives pacing more than plot events. Readers expect:

- **Slow-burn tension** in the first third — proximity, glances, loaded conversations that are not quite romantic yet
- **Escalating intensity** in the middle third, with setbacks that feel genuine rather than manufactured
- **Emotional reckoning** in the final third — the dark night of the soul must be earned, not sprung

Scene-level pacing is also important: quiet scenes of internal processing must alternate with high-stakes social scenes. Do not stack crisis on crisis; the reader needs breathing room to metabolize emotion. Flashback and reflection are common and acceptable, but must serve the present-tense emotional logic.

## Setting

Setting is not backdrop — it is pressure. High school is the dominant setting: hallways, cafeterias, locker bays, cars, and bedrooms carry enormous social weight because these are the protagonist's whole world. Settings must feel specific and grounded (a particular town, a named school, recognizable geography) rather than generic American suburb.

Common secondary settings: a summer job, a college campus visit, a family vacation or road trip, a creative-arts program, a best friend's house. Each setting should tighten or loosen the social constraints on the protagonist.

The **college threshold** (senior year, application season, the question of "where are you going?") is a productive pressure point: it imposes a natural deadline on the romance and forces identity questions. Use it deliberately if it appears.

## Archetypes

Readers recognize and want subversions of core archetypes. The most common:

- **The Misfit Protagonist** — smart, observant, not fully at home in their social world; the reader identification figure
- **The Magnetic Love Interest** — not necessarily popular in the traditional sense, but carries some quality (talent, confidence, otherness, vulnerability) that makes them hard to look away from
- **The Loyal Best Friend** — often provides comic relief and reality-check; must be a full character, not a sounding board
- **The Antagonist Peer** — bully, rival, or ex; functions to expose the social stakes and test the protagonist's self-worth

Subversion is expected: the jock who reads poetry, the popular girl with a secret wound, the quiet kid who turns out to be the most grounded person in the room. But subversion must be earned through scene and behavior, not stated.

## Length and Format

- **Word count:** 60,000 – 90,000 words is the commercial norm. Literary-leaning titles can reach 95,000; high-concept or shorter voices can land at 55,000. Below 50,000 reads as novella; above 100,000 signals pacing problems to agents and editors.
- **Point of view:** First-person is dominant and strongly preferred by readers. Close third-person is acceptable. Dual first-person POV (alternating chapters) is an established YA device and works well in this subgenre when both voices are genuinely distinct. Avoid omniscient — it creates emotional distance.
- **Tense:** Past tense is standard. Present tense is an accepted stylistic choice (see *The Sun is Also a Star*) but requires consistent execution and a reason — it should heighten immediacy, not exist by default.
- **Chapter length:** Short to medium chapters (1,500 – 4,000 words) are standard; long unbroken chapters slow the emotional momentum. Scene breaks within chapters are common.

## The Reader Contract

Readers of this subgenre accept pain as part of the bargain but require payoff. The romance must be central — not a subplot orbiting a larger adventure. The coming-of-age growth must be genuine: the protagonist at the end cannot be the same person who appeared on page one. Heat level is kissing through fade-to-black; no explicit content. The ending must provide emotional resolution — HEA (happily ever after) or HFN (happy for now) are both acceptable, but ambiguity about the romantic outcome is not.
