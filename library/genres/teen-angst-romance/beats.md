Teen-angst romance follows a recognizable structural sequence; these beats are reader expectations, not optional — skip or misplace one and the book feels incomplete.

## Structural Arc

The overall shape is: isolation → connection → complication → rupture → reckoning → reunion. The coming-of-age arc mirrors this: stasis → disruption → growth → crisis → transformation. These two arcs must reach their turning points near the same structural moments, or the book will feel like two stories loosely sharing a protagonist.

**Act One: World and Want**
Establish the protagonist's internal wound (the thing they believe about themselves or the world that is not quite true), their social reality, and the shape of their desire before the love interest arrives or becomes relevant. The reader must understand who this person is before they start to change. Plant the central thematic question in the first thirty pages.

**Act Two, First Half: Approach**
Proximity and escalating mutual awareness. The protagonists spend time together under circumstances that are not explicitly romantic. Each scene should tighten the emotional connection while the protagonists resist or deny what's happening. This is the slow-burn engine.

**Act Two, Midpoint: The Shift**
Something changes the nature of the protagonists' connection — a moment of genuine vulnerability, an unexpected revelation, a crisis that forces honesty. After the midpoint, denying the romantic dimension requires active effort. The midpoint is not the first kiss; it is the moment when both characters know something has changed.

**Act Two, Second Half: Complications and Stakes**
External pressure (social, family, circumstance) and internal resistance (fear, self-worth, old wounds) intensify. The relationship deepens, but the obstacles are real. This is also where the protagonist's coming-of-age arc crisis occurs — what they believe about themselves is tested, usually through the relationship.

**Act Three: Rupture and Reckoning**
The all-is-lost moment: the relationship breaks. The cause should be embedded in the story's central thematic problem — not a contrived misunderstanding, but a genuine collision of the protagonists' fears or wounds. The protagonist must face their internal wound without the relationship as support. This section is the emotional climax of the coming-of-age arc.

**Resolution: Reunion and Transformation**
The protagonist chooses to act from a changed internal position. Resolution comes from the protagonist's growth, not from external circumstances fixing themselves.

## Obligatory Scenes

These six scenes are specific contractual obligations. Readers notice their absence.

**The Meet (or Re-Meet)**
The first scene in which the protagonists encounter each other with any emotional charge. Must do three things simultaneously: establish who each person is, create some form of friction or charge between them, and make the reader want to see them in the same room again. A flat, purely informational introduction wastes the scene. If this is a re-meet (second-chance trope), the history between them must be legible immediately.

**The Almost Kiss**
A scene — usually in Act Two's first half — that builds physical and emotional proximity to the point where a kiss feels inevitable, then is denied: an interruption, a loss of nerve, a deliberate pull-back. Function: confirms mutual desire without resolving it, forces the reader to keep turning pages. The almost-kiss is often more emotionally charged than the real first kiss. It must be earned by prior scenes building to it; it cannot arrive as the first moment of closeness.

**The First Kiss**
The first real physical expression of the romantic connection. Must carry emotional weight proportional to the story's investment in the relationship. It should happen at a moment of genuine emotional openness, not just a convenient plot lull. The circumstance, setting, and internal state of both characters matter. Avoid the generic "they looked at each other and then suddenly they were kissing" — the reader wants specificity: who moved first, what it felt like, what it meant.

**The Misunderstanding (or Rupture)**
The scene that breaks the relationship or brings it to the brink. The misunderstanding must be grounded in the thematic problem — it should reveal something true about one or both characters' fears or failures, not be a simple "she overheard the wrong thing." If it's a rupture rather than a misunderstanding, one or both characters must have done something that genuinely justifies the break. The reader must feel the break is real, not manufactured.

**The Grand Gesture**
One protagonist makes a public or emotionally exposed move to demonstrate that the relationship is worth the risk of full vulnerability. The gesture should be specific to this story and these characters — not a generic bouquet or apology speech. It should cost the character something real (pride, social safety, comfort) and should grow out of what the story has taught us about what matters to them. It does not have to be literally public, but it must be emotionally exposed.

**The HEA or HFN**
The closing emotional note. HEA (happily ever after) — the relationship is secure and the protagonists' futures are explicitly aligned. HFN (happy for now) — the relationship is real and meaningful but the future is open, often because the threshold (graduation, a move) makes "forever" implausible. HFN is increasingly the standard for teen-angst romance because it is more honest about the developmental reality. What is not acceptable: ambiguity about whether the relationship survived, or a resolution that is romantic but does not address the protagonist's internal arc.
