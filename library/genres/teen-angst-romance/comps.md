Teen-angst romance comps should demonstrate emotional depth, a central romance braided with coming-of-age growth, and a contemporary real-world setting — the eight titles below are the genre's defining reference points.

## The Fault in Our Stars — John Green (2012)

The benchmark for emotional intensity and literary voice in YA romance. Two teenagers with cancer meet at a support group; the romance is inseparable from confronting mortality and meaning. Demonstrates: first-person voice with exceptional interiority, thematic weight carried lightly through dialogue, and a refusal to protect the reader from genuine loss. Comp use: when your book has high literary voice, emotionally difficult subject matter, and a romance that forces characters to confront something larger than themselves.

## To All the Boys I've Loved Before — Jenny Han (2014)

A love-letter premise triggers a fake-relationship trope that becomes real. The warmth, specificity of family dynamics, and the protagonist's distinctive inner life make this the comfort-with-depth benchmark. Demonstrates: family as active pressure on the romance, a protagonist whose identity is closely tied to her cultural and domestic world, and how a light-seeming premise can carry real emotional stakes. Comp use: when your book is warm in tone, family-forward, and uses a romantic premise that evolves into genuine emotional investment.

## Simon vs. the Homo Sapiens Agenda — Becky Albertalli (2015)

A gay teenager navigating a secret online relationship and the threat of outing. The gold standard for voice-driven YA romance: Simon's narration is funny, specific, and deeply anxious beneath the humor. Demonstrates: how coming out and first love can be structurally identical arcs, how secondary characters can be fully drawn without stealing focus, and how to write romantic tension in correspondence. Comp use: when your book features LGBTQ+ identity, a witty/anxious first-person voice, or a romance that is complicated by what must be hidden.

## Eleanor & Park — Rainbow Rowell (2013)

Dual first-person POV following two social outsiders who fall in love over comic books and mixtapes in 1986. The most emotionally unsparing book on this list: Eleanor's home situation is genuinely threatening, and the romance cannot fix it. Demonstrates: dual POV done with radical voice distinction, how class and family trauma shape what teenagers believe they deserve, and how to end a teen romance with honesty rather than fantasy. Comp use: when your book features dual POV, significant class or family difficulty, or a romance that must coexist with a genuinely hard real-world situation.

## The Sun is Also a Star — Nicola Yoon (2016)

Two strangers in New York spend a single day together as one of them faces deportation. Written in present tense with multiple POVs including minor characters. Demonstrates: structural urgency created by a ticking clock, how to give thematic weight to fate and coincidence, and how to execute present tense at novel length with consistent effect. Comp use: when your book has a compressed timeline, high thematic ambition, or explores cultural identity as a central element of the romance.

## Anna and the French Kiss — Stephanie Perkins (2010)

An American girl sent to school in Paris falls for the charming, complicated boy in her friend group. The definitive "setting as character" and will-they-won't-they slow burn. Demonstrates: how to sustain romantic tension across an entire novel without the protagonists getting together until the third act, how a specific and immersive setting elevates romantic stakes, and how to write a love interest who is genuinely flawed in ways that matter. Comp use: when your book features a vivid or aspirational setting, a slow-burn structure, or a love interest whose flaw is the actual obstacle.

## Amy & Roger's Epic Detour — Morgan Matson (2010)

A road trip across America after a family tragedy becomes a journey toward grief and self. Demonstrates: how physical movement and changing setting can structure emotional progress, how to embed grief honestly in a romance without making the book about grief, and the road-trip format as a forced-proximity engine. Comp use: when your book uses travel or physical journey as its structural frame, features loss as a thematic element, or is episodic in structure.

## They Both Die at the End — Adam Silvera (2017)

Two strangers who learn they will die today spend their last hours together. Dual first-person POV. The most emotionally demanding comp on this list — the HFN is structurally predetermined. Demonstrates: how to create enormous romantic investment in a compressed timeline, how thematic weight (mortality, what makes a life meaningful) can be carried by a romance, and how dual POV can allow two full character arcs to intersect without either diminishing the other. Comp use: when your book has an unconventional or bittersweet ending, uses dual POV with structural symmetry, or centers on a romance made urgent by loss or impending separation.
