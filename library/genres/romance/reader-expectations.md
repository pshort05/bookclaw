Readers expect a story whose central spine is a love story, where two people navigate obstacles — emotional, professional, logistical, or circumstantial — on the way to a guaranteed emotionally satisfying ending (Happily Ever After or Happily For Now). These two promises — the love story is the plot, and the ending is optimistic — are the genre's inviolable core; everything else varies.

This is the generic Romance umbrella. It behaves like Contemporary Romance in almost every respect — that is the genre's default mode and its commercial center of gravity — but it is deliberately broader: it also welcomes variations that fall outside strict present-day contemporary, such as historical, paranormal, romantic-suspense, small-town, sports, or lightly speculative settings. Where a book commits fully to one of those, the more specific genre guide (e.g. contemporary-romance, dark-romance, romantasy) is the better fit; use this umbrella when the book is romance-first and the setting is flexible or blended.

## Tone & Mood

Romance spans a wide tonal range — from sharp-tongued banter comedy to emotionally raw women's fiction — but the baseline expectation is warmth, emotional honesty, and optimism about love. The reader must believe the protagonists deserve happiness and that the relationship, once secured, will last. Humor is common and welcomed; even emotionally heavy books typically have moments of levity. The tone should never undercut the sincerity of the emotional stakes. Cynicism is a character flaw to be worked through, not a narrative stance.

## Pacing

Romance moves in waves: attraction, complication, deepening, crisis, resolution. The romantic tension should be established early (within the first few chapters) and maintained through the obligatory dark moment — the point where the relationship appears to fall apart — before the earned resolution. Subplots (professional conflict, family dynamics, friendship, external threat) run alongside the romance but must not displace it. The reader's primary question at every chapter break should concern the relationship, not the subplot.

## Setting Conventions

The setting should feel lived-in and specific; vague or interchangeable locations dilute reader investment. Unlike strict contemporary romance, the umbrella imposes no restriction on period or realism: present-day cities, small towns, workplaces, and milieu communities (sports teams, publishing, food industry, academia) are the default, but a historical era, a paranormal layer, or a suspense/thriller frame are all permitted so long as the love story stays the plot's spine and the setting does not overpower it. When the world carries speculative or historical weight, keep its exposition subordinate to the relationship — worldbuilding serves the romance, never the reverse. Whatever the setting, ground it in concrete, credible detail so the reader invests.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is typically navigating competing demands (career, family, self-worth) while holding a wound or belief about themselves that the relationship will ultimately challenge. Common tropes: enemies-to-lovers, forced proximity, second-chance romance, grumpy/sunshine contrast, friends-to-lovers. The love interest must be a full character, not a fantasy object — readers need to understand why this specific person unlocks growth in the protagonist. A cast of supportive secondary characters (best friend, sibling, colleague) grounds the romance in community.

## Length & Format

75,000–100,000 words for a standalone; series installments often run 70,000–90,000 words. Dual point-of-view (alternating chapters between both protagonists) has become the genre standard over the past decade. Heat level varies from sweet/closed-door to explicit; the market expectation should be matched. An epilogue — showing the couple's future — is strongly expected by readers and its absence is frequently noted in reviews.
