Prose superhero tropes are genre contracts with the reader — execute them with earned specificity rather than rote formula, and subvert them only when you understand exactly what expectation you are dismantling.

## Core Structural Tropes

**Origin story:** Every prose superhero novel must reckon with origins, even if it subverts the convention. The origin is not the story — it is the premise that makes the story possible. Bury it in backstory, start in media res, or invert it, but do not skip the question of how this person became powered and what that rupture cost them.

**Secret identity / dual persona:** The double life is the engine of civilian-world conflict. The pressure to maintain a false self in one domain while being entirely genuine in another generates the genre's most productive dramatic irony. The audience knows what the character's loved ones do not — use that gap consistently.

**Equally matched counterpart:** The villain shares something structural with the hero: a common origin source, a mirrored philosophy, or an equivalent power type. When the counterpart relationship is absent, conflict feels arbitrary. The best superhero prose shows that the villain is what the hero could have become under different conditions.

**Team-up / alliance of rivals:** Two heroes with competing methods or values are forced to cooperate. The friction must be principled, not merely personality-based. The resolution should change both characters rather than confirming one was simply right.

**The Achilles' heel:** A specific, known vulnerability — physical, emotional, or circumstantial — that gives the protagonist a credible path to defeat. Without it, the hero reads as invincible and tension collapses. Establish it early; let the antagonist exploit it before the climax.

**High-stakes escalation:** Each act raises the consequence level. This becomes mechanical without grounding in character cost — ensure each escalation is paid for in something the protagonist values, not merely in destruction-at-larger-scale.

## Power and Identity Tropes

**Puberty powers:** Abilities activated or triggered by adolescence, tied directly to identity formation. Used heavily in school/academy subgenres. The internal crisis of powers-as-metaphor-for-growing-up must be taken seriously, not used as cheap YA shorthand.

**Self-experimentation:** A scientist, engineer, or obsessive creates their own powers through dangerous research. Positions the powered individual as the author of their own transformation rather than a passive recipient of cosmic luck — changes the ethical weight of the origin significantly.

**Rapid mastery:** The hero learns to use their powers implausibly fast. This is a documented reader complaint when unchallenged by cost or failure. If rapid mastery is a feature of your subgenre (power fantasy, progression fiction), make the mastery cost something in a different domain — relationships, secrecy, sanity.

**Powers as personality substitute:** Characters defined entirely by what they can do rather than who they are. Especially damaging with secondary characters. Every powered character needs a want and a fear that have nothing to do with their abilities.

**The chosen one:** A protagonist who receives powers through cosmic destiny rather than earned merit. Readers find passive, destined heroes predictable. If chosen-one framing is used, the protagonist must earn their destiny through choices — not simply fulfill it.

## Worldbuilding Tropes

**The default city:** New York or a functional stand-in where 95% of powered activity concentrates. Choosing a different city — or an invented one — signals deliberate worldbuilding choices to genre-literate readers. Either embrace the default city's cultural weight or justify your choice.

**Minimal collateral damage:** Catastrophic battles end with improbably low civilian casualties. Readers of deconstructive and darker superhero prose increasingly expect real collateral damage and its consequences — survivors, lawsuits, political backlash, grief. A city block destroyed without aftermath is a missed opportunity.

**Institutional containment failure:** Supervillains break out of maximum-security containment at whatever frequency the plot requires. Only works if the in-world explanation is consistent — "they always escape" is not a worldbuilding rule, it is a hand-wave.

## Villain Tropes

**The mirror nemesis:** The villain who began as a hero, or could have. Creates the richest structural opportunities: the climax is a dialogue between two philosophies, not just a clash of power sets.

**The true believer:** The antagonist who is not wrong about the problem, not wrong about the stakes — only wrong about the method. *Soon I Will Be Invincible*'s Dr. Impossible and the Epics of *Steelheart* operate in this space. These villains require the author to give them a genuinely coherent argument the reader cannot fully dismiss.

**Villain concentration:** Intelligent supervillains commit crimes in cities with the highest concentration of heroes, which is objectively irrational. This is a genre artifact from comics geography — either acknowledge it in-world or resolve it.
