Prose superhero fiction's thematic core — the obligation that power creates — is more stable than its tone; every other theme in the genre is an elaboration of that central question.

## Power and Obligation

The genre's foundational theme, and the one readers most reliably expect. The specific formulation matters: not "power requires restraint" but "power creates obligation to others before self." Every plot decision about how the protagonist uses or withholds their abilities is a thematic statement about this question. Stories that ignore this theme — where power is simply a tool the protagonist uses without ethical weight — fail to satisfy readers who came for a superhero novel rather than an action thriller with superpowers.

The most interesting variations push on the obligation's limits: What if the obligation cannot be met? What if the obligation is itself coercive? What if the hero fulfills every obligation and the world is still worse for it?

## Identity Under Pressure

The civilian identity and the hero identity are not two people — they are two facets of one person whose coherence is under constant stress. Prose's advantage over comics is the sustained interiority to render this conflict at depth. The theme is not simply "who am I really?" but "which commitments define me when I cannot honor all of them?" The mask is both protection and prison; the story is about the cost of maintaining it and what happens when it fails.

## Legitimacy of Violence

Superhero fiction is a genre in which the protagonist regularly commits violence against other human beings and the narrative frames this as heroic. This is a genuine ethical problem, and prose — unlike visual media where kinetic action can be aesthetically beautiful — forces the reader to sit inside a character's mind during violence. The question of when violence is legitimate, whether restraint is possible, and who pays the cost of hero-inflicted collateral damage is not optional subtext. It is structural. Stories that do not engage with this question produce villains with incoherent motivations and climaxes that feel morally hollow.

## Isolation and Found Family

Extraordinary power is isolating: the hero cannot be fully known by those they protect. The tension between the desire for genuine intimacy and the necessity of concealment drives most civilian-life subplots. The resolution is usually found family — the team or inner circle who know the full self. The thematic risk is sentimentality: found family rings true only if the protagonists demonstrate it costs something to be known.

## Systemic Corruption vs. Individual Heroism

Can one person — even an extraordinarily powerful one — fix a broken system? The genre's deconstructive strand argues no, and dramatizes this through institutional antagonists (government agencies, corporations, media) that neutralize or co-opt heroism. The earnest strand argues yes, provisionally, with cost. Both positions are available; what is not available is the story that ignores the question. Any world with powered individuals will have developed institutional responses to them, and those responses will reflect the values and interests of whoever built the institutions.

## The Cost of Heroism as Chosen Life

The hero's choice is not the origin moment — that is usually accidental. The hero's choice is every subsequent decision to continue despite the cost. Relationships end. Careers dissolve. Futures narrow. The story must periodically reckon with the aggregate cost in specific, personal terms: not "heroism is hard" as an abstraction, but "this is the third time this year I have missed something that mattered to someone I love." The theme fails when the cost is stated but not dramatized.

## Power as Social Inequality

In any world where superpowers are unequally distributed — by birth, accident, economics, or design — the distribution of power is a distribution of social advantage. Who has powers, how they acquired them, whether they can be bought or suppressed, and who controls access to powered individuals are all political questions. Prose superhero fiction that treats power as politically neutral produces a world that doesn't hold together on examination. The most durable titles in the genre engage this directly: *Wild Cards*, *Worm*, *Hench*, *Dreadnought* all treat power distribution as inseparable from questions of class, race, and institutional control.
