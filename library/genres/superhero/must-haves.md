Non-negotiable elements for prose superhero fiction — their absence produces a story that does not satisfy genre readers, regardless of prose quality or originality.

- [ ] **A power system with consistent, established rules.** The reader must understand what the protagonist can and cannot do, and those limits must not shift to serve the plot. The rules do not need to be explained in an infodump — they need to be demonstrated through action, cost, and failure. Inconsistency is one of the most common causes of one-star reviews in the genre.

- [ ] **Power cost and consequence.** Powers must demand something: energy, physical toll, psychological strain, time, secrecy, or relationship. Power without cost produces a protagonist who cannot credibly lose and a narrative without tension. The cost must be dramatized, not merely stated — show the depletion, the aftermath, the thing the hero cannot do because they spent themselves on heroism.

- [ ] **A villain with coherent internal logic.** The antagonist must have a motivation the reader can understand and, at minimum, track — even if they cannot endorse it. "Evil for evil's sake" is a genre killer. The villain should believe they are correct. Their scenes should make their position feel real. The best prose superhero fiction makes the reader briefly wonder if the villain has a point.

- [ ] **A civilian identity with genuine weight.** The hero's non-powered life must contain relationships, obligations, and wants that matter independently of the hero identity. If the civilian life exists only as a cover story, the dual identity has no cost and no dramatic function. At least one civilian relationship must be actively endangered by the hero life.

- [ ] **An explicit choice to be a hero — or not.** The protagonist must consciously choose heroism at least once, with a real alternative available. Powers do not automatically produce heroes; the ethical decision to act for others at personal cost is what distinguishes a superhero story from an action story. This choice must occur under genuine pressure, not moral certainty.

- [ ] **Escalating stakes grounded in character, not spectacle.** Each act should raise what the protagonist stands to lose, and that loss must be personal before it is global. World-ending threats mean nothing if the reader does not first care about the specific person standing in front of them. Ground the stakes in relationship and identity before scaling to society.

- [ ] **A power system that creates problems, not just solutions.** Powers should complicate the protagonist's life in ways beyond combat. Strength that breaks things the hero did not mean to break. Telepathy that cannot be turned off. Invulnerability that makes intimacy impossible. The power should be both the hero's greatest asset and the source of their most specific difficulties.

- [ ] **At least one scene of genuine, irreversible civilian cost.** The hero's dual life must damage something that cannot be repaired — a relationship ended, a secret broken, an opportunity permanently lost. Without this, the stakes of the secret identity are theoretical.

- [ ] **A protagonist who changes as a result of the story.** The beginning-state and end-state must be measurably different in terms of belief, method, or relationship. Change driven by external plot events (the villain is defeated) is insufficient; the change must be internal and character-driven.

- [ ] **Action sequences with physical and spatial clarity.** Readers of prose superhero fiction cannot see the fight. Every sequence must establish position, constraint, and consequence clearly enough that the reader can track what is happening and why the outcome matters. Visual confusion during fights is a prose-specific failure mode.
