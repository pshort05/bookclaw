The obligatory scenes of prose superhero fiction are not decorative — each is a structural load-bearing point where the reader's investment is either earned or forfeited; missing one leaves a gap the reader will feel even if they cannot name it.

## Beat 1: The Ordinary World / Before

Show who the protagonist is before they become powered. Readers need to know what is at stake — what civilian life this person is giving up, what they want that heroism will make harder. Skip this scene and the cost of heroism has no baseline to be measured against. The pre-powers world does not need to be long, but it must be specific: a specific job, a specific relationship, a specific want that the story will systematically endanger.

## Beat 2: The Origin Trigger / Activation Event

The event that causes, activates, or reveals the protagonist's powers. This is the single most cliche-saturated scene in the genre — documented failure modes include: the death of a loved one used as generic trauma fuel, the radioactive accident with no interiority, the cosmic gift that asks nothing in return. The scene must carry psychological weight specific to this character. The sensation of powers activating is one area where prose can deliver what visual media cannot: the body changing, the terror or exhilaration, the identity rupture happening in real time.

## Beat 3: Limits Discovered / Rules Established

The hero encounters the edges of their power — physical, moral, and circumstantial. This scene establishes the power system's rules, defines what the protagonist cannot do, and makes the audience understand where the character can fail. Its absence makes the protagonist feel like a cheat code. The limits discovered here are the constraints the antagonist will later exploit. Make them specific and consequential.

## Beat 4: First Villain Contact / Antagonist Established

The first scene in which the antagonist is understood as a genuine threat. Not necessarily a full confrontation — the villain's introduction may be indirect, witnessed, or implied — but the reader must finish this beat understanding that the antagonist cannot be defeated simply by showing up with more power. The villain should visibly not lose this encounter. Ideally the hero's response to the villain's existence reveals something about both of them.

## Beat 5: The Public Reaction Scene

Society, government, media, or ordinary civilians responding to the hero's existence. This scene grounds the story in a world rather than a vacuum and establishes the institutional context — supportive, hostile, exploitative, or bureaucratic — in which the hero will operate. Its absence produces a city that feels unpopulated by consequence. The public reaction also defines the social cost: is this person celebrated, feared, regulated, or hunted?

## Beat 6: The Civilian Cost Scene

The moment when heroism directly damages the protagonist's civilian life in a specific, irreversible way. A relationship ends. A secret breaks. A job is lost. A person the hero was protecting is hurt. This is not a training complication — it is a real loss. Without this scene, the stakes of the dual identity remain abstract. The cost must be paid before the climax; paying it at the climax is too late and too convenient.

## Beat 7: Identity Crisis / Unmasking Pressure

The secret or public identity under threat — literal, social, or existential. The protagonist is forced to confront which self is more real, or whether the division is itself the problem. This beat can be externally driven (someone is getting close to the truth) or internally driven (the protagonist can no longer maintain the performance). Either form must force a choice that changes something going forward.

## Beat 8: The Black Moment / Dark Night

Approximately three-quarters through the narrative. The antagonist has the upper hand; the protagonist's plan or method has failed; the hero appears defeated on their own terms — not just physically overwhelmed, but strategically outmaneuvered or morally undermined. This is the scene that demands internal transformation. If the protagonist simply needs more information or a power boost to get back up, the black moment has failed its function. The transformation required must be psychological, ethical, or relational.

## Beat 9: The Climactic Confrontation

The final face-off in which the villain's motivation intersects with the hero's arc. The best superhero prose makes this a collision of two philosophies enacted through physical or strategic conflict — not merely a power contest. The reader should be able to articulate both positions coming out of the scene. The outcome should be determined by something the protagonist changed internally in Beat 8, not by a deus ex machina power-up or a convenient villain mistake.

## Beat 10: Resolution / New Equilibrium

The hero's new equilibrium after the confrontation. If the protagonist is functionally identical to who they were before, the arc has failed. The new equilibrium should reflect what was lost (civilian cost), what was gained (growth, clarity, alliance), and what remains unresolved (series hook or thematic remainder). In a series, this beat must also establish the next cycle's premise without undoing the current book's closure.
