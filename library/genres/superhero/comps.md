Verified prose superhero novels and novellas — all confirmed as prose fiction, not graphic novels or comics — spanning the genre's major tonal registers and subgenres.

## Steelheart — Brandon Sanderson (2013)

In a world where a cosmic event grants random people superpowers that corrupt them absolutely, a young man joins the Reckoners, a rebel group dedicated to eliminating the malevolent super-powered "Epics" who have seized society. The point-of-view character has no powers, which forces the novel to solve the protagonist-vs.-Superman-level-villain problem with strategy and research rather than power escalation. Book 1 of the Reckoners series (four novels: *Steelheart*, *Firefight*, *Calamity*, *Lux*). Comp use: power systems with real cost and consequence; ordinary protagonist in a powered world; YA-accessible dark register.

## Soon I Will Be Invincible — Austin Grossman (2007)

Dual first-person novel alternating between the world's greatest criminal mastermind and a rookie cyborg superhero assembled into a team to stop him. The modern touchstone of prose superhero fiction. Widely cited as the definitive example of using interiority to make a villain sympathetic and comprehensible — the villain chapters are consistently described by readers as more compelling than the hero chapters. Standalone. Comp use: dual POV hero/villain; deconstructive register; literary sensibility; villain-as-protagonist.

## Vicious — V.E. Schwab (2013)

Two former college roommates discover that near-death experiences generate superpowers and spend a decade positioning themselves to destroy each other — no costumes, no clear moral lines, no conventional hero. The novel refuses the genre's ethical framing: both protagonists are morally compromised and the reader is left to judge. Book 1 of the Villains series. Comp use: no-hero register; dual antagonist structure; literary dark fiction crossover; non-costume superhero fiction.

## Worm — Wildbow (J.C. McCrae) (2011–2013)

A bullied teenager who gains the power to control insects becomes enmeshed in a morally complex cape underworld where heroes and villains are more complicated than their classifications suggest. A web serial (~1.68 million words) available free at parahumans.wordpress.com; no traditional print edition. The genre's most sustained exploration of power cost, institutional complexity, and deconstruction. Comp use: dark deconstructive register; power cost at extreme length; morally complex ensemble; readers who want depth over pacing.

## Hench — Natalie Zina Walschots (2020)

An office temp doing gig-economy work for minor supervillains is injured as collateral damage by a powerful hero and begins using data science to measure the true human cost of heroism. A systemic critique of superhero collateral damage told from the perspective of the people heroes harm. Standalone. Comp use: deconstructive register; villain/civilian POV; satirical examination of hero legitimacy; adult literary crossover.

## Dreadnought — April Daniels (2017)

A closeted trans teenage girl inherits the powers of Earth's greatest fallen hero, which transforms her body — forcing her coming-out simultaneously with having to defeat a dangerous cyborg villain. Powers are not metaphorical here; they are literally a vehicle for identity transformation. Book 1 of the Nemesis series. Comp use: earnest register; identity-as-transformation; YA; powers as vehicle for character arc.

## Ex-Heroes — Peter Clines (2010)

A group of superheroes defend a fortified Los Angeles movie lot against zombie hordes while confronting threats from former allies now transformed by the infection. Genre hybrid (superhero + zombie horror). Book 1 of the Ex-Heroes series. Comp use: dark register; genre mashup; ensemble; accessible commercial fiction.

## Super Powereds: Year 1 — Drew Hayes (2013)

Five college students classified as "Powereds" — people with uncontrollable abilities — enter an experimental program and secretly enroll in a university's Hero Certification Program alongside students who can control their powers. Power school / academy subgenre. Self-published series of four volumes. Comp use: academy subgenre; earnest register; power-control-as-growth arc; episodic structure.

## Please Don't Tell My Parents I'm a Supervillain — Richard Roberts (2014)

A thirteen-year-old daughter of retired superheroes accidentally gets labeled a supervillain when her newly manifested mad-science powers cause a misunderstanding — and she turns out to be frighteningly good at it. Lighter register; middle-grade to YA. Self-published series. Comp use: younger audience; comedic register; villain-protagonist with comic misunderstanding premise.

## Wearing the Cape — Marion G. Harmon (2011)

A young woman gains superstrength after a catastrophe and joins a Chicago superhero team, wrestling with her new identity while facing threats the team is not prepared for. Earnest register; self-published series with a dedicated readership. Comp use: earnest optimistic register; city-as-character; civilian-to-hero arc; adult audience.

## Wild Cards — edited by George R.R. Martin and Melinda M. Snodgrass (1987–present)

Shared-universe prose anthology set in alternate post-WWII America where a 1946 alien virus creates super-powered "Aces" and mutated "Jokers." 34+ volumes with 40+ contributing authors (including Melinda Snodgrass, Walter Jon Williams, and others). The long-form shared universe with the deepest treatment of powered individuals as a social and political minority. Note: a separate Marvel Comics adaptation exists — the Wild Cards books themselves are prose. Comp use: social/political treatment of powered individuals; ensemble; long-form world; adult literary SF crossover.

## After the Golden Age — Carrie Vaughn (2011)

The non-powered adult daughter of the world's greatest superhero couple works as a forensic accountant and becomes entangled in a supervillain's criminal trial. A procedural mystery set in a superhero world, told from the perspective of the one person in the story who cannot fly. Standalone. Comp use: non-powered protagonist; deconstructive/procedural register; family legacy theme; adult audience.
