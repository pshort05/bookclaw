# Space Romance — Must-Haves (non-negotiable checklist)

Skip these and the manuscript fails the fundamental requirements of the genre. Treat as a
revision pass.

- [ ] **Establish immediate competence** — both leads demonstrate high-level competence in their
  space role within the first three chapters; readers must believe they belong there before
  caring whether they survive. Competence is the primary basis of attraction in this genre.
- [ ] **Make the isolation load-bearing** — the space setting must do structural work: characters
  cannot leave, call for help, or retreat to separate lives. If the plot would function identically
  in a hotel or a remote cabin, the space setting is decorative, not functional.
- [ ] **Ground the environment in tactile detail** — life support sounds, suit weight, communication
  lag, meal logistics, sleep rotation, the specific hazards of the scenario. Readers forgive
  implausibility if the texture of daily life feels earned; they do not forgive a setting that
  exists only as a backdrop.
- [ ] **Keep romance the primary driver** — every external crisis (hull breach, system failure,
  hostile contact, supply shortage) must exist to force emotional intimacy, not to showcase
  worldbuilding or technical problem-solving. If the mission plot could carry the book without the
  romance, the priorities are inverted.
- [ ] **Earn the acceleration** — forced proximity compresses the romance timeline. Justify it:
  shared near-death experiences, watches standing alone in the dark, the removal of social
  performance that comes with total isolation. The reader must feel why these two people,
  specifically, move fast — not just that proximity did it.
- [ ] **Deliver the HEA in the world they actually inhabit** — the resolution must be legible
  within the space setting. A vague "they'll figure it out" ending after 85,000 words of isolation
  does not satisfy. Where are they going? What is the shared future? Ground it in operational
  reality.
- [ ] **Force genuine interdependence** — both characters must need the other to survive or
  succeed. Asymmetric dependency (rescuer and helpless rescued) without eventual reversal breeds
  passivity. The best space romance shows each lead saving the other at least once.
- [ ] **Distinguish this from space fantasy** — no fated mates, resonance bonds, or magic. If the
  romantic attraction is governed by supernatural compulsion rather than choice, the book is
  space fantasy romance, not space romance. The attraction must be earned by character and
  circumstance.
- [ ] **Give the wound a home in the setting** — each lead's internal flaw or emotional wound must
  connect to their reason for being in space: running from something, proving something, mourning
  something. The space setting and the character arc must mirror each other.
