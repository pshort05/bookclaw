# Space Romance — Beats

The dominant structure is *Romancing the Beat* (Gwen Hayes), four phases, continuously
alternating the romance arc with the survival/mission plot arc so every external crisis forces
emotional escalation and every emotional retreat makes the external danger worse. The space setting
does one thing the romance structure cannot: it removes the exit. Characters cannot quit, leave
for home, or call in backup — the isolation locks the beats in place and makes retreat purely
internal.

## Phase 1 — Setup

- **Introduce Protagonist 1 (1–5%)** — establish professional identity, skill set, and the
  emotional wound they arrived in space to outrun (grief, failure, estrangement, burnout).
- **Introduce Protagonist 2 (5–10%)** — the counter-perspective; if they are rivals or strangers,
  show them at competent, independent operation before forced proximity eliminates that independence.
- **The collision (10–15%)** — they meet under operational pressure: a docking emergency, a hostile
  briefing, or a first mission where they must immediately rely on each other to survive.
- **No Way #1 (15–20%)** — each states (internally or to a crewmate) why entanglement is
  impossible: mission protocol, prior history, incompatible objectives, or the simple math that
  romantic distraction in space kills people.
- **The lock-in (20–25%)** — the narrative seals the exit: a course commitment, a system failure,
  a communication blackout, or a shared mission that makes separation a death sentence. They cannot
  walk away.

## Phase 2 — Falling

- **No Way #2 (25–30%)** — despite close quarters they maintain professional distance, usually
  through competence-display and the tactical management of physical space aboard a vessel.
- **The inkling (30–40%)** — shared danger strips the professional mask: one witnesses the other's
  hidden fear, grief, or surprising tenderness; physical awareness follows competence-respect.
- **Midpoint of love (~50%)** — a false high: a survival event so intense it forces an admission
  (a near-death watch, a spacewalk alone, a communication from home that breaks one of them open);
  they cross an emotional or physical line and believe, briefly, that they can contain it.

## Phase 3 — Retreating

- **Inkling of doubt (55–65%)** — the emotional exposure terrifies one or both; they re-weaponize
  the mission as an excuse to pull back; professional distance becomes deliberate coldness.
- **Deepening doubt (65–70%)** — an external crisis (system failure, mission compromise, outside
  threat) forces them to question whether attachment is liability; one blames the relationship for
  a near-disaster.
- **Retreat & shields up (70–75%)** — they revert to Phase 1 operational facades; the cost is now
  visible: the ship feels larger and colder, efficiency drops, they are worse at the mission without
  each other.
- **The breakup / black moment (75–80%)** — the ultimate conflict: a revelation, a choice, or an
  external threat forces them to a rupture with real stakes — a mission-ending secret, a decision
  that feels like betrayal, or an honest confrontation with the wound that brought them to space.

## Phase 4 — Fighting

- **Dark night of the soul (80–85%)** — separated emotionally within the same hull; each faces the
  mission objective alone and finds it hollow. The space around them becomes a mirror for internal
  isolation.
- **Wake up (85–90%)** — the realization that the fear driving retreat (the wound, the rule, the
  mission) is the same thing that brought them to space to begin with; staying alone is not safety,
  it is the original wound.
- **The grand gesture (90–95%)** — a concrete, risk-bearing act: breaking protocol to save the
  other, transmitting a confession across a communication delay, physically choosing the other over
  the mission objective.
- **Whole-hearted & epilogue (95–100%)** — the HEA or HFN landed in the specific language of
  their space life: a shared posting, a destination chosen together, a mission they now run as a
  pair. Not a return to Earth normalcy — a future built in the context they actually inhabit.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)

- **The competence scene** — early and unambiguous: both leads execute their space roles
  flawlessly under pressure. Readers must respect them as professionals before rooting for them as
  lovers. Competence is the primary basis of attraction in this genre.
- **The environment scene** — a moment where the space setting itself is rendered with sensory
  precision: the sound of a hull under stress, the weight of a suit, the specific dark of a
  viewport between stars. The setting must feel inhabited, not sketched.
- **The forced-vulnerability scene** — a crisis strips the operational mask (a systems failure
  alone in the dark, a grief trigger from a communication from home, a physical injury requiring
  care) and the other character offers presence instead of tactical advice.
- **The first honest admission** — not necessarily romantic declaration; the moment one character
  tells the other a true thing about themselves they have told no one else. The intimacy of
  confession under isolation.
- **The choice scene** — the climax: one character explicitly chooses the other over a safer or
  more professionally defensible option, at visible personal cost. The sacrifice must be specific
  and legible, not abstract.
