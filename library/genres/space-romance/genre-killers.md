# Space Romance — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The broken promises, tonal betrayals, and structural failures that trigger DNF reactions and
one-star reviews in space romance.

- **Hard SF swamping the romance** — exceeding roughly 30% mission/technical plot without
  advancing the romantic arc in the same scene. Readers opened a romance, not a survival thriller
  with a romantic subplot. If the crew is fixing the ship for two chapters and the leads barely
  exchange a loaded glance, the book has lost the genre.
- **A "green-screen" space setting** — hollow backdrop: a spacecraft that might as well be an
  office building, with no tactile detail, no life-support tension, no sense of what daily life
  actually costs in this environment. The setting must feel inhabited. Readers who pick up space
  romance specifically want to live in that world, not just visit it for plot convenience.
- **Competence theater replacing emotional intimacy** — leads who spend their scenes showcasing
  technical skill at each other without ever becoming vulnerable. Competence earns attraction;
  it does not substitute for the emotional beats that make readers invest.
- **Premature isolation resolution** — bringing in a rescue ship, restoring communications, or
  reintroducing civilization too early. The external pressure that locks the characters together
  must hold until the emotional arc is complete. Premature resolution lets characters escape each
  other, and they will.
- **The "simple misunderstanding" black moment** — an Act III rupture a single direct conversation
  would resolve. The breakup must derive from the specific stakes of their situation: a
  mission-critical secret, a conflicting exit plan, a genuine incompatibility of post-mission
  lives. The isolation means there is no excuse not to have the conversation; if they do not, the
  writer must earn the silence.
- **Unequal survival load** — one character carrying all the competence and all the emotional
  labor while the other is passive, panicked, or perpetually rescued. Both leads must pull their
  weight operationally; readers lose respect for a character who cannot survive their own genre.
- **Fated mates or magical bonding presented as space romance** — introducing supernatural
  compulsion (resonance bonds, genetic compatibility mandates, alien mate cycles) collapses the
  genre boundary into space fantasy romance. Readers who wanted choice-based attraction feel
  cheated; readers who wanted fantasy romance feel misled by the marketing.
- **No life beyond the mission** — characters who exist only as their space role, with no past, no
  home, no relationships outside the current mission. The isolation only generates intimacy if
  readers know what each character is isolated from. The backstory wound must be legible and
  personal, not generic.
