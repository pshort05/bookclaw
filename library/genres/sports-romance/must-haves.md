Every sports romance must satisfy all ten of these elements; missing any one will cost the book its genre identity and likely its readership.

- [ ] **Sport creates real stakes, not just setting.** The athletic career must generate conflict that directly threatens or complicates the romance — not just provide a backdrop. Contract deadlines, trade threats, injury, performance pressure, and media scrutiny are the engines of the plot. If the sport could be removed from the book without changing the romance arc, it is not a sports romance.

- [ ] **The athlete reads as an athlete.** Their life is structured by the sport: their schedule, their physical relationship to their body, their emotional regulation strategies, their fear of obsolescence. They must speak and think with insider specificity — the vocabulary, the logistics, the culture of their specific sport. A character who happens to play a sport is not the same as a person shaped by athletic life.

- [ ] **Sport-specific research is accurate.** Rules, terminology, league structure, contract mechanics, and cultural norms must be correct for the specific sport featured. Hockey readers will identify an incorrect icing call. Football readers will notice an impossible play. Research to the standard of a knowledgeable fan of the sport, not a general sports fan.

- [ ] **Dual arc: career and romance run in parallel.** The season or career arc and the romance arc must intersect structurally. They do not run sequentially — they run simultaneously, and the major turning points of one arc must coincide with or directly cause the major turning points of the other.

- [ ] **HEA or HFN is mandatory.** Sports romance is a romance genre. The emotional throughline must resolve with a credible, earned happy ending. Dark endings, ambiguous endings, and "the reader can decide" endings are genre violations. The HEA must show both protagonists choosing each other and having a viable path forward.

- [ ] **Love interest has their own substantive interiority.** The love interest is not a reward or an accessory. They have their own career, their own stakes, their own reason to resist the relationship, and their own arc. Dual POV is the standard mechanism for establishing this — but even in single-POV books, the love interest must be a full person whose desires and losses matter independently of what they mean to the athlete.

- [ ] **Game-day or competition scene at major structural beats.** There must be at least one high-stakes athletic scene at the midpoint and one near the climax that serve as romance turning points, not sports-documentary interludes. The emotional state of the romance must be present and active in the athletic scene.

- [ ] **The dark moment arises from the athletic world.** The primary conflict that separates the protagonists must come from the specific pressures of the athletic career — not a generic misunderstanding, not a secret kept for generic reasons, not a character flaw unconnected to the sport. Trade deadline, injury, contract, career crisis, media exposure: the sport causes the dark moment.

- [ ] **Physical and emotional intimacy are both earned.** The heat level (whether explicit or closed-door) must be consistent with the cover and marketing signal. The physical intimacy must be emotionally grounded — readers who track both the emotional and physical arcs will notice if they are disconnected. The sex scenes or intimate scenes should escalate the emotional stakes, not pause them.

- [ ] **The team and athletic world function as a real environment.** Secondary characters from the athletic world — teammates, coaches, agents, trainers — must feel like real people with their own interests, not furniture. The team-as-found-family dynamic, when present, must be established through specific detail rather than told. The athletic world should feel inhabited, not costumed.
