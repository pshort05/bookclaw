Science fiction built around technological gateways between worlds, where the engineering, physics, and costs of the portal device are as central to the story as the destination.

## Tone & Mood

The dominant tone is scientific rigor applied to the impossible. Characters approach the portal and its destination through observation, hypothesis, and testing — the mood is one of disciplined awe rather than fairy-tale wonder. Even when what lies on the other side defies current understanding, characters attempt to explain it through logic, mathematics, and physics before accepting it as permanently unknowable. Readers expect intellectual engagement: the story rewards careful attention to how the gateway works and what the rules governing it mean for the characters. Paranoia about contamination, exploitation, and unintended consequence is a recurring emotional texture.

## Pacing

Pacing is driven by the tension between scientific process — which is methodical and slow — and the mounting urgency created by what the portal reveals. Early chapters establish the gateway's parameters and costs: energy requirements, transit time, what can and cannot be sent through, what comes back wrong. Mid-story accelerates as the destination proves more complex or dangerous than initial probes suggested. The climax typically turns on a constraint of the portal's physics that has been established early and must now be used or circumvented under pressure.

## Setting Conventions

The gateway facility — gate room, laboratory, command center — is typically sterile, institutional, and high-security. The contrast between this controlled environment and the destination's alien or chaotic character is a primary visual and atmospheric motif. Destinations may include parallel Earths, distant planetary systems, other dimensions, or temporal displacements; in each case the world's physical laws must be established and adhered to. Government, military, and corporate interests compete over control of the gateway, generating political tension alongside the scientific and exploratory narrative.

## Character Archetypes

The scientist or engineer who designed or best understands the gateway is a central figure — often the protagonist or a critical ally. Military and government handlers add friction by prioritizing strategic extraction over rigorous science. The first transit crew typically includes a skeptic, a true believer, a technical specialist, and someone whose personal stakes in what lies on the other side compromise their judgment. Antagonists may be institutional (rival programs, cover-up operations) or environmental (the destination itself presenting existential threat).

## Length & Format

Portal science fiction runs 80,000–120,000 words. Series structures are common when the destination world is complex enough to support sustained exploration across multiple books. Third-person limited or omniscient narration is more common than first-person, reflecting the genre's tendency toward ensemble casts and multi-perspective coverage of complex events. Technical accuracy or plausible extrapolation from current physics is expected; handwaving the gateway's mechanism entirely reads as fantasy rather than SF.
