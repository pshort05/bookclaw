Readers expect a world where circuit boards and spell-work coexist without apology, and where the friction between them drives the story.

## Tone & Mood

Cyberfantasy occupies a tonal middle ground between cyberpunk's gritty fatalism and fantasy's sense of mythic stakes. Readers expect darkness and moral complexity — this is not light fare — but also wonder: the magic system should genuinely feel magical even when it is running on corrupted firmware. The tone is typically propulsive and atmospheric, with moments of pulpy energy balanced against genuine weight. Irony and dark humor are acceptable; cynicism should not crowd out the possibility that something extraordinary is happening. The hybrid premise requires tonal confidence — readers who came for elves with assault rifles do not want an author who seems embarrassed about it.

## Pacing

Pacing is fast to medium-fast. The cyberpunk inheritance means readers expect kinetic action sequences, heist structures, and high-stakes infiltrations. The fantasy inheritance adds space for world-building discovery — a new magic system, an unexpected faction, a mythic backstory that recontextualizes the present. The ideal rhythm alternates between forward momentum and earned pause: a chase scene followed by a moment of lore, a magical revelation followed by an operational problem it immediately creates. Readers are patient with world-building investment early in the story if it pays dividends in later plot complexity.

## Setting Conventions

The defining setting feature is productive collision: technology and magic inhabit the same world and cannot be cleanly separated. Dragons run megacorporations. Wizards and hackers compete for the same access points. The best cyberfantasy settings develop explicit rules for how magic and technology interact — amplifying, interfering, or existing in separate jurisdictions that are beginning to overlap. Readers expect a stratified urban environment where power and access are unevenly distributed. The world should feel lived-in and coherent rather than a collision of genre props.

## Character Archetypes

The augmented protagonist — bridging magic and technology, often uncomfortably — is the genre's signature figure. Street-level operatives, corporate fixer-assassins, and rogue academics who understand both systems all fit. Fantasy contributes archetypes the cyberpunk tradition lacks: the ancient being adapting badly to modernity, the chosen figure whose destiny conflicts with survival, the guardian of a tradition that technology is rendering obsolete. Ensemble casts organized around complementary magical and technical skills are a natural fit. Antagonists tend to be institutional — corporations, guilds, or hybrid entities controlling access to power across both domains.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–120,000 words, with series being the commercial norm. The hybrid premise carries significant world-building overhead; novels shorter than 70,000 words often feel under-developed. Series allow the magic-technology interface to be explored across multiple threat scenarios and political configurations. Third-person limited or multiple-POV structures are common. Fast-chapter structures with chapter-ending hooks suit the genre's kinetic pacing. Appendices or in-world documents (corporate memos, guild codices, technical specifications written in hybrid register) are an optional but fitting format choice.
