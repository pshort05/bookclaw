- Cyberfantasy blends cyberpunk's dystopian high-tech aesthetics with fantasy's magical elements. Writing in this genre requires balancing densely atmospheric prose with rapid-paced dialogue, speculative worldbuilding with systematic magic, and noir sensibilities with mythic resonance. This guide provides practical stylistic conventions for crafting cohesive cyberfantasy narratives.
- Primary Characteristics:
- Skeptical and world-weary: Characters distrust institutions (corporate, magical, governmental) while possessing wry humor
- Employ varied sentence length for rhythm: short, punchy sentences for action and tension; longer sentences for atmosphere and reflection
- Use fragmentation judiciously (incomplete sentences in dialogue and tight scenes) to convey urgency and character voice
- Prioritize visual and tactile sensations that merge technology and magic: neon reflecting off rain-slicked streets; the heat of magical sigils burning through synthetic skin; the hum of processors and chanted spells overlapping
- "The scratchware jack was old enough to remember when tech and spell hadn't yet learned to speak the same language. Now the distinction hardly mattered—Kess ran her bleeding fingers across the port and felt both cybernetic stimulation and magical binding light up her neural pathways like a grid. The data-spirits answered first, whispering their secrets through implanted synapses. The bound elementals followed half a beat later, their presence heavier, older, less inclined to bargain."
- Characteristics:
- Terse and functional: Characters speak to convey information and establish power dynamics, not to explain the world to readers
- Speech patterns differentiate character: A corporate mage speaks differently than a street-level hacker; an ancient spirit differently than a hired muscle
- Subtext beneath text: What characters don't say matters as much as what they do
- Use contractions liberally (cyberpunk convention: future speech patterns remain informal)
- Avoid exposition-heavy dialogue; characters assume shared knowledge
- "Someone had to. Your corporate contacts weren't returning calls."
- Character Development & Archetypes
- Recurring Character Types:
- The Augmented Outcast: Hybrid human/machine/magical entity living on society's margins; identity fractured between technological and mystical enhancements
- The Street-Level Practitioner: Hacker, spellcaster, or technomancer using whatever tools remain available; resourcefulness born of necessity
- Characterization Through Augmentation:
- Show character and background through modification choices: A corporate mage might favor elegant, invisible enhancements; a resistance fighter uses visible ones as identity markers
- Explore identity fracture: Is the character still themselves after magical binding or cybernetic replacement? This is central to cyberfantasy themes
- Mention both mundane and magical/technological reality: A scene includes ordinary people alongside augmented ones; traditional magic users working alongside technomancers
- Augmentation/Enhancement: Characters are routinely augmented with technology or magical modifications; identity becomes fluid and fractured
- Resistance against control: Characters fighting against systems designed to exploit or surveil them
- Create linguistic markers for different subcultures: corporate speakers use formal diction; street characters use slang and contractions
- Opening establishes character in functional survival mode within oppressive system; hints at larger conflict
- Inciting incident disrupts status quo; character pulled into larger world or conspiracy
- Rising action alternates between intimate character moments and large-scale revelations; shows how personal and systemic stakes interconnect
- Climax often bittersweet: victory comes at cost; system remains largely intact despite protagonist's success; personal victories may carry pyrrhic elements
- Resolution shows character's changed relationship to system; rarely feels like triumph, more like shift in power dynamics
- Scene Pacing:
- Alternate between high-action sequences and quiet investigation/reflection scenes
- Use dialogue-heavy sequences for rapid exposition and character development
- Employ description-rich passages sparingly but effectively; impact through precision rather than volume
- End chapters on character realization or complication rather than action beats alone
- Technology and Tradition: How do ancient practices adapt to industrial-scale application? What's lost and gained?
- Weave themes through character choices and consequences, not authorial commentary
- Explore how both magic and technology distance people from direct consequence (intermediaries, abstractions, interfaces)
- Paragraph breaks indicate shift in focus: tight, short paragraphs for action; longer paragraphs for atmosphere and description
- Multiple POV characters shift between scenes to show how different actors experience the same world
- Before finalizing scenes, verify:
- Do characters speak in distinct voices reflecting background and function?
- Is worldbuilding integrated through action and detail, not exposition?
- Does the scene show both magical and technological elements or explain why one dominates here?
- Are consequences present? (Actions have costs; solutions create new problems)
- Does the scene advance character understanding or plot complication?

- The genre is characterized by several defining features. First, it maintains cyberpunk's fundamental aesthetic and thematic concerns—urban decay, corporate dominance, technological augmentation, and social stratification—while introducing magical or supernatural elements that function as integral parts of the world rather than mere decoration. Second, it typically presents magic through a systematic, almost technological lens, treating spells and enchantments as programmable forces that can be studied, commodified, and industrialized. Finally, cyberfantasy inherits cyberpunk's focus on marginalized protagonists navigating oppressive systems, whether those systems are technological, magical, or—most often—both.[5][6][7][8][9][10]
- The roots of cyberfantasy cannot be separated from the emergence of cyberpunk itself in the 1960s and 1970s. During this period, the New Wave science fiction movement—featuring authors like Philip K. Dick, Michael Moorcock, Roger Zelazny, John Brunner, and J.G. Ballard—began exploring the dark underbelly of technological progress, creating dystopian near-futures that rejected the utopian visions prevalent in earlier science fiction. These writers laid the groundwork for cyberpunk's critical examination of technology's impact on society, identity, and human relationships.[14][15][16][17][18]
- Shadowrun's success spawned numerous novels (over 50 in English alone) and inspired other creators to explore the fusion of these seemingly incompatible genres. The franchise showed that magic and technology could interact in complex ways: shamans might use cyberware, corporations could employ both mages and hackers, and ancient magical traditions could adapt to modern urban environments.[19][20][11][12]
- The 2000s and 2010s saw cyberfantasy evolve beyond its Shadowrun origins into more diverse expressions. Max Gladstone's Craft Sequence, beginning with Three Parts Dead in 2012, reimagined a fantasy world where magic operates through legal contracts and corporate structures, creating what has been described as "fantasy cyberpunk" or "legal thriller meets urban fantasy meets cyberpunk". Gladstone's work demonstrated that cyberpunk's concerns—corporate power, urban environments, systemic oppression—could be explored through purely magical means without requiring literal technology.[25][26][27][28][29]
- The genre has also seen increased experimentation with how magic and technology interact. Authors are exploring technomancy (magic channeled through technology), magitech (technology powered by magic), and settings where the distinction between the two becomes meaningless. This has led to works that exist in genuinely ambiguous spaces between science fiction and fantasy, where neither element dominates but both shape the narrative in essential ways.[35][36][37][38][39][40]
- The genre frequently employs heist narratives, where characters must steal magical artifacts or data, navigate corporate-magical security systems, and assemble diverse teams of specialists. This structure allows authors to showcase both magical and technological worldbuilding while maintaining high-paced, action-oriented plotting.[10][13][9]
- The genre also employs mystery and investigation structures, drawing on cyberpunk's detective fiction roots and fantasy's tradition of characters uncovering hidden magical knowledge. These narratives explore how magic and technology interact, often revealing that what appeared to be purely technological is actually magical (or vice versa).[7][27][28][5][25]
- Cyberfantasy settings share certain consistent features. Urban environments dominate, typically presenting massive cities where wealth concentrates in gleaming towers while poverty festers in lower levels or sprawling slums. These cities are characterized by neon aesthetics, perpetual rainfall or overcast skies, and the juxtaposition of advanced magical-technological infrastructure with social decay.[45][46][47][5]
- Information and surveillance play crucial roles, with the genre exploring how both technological and magical means of monitoring, controlling, and manipulating populations interact. Privacy becomes a central concern when both hackers and diviners can access personal information, and when memories themselves become hackable.[32][34][5][6][8][31]
- The genre's anti-capitalist critique remains consistent with cyberpunk's origins, but cyberfantasy often intensifies this critique by showing how corporations would commodify and exploit even supernatural forces. When magic becomes industrialized, it subjects ancient traditions and spiritual practices to the same profit-driven logic that cyberpunk shows corrupting technology.[5][6][8][46][9][10][44]
- The incorporation of fantasy elements allows cyberfantasy to explore questions of tradition versus progress in ways pure cyberpunk cannot. Characters must navigate between ancient magical knowledge and cutting-edge innovation, asking whether old ways of understanding power remain relevant in transformed contexts.[3][27][37][47][25][35]
- Post-colonial and cultural perspectives increasingly inform cyberfantasy, particularly in works by authors like T.R. Napper, Saad Hossain, and others who center non-Western settings and cultural frameworks. This represents a deliberate expansion beyond cyberpunk's historically Western focus, exploring how different cultural traditions might interact with both technological advancement and magical systems.[33][31][32]
- This variant emphasizes contemporary urban settings where magic exists hidden within modern technological society. Unlike traditional urban fantasy's focus on supernatural creatures coexisting secretly with mundane humans, urban fantasy cyberpunk explores how magical and technological systems actively shape and compete with each other in visible, institutional ways.[48][42][47][3]
- Works in this category, such as Max Gladstone's Craft Sequence and Rachel Aaron's Heartstrikers and DFZ series, present worlds where magic operates through structures that mirror technology—contracts, networks, systematic applications—creating a genuine fusion rather than mere coexistence.[49][50][26][27][25]
- This subgenre presents worlds where a technological civilization has collapsed, leaving behind both advanced devices and unleashed magical forces. Characters navigate settings where ancient technology functions like magic (because its workings are no longer understood) while actual magic has emerged or returned. The Horizon Zero Dawn video game exemplifies this approach, as do numerous post-apocalyptic settings where survivors scavenge both tech and arcane artifacts.[51][37][52][53]
- This subgenre treats magic and technology as fundamentally interconnected or identical. Characters might be technomancers who hack reality through magical means, or programmers whose code functions as spellcasting. The M.K. Gibson Technomancer series exemplifies this approach, presenting a post-apocalyptic world where characters use technology to channel supernatural powers.[55][38][39][40][35]
- Identity and Transhumanism—Cyberfantasy inherits cyberpunk's questions about what makes someone human when bodies and minds can be augmented, modified, or replaced. The genre adds magical dimensions to these questions: If someone's consciousness is bound to a magical artifact, are they still themselves? If someone's personality is overwritten by a possessing spirit or memory manipulation spell, where does their identity reside?. The fusion of magic and technology creates new possibilities for transformation that blur boundaries between human, machine, and supernatural entity.[56][57][34][31][32][6][8]
- The Fusion of Tradition and Innovation—Cyberfantasy uniquely explores tensions between ancient magical traditions and cutting-edge innovation. Characters must navigate between respect for historical knowledge and adaptation to transformed circumstances, asking whether traditional ways of understanding power remain relevant or require radical revision.[37][3][25][35]
- Cybernetic and Magical Augmentation—Characters routinely undergo both technological and magical enhancement, from implanted cyberware to runic tattoos that grant powers. These augmentations blur the boundaries between natural and artificial, human and posthuman.[57][6][8][5]
- Hackers and Street Mages—Protagonists often include both technological hackers who manipulate digital systems and magical practitioners who work outside official channels. These character types represent bottom-up expertise that challenges institutional control.[7][6][5]
- Information as Commodity and Weapon—In cyberfantasy worlds, knowledge—whether technical specifications, magical formulae, or compromising data—holds enormous value. Characters quest for information, trade it, and use it to gain advantage over more powerful enemies.[6][46][5]
- Implanted Memories and False Identities—Characters discover their memories have been altered, their identities fabricated, or their entire lives constructed for purposes they don't understand. This trope enables exploration of consciousness and selfhood while creating noir-influenced mysteries.[31][32][34]
- Ancient Magic in Modern Contexts—Traditional magical practices persist but adapt to changed circumstances, with spellcasters using smartphones as ritual tools or ancient spirits inhabiting computer networks. This trope explores cultural continuity and adaptation under pressure.[39][35][37]
- Memory Palaces and Virtual/Mental Spaces—Characters access digital networks, magical mindscapes, or hybrid psychic-technological spaces. These environments provide settings for conflict and revelation while exploring the relationship between consciousness and reality.[22][32][31][5]
- The Artifact—Objects that bridge magic and technology—enchanted cyberware, spell-inscribed processors, ancient magical items with technological capabilities—symbolize the fusion that defines the genre while raising questions about power, history, and progress.[35][9][10]
- Gladstone's Craft Sequence represents a unique approach to cyberfantasy, presenting a fantasy world where magic operates through legal contracts, corporate structures, and systematic applications that mirror technology without being technological. The series includes Three Parts Dead (2012), Two Serpents Rise (2013), Full Fathom Five (2014), Last First Snow (2015), Four Roads Cross (2016), Ruin of Angels (2017), and Dead Country (2023), with Wicked Problems released in 2025.[28][29][26][27][25]
- Gibson's Technomancer series (To Beat the Devil and subsequent volumes) explicitly blends cyberpunk and urban fantasy, presenting a post-apocalyptic world 175 years after the biblical apocalypse where demons rule Earth and the protagonist Salem uses nanotechnology-enhanced abilities to combat supernatural threats. The series embraces its genre fusion, described as "action-adventure that blends cyberpunk, supernatural, and urban fantasy in a dystopian setting".[38][55]
- Gibson's work represents cyberfantasy's pulp adventure roots, emphasizing action and dark humor while exploring how technology and supernatural forces interact when both are genuinely present in the world. The series demonstrates the genre's range, showing that cyberfantasy can encompass both literary explorations of power and economic systems (as in Bennett and Gladstone) and more straightforwardly entertaining adventures.[38][55]
- This guide demonstrates that cyberfantasy has evolved from niche experiments into a mature genre with diverse voices, sophisticated worldbuilding, and serious engagement with contemporary concerns. By fusing cyberpunk's critique of technological capitalism with fantasy's imaginative possibilities, the genre offers unique perspectives on power, identity, resistance, and the future of human societies. The works published between 2020 and 2025 show cyberfantasy continuing to evolve, incorporating diverse cultural perspectives, experimenting with how magic and technology interact, and finding new ways to explore timeless questions about what it means to be human in systems designed to control and exploit us.
