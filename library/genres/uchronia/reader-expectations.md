Uchronia readers come for rigorous "what if" logic — they want to trace consequence chains from a plausible historical divergence through a fully realised alternate world, not just spot the differences from the real timeline.

## Tone and Mood

Uchronia sits on a spectrum from thriller-paced espionage (Robert Harris's *Fatherland*, Len Deighton's *SS-GB*) to meditative, literary alternate history (Jo Walton's *Farthing*, Kim Stanley Robinson's *The Years of Rice and Salt*). Regardless of where a story lands on that spectrum, readers expect a tone of **intellectual seriousness**: the alternate world must be treated as real, not as a playground for ironic distance. Comedy and satire are subgenres in their own right; standard uchronia readers do not want winking at the camera.

The dominant mood is one of **uncanny familiarity** — the world should feel close enough to our own that the differences register as wrong or disturbing, not merely exotic. When done well, readers feel a persistent low-grade unease, the sense that history sits on knife-edges.

## Pacing

Pacing expectations split by subgenre. **Thriller-inflected uchronia** (Nazi-occupation, Confederate victory, Cold War gone hot) moves at genre-thriller pace: short chapters, escalating stakes, a ticking clock. **Literary uchronia** (civilisational-scale divergences, multi-generational sagas) moves slower, with room for reflection and digestion of worldbuilding.

In both modes, readers expect the alternate world to be **revealed incrementally through story action**, not delivered in front-loaded exposition. The divergence point and its consequences should emerge as the protagonist encounters them, not as an authorial lecture in chapter one. Readers tolerate a slow opening only if the worldbuilding revelations arrive steadily — the "discovery drip" that shows how different this world is must never pause for more than two or three chapters.

## Setting

The setting must do more than rename streets and change flags. Readers expect the **physical and social texture of the alternate world**: the propaganda posters, the food shortages, the linguistic borrowings from occupying cultures, the way ordinary people have adapted their daily lives. Details that are specific and unglamorous carry the most weight — not the grand architecture of the regime but the small accommodations citizens have made.

**Historical grounding is mandatory.** Readers come with real-world knowledge of the divergence period and will notice anachronisms, wrong troop movements, and misattributed technologies. The author's research must be visible without being ostentatious — the right detail dropped at the right moment, not a Wikipedia tour.

Settings that work best are ones where the **power structures of the alternate world are embodied in place**: the checkpoints, the restricted zones, the monuments that never existed, the absence of monuments that should exist.

## Character Archetypes

- **The native inhabitant who knows no other world.** The most common and most effective POV: a character for whom this timeline is simply reality, whose blind spots mirror the reader's entry-point. Their gradual discovery of the divergence (or their complicity in the alternate order) drives most of the story's tension.
- **The historian or archivist.** Carries knowledge of what should have been, consciously or not. Often the protagonist in literary uchronia; their perspective allows the author to surface the "what if" logic explicitly without making it feel forced.
- **The collaborator or compromised figure.** A character who has made moral accommodations to the alternate regime. The genre's most interesting moral territory lives here — readers expect ambiguity, not condemnation.
- **The true believer in the alternate order.** The ideological villain, but effective uchronia humanises them: they are not cartoon fascists but people who genuinely believe in the rightness of their world.
- **The resistance member.** A genre staple, but readers are fatigued by triumphalist resistance narratives. The resistance should face genuine costs and uncertain outcomes.

## Length and Format

Most standalone uchronia novels run **90,000–130,000 words**. Series with multi-generational or civilisational scope (Turtledove's *Worldwar*, Robinson's *The Years of Rice and Salt*) run longer. Shorter uchronia (60,000–80,000 words) works well for thriller-paced single-timeline stories like *Fatherland* or *SS-GB*.

Readers expect **chapter-level variation in POV** in multi-strand narratives, with each POV serving a distinct function in illuminating the alternate world from a different angle. Single-POV uchronia must compensate with denser worldbuilding texture and a more propulsive plot.
