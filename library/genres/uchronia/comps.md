The established uchronia canon ranges from thriller-paced occupied-Europe mysteries to multi-generational civilisational epics; position your manuscript against these titles to set reader expectations accurately.

## Fatherland — Robert Harris (1992)

**The template for occupied-Europe thriller uchronia.** Set in a 1964 Greater Germany where the Nazis won World War II, Harris follows detective Xavier March as he investigates a murder that leads to the suppressed secret of the Holocaust. The novel's craft achievement is making the alternate world feel bureaucratically real — the Gestapo, the ministries, the property laws — while running a conventional thriller plot that delivers the "what if" revelations in the form of clues. **Comp use:** cite for Nazi-victory scenarios, occupied-Europe settings, or thriller-paced alternate history where the investigation is the vehicle for worldbuilding revelation.

## SS-GB — Len Deighton (1978)

**The grittier, more procedural counterpart to *Fatherland*.** Britain has fallen; Scotland Yard detective Douglas Archer works under SS supervision. Deighton's alternate Britain is rendered through bureaucratic texture, class friction, and the specifics of German occupation administration — less thriller-paced than Harris, more sociological. **Comp use:** cite for Nazi-occupation-of-Britain scenarios, procedural detective framing, or worldbuilding that foregrounds how ordinary institutions adapt to occupation.

## The Man in the High Castle — Philip K. Dick (1962)

**The ur-text of American uchronia.** America has been partitioned between Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan. Dick's novel is less interested in plot mechanics than in ontology: what is the nature of reality in an alternate history, and who gets to say which timeline is "real"? The novel-within-the-novel (*The Grasshopper Lies Heavy*, which depicts our timeline as fiction within the alternate world) is the genre's most discussed metafictional device. Winner of the Hugo Award. **Comp use:** cite for Nazi/Japanese victory scenarios, literary and philosophical uchronia, or stories that interrogate the nature of historical reality rather than simply depicting an alternate one.

## Farthing — Jo Walton (2006)

**The finest British uchronia of the 21st century.** In a 1949 Britain that made a compromise peace with Hitler in 1941, a murder at a country house weekend draws in a Scotland Yard detective and an aristocratic young woman. Walton works within the cosy English-murder-mystery form to show how fascism infiltrates a class-stratified society through politeness, preference, and the desire to preserve social order. The first book of the Small Change trilogy (*Farthing*, *Ha'penny*, *Half a Crown*). **Comp use:** cite for peace-with-Hitler scenarios, character-driven literary uchronia, or stories that examine how ordinary people accommodate the slide toward authoritarianism.

## The Years of Rice and Salt — Kim Stanley Robinson (2002)

**The most ambitious scope in the genre.** The premise: the Black Death kills 99% of Europe's population; the next 700 years of history unfold without European civilisation's dominance. The novel follows recurring souls across multiple lifetimes and civilisations — Islamic, Chinese, Native American, Indian. Robinson's alternate world is a genuine attempt to imagine what modernity would look like without the specific history that produced it. Literary, philosophical, and structurally unconventional. **Comp use:** cite for civilisational-scale alternate history, multi-generational or reincarnation narratives, or uchronia in non-European settings.

## 1632 — Eric Flint (2000)

**The foundational text of "American town transported to the past" uchronia.** A small West Virginia mining town is transplanted to 17th-century Germany during the Thirty Years War. Flint's novel is an optimistic, populist alternate history: the town's modern skills and democratic values accelerate the Enlightenment and reshape European history. Launched an enormous shared-world series. **Comp use:** cite for technological-uplift alternate history, optimistic or progressive-politics uchronia, American exceptionalism narratives, or accessible rather than literary alternate history.

## The Guns of the South — Harry Turtledove (1992)

**The genre's most rigorous single-divergence novel.** Time travellers deliver AK-47s to the Confederate Army of Northern Virginia in 1864; the Confederacy wins the Civil War. Turtledove then follows the consequences through Reconstruction — the presence of modern weapons does not resolve the underlying political and social contradictions of the Confederacy, and the novel refuses a triumphalist Confederate victory narrative. **Comp use:** cite for Civil War alternate history, single-technology-change premises, or uchronia that insists on following consequences through rather than stopping at the divergence's immediate result.

## Ruled Britannia — Harry Turtledove (2002)

**Turtledove's most literary work.** The Spanish Armada succeeded; England has been under Spanish occupation for ten years. William Shakespeare is simultaneously commissioned to write a play glorifying Philip II and recruited by the English resistance to write a play that will trigger an uprising. The novel is dense with period detail and literary reference, moving at a slower pace than Turtledove's military-history series work. **Comp use:** cite for occupied-England scenarios, literary or character-driven uchronia, or stories that use a historical artistic figure as protagonist.
