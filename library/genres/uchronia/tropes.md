Uchronia has a recognised set of tropes that readers expect to encounter in some form; the craft lies in deploying them with specific historical grounding rather than in generic form.

1. **The Divergence Point Reveal.** The moment when the reader (and often the protagonist) learns precisely what changed and when. This can arrive as exposition, as a found document, or as a piece of dialogue — but it must arrive. Uchronia that withholds the divergence point entirely frustrates readers who came specifically for the "what if" logic.

2. **The Occupied Homeland.** A once-free society now under foreign or ideological domination — Nazi-occupied Britain (*SS-GB*, *Fatherland* set in Greater Germany), Confederate America (*The Guns of the South*, *Farthing*). Readers expect the occupation to have transformed everyday life at the granular level: language, currency, rationing, surveillance, propaganda.

3. **The Museum of Lost History.** Documents, artifacts, or institutions that preserve the suppressed real history — underground presses, contraband books, hidden archives. In *The Man in the High Castle*, this role is played by the titular novel-within-the-novel. Functionally, it allows the author to surface what was lost without authorial intrusion.

4. **The Collaborator's Dilemma.** A protagonist or supporting character who has survived by accommodating the alternate regime faces a moment where accommodation is no longer possible. The genre's richest moral territory: collaboration is never pure cowardice but always a mixture of pragmatism, love, and rationalisation.

5. **The Resistance Cell.** A small group working to restore the original timeline or overthrow the alternate order. Works best when the resistance is fractured, compromised, and uncertain of success. Readers are tired of competent, ideologically pure resistance organisations.

6. **The True-Believer Antagonist.** An ideological villain who is not a cartoon but a person who genuinely believes in the rightness of the alternate world. The most unsettling antagonists are those whose logic is internally consistent.

7. **The Technological Divergence.** When the point of change is technological rather than political — the South wins because of the AK-47 delivered to Lee (*The Guns of the South*), or gunpowder arrives in Europe a century later, or steam power develops in the Roman Empire. These stories must show the cascade: one technology change has political, social, and military consequences.

8. **The Investigator Who Shouldn't Investigate.** In thriller-inflected uchronia, a detective, journalist, or official whose assigned case leads them to discover what should not be found — the seam of the alternate history's founding crime. Robert Harris's *Fatherland* runs entirely on this engine.

9. **The Counterfactual Text-Within-the-Text.** A document, novel, or film within the story that depicts our real history (or another divergence) — treated as fiction within the world. Philip K. Dick's *The Man in the High Castle* is built around this trope; it both deepens the worldbuilding and raises metafictional questions about which history is "real."

10. **The Point of No Return.** A moment, usually near the midpoint, when the protagonist could step back into safe complicity but chooses not to. This is the genre's equivalent of the thriller's "crossing the threshold" — after this, the alternate world's enforcers are coming for them.

11. **The Civilisational Audit.** In longer, more literary uchronia, a section or chapter that surveys the alternate world at scale — its art, its philosophy, its sciences — showing what flourished that was suppressed in our timeline and what was lost. Kim Stanley Robinson uses this extensively in *The Years of Rice and Salt*.

12. **The Pyrrhic Ending.** Uchronia rarely ends with triumphant restoration of the original timeline. The preferred ending is ambiguous or costly: the protagonist survives but the world does not change; or changes slightly but at great personal cost; or the alternate world is shown to contain its own forms of justice and beauty alongside its horrors. Readers who want unambiguous victory should be reading thrillers.
