Uchronia follows a recognisable structural arc built around the gradual revelation of both the alternate world and the protagonist's place within it; readers arrive with expectations about which scenes must appear.

## Obligatory Scenes

These are the scenes readers of uchronia have been conditioned to expect. Their absence creates a sense of incompleteness; their presence, when handled freshly, delivers the genre's core satisfactions.

**1. The Immersive Normalisation Scene**
The opening must establish the alternate world as mundane and lived-in, not as a spectacle. The protagonist goes about ordinary business — a commute, a meal, a conversation with a colleague — and the reader assembles the alternate reality from environmental details rather than from authorial description. The Nazi checkpoint in the background, the Confederate flag on the courthouse, the Roman numerals on the calendar. This scene must resist the temptation to list the differences; it must dramatise them as unremarkable to the people who live there.

**2. The Divergence Point Exposition Scene**
At some point — in thriller-inflected uchronia, this arrives early; in literary uchronia, it may be delayed — a character, document, or artefact explicitly surfaces the divergence: what changed, when, and why. This can be a history-lesson scene (a character explaining to another), a found document (the underground text, the contraband book, the suppressed archive), or a piece of dialogue in which the alternate history's founding assumptions are stated aloud. Readers need this moment to calibrate their understanding of the world.

**3. The System-as-Normal Scene**
A scene in which a character enforces or performs the logic of the alternate order without moral distress — a bureaucrat processing paperwork, a soldier following routine orders, a citizen informing on a neighbour. This scene is not about villainy; it is about normalisation. It shows the reader how thoroughly the alternate world has colonised ordinary moral imagination, and it functions as a threat: this is what the protagonist is surrounded by.

**4. The Discovery Scene**
The protagonist encounters something that cannot be explained within the alternate world's official narrative — a discrepancy, a suppressed document, an anomalous stranger, a forbidden object. This is the inciting incident of the mystery layer most uchronia carries. The Discovery Scene must be specific and concrete, not atmospheric; the protagonist must actively investigate rather than passively stumble.

**5. The Cost Scene**
A scene in which a character pays a real price for knowing too much, resisting, or simply being on the wrong side of the alternate order's categories. This is the genre's equivalent of the thriller's "body in the car park" — it establishes that the stakes are lethal and that the world is not safe for people like the protagonist. The cost must fall on someone the reader cares about.

## Structural Beats

**Act One: Immersion and Rupture**
- Open in media res within the alternate world (Normalisation Scene).
- Establish the protagonist's position within the alternate order: complicit, marginal, or already suspicious.
- Deliver the first anomaly — the crack in the official narrative (Discovery Scene, first form).
- Introduce the antagonist or the antagonistic system as a presence the protagonist must navigate.

**Midpoint: Commitment**
- The protagonist crosses a threshold — they actively seek the truth, join the resistance, or take an action that makes them visible to the alternate order's enforcers.
- The Divergence Point Exposition Scene typically arrives here or just before, as the protagonist now has context to understand what they have found.
- The alternate world's full machinery becomes legible: the reader and protagonist now understand the founding crime.

**Act Two Second Half: Escalation and Cost**
- The Cost Scene — a significant loss that proves the stakes.
- The resistance or investigation is compromised; the protagonist's allies are fewer or less reliable than believed.
- The protagonist reaches the "dark night" moment: the system seems unbeatable, their knowledge seems useless, their losses seem to have been for nothing.

**Act Three: Resolution Without Restoration**
- The final confrontation is rarely about restoring the original timeline. It is about the protagonist's individual fate within the alternate world, or about one small act of witness or resistance that may or may not matter.
- The ending must honour the genre's commitment to consequence: if the protagonist acts, something changes — but rarely everything. Uchronia ends in complexity, not triumph.
- The final image should gesture toward the ongoing existence of the alternate world: this did not end when the protagonist's story did.
