Every uchronia manuscript must pass these tests before it goes to readers; these are the non-negotiable elements that distinguish competent alternate history from the failures that generate one-star reviews.

- [ ] **A specific, dateable divergence point.** Not "the South won the Civil War" but "on 17 September 1862, Lee's Special Order 191 was never found, the Army of Northern Virginia survived Antietam, and Lincoln could not issue the Emancipation Proclamation." The divergence must be identifiable as a single moment or a small cluster of moments, and the author must know this point precisely even if the text reveals it gradually.

- [ ] **A visible consequence chain.** The reader must be able to trace how the divergence caused the world that now exists. Not every link needs to be stated, but the major causal steps must be either explicit or clearly implied. If the reader cannot reconstruct the logic — how we got from the divergence to the present of the story — the world will feel arbitrary.

- [ ] **Historical research that shows in the texture.** Every scene set in the alternate world should carry specific details sourced from real historical research: the correct rank insignia, the actual slang of the period and culture, the real foods, the genuine propaganda aesthetics. Generic "1940s feel" is not enough. The author should be able to cite the sources for their specific details.

- [ ] **An alternate world that has been running for long enough to have normalised.** Even if the divergence was recent, the world must show signs of adaptation — people have rebuilt their lives around the new order, institutions have adjusted, language has shifted. A world that feels like the original history with a few bad decisions grafted on top is a failure of worldbuilding.

- [ ] **At least one character who is a genuine product of the alternate world.** A native inhabitant who has no nostalgia for the real timeline, whose values and assumptions were shaped entirely by the alternate order. This character is essential for showing the reader how the alternate world reproduces itself ideologically.

- [ ] **Moral complexity in every faction.** The resistance must not be purely virtuous; the collaborators must not be purely cowardly; the enforcers of the alternate order must not be purely evil. Every faction must contain people acting from recognisable human motivations: love, fear, ambition, habit, genuine belief.

- [ ] **The Obligatory Scenes present and specific.** The Normalisation Scene, the Divergence Point Exposition Scene, the Discovery Scene, and the Cost Scene must all appear (see beats.md). Each must be dramatised, not summarised.

- [ ] **A protagonist whose personal stakes are entangled with the historical stakes.** The alternate world's politics must impinge directly on the protagonist's life, relationships, or survival — not as background colour but as the engine of the plot. If the protagonist could live the same story in the real timeline, the alternate world is doing no work.

- [ ] **An ending that does not restore the original timeline (unless this is earned and central to the premise).** The default ending for uchronia is one in which the protagonist's arc concludes but the alternate world continues. Deus-ex-machina restorations that undo the divergence are a genre-killer unless the entire novel has been building to that possibility as its explicit premise.

- [ ] **No anachronistic technology, politics, or vocabulary in the alternate world.** Every element of the setting must be derivable from the divergence point. Technologies, political movements, or social attitudes that require the real timeline's specific history to exist cannot appear in the alternate world unless their presence in the alternate world is explicitly explained by the alternate timeline's own causal chain.
