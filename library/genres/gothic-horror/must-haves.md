- Following the height of the 1790s boom, Gothic fiction evolved during the Romantic period, becoming increasingly intertwined with Romantic preoccupations regarding nature, individualism, and the limits of scientific rationality. Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley's Frankenstein (1818) marked a watershed moment in the genre's development, fundamentally transforming the nature of Gothic villainy. Rather than featuring a supernatural demon or corrupted nobleman as the antagonist, Shelley presented the creature as a manifestation of human hubris and scientific folly—representing not evil incarnate but rather human culpability and moral responsibility. The novel's frame narrative, constructed through letters and journal entries, established the epistolary form as a quintessential Gothic technique for building atmosphere and fragmentation.[3][4][1]
- The Victorian era, spanning the mid-nineteenth through early twentieth centuries, became the most productive period for Gothic literature. The rapid industrialization, urbanization, and social upheaval of the nineteenth century generated profound anxieties that found expression through Gothic metaphors. Bram Stoker's Dracula (1897) synthesized eighteenth-century epistolary traditions with Victorian anxieties regarding sexuality, empire, disease, and degeneration. The novel's constructed narrative—assembled from letters, diary entries, newspaper clippings, and phonograph recordings—creates a polyphonic testimony to a reality-defying threat, establishing the epistolary form as essential to Gothic's atmosphere of fragmentation and collective effort against incomprehensible evil.[5][6][7][8][9]
