Readers expect a story where the past is not past — where architecture, inheritance, and unresolved history press down on the present with the weight of something alive and malevolent.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is brooding, formal, and atmospheric, with dread that accumulates slowly rather than striking suddenly. Readers expect prose that carries the weight of the genre's aesthetic inheritance: long sentences that coil back on themselves, descriptions of decay rendered in loving detail, interiority that cannot fully trust its own perceptions. The gothic mode requires a sustained double register — events that can be read as supernatural or psychological, at least for the first two acts. True horror in this genre comes less from external threat than from the revelation of what the protagonist's family, house, or history actually contains. Despair and beauty coexist; elegance is always slightly rotting.

## Pacing

Pacing is slow and deliberate by design. Gothic horror builds dread through accumulation: the stain that grows larger, the portrait whose expression shifts, the servant's answer that contains one word too many. Readers expect long atmospheric passages and are suspicious of novels that rush; the genre's pleasures are inseparable from its pace. The first act establishes the house and its weight. The second act involves investigative discovery — often research into family history, old documents, sealed rooms — that reveals the nature of what has been inherited. The third act brings confrontation: with the secret, the supernatural entity, or the protagonist's own culpability. The resolution should leave something unresolved; complete catharsis is genre-inappropriate.

## Setting Conventions

The house, estate, or institution is the genre's central character and must be rendered with architectural specificity: named rooms, particular stairwells, the quality of light at different hours, the way sound travels or fails to travel. Landscape mirrors interior state — moors, marshes, cliffs, forests in all their gothic conventionality are expected rather than avoided, because readers come to this genre for those conventions fulfilled with skill. The setting should have a history that precedes the protagonist by at least two generations; ideally the house itself carries the record of what happened in its materials (bloodstains, bricked doors, subsidence, cold rooms). Remote settings that enforce isolation are standard.

## Character Archetypes

The Inheritor arrives to claim a property or legacy and discovers the inheritance is not what it appeared. The Obsessive is consumed by the house's history or the supernatural presence to the point of unreliability. The Faithful Retainer knows more than they will say and is bound by loyalty to something other than the protagonist. The Rationalist attempts to explain events through natural cause and is usually wrong in ways that matter. The Ghost or Revenant embodies the specific injustice or transgression the house has been built to conceal; its nature should be specific rather than generic.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–110,000 words. Standalones are the genre norm, though companion volumes sharing a setting or family are common. First-person narration with an unreliable narrator is a canonical choice; close third-person achieves similar effects. Epistolary elements (letters, journals, newspaper clippings) are genre-conventional and reader-expected as a mechanism for the protagonist to access the house's history. The ending should not be entirely dark — survival with damage is more gothic than pure annihilation — but restoration and happiness are tonally incompatible with the genre.
