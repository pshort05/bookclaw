## Failure Modes

- Vocabulary: Elevated and precise. Favor words with weight and texture (e.g., opulent, decrepit, languid, oppressive, visceral, abyss). Avoid modern slang or overly casual contractions.
- 5. Negative Constraints (What to Avoid)
- Avoid Jump Scares: Do not rely on sudden, cheap shocks. Horror must be derived from dread—the anticipation of the scare.
- Avoid Clinical Descriptions: Do not describe wounds or ghosts with medical or scientific detachment (unless the POV is a specific scientist archetype). Describe them through the lens of horror and wrongness.
- Write in a lush, atmospheric style that emphasizes dread, decay, and the psychological deterioration of the protagonist. Use complex sentence structures and elevated vocabulary. Personify the setting (architecture and weather) as a hostile force. Focus on sensory details related to light/shadow, cold, silence, and rot. Dialogue should be formal, laden with subtext, and emotionally heightened. Avoid modern slang and fast-paced action; prioritize suspense and slow-building tension.
- Common Pitfalls & Solutions addresses clichés, excessive description, insufficient characterization, pacing errors, and over-explanation—providing specific remedies for each.[1][5]
- Best Practice: Vary vocabulary to avoid monotony. A single atmospheric word carries more weight than repetitive clustering. Let the prose breathe between intense descriptions.
- Vary sentence openings to avoid predictability
- Avoid clichés by seeking unexpected comparisons
- Urban Gothic presents decay within civilization itself—crumbling urban infrastructure, institutional buildings, the failure of modernity
- Avoid one-dimensional characters; grant even antagonists comprehensible motivation
- Gothic dialogue should avoid modern colloquialisms unless contemporary setting demands them
- Colloquial: "Don't go in there. It's dangerous."
- Sacred Transgression: Desecration of holy spaces, perversion of religious authority, and corruption of spiritual symbols suggest institutional failure.
- Locks & Chains: Contain dangerous forces; their failure releases horror
- Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them
- Solution: Balance description with action and dialogue. Each environmental detail should serve narrative purpose. Edit ruthlessly; delete beautiful sentences that don't advance story.
- Pacing Mistakes
- Gothic fiction frequently depicts corruption of religious sites and symbols, desecration of sacred spaces, and the perversion of religious authority. Monasteries, convents, and churches become locations of sexual violation, murder, and blasphemy, suggesting the vulnerability of spiritual spaces to human wickedness. This motif emerged historically from Protestant anxieties regarding Catholic monasticism but evolved into a broader statement about the failure of institutional authority to protect the vulnerable.[35][19][30]
