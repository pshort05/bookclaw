Readers expect a world where gunpowder and magic coexist under genuine tension, set against the revolutionary energy of an era on the cusp of industrialization.

## Tone & Mood

The tone balances swashbuckling adventure with philosophical unease. Readers expect a world in transition — old orders crumbling, new technologies disrupting established power — and want to feel that instability in the prose. There is room for heroism and humor, but the best entries carry an undercurrent of consequence: guns kill democratically, and magic that once guaranteed noble supremacy no longer does. Cynicism about institutions sits alongside genuine idealism in protagonists.

## Pacing

Pacing is brisk and action-oriented, with battlefield sequences and political intrigues alternating in roughly equal measure. Readers expect set-piece combat — sieges, naval engagements, skirmishes in cobblestone alleys — interspersed with quieter scenes of faction negotiation and magical discovery. The middle act often involves a training or journey sequence that earns the climactic confrontation. Slow world-building passages work only when tightly tied to plot momentum; extended lore dumps without dramatic stakes lose readers quickly in this genre.

## Setting Conventions

The default analogue spans roughly the late seventeenth to early nineteenth century: think Enlightenment salons, revolutionary barricades, Napoleonic campaigns, and early factory floors. Settings typically include at least one great city serving as a political hub, a frontier or colonial territory where social rules bend, and a battlefield or contested border. Magic systems in this genre must be codified and limited — readers expect rules that interact meaningfully with gunpowder technology, creating strategic choices rather than easy solutions. Whether magic is rare and aristocratic or newly accessible to commoners is a central worldbuilding decision that shapes every political conflict in the story.

## Character Archetypes

The Soldier-Mage straddles both power systems and belongs fully to neither military nor arcane establishment. The Revolutionary or Reformer uses the leveling effect of firearms to challenge magical elites. The Old Guard Sorcerer views guns as an existential threat and acts accordingly. The Engineer or Artificer hybridizes both traditions, building weapons that channel or counter magic. Rogues and Privateers operate at the margins where neither army nor guild has full authority. Female characters who exceed period gender constraints are common and expected; their struggle against those constraints should feel grounded rather than anachronistic.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 90,000–120,000 words. Series are the dominant format, with each volume resolving a contained military or political crisis while advancing a longer arc. Standalone novels work but must compress the world-building considerably. Chapters run short to mid-length (2,000–4,000 words), with chapter-ending hooks suited to the genre's adventure-fiction roots. Maps and appendices for magic-system rules are common reader expectations in longer series.
