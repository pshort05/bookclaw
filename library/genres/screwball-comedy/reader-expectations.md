Fast-paced romantic comedy built on rapid-fire dialogue, escalating misunderstandings, and two mismatched people forced together by circumstance until the chaos resolves into love.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is kinetic, witty, and relentlessly irreverent. Characters speak faster, react bigger, and find themselves in situations that snowball beyond all proportion. The humor is primarily verbal — overlapping dialogue, callbacks, non-sequiturs with perfect timing — and situational, arising from the mismatch between a character's dignity and their circumstances. The mood should feel breathless and slightly unhinged while never tipping into mean-spirited; the comedy deflates pretension, but characters must remain likable enough to root for. Warmth underpins the chaos — readers should sense these two impossible people are meant for each other, especially when they are at their most obnoxious.

## Pacing

Pacing is the genre's defining feature. Scenes move fast and conversations faster; internal monologue is minimal because characters act first and understand themselves later. Misunderstandings compound — each attempt to fix a problem creates a worse one. The structure is a tightening spiral: thrown together (Act 1), forced into escalating absurdity (Act 2), peak mutual disaster (the black moment, which is often genuinely funny), then the truth cuts through and the romantic resolution arrives with weight earned by everything before it. Pacing never fully relaxes until the final pages; even tender scenes carry energy.

## Setting Conventions

Screwball comedy needs a pressure-cooker container that keeps the protagonists in proximity despite their mutual exasperation: a road trip, a shared workplace, a mismatched living situation, an inherited estate. The setting should generate its own absurd logic — a country house where nothing works, a chaotic newsroom, a race with arbitrary rules. The containment mechanism must be established early and respected throughout. Contemporary settings dominate; period screwball (1930s-style) remains a viable subgenre.

## Character Archetypes

The two leads are opposites forced into proximity by a situation neither controls. One is typically organized, status-conscious, or goal-oriented; the other is chaotic, impulsive, or indifferent to conventional expectations. The tension between them comes from the fundamental incompatibility of their approaches to life, not from dislike — readers must believe that underneath the bickering there is recognition and even admiration. Both characters must be funny; a straight man paired with a comedian produces farce, not screwball. Supporting characters function as accelerants: a meddling family member, a rival suitor, a well-meaning friend who misreads every situation. No antagonist is strictly necessary; the plot provides the opposition.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 70,000–90,000 words. First-person or tight third-person is common because the comedy is driven by the gap between what the narrator perceives and what is actually happening. Dual-POV works when both characters are reliably funny with distinct comedic voices. Chapter lengths should be short to medium (1,500–2,500 words); long chapters slow the comedic tempo. Chapter titles are a common convention and can themselves be jokes. The HEA or HFN is required. Heat level is typically sweet to sensual.
