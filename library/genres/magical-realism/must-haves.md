Gabriel García Márquez stands as the canonical figure associated with this explosion of interest in magical realism, and his 1967 novel One Hundred Years of Solitude fundamentally altered the global literary landscape. This masterwork, which chronicles the rise, decline, and cyclical renewal of the Buendía family in the fictional Colombian village of Macondo across seven generations, has been called the quintessential work of magical realism. García Márquez himself explained the prevalence of magical and surreal elements in his fiction by stating: "In Mexico, surrealism runs through the streets. Surrealism comes from the reality of Latin America," yet crucially, he distinguished his approach from that of surrealist poets and painters by grounding his work in anecdotes rather than symbolic abstraction.
