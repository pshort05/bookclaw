Magical Realism readers expect the impossible to arrive without announcement and be absorbed by the narrative world without disrupting its emotional logic.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is lush, contemplative, and emotionally layered, with the magical elements functioning as extensions of psychological and cultural truth rather than as plot mechanics. The mood should feel like heightened reality — more vivid and patterned than ordinary experience, but never fantastic in the escapist sense. The magic does not solve problems or grant wishes; it reveals, embodies, and intensifies. Grief becomes a physical weather system; love produces literal fevers; ancestral shame takes root in the soil of a family's land. The emotional register is serious and often elegiac, concerned with history, loss, and the persistence of the past.

## Pacing

Time in Magical Realism is rarely linear. Pacing moves through loops, digressions, and returns — the same event revisited from different angles or at different scales of time. Readers accept and enjoy this non-linearity because the circling is itself meaningful: the form mirrors the genre's thematic concern with how the past refuses to stay past. Individual scenes can be slow and sensory-dense; the larger narrative moves in rhythms rather than toward a climax in the conventional thriller sense. Readers who come expecting plot momentum will be wrong-footed; readers in the right frame will find the rhythm deeply satisfying.

## Setting Conventions

Settings are culturally specific and rooted in place: a Colombian coastal town, a Mexican village, a Caribbean island, a Brazilian family estate. The magical elements are native to that place — local folklore, indigenous cosmology, Catholic saint-veneration, slave-era memory — not imported from European fantasy tradition. The setting carries historical weight: colonialism, land dispossession, political violence, and generational trauma are the soil the magic grows in. Contemporary Magical Realism extends the mode to urban settings and diaspora contexts, but the cultural rootedness and the specific relationship between community memory and the fantastic remain essential.

## Character Archetypes

Characters are embedded in family and community rather than isolated individuals pursuing quests. Matriarchs who hold generations together while quietly breaking apart are a signature figure. The witness-narrator — someone on the margin of the central action, observing a family or community across decades — appears frequently. Magical ability or affliction tends to pass through bloodlines rather than selecting chosen individuals. Antagonists are rarely personified; the forces working against characters are historical, institutional, or supernatural in the diffuse sense of a place being cursed by what was done there. Characters accept the magical without the shock a realist character would display.

## Length & Format

Novels run 70,000–100,000 words, with some landmark texts (Garcia Marquez, Allende) running longer. The literary novel form is standard; genre packaging (fantasy covers, marketing as escapist fiction) actively misrepresents reader expectations. First-person and close-third narration both appear, as does a distant but intimate omniscient voice that treats the community as the true protagonist. Epilogues and codas that jump forward in time are common, reinforcing the genre's concern with legacy and aftermath. Standalone novels are the norm; series are unusual.
