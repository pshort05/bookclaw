- A Comprehensive Guide to Magical Realism: History, Forms, Themes, and Contemporary Practice
- Magical realism is a distinctive literary genre that seamlessly integrates extraordinary magical elements into otherwise realistic, everyday settings without explanation or justification. Rather than treating the magical as remarkable or requiring defense, magical realist authors present fantastical occurrences as natural, mundane aspects of their fictional worlds—a narrative approach that challenges Western rationalist paradigms and Western philosophical assumptions about what constitutes "reality." Emerging initially as a visual art movement in 1920s Germany and Italy before flourishing as a literary phenomenon in mid-twentieth-century Latin America, magical realism has evolved into a global literary movement encompassing diverse cultures, languages, and perspectives. The genre functions simultaneously as an aesthetic innovation and a postcolonial strategy, allowing authors from previously colonized societies to privilege indigenous worldviews, oral traditions, and alternative epistemologies alongside Western rationalism. Contemporary magical realism continues to expand and evolve, with contemporary authors employing the genre to address twenty-first-century concerns including climate change, technological alienation, migration, identity politics, and historical trauma.
- The term "magical realism" originated not in literature but in the visual arts, emerging from a specific historical moment of aesthetic reaction against Expressionism. In 1925, German art critic and theorist Franz Roh published an essay titled "Magical Realism: Post Expressionism" in which he coined the German term magischer Realismus to describe a painterly movement known as Neue Sachlichkeit (New Objectivity). Roh distinguished this new aesthetic from the exaggerated emotionalism and distortion characteristic of Expressionism, praising instead the return to realistic representation that incorporated a subtle sense of mystery. The movement, which influenced German and Italian painters during the interwar period, was marked by highly detailed, photographic clarity combined with an uncanny or dreamlike quality that revealed what Roh termed "the magical nature of the rational world." Importantly, Roh explicitly distinguished magical realism from Surrealism, noting that while both movements incorporated mysterious or supernatural elements, magical realism maintained a focus on material objects and the actual existence of things in the world, whereas Surrealism ventured into abstract, psychological, and subconscious realms.[1][2][3]
- This visual art movement remained relatively obscure until the 1950s, when literary critics began recognizing similar aesthetic principles in Latin American fiction.
