Readers expect moral complexity, credible danger, and a world where the rules are breakable and breaking them has weight.

## Tone & Mood

Crime fiction spans a wide tonal range — from the bleak fatalism of noir to the dark wit of heist capers — but every subgenre shares a core expectation: the world is dangerous and the law is insufficient. Readers expect moral ambiguity. Protagonists may be criminals, compromised investigators, or ordinary people caught in systems larger than themselves. The tone calibrates to subgenre: noir is cynical and atmospheric; procedural is cool and methodical; heist fiction is kinetic and often playful; literary crime is introspective and slow-burning. Whatever the register, readers expect the author to take the violence, consequence, and human cost seriously, even when the surface tone is darkly comic.

## Pacing

Crime fiction readers expect structural momentum. The genre is fundamentally plot-driven, and pacing is organized around information: the crime, the investigation or plan, the complication, the reversal, the resolution. Heist narratives move fast, with ensemble choreography and escalating pressure. Noir tends to slow-burn through atmosphere and digression before delivering its gut-punch. Procedurals use chapter breaks to deliver small revelations that pull readers forward. Regardless of subgenre, the story must never feel like it is marking time. Every scene should move the plot, reveal character under pressure, or advance the reader's understanding of what actually happened.

## Setting Conventions

Crime fiction is intensely place-specific. The city — its neighborhoods, hierarchies, and hidden geographies — is almost always a primary element. Readers expect settings they can navigate: the precinct, the dive bar, the locked estate, the casino floor. Class and economics are embedded in setting; the genre is deeply interested in who has access to what spaces and why. Urban environments dominate, though rural noir and small-town crime fiction have strong readerships. The physical space of the crime itself carries symbolic weight and demands careful attention.

## Character Archetypes

Crime fiction supports a wider range of protagonist types than any other popular genre. The hard-bitten detective (private or public), the criminal mastermind, the reluctant insider, the first-time offender, the morally compromised cop, the journalist following a thread — all are genre-standard. Antagonists tend to be intelligent and motivated rather than cartoonishly evil; the best crime fiction makes the reader understand, if not sympathize with, what drove the crime. Ensemble casts are common in heist fiction. Femme fatales, corrupt officials, and loyal but doomed sidekicks are recurring types, though contemporary crime fiction often subverts or inverts them.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 70,000–120,000 words. Standalone novels are more common than in genre-fantasy; series with recurring investigators or criminal crews are commercially reliable. Chapter lengths are typically short to medium, with cliffhanger endings standard in commercial crime. Dual timeline structures (the crime and the investigation running in parallel) are a widely used format. First-person narration is traditional in hard-boiled and noir; third-person limited is common in procedurals. Prologues showing the crime before cutting to the investigation are a well-established genre convention.
