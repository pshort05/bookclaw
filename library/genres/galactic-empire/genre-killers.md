Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- The Planet-Killer: The ultimate threat. A weapon or ship capable of destroying a biosphere. It represents the absolute authority of the State over Nature.
- Thematic Integration identifies central thematic elements (power, technology's impact on humanity, imperialism, decline, and individual agency) and warns against common pitfalls like treating empire as inevitable or flattening alien cultures.
- Style Specifics provides a practical "do's and don'ts" checklist addressing prose economy, character grounding, technological integration, and the presentation of complex imperial systems.
- Avoid omniscient narration; maintain consistent POV within scenes while allowing different characters' perspectives to build complexity.
- Avoid "explaining" technical terms through dialogue; trust readers to absorb terminology through context.
- Avoid info-dumping: Distribute world-building information across multiple scenes and contexts.
- Decline and transformation: Treat imperial decay not as failure but as transformation; suggest what emerges after empires fall.
- Avoid treating any perspective as completely innocent; show costs and contradictions across all positions.
- Avoiding Common Pitfalls
- Don't treat empire as inevitable or natural; present it as contingent political formation.
- Avoid glorifying imperial expansion; show what conquest costs colonized peoples.
- Don't flatten alien or non-imperial cultures into exotic backdrop; develop them with internal complexity.
- STYLE SPECIFICS: DO'S AND DON'TS
- DON'T
- Don't explain technology through lengthy exposition; trust readers to absorb through context and use.
- Don't flatten imperial systems into simple good-vs-evil; create genuinely complex political positions and contradictions.
- Don't treat marginalized populations as mere backdrop; develop their perspectives, cultures, and agency.
- Don't sacrifice character for world-building; even in vast stories, readers care about individuals.
- Don't ignore the violence embedded in empire; if your story presents conquest, show what it costs.
- Don't use overly technical language that requires glossary; find accessible ways to convey complex ideas.
- Don't tell when you can show; demonstrate social hierarchies, technological capability, and cultural difference through scenes.
- Don't create perfect empires or perfect rebels; complexity and contradiction feel more authentic.
- Maintain consistent POV within scenes; avoid head-hopping.
- Avoid clichés; develop fresh comparisons reflecting your specific fictional universe.
- Imperial complexity: Does the scene avoid simplistic treatment of empire, resistance, or technology?
- Register: High, formal, and authoritative. Avoid casual slang or "earthy" colloquialisms.
- Naming Conventions: Use Greco-Roman, Prussian, or distinct "High Sci-Fi" sounding names. Avoid common 21st-century names (e.g., use "Valerius," "Kaelen," or "Thrawn," not "Steve" or "Kevin").
- Avoid "Technobabble" Overload: Focus on what the tech does (e.g., "the shields buckled"), not the quantum physics of how it works, unless the protagonist is an engineer.
