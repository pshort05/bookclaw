Readers expect civilizational-scale conflict played out through politics, warfare, and the slow machinery of empire across hundreds of star systems.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is operatic and serious, with room for genuine grandeur alongside corruption, tragedy, and institutional decay. Readers want to feel the weight of history — that events have consequences spanning generations — while still following individual characters whose personal stakes anchor the epic sweep. Cynicism about power is welcome and expected; the genre rarely produces uncomplicated triumphalism. The best entries sustain a tone of controlled melancholy beneath the spectacle: empires are inherently temporary, and readers understand this even when characters do not.

## Pacing

Pacing alternates between intimate political scenes — court intrigue, backroom negotiation, personal betrayal — and large-scale military or catastrophic events. Neither should dominate at the expense of the other. Readers expect the early chapters to establish the breadth of the empire through contrasting perspectives (a core-world aristocrat, a frontier officer, a colony-world rebel), then converge those threads as the central crisis develops. Long chapters and substantial subplots are tolerated because the genre demands scope; however, every subplot must connect to the central power struggle or it reads as padding.

## Setting Conventions

The Capital World is almost always a setting: decadent, inward-looking, disconnected from the suffering at the empire's edges. The Frontier or Border Region provides contrast — dangerous, pragmatic, where the real work of holding the empire together happens. The Deep Periphery (rebel-held space, alien territory, or lost colonies) functions as the place where the empire's contradictions become unavoidable. Readers expect world-building to be dense but legible: distinct planet cultures, coherent military hierarchies, and political factions that have comprehensible agendas. The politics must be at least as interesting as the space battles.

## Character Archetypes

The Reluctant Heir or Outsider Who Understands the System sees institutional rot that insiders have normalized. The Military Commander serves the empire while privately questioning its legitimacy. The Court Schemer operates in the capital with genuine competence and no fixed loyalty. The Revolutionary or Separatist Leader embodies the case against the empire, usually with moral complexity rather than simple villainy. The Ancient Institution (a religious order, a diplomatic corps, an intelligence service) has its own agenda that predates the current emperor and will outlast the next one.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 100,000–150,000 words; series spanning three to six volumes are the default format. Single-volume entries require tight scope — usually one succession crisis or one war — to avoid feeling truncated. Multiple POV chapters are expected; five to eight POV characters across the empire is common. Maps and appendices are standard reader expectations and should be delivered with care.
