# Suspense Thriller Comparable Titles

## Gone Girl by Gillian Flynn (2012)

Dual unreliable narrators — wife and husband — reframe each other's version of a disappearance. Establishes the "twist at the midpoint" structure and the domestic-threat template that defined the decade.

## The Girl on the Train by Paula Hawkins (2015)

An alcoholic commuter witnesses something from a train window and inserts herself into the investigation. The unreliable witness narrator, obsessive observation, and suburban malice are genre touchstones.

## Big Little Lies by Liane Moriarty (2014)

Three women in a coastal town are connected by a death at a school fundraiser. Combines domestic suspense with social satire; demonstrates that the threat can be embedded in friendship and community.

## Behind Closed Doors by B.A. Paris (2016)

A perfect marriage conceals a systematic, calculated horror. Effective for its slow revelation structure and the way ordinary domestic detail becomes sinister.

## The Silent Patient by Alex Michaelides (2019)

A famous painter shoots her husband and never speaks again. The therapist investigating her silence is as unreliable as his subject. Strong example of the twist built into character rather than plot mechanics.

## The Woman in the Window by A.J. Finn (2018)

An agoraphobic woman believes she has witnessed a crime from her house — but her perception and memory are medically compromised. Updates the Rear Window premise with an explicitly unreliable protagonist.

## You by Caroline Kepnes (2014)

A bookstore clerk becomes obsessed with a customer; the entire novel is narrated by the stalker. Inverts reader identification to create tension between sympathy and horror.

## Sometimes I Lie by Alice Feeney (2018)

A woman wakes in a coma with no memory of what put her there. Three timelines converge on a revelation about perception and self-deception; a strong example of the amnesia-narrator device used structurally rather than as gimmick.
