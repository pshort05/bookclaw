Verified comparable titles for steampunk fiction — each confirmed as a genre example, with notes on what it models well and where it sits on the steampunk spectrum.

## Boneshaker by Cherie Priest (2009)

**Core steampunk credentials:** Alternate Civil War-era American West. A drilling machine ruptures the ground beneath Seattle, releasing toxic gas that creates zombie-like "rotters"; the city has been walled off for sixteen years. A mother follows her son into the quarantined zone to clear her late husband's name. Won the 2010 Locus Award for Best Science Fiction Novel; nominated for Hugo and Nebula.

**What it models:** American steampunk (non-British setting), horror-steampunk crossover, industrial horror as genre beat, working-class protagonist with no technical training who is nonetheless defined by her relationship to machinery and its consequences. Exceptionally useful comp for dark-register steampunk, for stories set in the American West or frontier, and for narratives where the technology has already caused the disaster and the story is the reckoning.

## Mortal Engines by Philip Reeve (2001)

**Core steampunk credentials:** Far-future secondary world. London is a massive predatory traction city that consumes smaller towns for fuel and resources under the ideology of "Municipal Darwinism." Steam-powered mobile cities, Victorian-style guild hierarchies, airships. Won the Nestle Smarties Book Prize Gold Award and the 2003 Blue Peter Award for Best Book.

**What it models:** Secondary-world steampunk (no need for historical grounding), the city as technology as antagonist, class hierarchy embedded in mechanical systems, YA adventure pacing with genuine political stakes. Use as a comp for far-future or secondary-world settings, for stories where the technology is a civilization-level force rather than individual invention, and for YA steampunk with structural social critique.

## Leviathan by Scott Westerfeld (2009, trilogy)

**Core steampunk credentials:** Alternate World War I. Central Powers ("Clankers") use steam-powered mechanical walkers; the Entente ("Darwinists") deploy genetically fabricated living creatures as weapons. YA, illustrated by Keith Thompson. The technology sits at the steampunk/dieselpunk boundary (early 20th-century rather than strictly Victorian).

**What it models:** Biopunk-steampunk hybrid, dual POV (working-class soldier / aristocrat in disguise), illustrated worldbuilding, the gender-disguise trope in a mechanized-warfare setting. Comp for YA steampunk, for stories featuring military technology as world engine, and for narratives that play the class-crossing and gender-constraint tropes simultaneously.

## The Difference Engine by William Gibson and Bruce Sterling (1990)

**Core steampunk credentials:** Alternate 1855 Britain where Charles Babbage successfully built a mechanical computer. Lord Byron leads an Industrial Radical Party; technology has transformed society; rival factions pursue mysterious punch cards through a smog-choked London. Nominated for the Nebula Award (1991) and the Campbell Memorial Award (1992). The novel that codified steampunk as a literary genre.

**What it models:** Political steampunk, the difference engine (mechanical computation) as world-altering technology, dense alternate-history construction, the conspiracy thriller structure applied to steampunk. Comp for literary, politically serious, or dense-world-building steampunk. Note: pacing is slow and novelistic rather than adventure-forward; not a pacing model for commercial steampunk.

## Soulless by Gail Carriger (2009, Parasol Protectorate series)

**Comp with classification caveat:** Carriger's novel is published and shelved as steampunk paranormal romance, but is more accurately gaslamp fantasy — the world engine is supernatural (vampires and werewolves integrated into Victorian society) and the steam technology is aesthetic background rather than causal plot driver. Use it as a comp for tone (comedic Victorian manners, witty dialogue, romance-adventure blend) and for the female protagonist navigating social constraint through wit rather than force, not for steampunk technological worldbuilding.

**What it models:** Comedic register, paranormal romance crossover, female protagonist in Victorian social constraint, series worldbuilding, the found-family romantic team. Comp for lighter-toned, romance-forward, socially comedic steampunk or gaslamp fantasy.

## Perdido Street Station by China Mieville (2000, New Crobuzon series)

**Core steampunk credentials:** Industrial city of New Crobuzon where a scientist accidentally releases mind-devouring slakemoths. Steampunk aesthetics (steam engines, gas lamps, airships, railways) combined with weird fiction and socialist political critique. Described by John Clute as "the best steampunk novel since Gibson and Sterling's The Difference Engine."

**What it models:** Grimdark or literary steampunk, technology entangled with capitalist exploitation, the socialist critique of industrial power structures, weird fiction crossover, non-human races and constructed beings with complex political status. Comp for dark, literary, politically explicit steampunk. Note: not a commercial adventure pacing model; the novel is 867 pages and deliberately challenging.

## The Iron Duke by Meljean Brook (2010, Iron Seas series)

**Core steampunk credentials:** Alternate Europe occupied for two centuries by the Mongol Horde, who used nano-agent "bugs" to control the population; liberated England is rebuilding with steam technology. A pirate-turned-duke investigates a body dropped on his ship alongside Inspector Mina Wentworth. Credited by genre reviewers with defining steampunk romance as a distinct subgenre.

**What it models:** Steampunk romance with genuine alternate-history consequence (the nano-agent occupation has lasting social effects), the female detective protagonist, empire and colonialism as backstory with real plot weight, steam technology with internal logic. Comp for romance-steampunk hybrids and for stories where the alternate history is about occupation and liberation rather than British imperial expansion.

## Everfair by Nisi Shawl (2016)

**Core steampunk credentials:** Alternate-history Congo where Fabian Socialists and African American missionaries purchase land from the Belgian king to create a refuge for escaped slaves, using steam-powered technology — including dirigibles and prosthetic "jewel hands" — to resist Belgian colonization. The most politically serious steampunk novel of the past decade.

**What it models:** Non-Western steampunk, colonial resistance as the genre's social-critique backbone, multiple POV structure across decades, the steam-powered prosthetic as literal self-making under oppression. Comp for steampunk that centers colonized or marginalized protagonists, for multi-decade or multi-POV structures, and for narratives where the technology is explicitly a tool of liberation rather than empire.
