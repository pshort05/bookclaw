Non-negotiable elements that must be present and functional in any steampunk novel — their absence or failure is the most common cause of reader rejection.

- [ ] **Technology drives plot, not atmosphere.** Steam-powered machinery, mechanical computation, airships, or automata must be causally responsible for the central conflict and must be the mechanism of its resolution. If removing all the technology from your synopsis leaves the story intact, you have written a historical adventure with steampunk decoration, not steampunk.

- [ ] **One foundational impossible thing, internally consistent.** The genre grants readers one major suspension of disbelief: working difference engines, steam-powered traction cities, biological fabrication. That one thing must be established clearly and operated with internal rules that hold throughout the novel. Every additional impossible element must be justified within those rules or earned through the world's logic.

- [ ] **A specific alternate-history or secondary-world setting with industrial geography.** The setting is not "Victorian England" as a vague mood — it is a place with named districts, specific technological infrastructure, and a visible class geography. Factory quarters, airship docks, guild halls, and slum neighborhoods do different narrative work; use the specificity.

- [ ] **A class, imperial, or gender power structure that creates real obstacles.** The Victorian-era social order must be an active antagonist, not background color. It must produce specific, named obstacles for the protagonist: a patent refused, a commission denied, an invention attributed to someone else, access blocked by birth or gender or colonial status.

- [ ] **A protagonist who makes or understands the technology.** The genre's signature protagonist is an inventor, engineer, natural philosopher, or mechanist whose relationship to the technology is active and creative. A protagonist who merely rides the airship and admires the automata is a tourist. The protagonist must be someone who can build, repair, repurpose, or subvert the machinery.

- [ ] **An invention set-piece where the protagonist engineers their way out of a crisis.** At least one scene — and ideally several — where the protagonist solves a life-or-death problem through technical ingenuity rather than combat or persuasion alone. The solution must be set up earlier in the narrative so it reads as earned, not convenient.

- [ ] **Social critique with teeth.** Steampunk without a critical stance toward the power structures it depicts is aesthetic cosplay. The "punk" in steampunk is an anti-establishment posture — someone is being exploited or suppressed by the system, and the protagonist is in conflict with that system, not a comfortable beneficiary of it.

- [ ] **A found family or crew whose working-class or marginal members are legible as people.** The crew must be characterized individually, not as interchangeable color. Their stakes in the outcome must be as legible as the protagonist's. They represent the human cost of the technology that the protagonist wields.

- [ ] **The technology's cost is made visible.** Coal must come from somewhere. The automaton's labor must displace someone. The airship's range must be limited by fuel and weather. Every technology's benefit must be accompanied by a legible cost that falls on specific people. This is what separates steampunk from tech-utopian fantasy.

- [ ] **An aerial or vehicle sequence that uses the world's technology for kinetic action.** The genre's visual signature must appear in motion, under pressure, with stakes. A single sustained chase, pursuit, or mechanical crisis involving the world's primary transportation or weapon technology is the minimum delivery.
