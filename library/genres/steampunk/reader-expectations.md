Steampunk readers want adventure-forward fiction set in a Victorian or Edwardian analog where steam-powered technology is the engine of the world — not decoration — and where the protagonist's ingenuity collides with rigid class and imperial power structures.

## Tone and Mood

**Sense of wonder is non-negotiable.** Readers come for spectacle: a city that walks, a ship made of living tissue, a difference engine deciphering treason. The opening pages must deliver a moment that says "this world is impossible and I want to live in it." Wonder does not mean lightness — Perdido Street Station is harrowing — but even dark steampunk carries an electricity of discovery.

**Social critique is the "punk."** A steampunk novel without friction against power structures — industrial monopolies, class stratification, colonial empire, gender constraint — is steam without punk. Readers who pick up steampunk expect the elegance-and-grit tension: gaslit parlors next to smoke-belching foundries, aristocratic codes colliding with mechanical pragmatism. The aesthetic alone is not enough. If your protagonist does not challenge something structural, reviewers will call it cosplay.

**Tone range:** adventure-comedic (Gail Carriger's register), serious adventure (Cherie Priest's Boneshaker), political literary (China Miéville). All three are valid. What is not valid: grimdark with steampunk as backdrop and no engagement with the genre's wonder and social dimensions.

## Pacing

**Adventure-forward is the default.** Steampunk reads fast: discovery, daring escapade, dangerous complication, invention under pressure. The ratio of action to reflection tilts toward momentum. Readers who want slow-burn political meditation tend to reach for literary SF; steampunk readers want propulsion.

**Invention set-pieces are obligatory.** At least one scene where the protagonist must engineer their way out of a problem — not fight, not persuade, but build or adapt or repurpose technology under constraint. This scene is the genre's signature. It must have stakes, time pressure, and a solution that feels both surprising and inevitable given what the reader has learned.

**Pacing trap:** stopping the narrative to explain the technology. Show the steam catapult in operation during a chase; do not deliver a lecture on its principles beforehand.

## Setting

**The world must be specific, not generic Victorian.** Establish early whether this is alternate-history Britain, an American West analog (as in Boneshaker), a secondary world with Victorian aesthetics (Mortal Engines), or something more remote. Vague "steampunk world" without specificity reads as lazy. Name the city, the empire, the technological factions.

**Industrial geography matters.** Class is written in space: the factory districts, the airship docks, the smog-choked slums, the club rooms of the powerful. Move the protagonist through these zones and let the contrast do thematic work. The city should feel like a character with competing interests, not a backdrop.

**Technology must have internal logic.** Steam power has limits: fuel weight, pressure tolerances, coal supply chains. Airships that ignore physics feel like magic with brass fittings. One foundational impossible thing (municipal Darwinism, biological fabrication, a working difference engine) earns readers' suspension of disbelief. Every additional impossible element must be justified within the established rules.

## Character Archetypes

**The inventor or engineer protagonist** is the genre's most common lead — someone who makes things, understands systems, and solves problems through applied ingenuity rather than combat prowess alone. This need not be a male genius; the genre has moved significantly toward female inventors and engineers (Priest, Carriger, Westerfeld's Deryn Sharp). The protagonist's relationship to their own making — pride, fear of what they've created, external resistance to their innovations — is often the emotional spine.

**The aristocrat as antagonist or uneasy ally** embodies institutional power. They are not simply villains; the most interesting versions are people who believe in the system that benefits them, generating conflict that is structural rather than personal.

**The working-class crew or found family** appears in almost every steampunk narrative. These are the people who keep the machinery running — mechanics, airship hands, factory workers, street urchins. They represent the human cost of the technology and often provide the moral grounding the protagonist lacks.

**The automaton or constructed being** raises the genre's recurring question: where does mechanism end and consciousness begin? Whether clockwork servant, fabricated creature, or reanimated body, the constructed being externalizes the genre's anxiety about technology replacing or devaluing human life.

## Length and Format

**Adult commercial steampunk:** 90,000–120,000 words is standard. Debut authors should aim at the lower end (80,000–100,000). Under-delivery at 60,000 words reads as incomplete world-building; over-delivery past 130,000 requires exceptional justification.

**YA steampunk:** 70,000–90,000 words.

**Point of view:** Third-person limited is the most common choice for adventure steampunk and allows close access to the protagonist's engineering problem-solving while maintaining the scope to show the world. First person is common in romance-adjacent and YA steampunk. Omniscient is rare and associated with literary steampunk (Miéville).

**Series:** Almost every major steampunk title is a series. Readers expect the world to continue. A standalone-with-series-potential — a complete story in one volume with an open world — is the practical structure for a debut. Avoid ending on a hard cliffhanger without a contracted second volume; steampunk readers have become wary of unfinished series.
