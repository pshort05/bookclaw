Steampunk's obligatory scenes are the moments readers come to collect — the invention under fire, the class confrontation, the aerial chase, the conspiracy exposed. A steampunk novel that delivers all five earns its genre label; one that omits them needs a compelling structural reason.

## Obligatory Scene 1: The World in Motion (Opening Technology Demonstration)

The first obligatory scene is not an info-dump — it is a demonstration. Within the first three chapters, show the world's technology operating under real conditions, with real stakes or costs. Not: "The difference engines hummed in their rows." But: "The differential engine chattered through sixteen hundred punch cards in forty seconds and returned an answer that was wrong, which meant someone in the ministry was lying." The reader must understand what the world's technology can do, what its limits are, and why those limits matter — all through action, not exposition.

**What it does:** Establishes the genre contract. Signals that technology will be causal (plot-driving) rather than atmospheric (decorative). Sets the baseline for what "normal" means in this world so that the later violations of normal land with weight.

## Obligatory Scene 2: The Class Confrontation

The protagonist encounters the power structure directly and is dismissed, obstructed, co-opted, or threatened because of who they are rather than what they know. An inventor whose patent is refused because she is a woman. An airship engineer told his access to the guild hall ends at the service entrance. A colonial subject whose mechanical genius is attributed to his European supervisor. The confrontation must be specific — not vague period atmosphere but a named person in authority doing a specific thing that blocks the protagonist's goal through the mechanism of the class or gender or colonial system.

**What it does:** Makes the genre's social critique concrete rather than thematic. Gives the protagonist a specific institutional antagonist to work around or against. Raises the personal stakes so the climax is not just about defeating a villain but about changing or escaping a structure.

## Obligatory Scene 3: The Invention Breakthrough Under Pressure

The protagonist must engineer a solution — not fight, not negotiate, but build, adapt, or repurpose — under time constraint and with imperfect materials. This is the genre's signature scene and must appear at a moment of genuine crisis: a chase, an imprisonment, a mechanical failure that will kill everyone aboard. The solution should use something the reader has already seen (a principle established earlier, a piece of salvaged technology, a flaw in the enemy's machine) and should feel both surprising and inevitable.

**What it does:** Pays off the inventor characterization. Differentiates steampunk from adventure fantasy where protagonists solve problems with swords or magic. Gives the reader the pleasure of watching a technical mind work at full speed under maximum pressure.

## Obligatory Scene 4: The Airship (or Vehicle) Chase or Pursuit

An extended kinetic sequence set aboard, around, or between flying or steam-powered vehicles. Altitude, speed, mechanical failure under fire, the physical reality of wind and gravity and coal consumption — these are the scene's raw materials. The best versions complicate the chase with a moral or emotional dimension: the protagonist is hunting someone they understand, or being hunted by someone they were wrong about, or forced to sacrifice the vehicle they love to save the people inside it.

**What it does:** Delivers spectacle. Uses the world's technology to generate action that could not exist in a non-steampunk novel. Forces the protagonist into a situation where both technical skill and human judgment are tested simultaneously.

## Obligatory Scene 5: The Conspiracy Reveal

The protagonist discovers what the antagonist's technology is actually doing or what the institution is actually protecting. The reveal must be specific, mechanical, and political — not "evil plans" but "the guild has been suppressing this invention for thirty years because it would have ended their monopoly on city heating," or "the ministry's difference engines are not calculating trade statistics; they are profiling labor organizers for arrest." The conspiracy must be rooted in the technology: the antagonist's power comes from controlling what the technology can do and who knows about it.

**What it does:** Reframes the protagonist's earlier defeats as having a cause beyond personal antagonism. Raises the stakes from personal to systemic. Makes the climax necessary — the protagonist cannot simply leave, because others will continue to suffer if the conspiracy stands.

## Obligatory Scene 6: The Sacrifice-for-Progress Choice

The protagonist must choose between a personal good (safety, relationship, their invention itself, the life of someone they love) and the larger goal that their ingenuity has made possible. This is not a trolley problem in the abstract; it is a specific, embodied loss. Cherie Priest puts Briar in the walled city and makes her choose between her son's life and clearing her late husband's name — and the two goals are in tension until the end. The choice must cost something real, not something that gets conveniently restored in the final pages.

**What it does:** Grounds the genre's technological wonder in human stakes. Prevents the climax from being purely mechanical. Forces the protagonist to articulate what they believe progress is worth, which is the genre's central question made personal.

## Structural Note

These six beats are not strictly sequential, but the opening technology demonstration must come first, and the sacrifice choice almost always comes last (or is the climax itself). The class confrontation is most effective in the first third; the conspiracy reveal in the second half. The invention breakthrough and the aerial chase can appear multiple times — a steampunk novel with only one chase scene is underdelivering.
