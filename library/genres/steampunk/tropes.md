Steampunk has a defined set of expected tropes that readers come to collect — deploy them with intention rather than by reflex, and ensure each one does narrative work rather than serving as set decoration.

## 1. The Airship

The genre's most iconic image: a vessel suspended by gas or mechanical lift, crossing industrial skylines or contested airspace. The airship functions as transport, status symbol, action arena, and metaphor for human ambition exceeding physical limits. It appears as a merchant vessel, a warship, a pirate prize, a refugee ark. Its presence signals freedom of movement across borders that would otherwise contain the protagonist. Do not include it as background; put the protagonist on one and let the physical reality — the altitude, the exposure, the coal consumption — drive a scene.

## 2. The Inventor Protagonist

A character who understands systems, makes things, and solves problems through applied knowledge rather than brute force or magic. Typically an outsider in their social context (wrong class, wrong gender, wrong politics) whose ability to build gives them leverage the establishment cannot simply neutralize. The tension between their creative capability and institutional resistance is the genre's core engine.

## 3. Class Crossing

The protagonist moves between social strata that the world insists are fixed. A factory girl who gains access to the inventor's guild, an aristocrat who must work alongside street mechanics, a colonial subject who masters the empire's own technology. This movement generates conflict on both sides: the lower orders who see the crosser as a traitor, the upper orders who see them as an intruder. The technology often enables or provokes the crossing — it is the ladder, or the reason the ladder exists.

## 4. The Clockwork Automaton

A constructed being — mechanical, biological-mechanical, or reanimated — that raises questions the human characters cannot answer cleanly. Does it suffer? Does it choose? Is its labor exploitation or function? The automaton is the genre's displacement of industrial-era anxieties about workers reduced to machines, and its presence in the story should generate moral unease, not just visual interest.

## 5. The Conspiracy at the Intersection of Power and Technology

Someone in the establishment is using technology to entrench or extend their control. A guild monopolizes a critical invention. An imperial ministry suppresses a device that would liberate the working class. A scientific brotherhood conceals what their experiment has produced. The conspiracy is why the protagonist's ingenuity alone is insufficient — the obstacle is not technical, it is political, and dismantling it requires dismantling the power structure around it.

## 6. The Found Family Crew

A group assembled by circumstance rather than blood: airship hands, fellow escapees, street urchins, disgraced engineers, repentant aristocrats. The crew provides the protagonist with loyalty that the institutions have refused them. Its dynamics — internal friction, moments of sacrifice, the member who betrays and the one who stays — are as important as the external plot. The crew also represents the social cross-section the protagonist could not access alone.

## 7. The Mad Scientist or Rogue Inventor

A figure who took the protagonist's same creative impulse further and produced something dangerous. Not simply a villain — the most useful versions are people who believed in progress and were right about the technology and wrong about the cost. The rogue inventor shows the protagonist what they could become, and forces them to define what limits they will impose on their own making.

## 8. Industrial Horror

The genre's dark mode: a technology that has escaped control, produced something monstrous, or revealed the human cost of mechanical progress. Boneshaker's zombie outbreak from industrial drilling is the model — the horror is inseparable from what the machine did, and reversing it requires understanding the machine. The industrial horror beat forces the story to reckon with what the technology actually costs.

## 9. Gender as Constraint and Subversion

Victorian and Edwardian settings carry built-in gender restriction. The genre's response is typically to put a protagonist in direct conflict with those constraints through the technology itself: a woman inventor whose work is attributed to her male supervisor, a female soldier disguised to serve in a mechanized corps, a non-binary person whose constructed identity challenges the binary social categories of the period. This trope is most effective when the gender constraint is specific and has mechanical plot consequences, not when it appears as vague period atmosphere.

## 10. The Heist or the Theft of Technology

Someone must acquire, destroy, or expose a piece of technology that the establishment is using against the people. The heist provides structure — reconnaissance, assembly of specialists, the plan, the execution, the unexpected complication — and gives the found-family crew specific roles. The technology being stolen or exposed is usually the conspiracy's instrument or evidence.

## 11. The Imperial Frontier

The story moves beyond the established center — beyond London or the capital — to a contested edge: a colony, a desert, an occupied city, a quarantined zone. The frontier is where the empire's rules are most brutal and where the protagonist has the most freedom to operate outside normal constraints. It also forces the story to confront what the empire is actually doing at its edges, rather than allowing the center's self-flattering narrative to stand.

## 12. The Invention That Changes Everything

A breakthrough device, process, or discovery that would fundamentally alter the power structure if it reached the public. This is the MacGuffin with purpose: whoever controls it controls the future, which is why the establishment will kill to suppress it and the protagonist will risk everything to release it. The invention must be specific enough to have clear consequences — not "a better engine" but "an engine that runs without coal, which breaks the coal barons' stranglehold."
