Readers expect to laugh at things they probably shouldn't, and to find that the laughter is the point rather than a defense mechanism.

## Tone & Mood

Dark comedy's defining tonal commitment is refusal: refusal to treat death, failure, suffering, or moral catastrophe with the gravity the surrounding culture insists on. The humor is not incidental but structural — the darkness is the source material, not an obstacle to the comedy. Readers expect a narrator who maintains ironic distance from situations that would destroy a realistic protagonist, and who communicates that distance to the reader as shared complicity. The tone can range from dry and deadpan to farcical and chaotic to bitterly satirical, but what readers do not expect is sentiment: genuine emotional appeals to feel bad about the bad things undermine the contract.

## Pacing

Dark comedy tends to move fast. The comic engine requires situations to escalate before the reader has time to feel appropriately terrible about them. Readers expect setups to pay off and payoffs to create new, worse setups. Digression is acceptable — often funny in itself — but the narrative should feel like it is building toward something, even if that something is a spectacular collapse. Quiet scenes establish the absurd normalcy that chaos will violate. Extended ruminative passages asking the reader to sit with suffering shift the register into something they did not sign up for.

## Setting Conventions

Setting in dark comedy is typically mundane, institutional, or bureaucratic — the office, the funeral home, the hospital ward, the government department, the small town where everyone knows everyone's business. The comedy often derives from the mismatch between the setting's official function (providing comfort, managing death, delivering services) and what actually happens there. Readers expect the setting to be complicit in the absurdity: the systems and environments that are supposed to manage the darkness of human experience instead make it worse, funnier, and more inescapable. Heightened or surreal settings are acceptable when the rules of the world are established early and applied consistently.

## Character Archetypes

Protagonists are typically hapless rather than malevolent — they make things worse through incompetence, cowardice, or the reasonable pursuit of self-interest under impossible circumstances. The true villain is often the system or entropy itself. Surrounding characters include the cheerfully oblivious, the catastrophically competent, the single sane person whose sanity is itself the joke, and the bureaucrat who enforces meaningless rules with maximum sincerity. Death, disgrace, and moral failure are not punishments for bad characters but occupational hazards for everyone.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 60,000–90,000 words; the genre supports shorter works well. Short story and novella forms have a strong tradition. The comic momentum of dark fiction is difficult to sustain at epic length without tonal fatigue. Chapter structures tend to be short and punchy. Third-person narration with access to multiple characters' obliviousness is common. First-person unreliable narrators who do not recognize how badly things are going are a classic dark comedy device.
