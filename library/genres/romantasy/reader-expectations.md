Romance set in a fully realized fantasy world, where the romantic arc is the primary storyline and must deliver a happy ending or happy-for-now, while the fantasy setting and magic system supply the conflict and stakes.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is emotionally intense and often lush, blending the heightened feeling of romance with the wonder and danger of a secondary world. Readers expect swooning alongside peril — the best romantasy makes both the love story and the world feel equally real and high-stakes. Dark or morally complex worlds are welcome, even common, as long as the emotional payoff remains. Banter and wit balance the heavier moments; the enemies-to-lovers trajectory and slow-burn longing are the dominant emotional registers. The fantasy darkness must never overwhelm the romantic hope that the HEA/HFN requires.

## Pacing

Pacing tends toward the epic — romantasy novels run long and the middle must sustain both plot momentum and romantic escalation simultaneously. The romance follows the standard beats (meeting, tension, false intimacy, black moment, resolution) but they are spaced across a fantasy quest, political intrigue, or magical crisis. The "black moment" often coincides with a high-stakes fantasy event. Dual-POV chapters that switch on cliffhangers are a structural staple. Romantic tension must not stall for extended worldbuilding passages; the two threads must interweave rather than alternate.

## Setting Conventions

The world must be internally consistent and feel lived-in, but exposition should be woven through action and dialogue rather than delivered in blocks. Magic systems range from soft (intuitive, rule-light) to hard (rigidly defined); romantasy skews toward soft-to-medium, since the romance takes priority over puzzle-box mechanics. Fae kingdoms, dark courts, and magical academies are the current dominant settings. The world must generate genuine physical danger that the protagonists face together, because shared peril is the engine that accelerates intimacy. Secondary world travel — quests, missions, escapes — is preferred over single fixed-location settings.

## Character Archetypes

The heroine is physically capable and possesses agency that matters to the plot — a warrior, assassin, spy, scholar with unusual power, or chosen figure. She need not be the most powerful person in the room, but she must not be passive. The hero is typically a dark, powerful, morally ambiguous figure: a fae lord, a general, a king, a villain-adjacent protector. The enemies-to-lovers and reluctant-allies-to-lovers trajectories are dominant. Found family supporting characters are expected and beloved; they must feel distinct, not interchangeable. Mentors and antagonists should have motivations that make sense on their own terms.

## Length & Format

Standard length is 90,000–130,000 words; the upper end is common. Dual-POV is the baseline expectation. Series are the norm — standalone romantasy is a harder sell; readers expect at minimum a duology. Maps and glossaries are welcomed. Heat level is most commonly sensual to explicit, though the cleaner end has grown as fantasy readers cross over. Chapters must end on hooks or emotional beats.
