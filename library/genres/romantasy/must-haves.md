## Essential Checklist

- Strategic Mandate: The protagonist must dominate the narrative through a high-stakes combination of capability and hidden wounds. Their power is only as compelling as the trauma they are actively navigating.
- Physically Capable: Whether a dragon rider, elite assassin, or battle-hardened warrior, the lead must possess the physical agency to survive the world’s high-stakes trials.
- Sexually Confident: To maintain agency, the protagonist must be an active participant in intimacy. Sexual desire is a tool of empowerment, never a passive experience.
- The Act-Break Decision: The protagonist must make at least one plot-altering choice per act that shifts the direction of the narrative.
- Rescue Fatigue Audit: Scrutinize every scene where the love interest saves the protagonist. If a rescue occurs, the protagonist must provide the intellectual or magical solution that makes the rescue possible.
- Strategic Mandate: The love interest must be more than a "dark aesthetic." They must embody the specific dangers of the world—Fae court lethality, dragon-rider ruthlessness, or vampire blood-lust—while maintaining a fierce, protective devotion.
- High Power Levels: They should hold significant political or magical dominance (e.g., General, King, High Lord), creating a power imbalance that the protagonist must learn to navigate or disrupt.
- The Protective/Obsessive Line: Manage the tension where fierce protection skirts the edge of possessiveness. This "edginess" is a market staple but must be balanced with respect for the protagonist's autonomy.
- Establish Enmity: Start with ideological or political opposition (e.g., warring kingdoms). It must be more than simple bickering; it must be a reason to kill one another.
- The supernatural bond must enhance character development, not replace it.
- Visceral Magic: The bond must be a "felt" experience. Use the magic system to reflect arousal, anger, or fear. The magic should respond to their heart rate and their desire.
- Consistent Behavioral Impact: Trauma must have a realistic psychological impact. If the protagonist has a history of betrayal, they must struggle with trust consistently, not just when it creates a "steamy" misunderstanding.
- Identity Reclamation: The character must move from hiding their true nature to embracing their role as a leader or a powerhouse, often symbolized by a change in their magical output or social standing.
- Chosen Family: Relationships with squads or courts must be treated as equally important to the central romance. These bonds provide the emotional safety net and collective power needed for the final conflict.
