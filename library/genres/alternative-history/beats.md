- Alternate history, sometimes referred to as allohistory or althist, is a subgenre of speculative fiction where one or more real historical events are altered, leading to divergent outcomes and newly imagined worlds. Unlike traditional historical fiction, which remains faithful to actual events, alternate history leverages a “point of divergence”—a moment where historiographical reality shifts to what might have been. The genre’s narrative structure often explores “what if?” scenarios about wars, elections, technological inventions, or other pivotal moments.[1][2][3][4]

- While closely related to historical fiction and science fiction, alternate history is set apart by its deliberate restructuring of actual events and explicit questioning of causality and contingency.[8][9][4]
- The genre has become a powerful tool to analyze the role of contingency, the construction of national or cultural identity, and the consequences of historical interpretation. By integrating speculative elements (from advanced technology to magic), alternate history frequently blurs the boundaries between science fiction, fantasy, and historical fiction, encouraging readers and scholars alike to interrogate the factual and the imagined. It also embraces narrative techniques such as detailed world-building, plausible alteration of cultural, political, and social landscapes, and the repurposing of historical figures in new contexts.[1][8][10][3][9]

- Point of Divergence (POD): The specific moment the timeline cracked away from actual history (e.g., a bullet misses, a storm sinks a fleet, a decision is reversed).
- The OTL (Original Timeline): A meta-term used to describe the reader’s actual history.
- Counterfactuals: The "What If" questions that drive the plot logic.
- The Balkalization of Great Powers: Large nations (USA, Russia, China) are fractured into smaller, squabbling city-states or commonwealths.
- Meta-Fiction/The Forbidden Book: A character in the ATL reads a sci-fi novel that depicts our OTL (e.g., The Grasshopper Lies Heavy in The Man in the High Castle).
- The Altered Map: A classroom scene or war room displaying a map with unfamiliar borders (e.g., The Confederate States of America, The Greater German Reich).
- Strange Currency: Characters handling coins/bills bearing the faces of different leaders (e.g., a King Washington thaler).
- The Butterfly: Symbolizing chaos theory and the ripple effect of small actions.
- Key Variance: One character knows the OTL. The conflict comes from the friction between the Traveler's knowledge and the ATL's reality.
- Show, Don't Lecture: Reveal historical changes through character interaction and setting details, not encyclopedic info-dumps.

- Characterization Guidelines
- Fictional Characters:
- Should react to the world as if it is normal, not strange.
- No "As You Know" Dialogue: Characters should not explain history to each other that they already know.
- Avoid "Time Travel" Tropes: Unless this is specifically a time-travel story, characters should have no awareness of the "Real" timeline.
- Maintain a grounded, authoritative tone. Treat the alternate timeline as absolute fact. strictly avoid anachronisms in dialogue and narration. Reveal history through environmental details and context ("The Butterfly Effect"), not exposition dumps. Historical figures must act consistent with their real-world personalities, adjusted only by their new circumstances. Characters are unaware of the "real" timeline.
