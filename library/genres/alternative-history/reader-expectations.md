Alternative history fiction asks "what if one event had gone differently?" and then rigorously follows the consequences of that divergence through characters who live inside the changed world without knowing it changed.

## Tone & Mood

Intellectually serious and often politically charged, with the texture of historical fiction grounding the speculative premise. Readers come for the thought experiment, but they stay for the human story inside it. The tone depends heavily on what the point of divergence implies: a world where the Axis won WWII carries a different moral weight than a world where Rome never fell. Do not let the cleverness of the premise substitute for emotional investment. The narrative should feel like history that actually happened — the characters inhabit their world fully, without metatextual awareness that it is "alternate."

## Pacing

Deliberate and architecturally structured. The divergence point must be established early, either explicitly (the reader witnesses the change) or implicitly (the world reveals itself as different through accumulating detail). Exposition must be managed carefully — readers need enough world-orientation to follow the plot without feeling lectured about what changed and why. Action and political plotting drive the middle; the climax typically forces the narrative to reckon with whether this world is better, worse, or simply different than the one that was lost.

## Setting Conventions

Historical accuracy for the period before the divergence point is required and readers will notice violations. After the divergence, extrapolation must be internally consistent — changes cascade logically from the root event, affecting technology, politics, culture, language, and social structure in ways that feel earned rather than arbitrary. Real historical figures may appear as characters; when they do, their behavior must be plausible given the changed circumstances. Iconic historical locations can appear transformed by the divergence. The texture of the alternate world — its propaganda, its fashion, its slang, its tensions — is a core reader pleasure.

## Character Archetypes

Characters are defined by their relationship to the alternate world's power structure: those who benefit from it, those who are crushed by it, and those who resist it. A character who suspects or discovers that history could have gone differently is a powerful but double-edged device — use it deliberately, as it risks breaking the immersive historical illusion. The antagonist is often an institution or ideology made concrete in a person, not simply a villain. Protagonists should be ordinary enough that their perspective lets the reader see the changed world freshly, extraordinary enough to intersect with the hinge events of this timeline.

## Length & Format

90,000–120,000 words; complex world-building justifies the upper range. Series common, particularly when exploring a sustained alternate timeline across multiple periods or characters. Third-person past tense is the dominant convention; first-person works in tightly scoped narratives. Chapter length: 3,000–5,000 words. Appendices, timelines, or author's notes explaining the divergence point and its historical basis are conventional and welcomed by readers — place them as backmatter so they do not front-load the reading experience.
