Domestic thriller places intimate psychological danger inside the home, where the people who should be safest become the primary threat.

## Tone & Mood

Claustrophobic, paranoid, and suffocating — the domestic setting that should signal comfort instead radiates menace. The reader should feel the walls closing in. Trust is the central resource being depleted: every revelation strips another layer of certainty from what the protagonist believes about their marriage, their household, their own memories. The prose style is often confessional and close, drawing readers into a narrator's perspective they are simultaneously asked to doubt. Dread builds through accumulation of small wrongnesses rather than overt threat. The emotional register sits between the slow creep of gothic unease and the sharp shock of crime fiction.

## Pacing

Deliberately controlled, with the tension of a screw being tightened. Domestic thrillers earn their twists through meticulous setup — chapters end on revelations that reframe what came before rather than cliffhangers that escalate external action. The first act establishes the surface normalcy and plants the seeds of doubt. The second act systematically dismantles the protagonist's certainty and isolates them from external support. The third act delivers the truth in a compressed, propulsive sequence that pays off every planted detail. Red herrings must be placed with care: readers who feel cheated by illogical misdirection will not forgive it.

## Setting Conventions

The home is the primary arena and must be rendered with architectural specificity: which rooms lock, which windows overlook the neighbor's yard, where the basement door is, how sound travels through the walls. A beautiful or aspirational home amplifies the horror — the gilded cage convention. Secondary domestic settings extend the claustrophobia: the school run, the neighborhood book club, the therapist's office. Readers should feel that even leaving the house provides no real escape, because the protagonist carries the threat with them in the form of their own compromised perception. Suburban and semi-rural settings are more common than urban ones; isolation is structural to the genre.

## Character Archetypes

The Unreliable Narrator — the protagonist whose trauma, addiction, sleep deprivation, or complicity in their own deception makes their account systematically suspect. The Perfect Spouse or Partner whose apparent devotion conceals either genuine threat or a secret the protagonist is not equipped to face. The Helpful Outsider — a neighbor, colleague, or new friend — who is either the one person seeing clearly or the most dangerous actor in the story. The Gaslit Victim whose certainty in their own perception is what the plot methodically destroys. The Child in the House whose safety raises the stakes without giving them agency.

## Length & Format

75,000–95,000 words. First-person or tight third-person close, almost always from a single viewpoint — multiple perspectives dilute the claustrophobia. Present tense is common and effective for immediacy. Short chapters with hooks sustain the compulsive reading experience the genre promises. Dual timelines (now vs. then) are a structural convention; they must intersect at a moment that reframes both.
