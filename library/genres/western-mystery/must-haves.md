# must-haves

These are non-negotiable elements. A Western Mystery without them does not satisfy genre expectations. Check this list before drafting and again before revision.

## Central Crime or Injustice Discovered Early

The story must open with or quickly introduce a concrete crime: a body, a disappearance, a robbery, or an accusation. Not a philosophical wrong or a rumor, but a tangible harm that demands investigation. Readers will not engage with a mystery that is vague or takes 50 pages to define.

## An Active, Persistent Investigation

The protagonist must actively pursue truth through clues, interrogations, observation, and inference. The investigation should escalate—each answer triggers a harder question, a stronger enemy, or a costlier choice. Readers resent passivity or coincidence solving cases; they expect the protagonist to make deliberate moves.

## Fair-Play Clues**

All clues necessary to solve the case must be available to the reader (though their significance may be hidden). When the reveal comes, readers should be able to flip back and find the clue planted in an earlier scene. This is not a rule to break—mystery readers are oath-takers; fair play is the genre's foundational promise. Hide meaning, not data; show the clue, let the significance arrive later through recontextualization.

## Frontier-Specific Evidence**

Investigation must rely on methods available to a frontier setting: reading sign and tracks, analyzing horse shoes and brands, examining physical objects, interpreting telegraph timing, auditing ledgers and land deeds, testing ballistic calibers, and reading human behavior. Avoid modern forensics (DNA, fingerprint databases, digital records) unless you are writing a neo-Western set in the modern day and can justify access.

## Frontier Constraint(s) Materially Complicating Plot**

The setting is not wallpaper. Distance, weather, scarcity, weak or corrupt law, and reputation economics must actively shape what the protagonist can discover, who they can trust, and what justice looks like. At least one scene should feature a constraint that forces a different approach—a snowstorm erases tracks, a flooded river blocks travel, an alibi is solid because a landmark was reached before a specific time.

## Suspects with Distinct Motives**

Introduce at least three suspects, each with a motive specific enough that readers can weight probability. "He wanted money" is weak; "He wanted to prevent the bank foreclosure on his ranch" is strong. Each suspect should want something the crime or investigation might provide or threaten.

## A Protagonist with Personal Stakes**

The sleuth investigates because the case is personally meaningful—duty, debt, local connection, or code. A hometown lawman. An outsider with a grudge against the victim or the crime's beneficiary. An officer whose jurisdiction or reputation is threatened. Readers expect the protagonist to risk something—safety, career, relationships—by pursuing the truth.

## Escalating Pressure**

The investigation must trigger opposition. A suspect or community force applies force to stop it—threats, bribes, legal action, smear campaigns, or violence. Pressure should escalate across the story; by Act III, the stakes should be high enough that the protagonist faces real consequence for continuing.

## A Clear Justice Outcome**

The crime must resolve through one of these mechanisms: arrest and trial (most often corrupted or distant), confrontation and self-defense, exile, death, or confession and social ruin. Justice does not have to be "fair" by modern legal standards; it should be consistent with frontier codes and the protagonist's values. Readers need closure—a sense that something has been settled, even if imperfectly.

## Consequences That Last**

Solving the crime changes the community and costs the protagonist something. The victory is not clean. Relationships break. A trust is shattered. The protagonist's moral certainty erodes. The town's power structure shifts. Readers expect ripples—not neatly tied endings but real ongoing costs and changes.

## Distinct Voice and Period Authenticity**

Dialogue and sensory detail should feel rooted in the frontier setting and era. Use period-appropriate language, objects, and concerns. Avoid modern idioms, attitudes, or technology without justification. The prose should be clear and gritty—lean, muscular sentences with concrete nouns and active verbs.

## A Mystery That Satisfies as a Puzzle**

The plot should hold together logically. Timelines should be defensible. Alibis should be solid until proven otherwise. Motive and opportunity should align. If you leave a plot hole, readers will find it. Before you finish, verify the puzzle: Can the culprit have done the crime in the time alleged? Do the clues point toward the solution fairly? Is there a detail that invalidates the ending?

## At Least 30% Devoted to Investigation**

The middle and third acts should be investigation-forward. Dialogue should include interrogations, interviews, and conversations driven by the need to know. Scenes should feature the protagonist making moves, analyzing evidence, and narrowing suspects. Avoid side plots that distract from the case; every subplot should deepen character or raise stakes around the investigation.
