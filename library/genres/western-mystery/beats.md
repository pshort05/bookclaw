# beats

Western Mystery typically follows a 4-act structure that interweaves investigation and frontier pressure. These beats are not rigid formulas but checkpoints—use them to scaffold your story and ensure pacing stays taut and stakes escalate.

## Act I: Disturbance & Commitment (0–25%)

**Establish the Frontier Order**
Open by showing the town or territory: who has power, what matters, what is fragile. Use visual and social detail—a church, a ranch headquarters, a reservation police office—to ground readers in the place and its unwritten rules.

**Present the Crime Early**
By page 25%, a central crime or injustice must be discovered. A body in an isolated location, a disappearance, a robbery, or an accusation that will ignite a community response. The crime should be concrete and consequential—not a minor theft or a theoretical wrong, but something that threatens person, property, or community standing.

**Introduce 3–6 Suspects with Distinct Wants**
Each suspect has a motive, an opportunity, and a secret unrelated to the crime (an affair, debt, stolen deed). Each should be visually distinct and want something the crime or investigation might provide or threaten. Readers should be able to track them; give each a resource (money, men, information, access) that marks their power.

**Plant Frontier Constraint #1**
Introduce a problem the frontier creates: weather closing a pass, distance making communication slow, a jurisdictional tangle, or community silence due to fear or loyalty.

**The Sleuth Commits**
The protagonist takes on the investigation not from abstract duty but from personal code or stakes. A hometown sheriff enforces because it's her job but investigates harder because the victim was a friend. A Pinkerton agent is hired but stays because the case touches his past. The protagonist should have skin in the game.

## Act II: Investigation & First Retaliation (25–50%)

**Gather Statements and Contradictions**
The sleuth interviews suspects and witnesses. Each conversation reveals motive but also raises questions. The sleuth builds a timeline, tracks alibis, and notes who is evasive, overly helpful, or protective.

**First Major Clue Reframes**
Around 30–35%, introduce a clue that recontextualizes the case. A ledger entry places someone who claimed to be elsewhere. A witness contradicts a suspect. A physical object ties to someone unexpected. This clue should not solve the case but should reframe who looks guilty and what the motive might be.

**Apply Pressure**
A suspect or community authority applies force to stop the investigation—intimidation, a bribe offer, an arrest threat, a smear campaign. The protagonist faces real consequence for investigating. A family friend asks the protagonist to back off. The mayor threatens the protagonist's job. A suspect's relative creates public pressure.

**Frontier Constraint Escalates**
The frontier complicates investigation further. A snowstorm delays travel. A river rises and cuts off a valley. A telegraph line goes down. Communication becomes harder. The sleuth must make do with limited resources, past information, and intuition.

**Sleuth Narrows Suspect List**
Through contradiction and motive analysis, the field narrows. Two or three suspects emerge as most likely. Others are less plausible but not eliminated. Readers should be taking sides—forming hunches about who is guilty.

## Act III: False Solution & Collapse (50–80%)

**Sleuth Forms a Plausible Theory**
The protagonist builds a narrative: suspect X did it because of motive Y, and the opportunity was Z. The theory feels sound and explains most evidence. Readers should believe it.

**A "Proof" Scene Seems to Confirm**
The protagonist acts on the theory—arrests the suspect, raids their property, or confronts them in front of witnesses. A piece of physical evidence seems to clinch it (a weapon, a receipt, a love letter). The culprit looks guilty.

**Theory Collapses Due to a Stubborn Fact**
A new detail emerges that invalidates the theory. The weapon couldn't have been used by the suspect (wrong caliber, wrong hand strength). The receipt is a forgery. The alibi holds after all. The suspect's secret (the affair, the debt) explains their behavior but not the crime. The protagonist is forced to release the suspect and start over.

**Stakes Peak**
The wrong person is about to face community justice (lynch mob, false trial, exile) before the protagonist can prove innocence. Or the real perpetrator is planning escape or further crimes. Or time is running out—a posse is forming, a rival lawman is taking the case, or evidence is being destroyed. The investigation is in crisis.

**Alternative Interpretations Converge**
With the false solution collapsed, the protagonist reconsiders evidence and reinterprets clues. What looked like guilt was panic. What looked like innocence was cleverness. A secondary suspect moves to the foreground. A previously overlooked character emerges as central. Readers should experience the reorientation alongside the protagonist.

## Act IV: Reveal, Showdown, Justice (80–100%)

**Sleuth Builds a Chain**
The protagonist assembles evidence into an ironclad narrative. Not through monologue but through action: confrontation reveals contradiction, contradiction demands proof, proof ties to the culprit. The chain is traceable—readers should be able to follow it.

**Confrontation Forces the Truth**
The protagonist confronts the culprit with evidence, not hunches. The culprit is cornered, denies, then is forced to respond to specific proof. Confession may come; alternatively, the culprit makes a desperate move—tries to flee, takes a hostage, sets a fire.

**Justice is Delivered**
Justice occurs through one of these mechanisms: court trial (rare in Western Mystery, often corrupt or unreachable), arrest and legal process, death in confrontation (self-defense or a showdown), exile (banishment from territory), or social ruin (the perpetrator loses standing, protection, and livelihood). The justice should feel inevitable—consistent with frontier codes and the protagonist's values.

**The Town Changes**
The crime and investigation have altered community bonds, power structures, or understanding. New alliances form. Old feuds simmer or heal. The community has confronted a truth about itself—complicity, vulnerability, or hidden corruption. Readers should sense the frontier has shifted.

**The Protagonist Pays a Cost**
Solving the crime has cost the protagonist something—trust broken with an ally, a moral line crossed, a friendship damaged, or a choice made that will echo. The victory is not clean. Readers expect the protagonist to be changed by what they learned and what they sacrificed.

## Scene-Level Beat Template

**Before You Write the Scene**

- [ ] Purpose: Does this scene deliver a clue, introduce/complicate a suspect, create pressure, or shift the protagonist's understanding?
- [ ] Frontier constraint: Which one complicates this scene? (distance, weather, scarcity, weak law, reputation)
- [ ] Immediate want: What does each major character want *right now* in this scene?

**During the Scene**

- [ ] An object appears: A physical thing (receipt, gun, saddle, letter, photograph) that grounds the scene.
- [ ] Subtext and protection: Someone protects someone else. Alibis are careful. Emotions run high because stakes are real.
- [ ] A change happens: New information, new danger, new commitment, or a shifted relationship by the end.

**After You Write the Scene**

- [ ] Update your suspect list: Motive, opportunity, and likelihood shifts.
- [ ] Update your clue log: What clue appeared, who found it, what it seems to point to, and what alternate meaning it might hold.
- [ ] Note the next question: What must the protagonist investigate next?
