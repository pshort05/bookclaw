Amateur-sleuth mystery puts the investigation in the hands of an ordinary person with no official authority, which means the narrative must constantly earn the reader's belief that this person can find what the police cannot.

## Tone & Mood

Warm but not cozy-by-default. The amateur sleuth subgenre spans a wide tonal range: gentle village mysteries with little onscreen violence, to harder-edged amateur investigations where the danger is very real. Whatever the tonal register, the protagonist's voice is the genre's primary pleasure — engaging, observant, often wry, and credibly human in their reactions to discovering a corpse or confronting a killer. The community is as much a character as the sleuth; readers want to feel embedded in the world, not just dropped into a puzzle.

## Pacing

Moderate, with investigation driving the structure. Each chapter advances the sleuth's understanding: a new witness, a discovered inconsistency, a piece of evidence that reorders the suspects. Chapters often end on a discovery that makes the next interview or location visit feel urgent. The pacing is not as compressed as a thriller, but the investigation must visibly move — scenes where the sleuth learns nothing and nothing changes are wasted pages. The official investigator's pressure on the amateur (to back off, to stop interfering) can provide pacing pressure when the mystery threatens to stall.

## Setting Conventions

A tight, contained community: a small town, a village, a professional milieu, a recurring social world (an art gallery, a bakery, a horse racing circuit, a quilting circle). The containment is structural — it limits the suspect pool and means the sleuth has plausible, repeated access to the people involved. The community has history, gossip networks, and embedded tensions that predate the murder and make it legible. The symbolic public heart of the community (the town square, the church fete, the local pub) often plays a role in either the crime or its revelation.

## Character Archetypes

The Amateur Sleuth must have a specific reason to investigate — curiosity alone is thin; a personal connection to the victim, a wrongly accused friend, or a threat to their community is stronger. They need a distinctive skill or knowledge base that gives them a genuine advantage over the official investigation: a retired pathologist's medical knowledge, a chef's insider understanding of poison, an antiques dealer's eye for fakes. The Official Investigator is typically skeptical or actively obstructive, but eventually concedes to the sleuth's findings. A recurring Watson-figure or community ally assists and provides the reader with someone to explain deductions to. The killer must be someone the reader has met; the solution must not rely on information the sleuth withheld from the reader.

## Length & Format

70,000–90,000 words. Series strongly preferred; readers invest in the sleuth's world and want to return to it. First-person or close-third-person, both work; first-person is slightly more common as the sleuth's voice is a primary draw. Chapter length: 2,500–4,000 words. The confrontation scene — where the sleuth names the killer, explains the method, and lays out the evidence — is a mandatory genre payoff and must be staged with care.
