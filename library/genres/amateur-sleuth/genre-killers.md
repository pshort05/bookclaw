Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do's | Don'ts |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | DO give your sleuth a unique skill or knowledge base. | DON'T make your sleuth a passive recipient of clues. They must actively seek them. |
- | DO plant clues in plain sight, disguised as mundane details. | DON'T introduce the killer or a crucial clue only in the final chapter (no "deus ex machina"). |
- | DO create a cast of suspects with believable, intersecting motives. | DON'T let your professional detective be incompetent just to make the amateur look good. Make them obstruct for a valid reason (procedure, jealousy, corruption). |
- | DO use the sleuth's internal monologue to model deduction for the reader. | DON'T have the sleuth withhold key deductions from the reader for a cheap surprise. |
- | DO raise the personal stakes for the sleuth as the story progresses. | DON'T rely on coincidence to advance the plot. The sleuth's actions should drive the investigation. |
- | DO ensure the murder method is plausible within your story's world. | DON'T use graphic, gratuitous violence. The focus is on the "why" and "who," not the visceral "how." |
- Template for an Investigative Interview:**
- 1.  **Establish Rapport/Confrontation:** (Open with a context-appropriate tone).
- 2.  **Ask Open-Ended Questions:** "Can you walk me through your evening?"
- 3.  **Probe for Details:** "What time exactly? Did you see anyone else?"
- 4.  **Introduce Contradiction/Evidence:** "That's interesting, because X said Y..."
- 5.  **Observe Reaction:** (Describe physical/verbal tells).
- 6.  **Follow-Up or Retreat:** Press further or thank them, noting their demeanor.
- Example (Cozy Tone):**
- > "So, Margo, you were arranging the prize dahlias until 6 PM?" Clara asked, smiling over her teacup.
- > "Oh yes, every Tuesday," Margo nodded, her eyes fixed on the sugar bowl.
- > "Must have been peaceful. No one to disturb you?"
- > "Not a soul." Margo's spoon clinked loudly against the porcelain.
- > Clara gently placed her cup down. "How strange. The postman says he saw you at the gate having a rather heated chat with the victim at 5:45."
- > Margo's hand froze. The silence stretched, thick and sweet as the honey.
-    **Pitfall:** A boring, passive sleuth.
- Fix:** Give them agency, a strong motivation, and a flaw that complicates the investigation (e.g., a gossip who people won't talk to).
-    **Pitfall:** Obvious villain from Chapter 1.
- Fix:** Give your real killer a compelling false alibi and a seemingly benign role. Make the most suspicious character a red herring with a secret unrelated to the murder.
-    **Pitfall:** Clues feel like a checklist, not part of the story.
- Fix:** Bury clues in character action, setting description, or off-hand dialogue. The bloody glove should be noticed because the sleuth is admiring the rose bush it's under.
-    **Pitfall:** The final explanation is an endless, tedious monologue.
- Fix:** Break up the revelation with reactions from the suspects, interruptions, and even moments of doubt. Make it a dynamic scene.
-    **Official Detective:** Terse, procedural, uses jargon. May be patronizing ("Leave it to the professionals, Ms. Jones") or reluctantly impressed.
-    **Wealthy Suspect:** Polished, controlled, often dismissive. May use formality as a barrier. ("I fail to see how my financial dealings are your concern.")
-    **Local Business Owner/Busybody:** Gossipy, opinionated, provides unsolicited backstory on everyone. Speaks in anecdotes.
-    **Reluctant Witness/Everyman:** Nervous, uses hedges ("I think," "maybe"), forgets details. Speech is fragmented.
-    **The Sleuth's Confidant:** Casual, supportive, asks the clarifying questions the reader has. Provides humor and grounding.
