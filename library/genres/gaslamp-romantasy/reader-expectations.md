# Gaslamp Romantasy — Reader Expectations

The genre's promise is atmospheric immersion in a gas-lit world where magic carries weight and
danger, social structures impose real costs on desire, and every charged glance across a séance
table or secret society threshold crackles with the tension of what cannot be openly said or felt.

## Tone & Mood

The dominant register is gothic romanticism: beautiful and oppressive in equal measure. The world
is gorgeous — velvet drawing rooms, fog-draped cobblestones, candlelit occult salons — and
quietly menacing. The magic is not joyful or triumphant; it is eerie, uncanny, often morally
ambiguous, carrying the scent of consequence. Deaths matter. Secrets fester. The past is never
safely past.

Emotional tone alternates between longing and dread. During non-romantic sequences: mounting
atmospheric pressure, the sensation that the rules of the world are slightly wrong, and careful
social performance that could collapse at any moment. During romantic sequences: breathless
proximity, language that does more work than it admits, the revelation that the beloved sees
clearly what the protagonist labors to conceal from the rest of society.

Never pitch the tone as adventure-forward. This is not swashbuckling fantastical history — it
is interior, atmospheric, and deeply concerned with what cannot be permitted. Wit exists but is
dry and ironic, not snappy or comedic. Horror adjacency is expected and welcomed; the line
between gaslamp romantasy and gothic romance is porous and productive.

## Pacing

Slower and more deliberate than contemporary romance or high fantasy. Readers arrive specifically
for the quality of longing — the interstitial moments between characters, the charged silences,
the letters that say everything obliquely. Pacing operates in waves: a long atmospheric swell
(worldbuilding, social scene-setting, occult discovery) releases into a charged intimate encounter,
then returns to pressure-building. Avoid back-to-back action set pieces; they dissipate the
tension the genre requires.

Dual track: the protagonist's investigation or magical complication advances in parallel with the
romantic arc, with each complicating the other at key inflection points. The magic plot should
raise the stakes of the romance (discovery means exposure; the ritual requires trust; the entity
demands a terrible price) and the romantic attachment should compromise the protagonist's handling
of the magical threat (divided loyalty, the need to protect the beloved from the truth).

Readers tolerate — and often prefer — a 20–30% slower opening than contemporary romance demands,
provided the prose and world-texture are rich enough to justify the pace. If the writing is thin,
the deliberate pacing reads as stalling.

## Setting Conventions

The setting is the primary character in gaslamp romantasy. Every location must carry period
authenticity and atmospheric specificity:

- **The gas-lit city** — London, Edinburgh, Paris, Vienna, New York at the Gilded Age's height,
  or a lovingly detailed original city-equivalent. Fog, lamplighters, hansom cabs, the smell of
  coal and river and expensive perfume. The city has a visible underworld (literal or figurative)
  and an occult geography the reader gradually learns to navigate.
- **The drawing room and the salon** — the social arena where constraint is enforced. Card games,
  musicales, dinner parties, suffragist meetings, spiritualist séances: the public face of a world
  with furious private hungers. Rules of decorum are the story's primary antagonist.
- **The secret society / magical institution** — the Order, the Academy, the Guild, the Circle,
  the Society for the Study of Anomalous Phenomena. A space where the rules of the surface world
  apply less rigorously but new hierarchies and obligations take their place. Access to this space
  is an earned privilege and a source of danger.
- **The liminal supernatural threshold** — the séance cabinet, the laboratory at midnight,
  the cemetery at the appointed hour, the abandoned manor with a door that was locked for a reason.
  Where the magic concentrates and the lovers are most exposed.
- **The empire's shadow** — 19th-century settings carry the weight of colonial extraction,
  evangelical moral authority, and rigid racial and class stratification. Gaslamp romantasy
  must not be naive about these conditions; they are not backdrop but active constraint on what
  the characters can do and be. The best titles in the genre reckon honestly with this without
  making it a lecture.

Avoid the sanitized, cosplay version of the period — the one where everyone is witty and
progressive and the corsets are just aesthetic. Bring the real social teeth.

## Character Archetypes

- **The hidden practitioner** — a protagonist who possesses magical ability, magical knowledge,
  or occult affiliation that would destroy them if widely known. Their power is tangled with
  their vulnerability. They may be a woman in a discipline that rejects women, a queer person
  in an era that criminalizes them, a person of marginalized background in a society that erases
  them, or simply someone who knows too much about what lies beneath the respectable surface.
- **The rational sceptic turned believer** — the love interest who arrives with a scientific or
  rationalist framework that the supernatural events dismantle. Their conversion is a character
  arc; their willingness to revise their worldview mirrors their opening to the protagonist.
  Often occupies a position of social authority (doctor, scientist, academic, investigator,
  nobleman) that the protagonist needs and distrusts.
- **The guardian of the secret** — a mentor or colleague figure inside the occult institution who
  holds knowledge the protagonist needs and dispenses it at costs. May be a genuine ally, a
  gatekeeper with their own agenda, or ultimately a betrayer. Often the source of the first third's
  tension before the romantic pairing crystallizes.
- **The society predator** — the antagonist who enforces the surface world's rules with specific
  intent to expose, destroy, or control the protagonist. May be a matriarch, a rival suitor, a
  church authority, or a faction within the magical institution. Not cartoonishly evil — genuinely
  believes they are maintaining necessary order.
- **The found family in the margins** — the ensemble of people who live at the edges of respectability
  by necessity: the medium who actually speaks to the dead, the chemist whose formulae are treated
  as female eccentricity, the man who dresses as a woman to survive, the colonial subject who
  speaks five languages and is addressed as a servant. They carry the genre's emotional weight
  and comedic relief; they are never props.

## Heat Levels

Gaslamp romantasy's period setting creates structural heat through restraint. A gloved hand
touched deliberately, a private name used in public, two people sharing a lamp after midnight —
these carry erotic charge precisely because the era forbids open expression.

- **Level 1 — Closed door / sweet:** chaste by page but intensely felt; the longing is the
  content. Period-appropriate courtship, stolen moments, charged correspondence.
- **Level 2 — Sensual / fade-to-black:** the scene approaches the bedroom or its equivalent
  and the narrative curtain falls. Period vocabulary permitted; no clinical anatomical terms.
- **Level 3 — Steamy / open door (mild):** at least one on-page scene; emotion-forward,
  moderately descriptive, with period-appropriate language choices.
- **Level 4 — Spicy / explicit:** fully on-page, detailed, multiple scenes; explicit vocabulary
  used deliberately. Less common in the genre but growing with the spicy romantasy market.
- **Level 5 — Erotic:** explicit and frequent; may incorporate power-exchange dynamics
  inflected by the period's power structures. Rare in gaslamp romantasy but present in
  gothic dark romance adjacencies.

The commercial sweet spot for the genre currently sits at Level 2–3, with LGBTQ+ titles
trending toward Level 3–4 as the market matures.

## Length & Format

Target **90,000–120,000 words**; the commercial centre is **95,000–110,000**. The genre's
atmospheric demands and the need to establish both a fully realized historical world and a
complete magical system require more room than contemporary romance. Under 80,000 risks feeling
rushed and under-realised; over 130,000 risks losing momentum in the long middle.

**Third-person past tense** is the dominant expectation, chosen to provide the narrative
distance appropriate to the era's social register and the gothic tradition. First-person past
tense works but must maintain period-appropriate interiority without becoming confessional in
a contemporary-romance mode. First-person present tense is the most jarring mismatch with the
setting's emotional texture — deploy with strong justification.

**Single POV or close dual POV** is standard. Omniscient POV in a classic Victorian mode
(used sparingly, with period-voice irony) is an advanced choice that can pay off handsomely
in the right manuscript. Multiple-POV ensemble approaches are unusual for the genre and risk
diluting the atmospheric focus.

Series format is common but not required. Many gaslamp romantasy titles are standalones or
loosely connected books within the same world. Do not assume series; complete the central romance
fully in one book unless you have explicit series architecture in place.
