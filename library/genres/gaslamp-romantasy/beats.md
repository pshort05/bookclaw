# Gaslamp Romantasy — Beats

The dominant structure is a modified four-act romance arc (after Gwen Hayes's *Romancing the
Beat*) woven with a parallel occult-investigation or magical-threat plot, in which neither arc
can resolve without the other. The romance is not a subplot and the magical threat is not
wallpaper — they are mechanically entangled. Think of it as a double helix: the strands run
parallel, cross at every major beat, and are structurally inseparable.

## The Four-Phase Structure

### Phase 1 — The Gas-Lit World (1–25%)

- **Open in atmosphere (1–5%)** — establish the period, place, and social texture before the
  protagonist arrives. The setting must feel inhabited, not costumed. The reader should smell
  the coal smoke, hear the carriage wheels, feel the weight of the social occasion. Introduce
  the surface world the protagonist is performing their way through before showing what lies beneath.

- **Introduce Protagonist 1 (5–10%)** — who they are publicly and who they are in private.
  The gap between these two versions is the story. Establish their magical nature or occult
  connection early, even if no other character knows it yet. Their internal flaw is a survival
  strategy developed in response to the concealment — not a personality defect but a rational
  adaptation to an unkind world.

- **Introduce the magical threat or occult mystery (10–15%)** — the event, discovery, or
  intrusion that initiates the main plot. A death that shouldn't be possible. A communication
  from something that shouldn't be able to communicate. A summons to a place the protagonist
  has been warned to avoid. The magical plot launches here and is the engine that will drive
  everything else.

- **Encounter with the love interest (10–20%)** — the first meeting. It should be charged
  with misapprehension: they do not yet know what the other is, and what they perceive is
  filtered through their respective social performances. The first meeting plants the hook
  of romantic interest while establishing the reason they cannot simply act on it. Whether
  the first meeting is antagonistic, formal, or tentative, it must end with each protagonist
  more preoccupied with the other than they intended to be.

- **"No Way" / The Prohibition (20–25%)** — both protagonists separately enumerate the
  reasons this attachment is impossible. The reasons should be specific, social, and real:
  not "I'm not ready for love" but "if this were known, I would lose the position that is
  my only independence," "if this were known, I would hang," "if this were known, the Order
  would expel me and the work would die." The prohibition must have genuine weight, or the
  slow burn has nothing to burn against.

### Phase 2 — The Approaching Dark (25–55%)

- **Forced proximity / the adhesion (25–30%)** — a circumstance that locks the protagonists
  into sustained contact they cannot escape without abandoning the magical plot. They must
  investigate together, or one holds information the other requires, or the supernatural
  situation will kill them both if they do not cooperate. The adhesion is not manufactured
  convenience — it should emerge organically from the magical plot.

- **Mutual revelation, first layer (30–40%)** — in the process of their forced collaboration,
  each protagonist learns something true about the other that contradicts their initial read.
  The skeptic sees evidence they cannot explain. The practitioner sees competence or
  integrity they did not expect. The queer protagonist perceives a recognition they have
  been starved for. These small revelations accumulate into the growing sense that this person
  might be, catastrophically, worth knowing.

- **The first trespass (40–50%)** — the moment when professional or social boundaries are
  crossed: a touch that lingers, a confidence shared that should not have been, a moment
  of protection or care that the era's rules prohibit between these two people. The trespass
  is typically small enough to be deniable but too significant to be forgotten. It is the
  first moment where the reader understands that the protagonists are already in the relationship
  even if neither will name it.

- **The false high / midpoint (50–55%)** — a temporary triumph on the magical plot front
  coincides with the furthest progress yet on the romantic arc: a real conversation in an
  unguarded moment, or a first kiss, or an explicit acknowledgment that something is happening
  between them. This is the midpoint of hope — the reader and the protagonists briefly believe
  the thing is possible after all. Make it luminous. The fall from it must be earned.

### Phase 3 — The Darkening (55–80%)

- **Escalation of the magical threat (55–65%)** — the occult mystery deepens, and the personal
  stakes rise. What was theoretical danger becomes direct danger. The magical threat is now
  explicitly entangled with the romance: the beloved is at risk, or the protagonist's power
  is being used against the beloved's interests, or a revelation about the magical situation
  threatens the romantic trust built in Phase 2.

- **The betrayal or exposure scare (65–75%)** — either a real betrayal occurs (the love
  interest is revealed to have been reporting to the antagonist, or the protagonist has been
  concealing information that endangered the beloved), or a near-exposure creates the
  conditions in which the betrayal becomes plausible. Trust fractures. The protagonists
  retreat to their Phase 1 defenses.

- **The séance that goes wrong / dark ritual (70–78%)** — the set-piece confrontation with
  the supernatural at its most dangerous. This scene detonates all prior equilibria and forces
  the protagonists into direct, unmediated contact with both the magical threat and each other.
  One of them is in genuine danger. The other has to choose: maintain the social and strategic
  distance that has kept them safe, or act in a way that makes their attachment undeniable.
  This scene is the genre's equivalent of the all-is-lost moment. Write it at full scale.

- **The rupture / false separation (75–80%)** — following the dark ritual's aftermath, the
  fracture between the protagonists becomes formal. They are no longer working together. The
  reasons for the rupture must be real — not a misunderstanding but a genuine conflict of
  values or obligations, or the revelation of a concealment so fundamental that the beloved
  has reason to feel genuinely betrayed. The rupture is the genre's darkest point; readers
  must believe it is real.

### Phase 4 — The Resolution (80–100%)

- **Dark night of the soul, both sides (80–87%)** — each protagonist confronts what they
  have lost and what they were protecting by creating the conditions for the loss. The magical
  threat has not resolved; it is now more dangerous without the partner. The social world has
  not become less hostile; the protagonist simply no longer has the energy to perform their
  way through it. Something has to give.

- **The cost accepted / the leap (87–92%)** — one or both protagonists chooses the relationship
  knowing exactly what it will cost. This is not a naive "love conquers all" moment — the cost
  is real and specific. The choice is made with full knowledge of the prohibition, the danger,
  the social exposure, and the answer is yes anyway. This beat is the emotional climax of the
  romance; it should arrive before the magical climax so the reader sees the protagonists
  face the supernatural finale as an established pair.

- **The magical confrontation / final ritual (90–95%)** — the occult plot resolves at its
  full dramatic scale. The protagonist's magical ability, newly freed from the necessity of
  concealment by the romantic choice made in the preceding scene, operates at full power.
  The love interest's presence and commitment is part of what makes the resolution possible.
  The supernatural entity, conspiracy, or threat is not simply defeated but transformed,
  bargained with, or released — gaslamp romantasy typically avoids the clean annihilation
  of the magical element, preferring an ending that acknowledges the world has been permanently
  changed.

- **The new equilibrium / HEA (95–100%)** — the aftermath. The social world has not
  magically reformed, but the protagonists have found the terms on which they will live within
  it or beyond its reach. The HEA in gaslamp romantasy rarely involves complete social
  acceptance — more often it involves the discovery of a pocket of genuine freedom within a
  still-constrained world, or the decision to build something outside the world's official
  structures. The ending must be specific and earned, not vague optimism. Show what their
  life looks like now.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers would feel cheated without these)

- **The period social scene** — a formal gathering (a dinner party, a musicale, a séance
  as social event, a salon, an academy lecture) in which both protagonists operate under
  full social constraint and are forced to communicate around, beneath, or through it.
  The scene establishes the social stakes before anything is risked.

- **The occult discovery scene** — the moment when the protagonist encounters the magical
  system's full power for the first time: a spirit that actually speaks, a spell that
  actually works, a dead thing that walks, an entity whose existence the rational mind
  cannot accommodate. This scene should be frightening before it is wondrous. It earns
  the reader's investment in the magical plot.

- **The unguarded moment** — a scene, usually private, where one protagonist sees the other
  without their social performance in place: genuinely afraid, genuinely joyful, genuinely
  themselves. The unguarded moment is the hinge on which the romance turns from interest
  to attachment.

- **The correspondence** — at minimum one scene of written communication between the protagonists
  where things are said on paper that could not be said aloud. The letter, the note, the
  coded message, the journal entry discovered: the 19th century's epistolary culture is a
  resource for emotional specificity and the perfect medium for gaslamp romantasy's oblique
  emotional register.

- **The moment of choosing** — an explicit decision point where the protagonist could take
  the safe path (deny the magical involvement, suppress the romantic attachment, give the
  antagonist what they want to make it stop) and chooses the dangerous one instead. The
  choice must be visible on the page — not merely implied in retrospect.

- **The cost rendered visible** — a scene late in the narrative where the consequences of
  the choices made across the book are laid out concretely: what was lost, who was hurt,
  what cannot be recovered. Gaslamp romantasy is not exempt from consequence. The HEA must
  be built on the rubble of the prior equilibrium, not arranged on top of it.
