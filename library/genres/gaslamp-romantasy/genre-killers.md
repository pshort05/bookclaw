# Gaslamp Romantasy — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The broken promises, tonal failures, and structural betrayals that collapse reader trust and
generate sustained critical damage in reviews.

- **Costume-party history** — a 19th-century setting that is purely aesthetic: gas lamps as
  vibe, corsets as fashion, carriages as transport, but no social teeth. If the period setting
  does not actively constrain what the characters can want, do, and say — if anyone can go anywhere
  and do anything and the era's power structures are purely background — the gaslamp has been
  removed from the romantasy. Readers arrive for the productive difficulty of the historical world.
  Give it to them.

- **Magic without consequence** — a magical system where power has no cost, no limit, no
  danger, and no moral complexity. The supernatural in gaslamp romantasy is specifically gothic:
  it is tied to death, desire, transgression, and the price of knowledge. Magic that works
  conveniently, without sacrifice or risk, converts a gothic atmosphere into a fairy-tale one.
  The genre relies on the magic being genuinely frightening.

- **The enlightened anachronism** — a protagonist whose attitudes toward race, gender, sexuality,
  empire, and religion are entirely those of a progressive 21st-century person, with no friction
  from the world they inhabit and no history that explains how they arrived at those attitudes.
  This pattern insults both the period and the reader. Historical people who held progressive
  views for their time did so against genuine resistance, at real cost, with partially formed
  frameworks. Show the friction.

- **The slow burn that stops burning** — a slow burn romance that stagnates rather than
  accumulates. If the protagonists are in the same emotional position at 60% as they were at
  30%, the slow burn has become a stall. Every scene with both characters present must move
  something — a revelation, a shift in perception, a new level of desire or danger. If it does
  not, the scene is filler dressed as restraint.

- **The magical system that contradicts itself** — rules established early in the narrative that
  are violated later for plot convenience. The magical world's internal logic is the structural
  integrity of the entire enterprise; violations feel like authorial cheating. Readers of fantasy
  track these rules carefully. If you need to change them, the change must be narratively earned
  — discovered, explained, and foreshadowed — not simply abandoned when inconvenient.

- **The deus ex occult** — a magical intervention that resolves the plot's central conflict
  without the protagonists having to make a meaningful choice or pay a meaningful cost. The
  entity appears and explains everything. The spell works perfectly on the first attempt. The
  supernatural danger conveniently retreats. Gaslamp romantasy's gothic contract requires that
  the resolution cost something real — the magic should require sacrifice, not deliver salvation.

- **LGBTQ+ tokenism in a queer-adjacent genre** — including queer characters as background
  decoration in a genre whose primary demographic is queer readers, without giving those characters
  genuine interiority, genuine danger, or genuine relationships. Alternatively: treating queerness
  as a revelation withheld from readers as a plot twist rather than simply the condition of the
  character's life. This demographic reads critically and carefully; tokenism registers immediately.

- **The historical accuracy double standard** — a narrative that imposes the era's real and
  brutal constraints on characters of marginalized identity (women, queer people, colonial
  subjects, the poor) while simultaneously exempting the narrative's love interests from those
  same constraints when they become inconvenient to the romance. If the constraints are real
  enough to create dramatic tension, they are real enough to cost the people who transgress them.

- **The purple-prose atmospheric collapse** — prose so focused on period atmosphere that the
  plot stalls for pages of fog and description. The elevated register of gaslamp romantasy is not
  permission for decorative languor. Every sentence should be doing something: advancing plot,
  deepening character, intensifying atmosphere as a direct support to the emotional beat in
  progress. A paragraph of beautiful gas-lit cobblestone description that could be cut without
  loss is a paragraph that should be cut.

- **The tidy resolution of historical harm** — an ending that resolves the social obstacles
  by having the antagonists simply change their minds, the institution reform itself, or society
  broadly accept the transgressive pairing by the final chapter. The gaslamp world does not heal
  so easily. The HEA in this genre is the protagonists finding the terms of their own life within
  a world that has not fundamentally changed — or choosing to live outside that world's official
  structures. An ending that asks the reader to believe the bigotry evaporates is a wish-fulfillment
  that the genre's tonal contract specifically refuses.

- **The passive protagonist** — a character who has things done to them by the magical plot
  and by the love interest, without making choices that actively shape either. Gaslamp romantasy's
  thematic heart is agency against structural oppression: the protagonist's exercise of choice
  under genuine constraint is what makes the HEA meaningful. A protagonist who is carried to
  their ending by the plot and the beloved has not earned it.
