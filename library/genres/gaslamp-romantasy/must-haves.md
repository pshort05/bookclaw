# Gaslamp Romantasy — Must-Haves (non-negotiable checklist)

Skip these and the manuscript fails the fundamental contract of the genre. Treat as a revision
pass before submission or publication.

- [ ] **Establish the period with specificity** — within the first two chapters the reader
  should know the approximate decade, the city or region, and the social tier of the opening
  scene. Period-authentic details must be specific enough to exclude other eras (not just "Victorian"
  but gaslight, hansom cabs, the particular smells and sounds of a specific time and place). The
  setting is a character; give it the same precision you would give a person.

- [ ] **Ground the magic in the world's logic** — the magical system must have internal
  consistency, clear costs or limits, and a relationship to the period's real occult history
  (spiritualism, theosophy, alchemy, natural philosophy, ceremonial magic traditions). Magic
  that is convenient and consequence-free betrays the genre's gothic contract. The magic should
  feel like it belongs to this specific historical moment, not imported from a secondary-world
  fantasy.

- [ ] **Make the prohibition concrete and costly** — the obstacle to the romance must be
  specific, historically grounded, and genuinely dangerous. A prohibition that readers cannot
  imagine causing real harm will not sustain a slow burn across ninety thousand words. If the
  prohibition is social (class, gender, race), show the mechanism by which transgression is
  punished. If it is magical, show at least one instance of the cost before the protagonists
  risk incurring it.

- [ ] **Honour the historical social reality** — the period's constraints on women, queer people,
  colonial subjects, the poor, and the non-Christian are not merely atmospheric. They should
  shape what characters can do, where they can go, who they can speak to, and what they can
  want. A historical fantasy that resolves its social tensions too easily (everyone is secretly
  modern in their values; the bigotry is performative and drops away on contact with the heroine)
  reads as dishonest and robs the genre of its primary source of dramatic tension.

- [ ] **Write prose at the genre's elevated register** — gaslamp romantasy is not a contemporary
  romance wearing Victorian costume. The prose must carry flavoring from the period's rhetorical
  tradition: longer sentences with subordinate clauses, a preference for indirection over
  directness in emotional passages, vocabulary that includes period-authentic terms without
  becoming a pastiche of Victoriana. The voice should feel like it was produced by someone
  who reads extensively in the 19th century while writing for a 21st-century reader.

- [ ] **Give the occult plot its own complete arc** — the magical mystery, threat, or investigation
  must be fully introduced, developed, and resolved within the narrative. The magical plot is
  not scaffolding for the romance; it is co-equal. Readers who arrive for the historical fantasy
  elements must be fully satisfied even if they are not primarily romance readers. The magic cannot
  simply dissolve in the HEA resolution.

- [ ] **Earn the slow burn** — the delayed consummation of the romance (emotional or physical)
  must be continuously fed with new information, new proximity, and new stakes. Slow burn that
  stalls rather than burns is a pacing failure. Every scene involving the two protagonists should
  advance their mutual understanding and desire, even if only fractionally. If a scene could be
  cut without affecting the romantic arc, cut it.

- [ ] **Locate and handle the LGBTQ+ dimension honestly** — even in titles where the central
  pairing is heterosexual, the gaslamp world is suffused with queer lives. At minimum, the
  historical reality of queer existence in the period should be present in the world. In titles
  where the central pairing is queer, the historical stakes of queer love in the period — the
  criminal law, the medical pathologization, the total social erasure — must be engaged honestly.
  Do not use the period setting as picturesque backdrop for a romance that behaves as though these
  stakes do not exist.

- [ ] **Deliver the unguarded moment** — at minimum one scene where a protagonist is seen
  without their social armor by the person who will love them. The unguarded moment is the engine
  of romantic attachment in this genre. If the protagonists are only ever seen performing, the
  romance will feel transactional rather than genuine.

- [ ] **Give the HEA specific content** — the ending must show what the protagonists' life
  actually looks like in its new configuration: where they live, how they have arranged their
  relationship to the social world, what they have built or found or claimed. Vague optimism
  ("they chose each other") is not an HEA; it is the absence of an HEA. The specificity of the
  earned ending is what gaslamp readers stay for.
