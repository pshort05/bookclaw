# Gaslamp Romantasy — Tropes

The genre runs on a menu of proven structural and emotional tropes; most successful titles
combine three to six of these in deliberate tension with each other. Pick your combination
before drafting; then ensure each chosen trope has its full arc rather than a cameo appearance.

## The Trope Menu (choose 3–6)

- **Forbidden by nature of magic** — the romantic pairing is structurally prohibited by the
  rules of the magical world: one practitioner's power nullifies the other's, the Order prohibits
  attachments between members, the entity or curse bound to one protagonist cannot coexist with
  the beloved's presence. The magical prohibition mirrors and amplifies the social one. This
  trope is the genre's engine: two people who cannot safely be together discovering they cannot
  safely be apart.

- **Secret society / dual life** — one or both protagonists belongs to an occult organization,
  secret order, or hidden magical institution that the other does not know about. The romantic
  tension hinges on accumulating evidence of something concealed, and the moment of revelation
  constitutes a critical romantic milestone. The secret life is not simply an information
  asymmetry — it represents a full alternate identity whose exposure would transform everything.

- **The skeptic converted** — the love interest arrives with a rigidly rationalist worldview
  (scientist, doctor, investigator, clergyman) and encounters evidence that dismantles it.
  Their conversion from skeptic to believer tracks exactly with their opening to the protagonist.
  The moment they accept the supernatural is the moment they become capable of accepting the
  love they have been rationalizing away.

- **Class / station transgression** — the romantic pairing violates the era's rigid class
  structure. The transgression may run in either direction, or both parties may find themselves
  simultaneously above and below each other along different axes of social stratification (a
  lady who is also a medium; a titled gentleman who is also a colonial subject; a wealthy woman
  whose gender makes her legally subordinate to men of half her fortune). The social stakes of
  the attachment are as concrete and dangerous as the magical ones.

- **Forbidden love in the queer register** — in an era that criminalizes or erases queer
  existence, the simple fact of the romantic pairing constitutes the central transgression.
  The magic may be a metaphor for queerness (a hidden nature that must be concealed), a
  resource that enables queer survival, or the mechanism by which the protagonists find and
  recognize each other across the noise of enforced performance. This trope carries particular
  emotional weight in gaslamp romantasy and demands historical honesty about what exposure
  would actually cost.

- **The dangerous bargain** — one protagonist makes a pact, compact, or agreement with a
  supernatural entity or institution that has consequences for the romance. The beloved may not
  know about the bargain. The terms come due at a point of maximum romantic vulnerability.
  The resolution requires renegotiating or breaking the bargain at personal cost — which is
  also the moment the romantic commitment is proven.

- **Ghosts and the beloved dead** — a prior loss (a dead sibling, a previous partner, a
  murdered mentor) haunts the protagonist literally or figuratively, and the magic of the
  world makes the haunting literal enough to be dangerous. The romantic arc requires the
  protagonist to choose the living over the dead — to let grief be grief rather than a
  perpetual postponement of joy. The love interest is often positioned as the living world's
  embodiment.

- **Enemies with the same cause** — the protagonists are adversaries (rival investigators,
  opposing faction members, competing heirs to a magical inheritance) who discover, partway
  through, that they are working toward the same goal from opposed directions. The merger
  of their efforts is also a merger of their lives, and the transition from tactical alliance
  to genuine attachment requires navigating the residue of their earlier opposition.

- **The mentor's betrayal** — a trusted authority figure (teacher, patron, guardian, superior
  within the magical institution) is revealed to have been working against the protagonist's
  genuine interests — not out of malice but out of a competing ideological commitment, or a
  prior bargain, or the belief that the protagonist's potential is too dangerous to be fully
  realized. The betrayal isolates the protagonist and throws them together with the love
  interest as their sole trustworthy remaining ally.

- **Dangerous knowledge** — one protagonist discovers something about the nature of the
  magical world (or about the other protagonist, or about themselves) that they should not
  have discovered. Knowledge carries consequence in the gaslamp world: it creates obligations,
  attracts enemies, and cannot be unfound. The romance is complicated because the beloved
  is now implicated in the knowledge, or because possessing it makes the protagonist a danger
  to the beloved.

- **The monster who chooses humanity** — one protagonist is supernatural in nature (revenant,
  dhampir, spirit medium possessed, practitioner whose magic has made them something other
  than merely human) and must actively choose to remain in the human world and the relationship,
  at increasing personal cost. The love interest's acceptance of their nature is the central
  emotional gift of the romance; the beloved's knowledge is also the greatest vulnerability.

- **The séance / ritual that goes wrong** — a key set-piece where an attempt to control or
  communicate with the supernatural results in something far beyond what was intended. This
  trope functions as both a magical plot engine and a romantic accelerant: the two protagonists
  are stripped of their social performances and forced into raw contact by the event's
  aftermath. The séance that goes wrong is the gaslamp romantasy equivalent of the dark night
  of the soul — it arrives at the 60–75% mark and detonates the prior equilibrium.
