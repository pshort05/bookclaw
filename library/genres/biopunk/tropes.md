- Biopunk stories typically feature several recurring elements: they explore the struggles of individuals or groups, often the product of human experimentation, against a typically dystopian backdrop of totalitarian governments or megacorporations which misuse biotechnologies as means of social control and profiteering. The fruits of biotechnology—such as human enhancement, extended longevity, and genetic modification—are rarely distributed equitably; instead, they are controlled by corporations and available only to the wealthy, creating profound class divisions. A distinctive feature of biopunk fiction is the "black clinic," an illegal laboratory, clinic, or hospital that performs unregulated and ethically dubious biological modification and genetic engineering procedures. Black clinics serve as settings for illicit enhancement, underground resistance, and the exploitation of desperate populations.[1][6][9]
