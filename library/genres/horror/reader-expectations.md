Broad horror fiction whose primary contract with the reader is to frighten, disturb, or unsettle — through threat to life, sanity, or identity — while delivering the catharsis that comes from confronting that fear in a controlled form.

## Tone & Mood

Horror's tonal range is wide — from slow atmospheric dread to visceral shock, from psychological gaslighting to cosmic indifference — but every sub-genre shares the same foundation: unease that builds and does not resolve comfortably. The best horror establishes a mood of wrongness early, something slightly off that the reader senses before the characters do. The threat to normalcy is as important as the threat to life; horror works when it makes familiar things feel dangerous. Tone must remain internally consistent: readers who pick up a psychological horror novel expect a different experience than those who pick up splatterpunk, and the prose should deliver on its implied promise from the first page.

## Pacing

Horror pacing is defined by the deliberate management of tension: stretch and release, approach and retreat, slow accumulation and sudden rupture. The genre is not a thriller; sustained high speed kills dread. The reader must have time to feel afraid. Build scenes slowly, let silence and implication do work, and reserve graphic horror or revelation for moments that earn the impact. The structure typically moves from normal world through escalating incursion to a crisis that cannot be survived unchanged. Subplots and secondary characters serve to widen the threat's reach and raise the cost of the central conflict.

## Setting Conventions

Setting in horror is rarely neutral. The haunted house, the isolated town, the wilderness, the institution — these function as closed systems that trap the protagonist and concentrate threat. Readers expect the setting to be established with enough physical specificity that they can imagine being trapped in it. The environment should reflect or amplify the internal state of the characters: a decaying house for a decaying family, an oppressive office for a story about institutional dehumanization. Weather, light levels, sound, and smell are tools for building and sustaining atmosphere.

## Character Archetypes

Horror protagonists are defined by their vulnerability: they lack the knowledge, the power, or the institutional support to deal with what they are facing. The ordinary person confronting the extraordinary is the genre's core figure. Skeptic-turned-believer arcs are common. Groups of protagonists allow the genre to pick them off, demonstrating threat through attrition. Antagonists range from monsters (supernatural or human) to forces with no consciousness at all — the threat need not be intelligent to be terrifying. Survivors carry scars; horror that leaves its protagonist unchanged has failed.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 80,000–110,000 words, though novellas (25,000–40,000 words) are a legitimate and historically significant form for the genre. First person and close third both work well; first person amplifies the reader's identification with the protagonist's deteriorating certainty. Series are uncommon in literary horror but viable in commercial sub-genres. The ending need not be happy — ambiguous and tragic endings are genre conventions — but it must feel earned rather than arbitrary.
