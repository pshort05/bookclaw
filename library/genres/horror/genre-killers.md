- The 20th century brought diversification and democratization. The pulp magazines Weird Tales and Unknown Worlds provided outlets for H.P. Lovecraft's cosmic horror and M.R. James's ghost stories. Lovecraft's creation of the Cthulhu Mythos transformed horror by emphasizing humanity's insignificance against incomprehensible cosmic forces, establishing what would become known as Lovecraftian or cosmic horror. The post-war era saw the rise of psychological horror through works like Robert Bloch's Psycho (1959) and the elevation of the serial killer as a horror figure.[3][6][7][2]
- The horror landscape includes numerous other subgenres: supernatural horror (ghosts, demons, paranormal entities), slasher horror (serial killers stalking victims), occult horror (witchcraft, satanism, demonic forces), survival horror (characters fighting to stay alive in hostile environments), dark fantasy (blending fantasy elements with horror), and numerous hybrid forms that cross genre boundaries.[23][16][19][17]
- The "final girl" in slasher horror—the last survivor who confronts the killer—has evolved from passive victim to empowered protagonist, particularly in contemporary metafictional works that interrogate gender politics. Unreliable narrators create ambiguity about reality versus delusion, sanity versus madness.[50][32][13][14][26][10]
- The "don't go in there" impulse, where characters enter obviously dangerous spaces, serves both narrative necessity and audience participation through dramatic irony. Splitting up increases vulnerability and isolation. Creepy children subvert innocence to suggest corruption or malevolence. Body transformation horror depicts unwanted physical change, loss of bodily integrity, or violation.[51][43][13][49][14][15]
- Stephen Graham Jones has emerged as one of horror's most important voices, particularly for his Indigenous perspective and metafictional approach to genre conventions. His Indian Lake Trilogy—My Heart Is a Chainsaw (2021), Don't Fear the Reaper (2023), and The Angel of Indian Lake (2024)—deconstructs slasher tropes while exploring cultural trauma, identity, and survival. The Only Good Indians (2020) was widely acclaimed as one of the decade's best horror novels.[34][32][10][40]
