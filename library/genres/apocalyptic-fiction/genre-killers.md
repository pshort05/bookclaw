- Atmosphere: Use longer, rhythmic sentences when describing landscapes or decay, but avoid flowery purple prose. The prose should feel as stripped-back and raw as the setting.
- Avoid: Passive voice. Things should happen to characters violently and suddenly.
- When generating prose for this story, adhere to a gritty, high-stakes apocalyptic style. Prioritize sensory details related to decay, discomfort, and danger. Keep dialogue stripped back and functional. Avoid optimism unless it is cynical. Ensure the pacing reflects the erratic nature of survival—long periods of tension punctuated by sudden violence.
- Second, apocalyptic fiction maintains a persistent presence of the old world through its remnants and ruins. Crumbling infrastructure, obsolete technology, scavenged literature, and decaying cities appear across nearly all post-apocalyptic narratives, serving functions far beyond mere setting decoration. These elements allow narratives to reflect critically on the excesses, values, and failures of contemporary civilization while simultaneously evoking nostalgia for lost worlds. The contrast between what was and what remains generates a "cultural estrangement," allowing readers to view their own societies from a defamiliarizing perspective.[10]
- This subgenre emphasizes survival in its starkest form—grounded, bleak, and stripped of science fiction elements. Works focusing on realistic post-apocalypse avoid high-tech solutions, genetic engineering, or speculative science, instead depicting humanity struggling with purely natural disasters or societal collapse through starvation, violence, and scarcity. The Road by Cormac McCarthy exemplifies this approach, presenting a stripped-down narrative focused on immediate human concerns in a world devoid of technology, hope, or redemption. These narratives often emphasize moral greyness, depicting characters forced into ethically compromised positions simply to survive.[30][31]
- Common Pitfalls to Avoid — Frequent errors in the genre with explanations of why they undermine narrative effectiveness
- Avoid omniscient: Generally distancing in apocalyptic contexts where intimacy and uncertainty enhance tension
- Avoid Over-Description: The most effective apocalyptic worldbuilding integrates setting naturally into action and character experience rather than halting narrative for exposition. Show the world through how characters navigate and interact with it.
- Silence and Omission: Use what you don't say as powerfully as what you do. Gaps in exposition can heighten tension and suggest psychological numbness.
- Avoid Purple Prose: Apocalyptic fiction typically demands clarity over ornate language. Readers need to understand the harsh realities your characters face.
- Avoid stereotypes: Differentiate characters beyond survival roles; grant each distinct personality, background, and perspective
- Avoid preaching; trust readers to recognize thematic resonance
- Subtext: What characters don't say often matters more than explicit speech
- Avoid constant action; use conflict pacing strategically
- 11. COMMON PITFALLS TO AVOID
- Flat secondary characters: Even minor characters deserve distinctness. Avoid reducing survivors to archetypal functions.
- Clichéd dialogue: Avoid overwrought expressions of despair or transparent exposition. Trust readers to infer emotional states.
