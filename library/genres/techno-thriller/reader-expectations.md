A thriller in which advanced technology — military systems, biotech, cyberweapons, or artificial intelligence — functions simultaneously as the central threat and the means of countering it, with technical authenticity central to the reader's trust.

## Tone & Mood

The dominant tone is urgent, authoritative, and laced with a specific kind of dread: the fear that systems too complex to fully control are already in motion. Readers expect to feel that the technology described is real, or credibly extrapolated — this is the genre's core compact with its audience. The emotional register moves between the procedural excitement of experts solving hard problems under pressure and the existential unease of realizing how much of civilization depends on systems that can be corrupted or weaponized. A degree of techno-optimism is conventional, but the genre accepts scenarios where the best available response is merely containing catastrophic failure.

## Pacing

Pacing is fast and information-dense. Chapters are short, often alternating between simultaneous crises or POV characters in different locations. The clock is always running: a cyberattack is spreading, a pathogen has been released, a rogue weapons system is en route. Readers expect technical problem-solving sequences detailed enough to feel authentic without slowing narrative momentum. Action sequences are interspersed with analysis and strategy. The climax typically requires both physical and technical resolution; one without the other feels incomplete.

## Setting Conventions

Settings are contemporary or near-future and grounded in real institutions: NSA operations centers, biotech research facilities, Pentagon war rooms, submarine control rooms, and corporate data centers. The genre's geography is global but operational details are specific rather than generic. Readers expect accuracy in how institutions actually work: chain of command, interagency friction, bureaucratic obstacles that complicate crisis response. The technology must be rendered with sufficient specificity to feel plausible; hand-waving is noticed and resented. Classified programs, zero-day exploits, CRISPR applications, autonomous weapons systems, and AI decision-making are all common technological subjects.

## Character Archetypes

The **Technical Expert** is the core protagonist: a specialist — cybersecurity analyst, bioweapons researcher, submarine commander — whose domain knowledge makes them uniquely capable of understanding and countering the threat. They are competent professionals rather than action heroes, though the genre frequently places them in physical danger their training did not cover. The **Government or Military Handler** provides institutional context and creates obstacles through politics or competing priorities. The **Rogue State or Terrorist Technologist** mirrors the protagonist's expertise while operating without ethical constraint. A **Political Decision-Maker** represents the layer at which technical reality meets policy.

## Length & Format

Standard novel length is 90,000–130,000 words; longer works are common when multiple technical threads require development. Multiple POV chapters are strongly preferred, allowing the narrative to track both the attack and the response simultaneously. Author's notes explaining the real-world basis of the technology are conventional and welcomed. Series built around recurring expert protagonists are common. The writing should be clear and unambiguous — the genre's clarity about how things work is a feature, not a constraint.
