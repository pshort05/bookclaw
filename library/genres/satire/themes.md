Satire's thematic spine — the moral argument the comedy is in service of. Without a unifying theme, satirical set pieces are just jokes; with one, they accumulate into an indictment. Pick two or three to anchor the manuscript.

- **The corruption of language** — power maintains itself by controlling what can be said, how things are named, and what meanings are permitted. Satire exposes how euphemism, jargon, and official discourse render atrocity invisible. The theme is not merely that language is misused but that the misuse is institutional and systematic: the language itself becomes the mechanism of oppression.

- **The banality of institutional evil** — harm at scale is executed not by monsters but by functionaries: people doing their jobs, following procedure, hitting their targets. The satirical argument is that the worst outcomes emerge from systems of ordinary competence operating within corrupt goals. Characters who are not evil — just efficient, obedient, ambitious — produce catastrophe.

- **Hypocrisy between stated values and actual behavior** — the gap between what institutions, societies, and individuals claim to stand for and what they demonstrably do. The satirical technique is not to point at the hypocrisy but to dramatize it so the reader arrives at the indictment without being told. Religion, government, and corporate culture are the traditional targets; the theme scales to any domain with formal values.

- **The self-defeating pursuit of power** — characters sacrifice everything (morality, relationships, integrity, eventually life or sanity) in pursuit of power or status, and the satire tracks how this pursuit is both pathetic and genuinely dangerous. The theme argues that the logic of dominance is inherently irrational — its final destination is ruin.

- **Ideology as a substitute for thinking** — the way systems of belief (political, religious, corporate, cultural) allow their adherents to stop reasoning and start reciting. The satirical target is not any specific ideology but the mechanism of ideological capture: the moment when a set of ideas becomes a filter that cannot be questioned from inside.

- **The complicity of the observer** — the reader, along with the protagonists, is implicated in the systems being satirized. Modern satire increasingly refuses a safe moral vantage point from which to watch. The theme is that distance is a fiction: the consumer, the bystander, the educated reader are participants in the systems they find appalling.

- **Progress as illusion** — the satirical argument that human societies have not meaningfully improved, only redistributed the same cruelties into new administrative forms. New technologies, new ideologies, new vocabulary — same extraction, same violence, same self-deception. Works best when the historical or speculative distance underlines the sameness.

- **The absurdity of certainty** — characters whose absolute confidence in their own rightness is the engine of everything that goes wrong. Certainty in the face of chaotic, intractable reality is the comic and tragic condition. The theme is not relativism but epistemological humility: the catastrophe is always caused by someone who knew exactly what they were doing.
