Calibration titles for satire — the canonical and contemporary works that demonstrate what the form can do at full power.

- **Catch-22 by Joseph Heller (1961)** — the definitive institutional-spiral satire; Heller's bureaucratic catch structures demonstrate how a self-sealing system traps every exit, and the novel's non-linear chronology mirrors the circular logic it critiques.

- **Candide by Voltaire (1759)** — the purest naïf-in-a-corrupt-world template, compressed to novella length; every episode is a self-contained satirical set piece that demolishes a different strand of Enlightenment optimism without ever letting the naïf understand what is happening to him.

- **Gulliver's Travels by Jonathan Swift (1726)** — the picaresque tour taken to its structural extreme; each voyage is a distinct satirical target (scientific hubris, political vanity, rationalism without humanity), demonstrating how displacement into an alien world creates critical distance that a realistic setting cannot.

- **Animal Farm by George Orwell (1945)** — the allegorical form executed with maximum efficiency; the farmyard displacement lets Orwell dramatize totalitarian self-corruption without the defensiveness a realist political novel would provoke, and the compression forces every scene to carry double weight.

- **Slaughterhouse-Five by Kurt Vonnegut (1969)** — the anti-war satire that refuses conventional narrative time to make its argument; the "so it goes" refrain is a masterclass in ironic understatement, and the novel demonstrates how formal experimentation and satirical intent can be the same gesture rather than competing impulses.

- **Vile Bodies by Evelyn Waugh (1930)** — Horatian satire of Bright Young Things and media culture at its most elegant; Waugh's detached, almost affectless prose voice sustains the ironic distance across a novel in which everyone behaves badly and nobody learns anything, and the brittle comedy is itself a satirical comment on the society producing it.

- **Thank You for Smoking by Christopher Buckley (1994)** — the contemporary Horatian corporate satire; Buckley's protagonist is himself the satirical target (a tobacco industry spokesman with genuine self-awareness and zero self-reform), demonstrating that the narrator-as-exemplar-of-the-problem is a viable and under-used satirical mode.

- **Small Gods by Terry Pratchett (1992)** — the satirical novel that refuses to sacrifice human warmth for critical distance; Pratchett targets religious institutions, divine authority, and the relationship between faith and power, and demonstrates that comedy can be simultaneously laugh-out-loud funny and philosophically serious without the two modes undermining each other.
