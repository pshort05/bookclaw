Satire's recurring structural devices — the moves that deliver the genre's comic-critical charge. Pick three to six that serve your central critique; using all of them produces a bloated, tonally incoherent manuscript.

- **The naïf in a corrupt world** — an innocent protagonist navigates a society whose moral bankruptcy is invisible to everyone except, increasingly, the reader. The naïf's literal sincerity exposes hypocrisy by taking official claims at face value. Works best when the naïf is not entirely stupid — merely, devastatingly, un-cynical.

- **Bureaucratic labyrinth** — characters trapped in self-defeating procedural systems that exist to serve themselves rather than any stated purpose. The catch-22 structure, where every exit is closed by the system's own rules. Most effective when the bureaucracy is depicted with loving accuracy — the specific form number, the contradictory directives — rather than vague institutional fog.

- **The euphemism machine** — language weaponized to make atrocities sound neutral or even positive: "enhanced interrogation," "collateral optimization," "downsizing." The satirical technique is to make the gap between the euphemism and the reality viscerally apparent, usually by showing the concrete thing the language is designed to obscure.

- **Failing upward** — incompetence is rewarded because the system selects for traits (obedience, self-promotion, ideological conformity) that correlate with incompetence. The genuinely skilled are sidelined or destroyed. Satire's most durable institutional critique.

- **The true believer** — a character who earnestly embodies the system's official values and, in doing so, becomes a perfect engine of the system's harm. No self-awareness; complete sincerity. The horror is that within the logic of the institution, they are doing exactly the right thing.

- **Performative virtue** — individuals and institutions execute elaborate rituals of moral commitment (diversity statements, charitable galas, environmental pledges, public apologies) that substitute performance for change. The satirical leverage: the performance is so polished it has wholly replaced the thing it performs.

- **The picaresque tour** — the protagonist moves through a series of corrupt or absurd environments (courts, churches, armies, corporations, media companies), each a self-contained satirical set piece. Structure exists to accumulate exposure rather than to drive a traditional plot arc.

- **The unreliable institutional voice** — an official narrator, report, manual, or authority whose confident pronouncements are obviously, demonstrably wrong. Works through dramatic irony: the reader sees through the text that the narrator cannot.

- **Circular logic / self-defeating policy** — a rule, law, or social norm that exists to solve a problem but structurally guarantees the problem's continuation. The satire arrives not from pointing at the circle but from showing characters earnestly running it.

- **The open secret** — everyone knows the thing that no one will say. The satirical mode is to dramatize the social machinery that enforces the collective silence, not the secret itself.

- **Catastrophic overreaction / disproportionate response** — institutions deploy maximum force, ceremony, or bureaucracy against trivial problems while ignoring catastrophic ones. Works through comic proportion; effective when the disproportion is precise.

- **The mirror society** — a fictional culture, species, or historical setting that stands in structural analogy to the target society. The displacement lets the reader see familiar arrangements with fresh eyes. Must be tight enough that the mapping is clear without being so on-the-nose that it collapses into allegory-by-dictionary.
