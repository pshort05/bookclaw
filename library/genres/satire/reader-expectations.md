Satire delivers on a precise contract: comedy as the weapon, folly and vice as the target, and moral clarity — however oblique — as the payload.

## Tone & Mood

Satire operates in one of two registers, and your choice governs every line:

- **Horatian** — urbane, affectionate, gently mocking. The world is absurd, human beings are foolish, and the narrator watches with amused disappointment rather than rage. Think Voltaire's *Candide*, Pratchett's Discworld. The reader laughs with a light sting. Appropriate when your target is universal folly (vanity, self-deception, bureaucratic idiocy) and you want the audience to recognize themselves.
- **Juvenalian** — sharp, contemptuous, morally outraged. The narrator's wit cuts to wound. Swift's *A Modest Proposal*, Heller's *Catch-22*. Laughter is uncomfortable because the horror underneath the joke is genuine. Appropriate when the target is institutional evil — war profiteering, systemic cruelty, corrupt power.

Do not mix registers without intention. An author who oscillates between gentle ribbing and livid condemnation produces tonal whiplash, not satirical range.

Irony is not optional — it is satire's load-bearing wall. The surface text says one thing; the meaning runs beneath it. Sarcasm is blunt; irony is precise. Prefer irony.

The voice must sustain a detached, almost clinical authority — the narrator observes with complete lucidity while everyone inside the world goes on being ridiculous. Lose that authority and the critique collapses into complaint.

## Pacing

Satire does not build toward emotional catharsis the way literary or genre fiction does. Its engine is **escalation of absurdity**. Start in a world slightly askew from reality, then tighten the screws. Each chapter should make the situation more extreme, more inevitable, more darkly funny than the last.

The pacing danger is stasis: satirical set pieces that repeat the same joke at the same pitch. Vary the technique (irony, hyperbole, juxtaposition, bathos) even when the thematic target stays constant. The reader should feel a slow accumulation of horror and comedy until the two become indistinguishable.

Exposition of the satirical target must be dramatized, not declared. A character explaining "the system is corrupt" is not satire. A character earnestly defending the system while the system destroys everything around them is.

Balance critique with comic momentum. If the didactic freight gets too heavy — if the essay is winning against the story — the novel loses readers even when the argument is correct. Keep action, character collision, and absurdist incident moving.

## Setting Conventions

Satire works in three modes:

- **Realistic heightened** — recognizable contemporary or historical world with its absurdities amplified to just past the breaking point. Wolfe's *Bonfire of the Vanities*, Waugh's *Vile Bodies*. The world looks like our world; the reader's recognition is the point.
- **Displacement / allegorical** — a fictional world that stands in for the real one. Orwell's animal farm. The displacement creates critical distance; readers can laugh at what they'd defensively dismiss if addressed directly.
- **Absurdist / surreal** — the rules of reality are suspended, and the satire operates by exposing what the impossible-made-real reveals about actual human behavior. Vonnegut's *Slaughterhouse-Five*, Kafka's influence on Heller. Works only when the absurdity illuminates, not merely decorates.

Whichever mode you choose, the setting must contain identifiable institutions — government, military, church, corporation, media, academia, the law — because satire requires a target with structural power. Targeting only individuals produces personal lampoon; targeting the system produces satire.

## Character Archetypes

- **The naïf** — innocent, optimistic, takes the world at its stated word. Their literal-mindedness exposes hypocrisy by colliding with it sincerely. Candide, Yossarian's less-cynical early self. The naïf's function is to ask the question the reader is already thinking.
- **The villain-in-power** — competent at maintaining their position, sincere in their ideology, incapable of self-examination. They are not cartoonishly evil; they are mundanely, systemically terrible. Their sincerity is more frightening than any moustache-twirling would be.
- **The satirist-proxy** — the one (barely) sane observer whose lucidity shows up the madness. May be the narrator, may be a character. Functions as the reader's moral anchor without delivering speeches.
- **The true believer** — deeply invested in the absurd system; regurgitates its logic earnestly. The comedy of the true believer is that they are not wrong within the logic of the system — the system itself is what's wrong.
- **The opportunist** — adapts fluidly to the corrupt order because the corrupt order rewards adaptation. Neither hero nor villain; a mirror.

Characters in satire are typically flatter than in literary fiction — they are types that embody ideas. This is not a failure; it is the genre's structural choice. Do not apologize for it by adding redemption arcs the form doesn't need. If you want character depth, give it to the naïf or the satirist-proxy; everyone else can stay sharp and two-dimensional.

## Length & Format

- **Word count:** 70,000–95,000 words is the contemporary commercial sweet spot. Classic satires have run shorter (Swift's novellas, Voltaire's *Candide* at ~35,000 words) and much longer (Heller's *Catch-22*, Wolfe's *Bonfire*). Match length to ambition: episodic satire can sustain a longer run; tight allegory wants compression.
- **POV:** Third person limited or third person omniscient are most common. Omniscient narration enables the detached, ironic authorial voice that is satire's traditional mode. First person works when the narrator is the naïf or an unreliable voice whose blindness is the joke.
- **Structure:** Episodic is common and appropriate — the picaresque (naïf travels through corrupt world) remains satire's dominant chassis. Three-act structure is not required, but the novel must build toward a conclusion that crystallizes the critique, even if the ending is bleak, circular, or deliberately unresolved.
- **Standalone vs. series:** Satire is almost always standalone. Series satire (Pratchett excepted) risks diluting the critique as the target becomes a universe rather than an argument.
