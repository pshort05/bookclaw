Satire's dominant structure is the picaresque escalation: a naïf or observer moves through a series of set pieces, each exposing a new facet of the target institution or society, with absurdity and horror accumulating until the critique is fully legible.

Three alternate structures that work within satire:

- **The allegorical arc** — a compressed fable structure (Orwell's *Animal Farm*) where the satirical argument maps onto a clear before/during/after: an existing order, its corruption, and its self-reproduction. Tighter, more parable-like; trades picaresque texture for moral clarity.
- **The institutional spiral** — the protagonist begins inside the system, tries to exit or reform it, discovers the system is self-sealing, and either escapes (ambiguously), is destroyed, or surrenders. Heller's *Catch-22* runs this structure across many interlocking spirals. Requires a strong satirist-proxy or naïf whose attempts to act rationally constitute the through-line.
- **The mock-epic / mock-documentary** — the narrative adopts a form (the epic, the government report, the corporate manual, the memoir) that is inherently comic given its content, and sustains the irony through structural commitment to that borrowed form.

## Obligatory Scenes

These are the scenes a satire reader expects. Omitting them is not innovation; it is a broken contract.

- **The introductory absurdity** — within the first chapter, an incident that establishes the rules of this satirical world: what is treated as normal here that would be scandalous or impossible elsewhere. This sets the ironic register and tells the reader how to read everything that follows. The absurdity must be specific and concrete, not vaguely "the system is bad."

- **The institution's self-justification** — an official, a document, a ceremony, or a meeting in which the target system explains itself sincerely, in its own language, for the first time. The reader sees the internal logic of the thing being satirized. This scene should make the institution's case as well as it can be made — the satirical force comes from the reader seeing through it while the institution cannot.

- **The collision of official language with reality** — a moment where a euphemism, policy, or official procedure meets the concrete human situation it was designed to manage, and the gap is made viscerally apparent. The bureaucratic form that cannot accommodate the dying patient. The loyalty oath taken by someone who doesn't believe in loyalty. The safety inspection of the facility that just exploded.

- **The competent villain or true believer in full operation** — a scene where the most effective servant of the corrupt system is shown being effective: hitting their numbers, satisfying their superiors, executing the policy with skill. Their competence inside the wrong system is the source of horror. Do not make this character stupid or overtly evil.

- **The moment the naïf or observer grasps the structure** — a scene of dawning comprehension where the protagonist finally sees the whole shape of the trap: the system is not malfunctioning; this is the function. This is satire's equivalent of the literary novel's epiphany. It should arrive late (two-thirds to three-quarters through) and it should be quiet, not melodramatic.

- **The failed exit or attempted reform** — a character tries to fix, expose, or escape the system through legitimate channels and the system absorbs, ignores, or destroys the attempt. This scene demonstrates that the problem is structural, not merely a matter of bad actors. Do not skip this; without it the satire becomes personal lampoon rather than institutional critique.

- **The conclusion that refuses false comfort** — satire rarely ends with redemption or triumph. The ending must crystallize the critique, not dissolve it. Options: the system continues unchanged; the protagonist escapes but the machine grinds on; the reformer is co-opted; the cycle restarts. An ending that resolves into hope must be earned with extraordinary care or it betrays the premise.
