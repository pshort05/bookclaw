Satire's non-negotiable requirements — the elements that make the critique land and the comedy cohere. Treat as a revision pass before the manuscript is declared finished.

- [ ] **A legible target** — the reader can identify within the first three chapters what system, institution, ideology, or human failing the novel is aimed at. Satire that is about "everything" is about nothing. The target should be specific (this kind of bureaucracy, this strain of ideology, this species of vanity) even when the scope is broad.

- [ ] **Irony that carries the critique** — the surface text consistently means something other than its literal statement, and that gap between surface and meaning is where the argument lives. Do not explain the irony. Do not let a character voice the moral. The irony must be self-evident to the reader and invisible to at least one character in the scene.

- [ ] **A consistent tonal register** — every scene operates in the same ironic mode. Chapters that abandon the register for earnest drama or sentimental comfort break the satirical contract. If the ending needs to be serious, earn that shift through deliberate escalation, not tonal collapse.

- [ ] **Concrete, specific absurdity** — satirical set pieces are built from precisely observed details: the actual form number, the exact corporate slogan, the specific procedural contradiction. Vague institutional wrongness is not satire. Name the thing. The specificity is what makes the exaggeration land.

- [ ] **A naïf, observer, or proxy whose perspective organizes the critique** — readers need a stable point of view from which to experience the satirical world. This character does not need to be sympathetic but must be lucid enough (or sincerely confused enough) that the reader can track what is happening and why it is wrong.

- [ ] **The institution or system depicted with enough fidelity to be recognizable** — satire that misrepresents its target (gets the bureaucracy wrong, misunderstands how political power actually works, strawmans its ideology) is not just inaccurate; it is ineffective. The critique must fit the thing.

- [ ] **Escalating stakes or absurdity** — the novel must build. Each act of institutional horror, each bureaucratic obstruction, each piece of euphemism must be larger, more absurd, or more damning than the last. A satire that runs at the same pitch throughout is a sketch stretched to novel length.

- [ ] **Comedy that pulls weight** — every joke, every piece of comic timing, every absurdist incident must serve the critique as well as the laugh. Comedy that is purely ornamental weakens the satirical argument by suggesting the author finds the subject more amusing than alarming.

- [ ] **No character-delivered sermons** — the moral argument must emerge from situation and structure, not from a character who stops the novel to explain the point. If a scene requires a speech to make its meaning clear, the scene is not working yet.

- [ ] **An ending that respects the premise** — the conclusion either sustains the satirical argument (the system continues; the corruption is unreformed) or subverts it with surgical precision. A conventionally happy ending that contradicts the novel's evidence is a structural lie.
