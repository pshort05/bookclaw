The failures that make readers put down a satire — broken contracts, tonal betrayals, and structural errors specific to this genre.

- [ ] **Preachiness** — a character (often the protagonist or a wise mentor figure) delivers the moral of the novel in direct speech. This kills the satire because satire's power is entirely located in the gap between what is shown and what is said. Once the author puts the argument into a character's mouth, the irony collapses and the novel becomes an essay with fictional furniture. If you find yourself writing this speech, the preceding scenes are not doing their job.

- [ ] **The target is already universally condemned** — satire aimed at things everyone already agrees are wrong (historical atrocities long since disavowed, cartoonishly evil fictional regimes with no real-world parallel) produces no friction. There is no satirical leverage when the audience is not implicated and does not need to be moved. Satire must have a live target: something the reader, or people the reader knows, participates in, defends, or has not yet recognized as a problem.

- [ ] **Tonal inconsistency without purpose** — switching from ironic comedy to earnest melodrama without structural intention. This is different from a deliberate Juvenalian shift toward rage or grief; this is the manuscript forgetting what it is. Scenes of sincere romantic attachment, sentimental family drama, or thriller-style urgency that do not serve the satirical argument signal that the author has stopped trusting the mode.

- [ ] **Vague institutional wrongness** — the system is corrupt, but the reader cannot tell how, specifically, it works. The bureaucracy obstructs in undefined ways. The ideology is generically bad. Satire runs on precision; the comic and critical force depends on the reader recognizing the exact mechanism being lampooned. Vagueness produces contempt without comprehension.

- [ ] **Strawmanning the target** — depicting the ideology, institution, or system in its weakest possible form so that demolishing it requires no effort. Real satire depicts the target as its own adherents see it — internally coherent, even attractive — and then exposes what that coherent system actually produces. A strawman target lets readers congratulate themselves for agreeing; it does not move them.

- [ ] **Protagonist exceptionalism** — the protagonist escapes the system's logic because they are simply smarter, more moral, and more clear-eyed than everyone around them. This is not satire; it is a hero narrative wearing satire's clothes. If the protagonist is immune to the satirical target, the target is not actually being satirized — it is being used as a backdrop for a bildungsroman. The protagonist should be entangled with what they are critiquing, even if only in complicity.

- [ ] **The unearned hopeful ending** — a conclusion in which the system is reformed, the corrupt officials are exposed and punished, and a better future is visible on the horizon, achieved without the satire having established how such change could plausibly occur within its own logic. Satire earned in hope (if at all) through structural argument, not through an authorial wish that contradicts the novel's entire evidence base. A hopeful ending that isn't earned reads as cowardice.

- [ ] **Comedy that outpaces critique** — a manuscript so funny that the reader is entertained without being disturbed. The comedy must always carry an edge; the moment a satirical scene produces only delight without discomfort, it has become farce. Check that every major joke contains something true and troubling, not just something clever.

- [ ] **Topicality without universality** — satire aimed so specifically at current events, specific individuals, or momentary cultural phenomena that it has no resonance outside its window of relevance. Effective satire uses the specific to reveal the universal: the target is this administration, this corporation, this cultural moment — but the mechanism being exposed is endemic to human institutions. Pure topicality is journalism.
