Memoir and Autofiction readers come for the specific pleasure of a "true" account held in unstable relationship with invention — the narrator's unreliability is not a flaw but the subject of the work.

## Tone & Mood

The tone is intimate, self-interrogating, and frequently uncomfortable in its proximity to the narrator's interiority. Readers expect to be close to a consciousness that is examining itself under pressure — not confessing cleanly, but circling, revising, withholding, and occasionally lying. The emotional register can span from wry and ironic to devastatingly raw, but the controlling quality is always the sense that the narrator is doing something difficult and not entirely sure they should be doing it. The moral complexity of writing about real people — family members, lovers, the self — should be felt in the prose rather than resolved by a disclaimer.

## Pacing

The pacing is essayistic and associative rather than plot-driven. Time moves by emotional logic: a smell triggers a decade-old scene; the narrator circles back to a childhood moment from three different angles; a present-tense frame advances slowly while the past expands around it. Readers accept and expect this non-linearity because the circling is the work — the narrator is constructing meaning from experience that resisted meaning when it was lived. Forward propulsion comes from deepening understanding rather than narrative event. The central question is not "what happens next" but "what does this mean, and does the narrator know?"

## Setting Conventions

Setting in Autofiction is anchored in autobiographical specificity — named cities, real institutions, places that have verifiable coordinates even if the events described may not. The narrative gains its particular charge from the implication that this happened here, to this person, in a world the reader also inhabits. Domestic spaces — kitchens, childhood bedrooms, the apartments of lovers — carry disproportionate weight as containers of memory. When the setting is historical or politically inflected (a specific regime, a cultural moment, an immigration story), that context should be present not as research but as lived atmosphere, the world the narrator breathed inside.

## Character Archetypes

The narrator-protagonist occupies a dual position: the experiencing self who lived through the events, and the narrating self who is now constructing a version of them. The gap between these two selves is where the genre lives. Other characters — family members, lovers, friends — appear as remembered and interpreted figures, with the narrator's perception of them constituting its own layer of meaning. The ethics of how real people are represented is part of the genre's subject matter; readers are alert to this and expect the narrator to be alert to it too. Secondary characters in memoir often feel more vivid than they might in realist fiction because the narrator has lived with them.

## Length & Format

Works run 60,000–90,000 words, though short lyric memoirs (under 50,000 words) have a strong tradition. The form is flexible and hybrid: prose poetry, numbered fragments, interleaved timelines, and second-person address are all in regular use. Autofiction in particular has embraced the very short chapter or section (half a page, a paragraph) as a structural unit. Author's notes addressing the relationship between memory and invention are common and expected — readers want the writer's account of the contract they are making, not a simple fiction/nonfiction binary.
