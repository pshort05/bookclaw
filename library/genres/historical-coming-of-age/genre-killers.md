Avoid these mistakes that can undermine your story.

## Failure Modes

- | Do | Don't |
- | :--- | :--- |
- | **DO** root character goals in period-appropriate desires (honor, security, family name, freedom within societal limits). | **DON'T** give characters modern sensibilities, ambitions, or reactions without a plausible, textured origin. |
- | **DO** use historical events as turning points that force personal decisions. | **DON'T** let historical pageantry overshadow the intimate character journey. |
- | **DO** show the protagonist learning practical period skills relevant to their survival/ascension. | **DON'T** rely on vague 'cleverness'; show specific, earned competence. |
- | **DO** have characters speak in ways that reveal their social standing and relationships. | **DON'T** use 'thee/thou' randomly or overload dialogue with outdated jargon. |
- | **DO** let the setting inflict real, period-plausible consequences for rebellion or transgression. | **DON'T** protect your protagonist from the genuine hardships and prejudices of their time. |
- Template:** [Social Ritual/Greeting] + [Core Message (often veiled)] + [Period-Specific Idiom/Closer]
- Examples by Context:**
-    **Noble to Servant (Edwardian):** "Jenkins, (Ritual) be so good as to inform my father I’ve returned from the hunt. (Message) And you might mention I spent the afternoon at the tenant cottages. The condition of the roof on the Miller’s place is quite… illuminating. (Idiom/Veiled Threat) One wonders what else has been overlooked."
-    **Youth to Mentor (Medieval Apprentice):** "Master Griggs, (Ritual) I’ve ground the pigments as you asked. (Message) But I was looking at the saint’s face on the chapel wall… the red in the cheek seems off. Could we not add a touch of yellow ochre to warm it? (Request showing growth) For the life in it, I mean."
-    **Across Class Lines (Regency):** A farmer to the squire's son: "Mr. Ellis, sir. (Ritual/Deference) The fence is mended. (Message) The sheep won't be troubling your father's garden again. (Implied: I've done my part) Hard to keep a creature from seeking better pasture, though, innit? (Idiom/Subtext: We're struggling.)"
-    **Pitfall:** The 21st-Century Protagonist in Period Costume.
-    **Fix:** Conduct deep research into contemporary diaries, letters, and conduct manuals to understand period-appropriate motivations, fears, and worldviews. Anchor their desires in their time.
-    **Pitfall:** History as a Static Backdrop.
-    **Fix:** Tie the protagonist’s personal stakes directly to historical events. The factory closure isn’t just setting; it's why their father is unemployed and drinking, creating the home conflict.
-    **Pitfall:** Info-Dump World-Building.
-    **Fix:** **The Iceberg Method.** Know 10x more than you write. Reveal details only through character action, need, or conflict. A character *using* a spinning wheel reveals its mechanics; a paragraph describing it halts the story.
-    **Pitfall:** Anachronistic Dialogue.
-    **Fix:** Create a "banished words" list for your era (e.g., "okay," "teenager," specific modern slang). Read dialogue aloud; if it sounds like a modern conversation, layer in period formality and social calculus.
-    **Pitfall:** A Passive Protagonist.
-    **Fix:** At every major plot point, ask: "What active choice does my protagonist make here?" Even inaction should be a conscious, character-driven choice with consequences.
-    **Aristocracy/Nobility:** Formal syntax, classical allusions, French/Latin phrases, indirect criticism, focus on honor, lineage, and politics. "One wonders, sir, if your enthusiasm does not outpace your discretion."
-    **Gentry/Professionals:** Correct grammar, reference to law/business/estate management, concern with reputation and propriety. "The terms of the contract, madam, are quite clear, and our reputation depends on adhering to them."
-    **Urban Trades/Merchants:** Direct, pragmatic, focused on money, goods, and practical outcomes. More likely to use colorful idioms. "You'll get your shipment when I see the color of your coin, not a moment sooner."
-    **Rural Labor/Peasantry:** Simple syntax, local dialect words, reference to land, weather, animals, and survival. Proverbs and folk wisdom. "A dry March and a wet May fill the barns with corn and hay. This bodes ill, it does."
