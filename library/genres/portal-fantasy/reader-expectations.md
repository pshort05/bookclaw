Fantasy in which a protagonist crosses from the real world into a fully realized secondary fantasy world through a magical doorway, bringing an outsider's perspective that shapes how the reader discovers the new world.

## Tone & Mood

The tone shifts across two registers that mirror the protagonist's journey: the mundane, often dissatisfying real world at the start, and the sense of wonder — sometimes shaded with danger — that pervades the secondary world. Readers expect to feel the protagonist's dawning awareness that the rules have changed. The mood in the secondary world can range from fairy-tale luminous to darkly mythic, but the sense of genuine otherness must be sustained. Nostalgia and displacement are recurring emotional undercurrents; the protagonist may want to go home and simultaneously fear losing what they've found.

## Pacing

The portal crossing should come early — within the first act — to avoid the real world overstaying its welcome. After crossing, pacing is driven by the protagonist's need to orient themselves in an unfamiliar world, which naturally generates questions, errors, and revelations. The middle act typically involves mastery: learning the world's rules, finding allies, discovering their role in whatever conflict exists. Readers expect the return question — can the protagonist go back, do they want to — to build through the second half and resolve at or near the climax.

## Setting Conventions

The secondary world must feel self-consistent and fully inhabited before the protagonist arrives; the portal fantasy reader expects to believe the world exists independently of the protagonist's presence. Visual and sensory contrast with the real world is essential — the other world should feel categorically different in atmosphere, color, sound, and social texture. Common secondary-world elements include non-human peoples, magic systems tied to the world's history, political structures in crisis, and a geography that rewards exploration. The portal itself is often a threshold object or place with its own mythology within the secondary world.

## Character Archetypes

The protagonist is typically an ordinary person from our world who brings modern sensibilities — pragmatism, skepticism, specific technical knowledge — that prove unexpectedly useful in the secondary world. A native guide who explains the world without becoming a simple exposition vehicle is essential. The secondary world usually contains a Chosen One framework that the portal protagonist either fulfills, disrupts, or sidesteps. Antagonists are rooted in the secondary world's internal politics rather than arriving from the real world.

## Length & Format

Portal fantasy runs 80,000–120,000 words for a primary novel. Series are common and often expected, as the secondary world's scope invites continued exploration. First-person or close third-person limited are both standard, keeping the reader locked to the outsider's discovering perspective. Returning to the real world between books is less common than sustained immersion; readers who commit to a portal fantasy typically want to stay in the secondary world.
