# Space Fantasy Romance — Must-Haves (non-negotiable checklist)

Skip these and the manuscript fails the fundamental requirements of the genre. Treat as a
revision pass.

- [ ] **Romance is structurally load-bearing** — the love story must be inseparable from the
  external political/cosmic arc. If you can remove the romance and the plot still works, the
  manuscript is a space opera with a romantic subplot, not a space fantasy romance. Every major
  external plot turn must force a romantic complication or be caused by one.

- [ ] **Ground the fated bond in felt experience** — a fated-mate or prophesied connection is
  only earned when the reader experiences it through scene-level sensation and consequence, not
  narration. Show the bond's physical and magical reality before the characters name it. Readers
  must believe in it before they are told to.

- [ ] **Make the alien world specific** — the space setting must be concrete and particular:
  distinct alien physiologies, specific political structures, a magic system with rules and costs,
  sensory details of alien environments. A fantasy castle with starship wallpaper is not a genre
  requirement; actual alien strangeness is. The world must feel impossible to transpose to a
  standard fantasy setting without losing something essential.

- [ ] **Establish political stakes before the romance peaks** — the external arc (galactic
  empire, inter-species treaty, cosmic trial) must be credible and in motion before the
  midpoint. Readers need to believe the political threat is real for the romantic sacrifice
  at the black moment to carry weight.

- [ ] **Give both protagonists genuine political and personal agency** — neither lead can exist
  solely as a function of the other's arc. Each must have their own goals, their own faction
  loyalties, and their own capacity to act independently before the bond draws them together.
  Avoid the pattern where one protagonist's only purpose is to love the other.

- [ ] **Calibrate the heat level to the fated-mate dynamic** — a fated bond creates an intrinsic
  intensity that must be matched by the emotional and (if applicable) physical heat level. If the
  book trends toward higher heat, the tension and release must be proportional to the cosmic
  stakes. Sweet-to-sensual is equally valid provided the emotional heat is operatic.

- [ ] **Earn the black moment through Act I stakes** — the crisis that separates the protagonists
  must be rooted in a specific, named conflict established in the opening act: a species law, a
  political obligation, an antagonist's leverage, a prophecy with a cost. A black moment
  caused by simple miscommunication or a misheard conversation fails the genre contract.

- [ ] **Deliver the HEA/HFN without erasing the world** — the resolution must be emotionally
  complete. The lovers claim each other. But the political/cosmic arc must also resolve
  credibly — destroyed, transformed, or set in a new equilibrium — so the HEA does not feel
  purchased at the cost of narrative logic. Readers of epic romantasy expect both.

- [ ] **Honor the scale with intimate moments** — the setting is galactic but the emotional
  experience must be personal. Every major scene needs at least one moment where the vast
  scale contracts to the space between two people. Epic scope without intimate grounding
  produces spectacle, not romance.
