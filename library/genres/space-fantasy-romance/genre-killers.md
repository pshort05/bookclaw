# Space Fantasy Romance — Genre Killers (avoid; these make readers DNF or one-star)

The broken promises, tonal betrayals, and structural failures that trigger DNF reactions and
one-star reviews.

- **Space as wallpaper** — the alien setting exists only as decoration. Remove the starships and
  alien courts, replace them with castles and fae, and the story is unchanged. Space fantasy
  romance readers are specifically seeking the cognitive estrangement of genuinely alien cultures,
  biologies, and magic systems intertwined with the romance. A setting that could be swapped
  without consequence is a setting that was never built.

- **Worldbuilding at the expense of romance** — extensive alien taxonomy, political history, and
  magic-system exposition that crowds out emotional intimacy. The worldbuilding serves the romance
  and its stakes; it never competes with it for page time. Chapters that advance the external arc
  without advancing the emotional arc are structural debt.

- **An underdeveloped fated bond** — a fated-mate or prophesied connection that is told rather
  than felt: the characters are informed they are cosmically destined for each other, accept
  this, and proceed. The bond must be earned through accumulated scene-level tension, physical
  evidence, and moments of recognition. Readers who came for the fated-mate dynamic will close
  the book if it is handed to them without cost.

- **Political stakes that collapse at the climax** — an Act I threat (galactic war, treaty
  collapse, species extinction) that is resolved off-page, resolved too easily, or simply
  abandoned so the romance can conclude. The external arc must be difficult to resolve and must
  cost the protagonists something real. A threat that evaporates to make room for the HEA reads
  as a false promise.

- **Insufficient consequences for inter-species or forbidden love** — the romance is positioned
  as transgressive (different species, opposed factions, violating cosmic law) but no one with
  real power ever makes that transgression cost anything. Stakes that are named but never
  enforced produce a sense of narrative bad faith.

- **A "simple misunderstanding" black moment** — the Act III rupture that a five-minute
  conversation between two rational adults would dissolve. The separation must arise from a
  genuine, named conflict with real structural roots: a duty that cannot be shared, an
  antagonist's leverage, a sacrifice the other protagonist was not consulted about. Anything
  less fails the emotional contract of the genre.

- **Agency stripped from one protagonist** — resolving the political arc by having the more
  powerful protagonist deploy their status, magic, or resources to solve the other's problems
  without their participation. Both leads must fight their own battles. This is doubly damaging
  when one protagonist exists primarily to be rescued by or to love the other, flattening them
  into a trophy rather than a partner.

- **Confusing "forbidden" with "abusive"** — a power-imbalanced pairing (ruler and political
  prisoner, conqueror and captive) where the more powerful figure uses coercion, manipulation,
  or force against the other and the narrative frames this as romantic. Darkness in the external
  arc is a feature; darkness directed by one lead against the other as a courtship strategy is
  not. The line must be explicit and maintained.
