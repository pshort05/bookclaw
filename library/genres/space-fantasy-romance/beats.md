# Space Fantasy Romance — Beats

The dominant structure is **Dual-Arc Romantasy** (romance arc + political/cosmic external arc), running
in continuous alternation so that every advance on one track creates a complication on the other.
The romance is always the primary arc; the political/cosmic arc is the escalating external pressure
that makes the romance impossible — and ultimately inevitable. *Romancing the Beat* (Gwen Hayes)
provides the four-phase romance spine; the external arc maps onto a classic three-act epic-fantasy
frame. Both arcs must complete together at the climax.

Structure is calibrated for 90,000–150,000 words. The percentages below use the midpoint
(~120,000 words) as the reference, but they hold proportionally across the full length range.

## Phase 1 — Collision (0–25%)

- **Establish Protagonist 1 (1–5%)** — ground the reader in their world: their species, political
  position, magical system, and the internal flaw (self-sufficiency, shame, duty over desire) that
  will block the romantic bond. Show the cosmos through their eyes before it cracks.
- **Establish Protagonist 2 (5–10%)** — the counter-perspective; they embody what P1 lacks or
  fears. In dual POV, show the same political landscape from a position of opposing interest.
- **The cosmic collision (10–15%)** — the first charged encounter. The setting must be specific:
  a treaty negotiation, a contested outpost, a magical trial's starting line, a marketplace
  on a border station. The attraction is immediate, unwanted, and dangerous.
- **No Way #1 (15–20%)** — each protagonist states (internally, to a trusted alien/political ally)
  exactly why this attraction is catastrophic: species law, political allegiance, fated-mate denial,
  the memory of a prior betrayal.
- **The adhesion (20–25%)** — the external plot forces them together in unavoidable proximity. The
  adhesion must carry real political, magical, or survival stakes — not mere convenience. Examples:
  a treaty requiring joint escort, a magical bond neither can sever, an assassination attempt
  requiring a bodyguard alliance, a shared trial neither can survive alone.

## Phase 2 — Falling (25–55%)

- **No Way #2 (25–35%)** — proximity breeds antagonism. Banter crackles across species difference,
  cultural misunderstanding, and political distrust. One observes the other's hidden competence,
  honor, or sacrifice and cannot reconcile it with the enemy they expected.
- **The inkling of desire (35–45%)** — professional or political respect bleeds into physical
  awareness. A moment of forced vulnerability — a magical healing, a crisis of conscience, a
  confession about what they stand to lose — opens a crack in the armor.
- **The fated-mate revelation or bond deepening (45–50%)** — the cosmic connection is named or
  acknowledged (a prophecy confirmed, a mating bond flaring, a magical resonance proven). Both
  protagonists know they are drawn together by forces larger than either of them. They have not
  yet accepted it.
- **Midpoint of love (~50%)** — a false high. A first kiss in an alien garden, a night in a
  shared outpost, a moment of genuine laughter in an impossible situation. They allow themselves
  to believe they can manage this — secretly, temporarily, without consequence. The reader knows
  better.

## Phase 3 — Retreating (55–80%)

- **Inkling of doubt (55–65%)** — the political/cosmic arc tightens. The cost of the bond
  becomes tangible: a treaty is threatened, a political rival exploits the vulnerability, a
  prophecy reveals the price the relationship demands. One or both pull back.
- **Deepening doubt (65–70%)** — external forces press harder. A faction weaponizes the
  relationship. One protagonist must appear to betray the other for political survival, or the
  bond itself becomes a tool someone else plans to use.
- **Retreat and shields up (70–75%)** — they return to their Phase 1 political facades. The
  coldness is practiced and deliberate. The emotional cost is visible to the reader even when
  hidden from the court.
- **The black moment (75–80%)** — the ultimate rupture. The external arc delivers an ultimatum
  that seems to make the romance permanently impossible: a forced political marriage to a third
  party, a revelation of betrayal (real or staged), an act of self-sacrifice that severs the bond
  before the other can stop it. This must be earned by the Act I stakes — not a
  misunderstanding a single message could resolve.

## Phase 4 — Fighting (80–100%)

- **Dark night of the soul (80–85%)** — both protagonists attempt to fulfill their political and
  cosmic duties without the other. The victory tastes hollow. The world they saved does not feel
  worth having without the person they gave up.
- **Wake up (85–90%)** — the internal flaw is named and rejected. The duty-over-love lie
  collapses. One (or both) recognizes that they have been choosing isolation in the guise of
  sacrifice.
- **The grand gesture (90–95%)** — a public, politically costly, cosmically significant act of
  commitment: renouncing a throne to cross star systems, weaponizing the fated bond against the
  faction trying to sever it, appearing before an alien court without political armor to claim
  the person they love. The gesture must risk something real — rank, safety, life, or the
  political victory they spent the entire book earning.
- **Resolution and HEA (95–100%)** — both arcs resolve together. The external threat is defeated
  or transformed. The bond is claimed, not merely survived. Close with a glimpse of the world
  they will build together: a small, intimate moment against the vast cosmic backdrop, confirming
  the HEA or a committed HFN that opens the series arc.

## Obligatory Scenes (readers feel cheated without these)

- **The competence scene** — early on, both protagonists must demonstrate their skill at
  navigating their specific world (a political negotiation, a magical or combat trial, a
  starship crisis) before readers are asked to root for them as lovers. Respect precedes longing.
- **The species/culture collision scene** — a moment where their different physiologies,
  worldviews, or cultural defaults produce genuine misunderstanding that is played for both
  tension and discovery, not comic relief. This grounds the "alien" in the relationship and
  distinguishes the genre from standard fantasy romance.
- **The forced-vulnerability scene** — an external crisis (magical backlash, an assassination
  attempt, a night stranded on a hostile world) strips one protagonist of their political armor
  and the other provides care instead of exploiting the exposure. This is the genre's emotional
  turning point.
- **The fated-bond scene** — a scene where the cosmic/magical connection is undeniable and
  witnessed by both. Not told in narration; lived by the characters. The reader must feel the
  pull before they are asked to believe in it.
- **The sacrifice** — one protagonist risks or surrenders something of genuine political or
  cosmic consequence for the other. The sacrifice must be irreversible in the moment it is made.
- **The claiming / HEA scene** — an explicit verbal and physical acknowledgment of the bond,
  given freely, not under duress. The cosmos may be vast but this moment is intimate. Do not
  rush it.
