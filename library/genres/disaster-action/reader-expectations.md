Disaster-action fiction pits human survival instinct against an overwhelming natural or man-made catastrophe, with rescue, sacrifice, and escalating stakes driving every scene.

## Tone & Mood

Urgent and visceral, with an undercurrent of awe at the scale of destruction. The disaster itself generates an almost sublime dread — readers want to feel small against the force being described. Heroism is earned through action under pressure, not through special status. The tone should resist cynicism: characters who sacrifice themselves or refuse to abandon strangers must be rendered with full emotional weight, not irony. Sentimentality is acceptable and expected at key emotional beats. The disaster also functions as a moral lens — it strips away social rank and exposes who people actually are.

## Pacing

Fast and relentless, with deliberate breathers to let the stakes register. The disaster should begin escalating within the first 20% of the novel and never fully relent — each resolved crisis should reveal a larger one. Short chapters (1,500–2,500 words) sustain momentum and allow the narrative to jump between multiple endangered groups. Intercutting between a rescue team, a group of trapped survivors, and the official command structure is a proven structural engine. Pacing slows only at emotional turning points: a death, a choice to abandon someone, a moment of unexpected human connection.

## Setting Conventions

The disaster is the primary setting, and its physical mechanics must be accurate and specific. A hurricane novel requires real meteorological behavior; a dam-break novel requires plausible hydrology; a train derailment requires geography that makes the rescue logistics genuinely difficult. Vague disaster sequences read as hollow. Iconic civilian infrastructure under threat — bridges, tunnels, hospitals, high-rise buildings, sports venues — grounds the stakes in recognizable danger. The command-and-control setting (emergency operations center, military HQ, newsroom) provides counterpoint to the ground-level survival story and should feel procedurally authentic. Urban settings generate claustrophobic peril; wilderness settings generate isolation and exposure.

## Character Archetypes

The Ignored Expert — a scientist, engineer, or analyst who correctly predicts the disaster and is overruled; their vindication establishes the threat's credibility. The Reluctant Hero — an ordinary person (paramedic, construction worker, off-duty firefighter) who possesses exactly the skills the situation demands. The Bureaucratic Obstacle — an authority figure whose caution or self-interest costs lives and generates reader frustration. The Self-Sacrificer — a character whose death is foreshadowed and lands as earned tragedy. The Survivor Group — a small ensemble of diverse civilians whose interpersonal dynamics provide the emotional spine between action sequences.

## Length & Format

80,000–100,000 words. Shorter than literary fiction because the pace demands it — every scene must either escalate danger or deepen a relationship that will be tested by danger. Multiple point-of-view third-person close is the standard structure, switching between perspectives at chapter breaks. Prologues showing the disaster's first moments before the main cast is introduced are a genre convention readers expect.
