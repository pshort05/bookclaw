# Style Guide: Warm, Witty Small-Town Contemporary Romance

## Core Identity

Write accessible, emotionally resonant prose that prioritizes character authenticity and quiet tenderness over manufactured drama. Your narrative voice functions as a warm, trusted friend observing the protagonist with humor, compassion, and genuine understanding. Every scene should create a sense of safety and belonging rather than manipulation or spectacle.

## Prose Mechanics

**Sentence Rhythm**
- Mix short, punchy declarative sentences (for emotional beats and character reactions) with longer, flowing sentences (for reflection, introspection, and setting details).
- Favor precision over embellishment; a perfectly chosen simple word outperforms purple prose.
- Read dialogue-heavy passages aloud to ensure natural cadence.

**Paragraph & Chapter Structure**
- Chapters run 2,500–3,500 words — long enough for emotional development, short enough to maintain reader engagement.
- End chapters on an emotional note (connection, realization, hope) or a question that creates forward momentum. Closed chapters work fine; avoid manufactured cliffhangers.
- Break paragraphs frequently to create white space, especially during dialogue or high emotion.

**Vocabulary & Tone**
- Write in conversational, accessible register. Assume an intelligent reader who doesn't need obscurity.
- Avoid melodramatic language or overwrought emotional description. Understatement often lands harder.
- Use specific, concrete details over vague adjectives. "The kitchen still smelled like burnt toast" beats "the kitchen was a mess."

## Narrative Structure

**Point of View**
- Third-person limited, deep inside the POV character's consciousness — readers should feel welcomed into their inner life.
- Maintain one POV per chapter or section; avoid head-hopping between characters (it fractures intimacy and confuses readers).
- Show the character's thoughts, doubts, and observations directly. Trust readers to understand implied information.

**Pacing & Tension**
- Tension emerges from internal conflict (fear, doubt, misunderstanding, yearning) and emotional stakes, not external threats or contrived obstacles.
- Pacing should feel steady and comfortable, never rushed. Readers should be able to breathe between beats.
- Avoid third-act breakups caused by a single misunderstanding or five-minute conversation. Conflict resolution requires genuine communication and growth.

**Scene Construction**
- Ground scenes in specific, functional details: the weather, the room, what characters are holding or touching.
- Each scene should reveal character through action and reaction, not exposition or summary.
- Open scenes with characters already in motion or in a meaningful moment; avoid extended setup.
- Close scenes with emotional clarity or a subtle turn that propels the reader forward.

**Settings & Small-Town Logic**
- Small-town settings create natural ensemble dynamics and recurring relationships. Leverage this.
- Use recurring locations (the diner, the town square, workplaces) to build familiarity and authentic detail.
- Show how community interconnectedness affects character decisions and relationships. These bonds matter.

## Dialogue

**Voice & Authenticity**
- Characters should speak like real people: incomplete sentences, pauses, non-sequiturs, interruptions, and nervous tics are all welcome.
- Avoid exposition-dumping through dialogue. Let characters converse naturally; let readers piece information together.
- Witty banter and gentle teasing work here, but never at the expense of character dignity or respect.

**Subtext & Revelation**
- What characters don't say often matters more than what they do. Include beats of silence or characters trailing off.
- Dialogue should reveal relationship dynamics, emotional state, and character voice simultaneously — not just move plot.
- Use dialogue tags sparingly ("said" is invisible and nearly always correct; avoid "breathed," "whispered," "purred").

**Format & Integration**
- New speaker = new paragraph.
- Include brief action beats between dialogue turns to ground the scene: *"She looked away before responding."*
- Let dialogue breathe; don't crowd exchanges with too many words per turn in tense moments.

## Intimate Moments

**Building Tension**
- Approach intimate scenes through longing, yearning, emotional vulnerability, and the anticipation of connection.
- Show physical awareness, attraction, and desire through small details: a character noticing the other's hand, breath, proximity.
- Make tension come from emotional barriers and internal hesitation, not artificial plot complications.

**Fade-to-Black Technique**
- Intimate scenes should fade to black gracefully before explicit physical details.
- The approach and anticipation matter more than the act. Focus on what the character *feels* — vulnerability, safety, joy, release.
- The aftermath is crucial: a morning-after scene, a quiet conversation, domestic tenderness. These moments anchor intimacy to relationship.

**Avoiding Pitfalls**
- Don't rush intimacy to meet a checklist. Wait until emotional stakes justify the scene.
- Avoid clinical or anatomically explicit language; suggestion and metaphor are more powerful.
- Never let an intimate scene feel obligatory or separate from character development.

## What to Avoid

**Character & Plot**
- [ ] Characters behaving out of character to create drama — every action must be motivated
- [ ] High drama or manufactured conflict (love triangles without purpose, infidelity, excessive angst)
- [ ] "Too stupid to live" protagonists lacking agency or decision-making capability
- [ ] Third-act breakups that feel arbitrary; earn any separation through misunderstanding, then earn the resolution

**Prose & Description**
- [ ] Purple prose, overwrought metaphor, or literary flourishes that slow emotional momentum
- [ ] Head-hopping between characters — stay faithful to one POV per chapter
- [ ] Graphic language, explicit content, or graphic intimate scenes (fade to black is the house style)
- [ ] Long static descriptions of setting or appearance; integrate details through character perception and action

**Narrative Choices**
- [ ] Telling readers how to feel ("She was heartbroken" vs. showing her frozen in place, unable to open his letter)
- [ ] Cheap sentimentality or pandering to reader emotions
- [ ] Unearned time skips or plot conveniences that bypass character work
- [ ] Mean-spirited banter, cruelty disguised as chemistry, or characters belittling each other

**Structural Issues**
- [ ] Chapters longer than 3,500 words or shorter than 2,000 (unless an exceptional scene warrants it)
- [ ] Cliffhangers that feel manipulative rather than organic to the story
- [ ] Ending scenes on resolution when you should escalate or leave a question open
- [ ] Excessive internal monologue or lengthy introspection — keep the pace moving

## Best Practices

**Authenticity & Heart**
- Ground stories in real-world obstacles: career changes, past heartbreak, skepticism about love, family complications.
- Let characters meet with humor, resilience, and the willingness to be vulnerable.
- Write hope without naiveté. Belief in love's redemptive power doesn't mean ignorance of human complexity.

**Humor as Thread**
- Weave humor throughout, even in tender moments. Self-aware characters who can laugh at themselves are more engaging.
- Humor often emerges from character personality and situation, not forced jokes.
- Witty banter builds intimacy between characters and reader alike.

**Belonging & Community**
- Emphasize chosen family, interconnected characters, and the sense of belonging as central to happiness.
- Small towns and tight communities naturally create supportive ensemble casts.
- Show how relationships — friendships, mentorship, community bonds — enable romantic growth.

**Earned Resolution**
- Every happy ending should feel deserved through genuine character growth, communication, and emotional work.
- Couples should earn connection through real conversation and mutual understanding, not circumstance.
- The satisfaction of closure comes from knowing the characters will actually make it work — not just that they kissed once.

## Reader Promise

Deliver a story that leaves readers believing in love, feeling seen and cared for, and departing with a sense of warmth and hope. The book should function as a refuge from high-stakes drama — a place where emotional connection matters most, good people navigate real obstacles authentically, and the promise of belonging feels tangible and earned.
