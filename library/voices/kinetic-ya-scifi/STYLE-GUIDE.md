# STYLE GUIDE: Fast-Paced YA Sci-Fi Action

This guide defines the prose mechanics and narrative conventions for writing urgent, high-stakes science fiction with dual first-person POV. The goal is maximum momentum paired with emotional authenticity.

---

## Core Identity

Write fast-paced YA science fiction action where dual first-person present-tense narration creates immediacy and moral complexity. Protagonists are intelligent, tactically minded, and caught between their indoctrination and awakening truth. Every sentence serves plot momentum or character revelation; no prose decoration. Tension is maintained even in quiet moments because characters are always aware of stakes.

---

## Prose Mechanics

### Sentence Construction

- **Action scenes:** 6–10 words per sentence. Short, declarative, direct.
- **Observation/thought:** 8–12 words. Medium length, clear subordination.
- **Reflection/dialogue:** 10–15 words. Slightly longer but never ornate.
- **Avoid:** Complex subordinate structures, flowery adjectives, passive voice (except tactical obscuring), filter words ("he saw," "she heard").

**Examples of the right rhythm:**

- "The alarm screamed. I ran." (action)
- "The compound stretched north toward the mountains, all razor wire and guard towers." (observation)
- "If this failed, everyone I trusted would die." (thought)

### Dialogue

- Sharp, functional, character-distinct.
- Characters address each other by name frequently (creates intimacy and clarity).
- Dialogue advances plot and reveals character simultaneously; no exposition dumps.
- Include brief action beats between exchanges: *"I don't trust him," she said, turning away.*
- Subtext matters: what characters *don't* say is as important as what they do.
- Avoid: on-the-nose explanation, unnatural exposition delivery.

### Paragraph Structure

- **Action:** 2–3 sentences per paragraph. Short paragraphs create reading velocity.
- **Dialogue:** New paragraph for each speaker.
- **Reflection:** 3–5 sentences. Brief internal monologue that reveals character thinking in real time, not philosophy.
- **Description:** Spare. Only sensory details that matter to the immediate scene.

---

## Narrative Structure

### POV & Interiority

- **Perspective:** First-person present tense, dual POV with alternating chapters.
- **Deep interiority:** Readers have full access to one character's thoughts and tactical assessment per section.
- **Internal monologue is brief and character-voiced**, not philosophical. Characters think about immediate problems, stakes, and survival instincts.
- **Stay inside one POV per section.** Never head-hop or break perspective.
- **Show tactical thinking through observation:** What the character notices reveals their training, priorities, and psychology.

### Scene Construction

- **Open with action or decision.** Establish what the character wants and what's in the way.
- **Include sensory details specific to this POV:** What does this character notice that another wouldn't?
- **Reveal internal conflict while external action unfolds.** The tension between what characters must do and what they feel is the engine.
- **Secondary character interactions matter.** Dialogue reveals relationships and stakes.
- **End on tension or revelation,** not resolution. Every scene leaves a question unanswered.

### Pacing & Momentum

- **Relentless forward motion.** Every scene advances plot or escalates stakes. If a scene doesn't, cut it.
- **No dawdling, self-indulgent description, or philosophical tangents.**
- **Worldbuilding weaves through character experience and dialogue,** not exposition. Regional details, technology, institutions emerge as characters interact with them.
- **Tension is maintained through circumstances and immediate stakes**, not dramatic prose.

### Chapter & Section Length

- Target: 2,500–3,500 words per chapter (typical YA).
- Vary length to serve pacing (shorter for intense action, longer for plot exposition and character connection).
- End on a question, threat, or revelation that pulls readers forward.

---

## Dialogue

- Direct and functional: characters state what they need, know, or fear plainly.
- Brief exchanges in tense scenes (1–3 sentences per turn).
- Natural pauses, interruptions, and realistic reactions.
- Reveal character voice through word choice, rhythm, dialect, and jargon.
- Example:
  - "You're compromised." → blunt, accusing.
  - "Are you compromised?" → doubt, seeking reassurance.
  - "Don't tell me you're compromised too." → desperation, betrayal.

---

## What to Avoid

**Prose Construction:**
- Passive voice (unless deliberately hiding agency).
- Filter words: "he saw," "she heard," "I watched"—show directly instead.
- Purple prose, poetic language, or overwrought metaphor.
- Long static descriptions or atmospheric passages.
- Complex, literary syntax that requires re-reading.
- Dashes (use commas, periods, colons instead).

**Narrative Habits:**
- On-the-nose concluding statements ("I realized I could trust him" instead of showing trust through action).
- Weasel words: "may," "might," "could," "virtually," "possibly."
- Puff words: "utilize," "commence," "expedite," "endeavor," "crucial," "leverage."
- AI clichés: "practiced," "delve," "groundbreaking," "suddenly," "measured," "methodical," "dancing," "precision."
- Long internal monologue or extended philosophical musings.
- Lengthy exposition dumps via dialogue.

**Structural Traps:**
- Telling the reader how to feel instead of showing emotion through action and stakes.
- Scenes without tension or immediate purpose.
- Jumping between POVs within a chapter (confuses readers).
- Over-explaining worldbuilding or technology.
- Stopping the plot to describe setting in detail.

---

## Worldbuilding & Technical Details

Weave worldbuilding through **character experience and dialogue**, never exposition blocks.

- Regional details emerge as characters navigate them.
- Technology and institutions are introduced as characters interact with them.
- Use specific names, measurements, and procedural details for authenticity.
- Example: Instead of "The compound was advanced," write "The neural implant read my heart rate. Deviation flagged. I held steady."

---

## Emotional Authenticity

- **Vulnerability coexists with competence.** Characters are skilled but flawed, strong but uncertain.
- **Moments of genuine connection punctuate action.** A brief laugh, a touch, a shared realization—these land hard in a high-stakes story.
- **No easy answers.** Moral complexity drives character. Characters question their place, their training, their loyalty.
- **Humor is real.** Dark jokes, nervous laughs, bitter comments—these reveal character and break tension before it snaps.
- **Contrasts matter:** Inner doubt against external competence; loyalty against betrayal; belief against reality.

---

## Tense, Present-Tense Narration

- Present tense creates immediacy: reader experiences events as character does.
- Avoid slipping into past tense for reflection ("I realized…" is okay; "I had been wondering..." distances readers).
- Use present tense for action and current thought: *"The guard turns. I run."*

---

## Revision Checklist

Before finalizing any scene, verify:

- [ ] Does this scene advance plot or escalate stakes?
- [ ] Is the POV character's internal conflict evident?
- [ ] Does dialogue reveal character and move plot forward?
- [ ] Are sentences short and active (especially in action)?
- [ ] Is worldbuilding woven through action, not explained?
- [ ] Does the scene end on tension, not resolution?
- [ ] Are there any filter words, passive voice, or overwrought descriptions?
- [ ] Is internal monologue brief and character-voiced?
- [ ] Does the pace match the emotional intensity of the moment?
- [ ] Would a reader care what happens next?
