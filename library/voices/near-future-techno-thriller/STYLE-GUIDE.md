# Style Guide: Pragmatic Near-Future Techno-Thriller

Rapid, procedurally authentic thriller where competent characters solve complex technical and corporate problems under time pressure. Tension builds through circumstances and stakes, not overwrought prose. Technology is present and important, but people and decisions drive the story.

---

## Core Identity

Write fast-paced technical narratives where clear, active prose prioritizes plot momentum and character agency. Technology is a tool or obstacle characters navigate, not a character itself. Tension emerges from ticking clocks, competing interests, and high-stakes technical/corporate obstacles—not from literary flourish or excessive interiority. Your prose is pragmatic and matter-of-fact; you let circumstances speak for themselves. Your protagonists are intelligent, resourceful, and solve problems through competence and clever thinking under pressure.

---

## Prose Mechanics

**Sentence Construction**
- 10–20 words typical. Active voice, direct construction.
- Favor simple, declarative statements: "He ran. The door exploded behind him."
- Avoid complex subordinate clauses or literary inversion.
- Short sentences in action; medium sentences in explanation.

**Paragraph Structure**
- 2–4 sentences in action sequences; 3–5 in description.
- Each dialogue turn in separate paragraph.
- Avoid single-sentence paragraphs except for dramatic emphasis.

**Vocabulary**
- Precise and accessible. No purple prose or obscure terminology.
- Technical terms integrated naturally without over-explanation.
- Specific numbers, measurements, brand names ground the world.
- Professional/corporate language mixed with natural speech.
- Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate ("get" not "obtain").

---

## Narrative Structure

**POV & Tense**
- Third-person limited, one character perspective per scene.
- Stay close to what the viewpoint character can perceive and think.
- Italics for brief, character-voiced internal thoughts—avoid extensive philosophical musings.
- Present or past tense acceptable if consistent.

**Scene Construction**
- Bold markdown scene headers with specific location, date, and time.
  - Example: "New York City, Monday, June 10, 2035, 10 am"
- Start late, end early. Enter at conflict or discovery; exit on raised stakes.
- Every scene advances plot or escalates tension. If not, cut it.
- 500–1500 words typical per scene.

**Pacing**
- Fast and propulsive. Characters constantly doing things, solving problems, responding to pressure.
- Multi-threaded POVs create dramatic irony and heighten suspense.
- Ticking-clock mechanism (deadlines, resource limits, technical failures) forces every scene to matter.
- Relentless forward momentum—no dawdling, minimal transitions.

**Technical Integration**
- Weave technical accuracy naturally into dialogue and action.
- Computer output in monospace or code-block format.
- Procedural details (security protocols, corporate hierarchies, near-future tech) add realism without slowing momentum.
- Show technical stakes through character action and decision-making, not lectures.

---

## Dialogue

**Authentic Voice**
- Natural, conversational, plot-advancing. Characters sound like real people in real situations.
- Address each other by name frequently—grounds conversation and creates natural pacing.
- Mix formal and casual speech based on context and character.
- Include realistic interruptions and quick exchanges.

**Functional Purpose**
- Dialogue reveals character, advances plot, and delivers information simultaneously.
- Avoid exposition dumps. Let characters interact naturally; readers piece information together.
- Brief action beats between dialogue: "She looked away before responding."
- Technical concepts and consent can be discussed plainly without being clinical.

---

## Descriptive Approach

**Scene-Setting**
- Minimal but effective. Only what's functionally relevant.
- Practical details: "There was a small table with a light on in the foyer."
- Skip extensive atmospheric passages unless they functionally matter to action or character.

**Technical Description**
- Focus on what characters can see, hear, physically interact with.
- Sensory details prioritize visual and auditory over smell/taste/touch.
- Only include sensory details that are plot-relevant or create immediate atmosphere.

---

## Character Voice

**Show Personality Through Actions & Speech**
- Not through internal narrative or description.
- Mix professional competence with human concerns and occasional dry humor.
- Brief, realistic internal reactions: "Shit," he thought.
- Balance technical expertise with vulnerability and authentic reaction.
- Characters think practically about immediate problems.

---

## What to Avoid

**Narrative Issues**
- Extensive internal monologue or philosophical musings.
- Purple prose or overwrought emotional descriptions.
- Long paragraphs of static description.
- Overly complex or literary sentence structures.
- Telling readers how to feel about events.
- Filter words: "he saw," "she heard," "they watched."
- Passive voice (except when tactical obscuring serves plot).

**Technical Issues**
- Over-explanation of technical concepts.
- Treating technology as separate from plot.
- Jargon without grounding in character experience.

**Pacing & Structure**
- Scenes without tension or forward momentum.
- Characters who don't actively solve problems.
- Chapters that meander or lack clear stakes.
- Excessive backstory or world-explanation.

---

## Practical Guidelines

**Show, Don't Tell**
- Reveal information through action and dialogue, not exposition.
- Character emotions through physical reactions: "His hands shook," not "He felt nervous."
- Background through natural conversation, not info dumps.
- World-building through incidental details during action.

**Pacing & Tension**
- Build tension through circumstances, time pressure, and stakes rather than dramatic prose.
- Keep scenes moving—characters should be doing things constantly.
- Include specific procedural details that add realism.
- Use ticking clocks (literal or figurative) to force momentum.

**Near-Future Technology**
- Neural implants, quantum computing, advanced surveillance, corporate AI—frame as tools characters use or obstacles they navigate.
- Specific but not didactic: mention the technology, show its effect, move on.
- Don't over-explain; let readers catch up through context.

**Multi-Character Handling**
- Each scene/chapter section focuses on one POV character.
- Clearly mark POV changes with scene headers (location/date/time).
- Maintain consistent voice within each character's perspective.
- Balance page time between major characters.

---

## Success Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:
- [ ] Is every sentence serving plot, tension, or character agency?
- [ ] Does the scene have clear stakes and end on raised tension?
- [ ] Is technical content integrated naturally through action/dialogue?
- [ ] Is pacing fast without sacrificing clarity?
- [ ] Does character voice come through despite minimalist prose?
- [ ] Are there no filter words or "telling" moments?
- [ ] Would readers want to continue immediately to the next scene?
- [ ] Is the balance of procedural realism and momentum maintained?
- [ ] Are technical details specific enough to feel authentic?
- [ ] Would a reader trust these characters to solve the central problem?

---

## Key Success Markers

- ✅ Momentum never sacrifices clarity
- ✅ Technology integrated as plot element, not exposition
- ✅ Characters are competent and resourceful
- ✅ Procedural realism creates credibility
- ✅ Multiple POVs create dramatic irony and suspense
- ✅ Time pressure forces every scene to matter
