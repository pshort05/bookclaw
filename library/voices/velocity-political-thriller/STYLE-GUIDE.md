# STYLE-GUIDE.md

## Core Identity

This is velocity-driven political and legal thriller prose: plot momentum over everything else. Your narrative is nearly invisible—a clear window into action and stakes rather than an ornament. Readers should feel the clock ticking, move through chapters as if compelled, and forget they're reading at all. Every sentence is doing essential work.

## Prose Mechanics

**Sentence Construction**
- Average 6–8 words in action sequences, 10–12 in exposition
- Short and declarative
- Avoid complex subordinate clauses
- Favor simple statements: "He ran. The door exploded behind him." not "As he ran toward safety, the door behind him violently exploded."
- Active voice (except tactical obscuring)

**Paragraph Structure**
- 1–4 sentences typical
- Single-sentence paragraphs for dramatic impact
- Frequent breaks create visual white space and reading velocity
- Never more than 5 sentences; cut to white space rather than pile on

**Vocabulary**
- Simple and common
- Anglo-Saxon over Latinate ("get" not "obtain")
- Active, concrete verbs ("ran" not "proceeded quickly")
- Adjectives sparingly; let nouns and verbs work
- Technical or institutional jargon only when necessary and immediately contextualized

## Narrative Structure

**POV Strategy**
- Third-person limited with multiple POVs per book
- Strategic jumps between protagonist, antagonist, secondary characters for dramatic irony
- Reader often knows danger the main character doesn't
- Fair-play clues for attentive readers; misdirection through selective information
- One POV per scene

**Chapter Architecture**
- 3–7 pages maximum
- Single clear focus
- Hard cuts between chapters (no transition prose)
- Mandatory hook ending: every chapter ends on an unanswered question, peril, critical discovery, looming threat, or withheld information
- Never resolve a chapter; always escalate

**Scene Construction**
- Start late, end early
- Enter at conflict or discovery, exit on cliffhanger
- 500–1,500 words typical
- Every scene advances plot or escalates stakes
- If it doesn't, cut it
- Ticking clock mechanism: time pressure forces every scene to matter

**Pacing Mechanisms**
- Velocity and momentum paramount
- Multi-threaded POVs create suspense through dramatic irony
- Relentless forward momentum
- No dawdling or self-indulgent description
- Tension maintained through circumstances and immediate stakes

**Setting Description**
- Minimal but precise
- Focus on details that affect action: "Three exits," "no windows"
- Institutional details for authenticity: "J. Edgar Hoover Building," "SCIF room"
- Don't lecture; integrate through character action and dialogue
- Avoid extensive atmosphere-building

## Dialogue

**Function and Voice**
- Functional, not clever
- Characters state what they need, know, fear plainly
- Brief exchanges—1–3 sentences per turn in tense scenes
- Deliver complex information through conversation
- Avoid exposition dumps; let characters interact naturally

**Technique**
- Frequent character name usage in dialogue
- Action beats between exchanges (brief, specific)
- Realistic interruptions and quick responses
- Subtext matters; avoid over-explanation

## What to Avoid

**Prose & Structure**
- Long, complex sentences with multiple clauses
- Literary or poetic language
- Extensive description or atmosphere-building
- Ambiguous prose requiring re-reading
- Obscure vocabulary
- Passive voice (except tactical obscuring)
- Filter words ("saw," "heard," "watched")

**Pacing & Plot**
- Chapters longer than 7 pages
- Scenes without clear stakes
- Chapter endings on resolution
- Meandering scenes without clear purpose
- Characters slowing momentum with introspection or philosophy

**Character & Tension**
- Experimental structure or unreliable narration
- Character development prioritized over plot
- Explaining stakes repeatedly
- Lengthy internal monologue or philosophical musings

**Common Pitfalls**
- Artificial tension (characters creating drama instead of reacting to circumstances)
- Over-sold emotion (let stakes speak for themselves)
- Delay or withholding information to manipulate readers unfairly
- Cheating with cliffhangers (fake danger or fake mysteries)
