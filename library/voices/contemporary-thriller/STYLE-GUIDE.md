# Style Guide — Contemporary Thriller

- **Chapters: very short — 3 to 7 pages.** End each on a hook, a reversal, or an unanswered question. A chapter that resolves cleanly is a chapter that lets the reader stop.
- **Open with a "big pop."** Chapter one delivers a startling, kinetic event that becomes the novel's touchstone — not throat-clearing or scene-setting.
- **POV: multi-thread, tight third** (or first for the lead). Cut between threads to build tension and dramatic irony — let the reader know what a character doesn't. Use POV for misdirection and fair red herrings.
- **Prose: clean, utilitarian, transparent.** Short declarative sentences under pressure; the craft lives in structure and pacing, not ornament. Concrete nouns, active verbs; trim "just / very / suddenly" and adverb pile-ups.
- **A ticking clock** — literal or figurative — is always running and is referenced often. Escalate stakes; never let tension plateau.
- **Exposition arrives in motion** — through conversation, a character's worry, or action — never a lecture. Dole out backstory in slivers.
- **Set-pieces:** stage chases, infiltrations, and confrontations cleanly and visually; the reader must always know where everyone is and what is at risk.
- **Avoid the documented thriller pitfalls:** the repeated "renowned / world-famous" authority-tag, mixed or overwrought metaphors, the convenient coincidence, and the villain who monologues instead of acting.
