# Intimacy Framing — Romantasy

Write this intimate scene the way romantasy readers expect it: the emotional
and power-dynamic stakes carry the scene, the fantasy world's rules stay
present, and the payoff is the shift in the bond between the two leads, not a
choreography inventory.

## Ground the scene in the bond, not just the body

- Open with what each character risks by being here — a vow, a rivalry, a
  vulnerability neither has shown before, the fear of being truly seen or
  truly known. Name it internally before (or instead of) narrating it.
- This is the moment the relationship's power dynamic is tested. Track the
  Power-Over -> Power-With arc explicitly: does this scene deepen mutual
  respect and consent, or does it let one partner's protection override the
  other's autonomy? The latter is a genre failure regardless of heat level.
- Track the emotional turn, not just the physical one — what changes between
  these two people by the scene's end that wasn't true at its start?

## Keep the world alive in the scene

- Do not let the intimate scene become a bubble outside the story. Weave in
  the world's magic system, its costs, or the stakes hanging over the pair
  (a curse, a bargain, a war, a bond) wherever it is natural — the fantasy
  and the romance are interdependent, even here.
- Favor sensation and mood over a clinical inventory of positions or acts;
  use the five senses to keep the reader inside the POV character's body and
  subjective experience (Female Gaze: her pleasure, her agency, her point of
  view, never an objectifying external camera).

## Consent and agency, always

- Consent is present, active, and legible on the page — never assumed, never
  glossed over, even under a fated-bond or magical-compulsion trope. If a
  bond or curse creates pull, the choice to act on it is still made freely
  on the page.
- A character's "no" or hesitation is honored immediately and without
  narrative punishment. The FMC (or the POV lead) retains her right to make
  dangerous choices; a partner's protectiveness never overrides her autonomy.

## Stay within the book's ceiling

- Match the explicitness of this scene to the book's declared content
  ceiling — do not escalate past what the author has set, regardless of how
  far the moment could otherwise go.
- When in doubt, imply and let the reader's imagination complete the beat
  rather than spelling out more than the ceiling allows.

## After the scene

- Land the emotional and power-dynamic consequence in the scene that
  follows — a look, a changed dynamic, a piece of vulnerability that can't
  be unsaid, a shift the fantasy plot can now build on. The intimacy should
  move both the romance and the story, not just fill a chapter quota.
