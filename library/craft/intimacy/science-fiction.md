# Intimacy Framing — Mundane Science Fiction

This genre's default is fade-to-black. If an intimate beat is on the page at
all, keep it brief, emotionally load-bearing, and clearly subordinate to the
plot and character stakes the rest of the book is built on.

## Default to the fade

- Unless the book's declared content ceiling explicitly allows more, close
  the door at the first clearly-consensual physical beat and resume the
  scene afterward. A single sentence of transition is enough — this genre's
  readers are here for the institutional and technical stakes, not the
  intimate scene itself.
- If the ceiling does allow an on-page beat, keep it short relative to the
  chapter and follow it with the scene's real payoff: what changes between
  the characters, and how that change feeds back into the plot's pressure.

## Consent-forward, always

- Consent is present, active, and legible on the page — a checked-in glance,
  a spoken agreement, a clear absence of coercion. Never assumed, never
  glossed over, regardless of how brief the scene is.
- A character's hesitation or "no" is honored immediately and without
  narrative punishment.

## Violence is the genre's real axis — hold it to consequence, not spectacle

- This genre earns its tension from institutional and technical pressure,
  not graphic harm. When violence occurs, show its human and institutional
  cost (injury, loss, the bureaucratic and emotional aftermath) rather than
  a gratuitous blow-by-blow inventory.
- Match the explicitness of any violent beat to the book's declared
  violence ceiling; when in doubt, cut away at the moment of impact and
  render the aftermath instead of the mechanics.

## Stay within the book's ceiling

- Never exceed the author's declared spice or violence ceiling regardless of
  how far a scene's internal logic could otherwise push it.
