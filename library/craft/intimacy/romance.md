# Intimacy Framing — Romance

Write this intimate scene with the emotional stakes doing the heavy lifting, not
the choreography. The reader is here for connection, vulnerability, and
consequence — the physical is in service of the emotional arc, never a
substitute for it.

## Ground the scene in emotional stakes

- Open with what each character risks by being here — trust, pride, a secret,
  a fear of being truly seen. Name it internally before (or instead of)
  narrating it.
- Track the emotional turn, not just the physical one: what changes between
  these two people by the scene's end that wasn't true at its start?
- Let interior monologue carry as much weight as action. A held breath, a
  half-formed doubt, a flash of an earlier wound — these do more work than an
  additional physical beat.

## Sensory grounding over graphic inventory

- Favor sensation and mood (warmth, weight, the catch of breath, a scent
  remembered) over a clinical inventory of positions or acts.
- Use the five senses to keep the reader in the character's body and point of
  view — this is felt, not watched.
- Pace with white space and short sentences at the peak; let the prose breathe
  the way the characters do.

## Consent-forward, always

- Consent is present, active, and legible on the page — a checked-in glance, a
  spoken "yes," a hand that could pull away and doesn't. Never assumed, never
  glossed over.
- Power imbalances (age, authority, vulnerability) are acknowledged in-text,
  not ignored for the sake of heat.
- A character's "no" or hesitation is honored immediately and without
  narrative punishment.

## Stay within the book's ceiling

- Match the explicitness of this scene to the book's declared content ceiling
  — do not escalate past what the author has set, regardless of how far the
  moment could otherwise go.
- When in doubt, imply and let the reader's imagination complete the beat
  rather than spelling out more than the ceiling allows.

## After the scene

- Land the emotional consequence in the scene that follows — a look, a
  changed dynamic, a piece of vulnerability that can't be unsaid. The intimacy
  should move the relationship, not just fill a chapter quota.
