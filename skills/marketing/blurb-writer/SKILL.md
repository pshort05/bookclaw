# Blurb Writer

Craft blurbs that make readers click "Buy Now."

## Blurb Formula (Fiction)
1. **Hook** — One explosive sentence
2. **Character** — Who are we rooting for? What makes them relatable?
3. **Setup** — What's their world? What do they want?
4. **Inciting Incident** — What goes catastrophically wrong?
5. **Stakes** — What happens if they fail? (Personal + universal)
6. **Cliffhanger** — End with a question that DEMANDS the reader buy the book
7. **Social Proof** — Comp line or review quote

## Blurb Formula (Nonfiction)
1. **Problem** — What pain point does the reader have?
2. **Promise** — What will this book deliver?
3. **Credibility** — Why should they trust this author?
4. **Preview** — What will they learn? (3-5 key takeaways)
5. **Transformation** — Who will they become after reading?
6. **CTA** — Why read this NOW?

## Length Guidelines
- Amazon description: 150-200 words (with HTML formatting)
- Back cover: 100-150 words
- One-line pitch: Under 25 words (see below)
- Elevator pitch: Under 50 words

## The One-Line Pitch (make a stranger want the book)

A one-line pitch is storytelling, not a summary. Its only job is to make the
listener immediately want to read the book — so build it from three
ingredients, not a plot recap:

1. **Surprise** — an unexpected element that snags attention.
2. **Mystery** — an open question the listener now needs answered.
3. **Conflict** — what the protagonist wants set against what opposes them.

Model: *"A meek, abused girl in suburban London discovers she can levitate —
and the adults around her immediately look for a way to profit from it."*
(Surprise: levitation. Mystery: how will they profit? Conflict: her wants
vs. theirs.)

Use it on the back cover and everywhere the book is mentioned; practice it
until it rolls off the tongue. If you cannot make someone want the book in
one sentence, the book's appeal isn't yet clear in your own mind — solve
that first.

## Comp Titles (positioning by comparison)

The "Social Proof" beat above leans on comps — books/media the reader
already knows and likes. Honest comps are one of the strongest selling
tools; refusing to comp ("nothing else is like it") reads as not being well
read. Frame comps one of these ways:

- **"X meets Y"** — e.g., "*The Girl on the Train* meets HBO's *Girls*."
- **"On the shelf alongside …"** — peers in the same genre lane.
- **"Will appeal to fans of …"** — titles the ideal reader has read.
- **"In the vein of …"** — a couple of ballpark comps.

Avoid: dead authors (use modern ones), runaway megahits (*Harry Potter*
meets *Hunger Games* reads as cliché), and comping nothing at all. Other
media (TV, film, musicals) is fair game when it fits.

## Anti-Patterns (Avoid)
- Starting with a question (overdone)
- "In a world where..." (cliché)
- Giving away the ending
- Using the word "must" more than once
- Being vague instead of specific
