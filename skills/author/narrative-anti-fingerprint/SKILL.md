# Narrative anti-fingerprint

Purpose: avoid the discourse-level patterns that cluster AI-generated fiction
into a single detectable region of narrative space. These tells live in plot
architecture, time handling, and how meaning is delivered — NOT in word choice.
Polishing prose does not remove them; only structural choices do. Follow the
critical directives in the draft itself, because they are expensive to fix later.

## Genre gate (read first)

- If the work is FICTION: apply all sections below.
- If the work is NON-FICTION (essay, argument, guide, memoir-as-argument):
  apply ONLY "Emotion delivery" and "Voice and reader engagement." A stated
  thesis, linear progression, and clear resolution are correct in non-fiction —
  do NOT treat them as tells. The non-fiction tell is flat, uniform competence
  and body-routed emotion, not structure.

## Critical (structural — get these right in the draft)

1. Do not state the theme. No character or narrator should articulate what the
   story means, name its lesson, or deliver a summarizing reflection. No
   back-cover "takeaway" line. Let the final image or action carry meaning and
   stop. Do not use dialogue as a vehicle for philosophical debate that
   co-authors the theme.

2. Do not tell the story strictly front-to-back. Break chronology on purpose:
   open in the middle, withhold the inciting event, use flashback, flash-forward,
   or ellipsis. Strict linear order is the default to avoid.

3. Vary emotional delivery. Do not route every feeling through physical
   sensation (tightening chest, cold sweat, racing pulse). Sometimes name the
   emotion plainly and move on; sometimes leave it unspoken. Mix registers.

## Secondary

4. Resist the single clean track. Include at least one subplot or thread that
   does not tidily serve the main theme, and let something go unresolved. Avoid
   the neat protagonist-driven ending resolved by internal understanding.

5. Allow moral ambivalence. The protagonist should not be cleanly sympathetic
   or clearly right. Leave genuine ambiguity in their choices.

6. Point at the real world. Where natural, name specific real books, authors,
   places, or brands rather than vague allusions ("an old novel," "a distant
   city").

7. Voice and reader engagement. Where the voice allows, consider direct reader
   address or a fourth-wall break rather than a hermetically sealed narrative.
   (This directive also applies to non-fiction: avoid a closed, impersonal
   register when the piece would benefit from addressing the reader.)

## Counter my own model defaults

8. Let intensity genuinely escalate. Do not flatten every peak into restraint.

9. Vary the narrative voice within the piece rather than holding one uniform
   register throughout.

10. Where a convention appears, subvert it rather than gracefully extending it.
    Avoid tidy epilogues as a reflex.

## What NOT to do with this skill

- Do not use it to justify rewriting flagged prose for style; the signal is
  structural. If a fix requires new material (a subplot, added ambivalence,
  a reordered timeline), surface it for author decision rather than inventing
  it silently.
- Do not apply the fiction directives to non-fiction (see genre gate).
