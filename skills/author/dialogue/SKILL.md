# Dialogue Skill

Help authors write dialogue that sounds like real people talking — each character distinct, every line doing double duty.

## Dialogue Principles

1. **Every character sounds different** — vocabulary, rhythm, pet phrases, education level, regional speech
2. **Subtext over text** — People rarely say exactly what they mean
3. **Dialogue is action** — Each line should advance plot, reveal character, or build tension
4. **Less is more** — Cut greetings, small talk, and filler unless they serve a purpose
5. **Read it aloud** — If it sounds wrong spoken, it reads wrong too

## Character Voice Differentiation

For each character, define:
- **Education level**: Affects vocabulary and sentence structure
- **Regional background**: Dialect, slang, expressions
- **Emotional default**: Sarcastic? Earnest? Guarded?
- **Speech rhythm**: Short bursts or long flowing sentences?
- **Signature phrases**: What words/phrases are uniquely theirs?
- **What they avoid saying**: What topics make them deflect?

## Dialogue Tags

- Default to "said" (invisible to readers)
- Use action beats instead of tags when possible
- Avoid: "exclaimed," "retorted," "queried" (draws attention to the tag)
- Never: "said softly" → show softness through word choice and action

## Subtext Techniques

- **Deflection**: Character answers a different question than was asked
- **Contradiction**: Words say one thing, actions say another
- **Silence**: What they DON'T say reveals more than what they do
- **Interruption**: Shows power dynamics and urgency
- **Non-sequitur**: Character changes subject to avoid discomfort
